Ep 47Random Effects

▶ Watch the video 8:00
On this pageThe 30-second versionWhy this existsThe ideasTrapsSay it out loudRecall drillConnectionsGlossary

Fixed effects throws away all the between variation, which costs you statistical power and any hope of studying time-constant variables — so random effects instead assumes the individual effects follow a distribution, buying precision back with an assumption, and its better variants let you keep the between variation and the within variation separately.

The 30-second version

  • Fixed effects works, but it discards a lot of interesting information.
  • Cost 1: you can't study anything that's purely between — birthplace has no within variation, so it's gone.
  • Cost 2: poor statistical power, because you threw out so much information.
  • Random effects doesn't remove all the between variation. It constrains the individual effects to follow a distribution.
  • Any assumption buys precision and risks being wrong. Plain random effects needs the individual effect to be unrelated to the controls and the treatment — usually false.
  • Hierarchical linear modeling predicts where you sit in that distribution using measurable characteristics. Common — more common than fixed effects in many fields.
  • Correlated random effects (the Mundlak estimator) includes both the mean and the demeaned version of each variable, separating between from within.

Why this exists

This chapter has been about the difference between between variation — Austria has different geography than England — and within variation — Austria's politics differ from one year to the next. Fixed effects controls away all the between variation and leaves you with the within, and it's effective at that.

There are downsides.

The ideas

1

The two costs of fixed effects

Plain English. You binned a lot of real information, and the bin had useful things in it.

Precisely.

Cost 1 — you can't study anything that's purely between. Want to know the effect of your birthplace? You can't use fixed effects to study that, because birthplace has no within variation. You threw out all the between variation, and anything interesting in there went with it.

Cost 2 — poor statistical power. You're discarding a lot of information you actually have, and discarding information makes your estimates less precise.

Anchor image. Throwing out the packaging to get at the contents — and finding the receipt was in the packaging.

2

What random effects does

Plain English. Don't delete the individual differences. Assume they follow a pattern.

Precisely. Random effects tries to deal with the between variation without getting rid of all of it — improving statistical performance and letting you study some of what lives in the between variation, while still closing some of the back doors you care about.

The mechanism: individuals really are different — Austria differs from England differs from France; you differ from me differs from our neighbour down the street. But instead of letting the individual effects be anything at all, random effects forces them to follow a distribution. You're willing to say something like: these effects follow a normal distribution.

The trade. Any time in statistics that you make an assumption, you give yourself back some statistical power — and run the risk that if the assumption is wrong, your results are bad. By constraining the individual effects rather than letting them run free, you may not remove all the between variation, but you get precision back.

Where it breaks. Doing just that doesn't always work well. For plain random effects to really close those between-variation back doors, you have to assume the individual effect is unrelated to all the control variables and the treatment — which is usually not the case.

3

Hierarchical linear modeling

Plain English. Don't just assume a distribution — predict where each individual sits in it.

Precisely. Hierarchical linear modeling is very common — probably more common than fixed effects in a lot of fields, though economics tends to prefer fixed effects.

It says: we have these individual effects, we'll allow them to follow distributions — and we'll use measurable characteristics to predict where you are in that distribution. So there's an individual effect for you, and things like your geography, which can be measured, are used to predict where your individual effect sits.

Why that helps. It gives more information about where you are on the distribution, so you understand your individual effect better — and crucially, it's now okay if your individual effect is related to geography, because you've incorporated that relationship into the estimation. That was exactly the assumption plain random effects couldn't survive.

Anchor image. Not just "people vary in height" but "and taller parents predict taller children." Explaining the variation instead of merely allowing for it.

Bonus. You don't have to stop at the individual effect. You can also model how the effect of one variable on another varies with individual characteristics — making it an alternative to interaction terms in a standard regression.

4

Correlated random effects and the Mundlak estimator

Plain English. Put both the individual's average and their deviations from it into the model.

Precisely. Correlated random effects is the other route: again allow the individual random effect to be related to characteristics you can measure. The typical implementation is the Mundlak estimator.

One variant is beautifully simple. Recall demeaning — take each individual's average and subtract it out. Here you do that, and use the demeaned variable in the model — but you also include the mean itself.

So you have two versions of each variable:

VersionWhat it carries
The meanThe between variation
The demeaned variableThe within variation

Put both in the model and you've separated the between and within variation, so you can use whatever mix of the two you want. Here's the between, here's the within — do what you like.

5

Where this leaves you

Plain English. Plain random effects blends between and within. The advanced versions hand you both, labelled.

Precisely. Standard random effects doesn't get rid of all the between variation — it gives you a mix of the between effect and the within effect. Use a more advanced method like hierarchical linear modeling or correlated random effects and it says: here's the between effect, here's the within effect, do what you will — improving your statistical modelling in the process.

Where it breaks. Random effects isn't always the right answer, but it's an attractive alternative to fixed effects for controlling away between variation, with nice statistical properties — as long as the assumptions you need actually hold. Those assumptions usually concern the relationship between the individual effect (or at least whatever part of it you haven't modelled with covariates, if you're using correlated random effects or hierarchical linear modeling) and the treatment variable you care about.

Say it out loud

Fixed effects works — but look at what it costs you. It deletes all the between variation, and that has two prices.

First, you can no longer study anything that only exists between people. Want the effect of your birthplace? Birthplace never changes within a person, so fixed effects has thrown out exactly the variation you'd need. Second, you've discarded a huge amount of information, which means your estimates get less precise.

Random effects is the alternative. Instead of deleting the individual differences, it says: fine, people differ — but those differences follow a distribution. Assume they're normally distributed, say.

And that's the classic statistical trade. Every assumption you make buys you precision and exposes you to being wrong. Here you get power back, at the cost of a claim about how individuals vary.

The problem with plain random effects is that for it to really close your back doors, the individual effect has to be unrelated to your controls and your treatment — which is usually just not true.

So there are two better versions. Hierarchical linear modeling doesn't just assume a distribution — it uses things you can measure, like geography, to predict where in that distribution you sit. Which means it's now fine for your individual effect to be related to geography, because you've modelled that relationship. And it's very widely used — probably more common than fixed effects outside economics.

And correlated random effects, usually via the Mundlak estimator, does something I find very tidy. You demean as usual — but you also put the individual's mean into the model. Now you've got two versions of every variable: the mean carries the between variation, the demeaned one carries the within. Both are in there, separately. Here's between, here's within, use whichever you want.

Recall drill

QWhat does fixed effects do to the between variation?

It controls all of it away, leaving only within variation.

QGive the two downsides of that.

You can't study anything that exists only between individuals, and you lose statistical power by discarding information.

QWhy can't fixed effects study the effect of birthplace?

Birthplace has no within variation — it never changes for a person — so the variation you'd need was exactly what got removed.

QWhat does random effects do instead?

It constrains the individual effects to follow a distribution rather than removing them, so it doesn't eliminate all the between variation.

QWhat's the general trade being made?

Adding an assumption buys back statistical power but risks bad results if the assumption is wrong.

QWhat assumption does plain random effects need, and why is that a problem?

That the individual effect is unrelated to all the control variables and the treatment — which is usually not the case.

QWhat does hierarchical linear modeling add?

It uses measurable characteristics — like geography — to predict where an individual sits in the distribution of individual effects.

QWhy does that fix the assumption problem?

Because it's now acceptable for the individual effect to be related to geography — that relationship is incorporated into the estimation rather than assumed away.

QHow common is hierarchical linear modeling?

Very — probably more common than fixed effects in a lot of fields, though economics tends to prefer fixed effects.

QWhat extra thing can hierarchical linear modeling do?

Model how the effect of one variable on another varies with individual characteristics, as an alternative to interaction terms.

QDescribe the Mundlak variant of correlated random effects.

Demean each variable as usual and include the demeaned version — but also include the individual's mean itself in the model.

QWhat does each of those two versions carry?

The mean carries the between variation; the demeaned variable carries the within variation.

QWhat does including both accomplish?

It separates the between and within variation so you can use whatever mix of them you want.

QWhat does plain random effects give you, versus the advanced versions?

Plain random effects gives a mix of the between and within effect. The advanced versions give you the between effect and the within effect separately.

QWhat do random effects' assumptions usually concern?

The relationship between the individual effect — or whatever part of it you haven't modelled with covariates — and the treatment variable you're interested in.

Glossary

Random effects
treating individual effects as draws from a distribution rather than removing them.
Hierarchical linear modeling
random effects where measurable characteristics predict an individual's position in the distribution of effects.
Correlated random effects
random effects allowing the individual effect to relate to measured characteristics.
Mundlak estimator
a correlated random effects approach including both the individual's mean and the demeaned variable.
