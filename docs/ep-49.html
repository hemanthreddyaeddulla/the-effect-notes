Ep 49Interrupted Time Series

Fit a time trend before the event, fit another after, and look at where they meet — and if you centre time at the event date, the whole thing becomes a regression with an interaction term where the jump you care about is just β₂.

The 30-second version

  • Fit a trend in the pre-treatment period, fit a trend in the post-treatment period, see where they line up at the cutoff.
  • Mathematically quite similar to regression discontinuity, a few chapters ahead.
  • Implement it with a regression on time, an after-treatment indicator, and their interaction.
  • Centre time so it equals zero at the event. Then the intercepts are the predictions at the cutoff, and the jump is β₂.
  • Pre-treatment line: intercept β₀, slope β₁. Post-treatment: intercept β₀ + β₂, slope β₁ + β₃.
  • You can add polynomials — just interact them with the after indicator too.
  • Two serious cautions: autocorrelation makes you over-reject, and the result is very sensitive to functional form.

Why this exists

Event studies compare what you saw to what you predicted you'd see. The big question is how do you build that prediction?

Interrupted time series is probably the simplest way, and it's a good method when you think there's likely to be a consistent trend over time, then another consistent trend afterwards, and you're interested in the difference between the two trends.

The ideas

1

The method

Plain English. Draw a line through the before, draw a line through the after, look at the gap where they meet.

Precisely. First fit a time trend — perhaps by regression — in the pre-treatment period. Then fit a time trend in the post-treatment period. Then look at where those two trends meet up at the cutoff.

Worked example of the shape. Suppose you have a declining trend beforehand, heading downward. Then you fit the post-treatment trend and it starts up higher. You predicted the trend would have continued down to here, and instead you ended up at there — that's a jump.

That's the name: a time trend going along, then the event interrupts it, and it moves on to something else.

Anchor image. A pencil line drawn along a ruler, the ruler nudged, and the line continuing from a different place. The offset at the join is what you're measuring.

Where it fits. This is, at least mathematically, quite similar to regression discontinuity, which arrives a few chapters later.

2

Implementing it with a regression

Plain English. Outcome on time, an after-indicator, and their interaction — with one crucial recentring.

Precisely. You could use advanced time series methodology to make a forecast, and honestly it's probably better if you do. But a simpler route is linear regression with an interaction term.

Regress the outcome on:

  • time — a continuous measure, maybe the week or year the observation was made
  • after — a binary indicator for being in the post-treatment period
  • time × after — the interaction

The crucial adjustment: take the time variable and subtract the cutoff date from it, so that time equals zero at the moment the event goes into effect. Now the interpretation is very straightforward:

PeriodafterInterceptSlope
Pre-treatment0β₀β₁
Post-treatment1β₀ + β₂β₁ + β₃

In the untreated period, after is zero so the β₂ and β₃ terms fall away, leaving β₀ + β₁·t.

Why centring matters so much. What you usually want is how things jumped at the cutoff — the immediate change at the moment treatment went into effect. And the intercept is the prediction at time zero. Since you just centred time so that zero is the event date, the prediction at the event is simply the intercept.

So compare the intercept in the untreated period, β₀, against the intercept in the treated period, β₀ + β₂. The difference — the jump at the intercept, which is the jump at the event — is β₂.

β₂ is your treatment effect.

3

Worked example: ambulance quality improvement

Worked example. A study of a quality improvement programme for ambulances — a way of making ambulances coordinate information better. Does it improve their performance? The treatment went into place at a particular time, and the question is what changed about the effectiveness rating.

Graphically, fit a straight line either side of the cutoff:

  • Left of the cutoff (untreated): a straight line sloping upwards slightly — but only a little.
  • Right of the cutoff (treated): an entirely separate line.

What you see at the cutoff: no real jump in the prediction. Which suggests there wasn't an immediate effect of the policy going into place.

But the slope gets steeper afterwards. So the reading is: no immediate effect, but the quality improvement programme seems to have made things better slowly, over time.

4

Two cautions

Caution 1 — you don't need a straight line, but functional form matters enormously.

You can add polynomials quite easily — just add an interaction between your after indicator and all the polynomial terms, which lets you fit non-straight shapes that your data may require.

But this is an obvious downside of the approach: it's very sensitive to the choice of functional form. It isn't a willy-nilly decision whether to use a linear form or a polynomial — it has big implications for what effect you find.

Why: a curve makes its weirdest predictions near the edges. A straight line might predict one thing at the end; add a curve and the middle barely changes while the edge changes a lot. And the edge is exactly where you're looking — right at the point the event goes into effect.

Anchor image. Extrapolating a curve is like pointing a laser. A tiny change in angle barely moves the beam nearby and moves it enormously at the far end.

Caution 2 — autocorrelation, which you almost certainly have.

Standard errors here are difficult, because you're likely to have autocorrelation in the error term — see the regression chapter. An observation in one period is likely correlated with the observation in the next.

If you run an interrupted time series with a plain regression, no bells and whistles, no adjustment for autocorrelation, you tend to over-reject the null. That is: you're likely to say you've found effects when you haven't found anything at all. Not good.

Say it out loud

Interrupted time series is the simplest way to build that counterfactual. Fit a line through the data before the event. Fit a separate line through the data after. Then look at where they meet at the cutoff — is there a jump?

And you can do this with a plain regression: outcome on time, on a binary indicator for being after, and on their interaction.

But here's the trick that makes it clean. Subtract the event date from your time variable, so that time equals zero exactly at the event. Why does that help? Because the intercept is your prediction at time zero — and time zero is now the event. So the intercept literally is the prediction at the cutoff.

Which means: before the event, the intercept is β₀. After the event, it's β₀ + β₂. So the jump — the whole thing you came here to measure — is just β₂. One coefficient.

Nice example: a programme to make ambulances coordinate information better. Fit a line before, a line after, and at the cutoff — no jump at all. So no immediate effect. But the slope gets steeper afterwards. So the programme didn't do anything on day one; it made things better gradually.

Two things to be careful about. First, autocorrelation. In time series data, this period is correlated with next period, and if you ignore that your standard errors are wrong in a specific nasty direction — you over-reject, meaning you'll announce effects that aren't there.

Second, functional form matters more than you'd think. Straight line or polynomial isn't a casual choice, because a curve does its strangest stuff at the edges — and the edge is exactly where you're taking your measurement.

Recall drill

QDescribe the interrupted time series method.

Fit a time trend in the pre-treatment period, fit another in the post-treatment period, and look at where they meet at the cutoff to see whether there's a jump.

QWhen is it a good method to use?

When you think there's a consistent trend before, another consistent trend after, and you're interested in the difference between them.

QWhat later design is it mathematically similar to?

Regression discontinuity.

QWhat three terms go into the regression implementation?

Time (a continuous measure), a binary indicator for being after treatment, and their interaction.

QWhat's the crucial adjustment to the time variable?

Subtract the cutoff date so that time equals zero at the moment the event goes into effect.

QWhy does centring time make interpretation straightforward?

Because the intercept is the prediction at time zero, and time zero is now the event — so the intercepts are the predictions at the cutoff.

QGive the intercept and slope in the pre-treatment period.

Intercept β₀ and slope β₁ — with after equal to zero, the β₂ and β₃ terms fall away.

QGive the intercept and slope in the post-treatment period.

Intercept β₀ + β₂ and slope β₁ + β₃.

QWhich coefficient is the treatment effect, and why?

β₂ — it's the difference between the two intercepts, which is the jump in the prediction at the event date.

QWhat did the ambulance quality improvement study find?

No jump at the cutoff, so no immediate effect — but a steeper post-treatment slope, suggesting the programme improved things slowly over time.

QHow do you allow for non-straight lines?

Add polynomial terms, interacting each of them with the after indicator as well.

QWhy is functional form choice so consequential here?

Because a curve makes its strangest predictions near the edges, and the edge — the event date — is exactly where you're measuring the jump.

QWhat's the standard error problem, and what specifically goes wrong?

Autocorrelation — one period's observation is correlated with the next's. Ignoring it makes you over-reject the null, so you'll claim effects that don't exist.

Glossary

Interrupted time series
fitting separate time trends before and after an event and measuring the discontinuity between them.
Centring the time variable
subtracting the event date so that time equals zero at the cutoff, making intercepts the predictions there.
Over-rejecting the null
declaring effects that aren't there, the consequence of ignoring autocorrelation.
Functional form
the shape assumed for the fitted trend, whose choice strongly affects the estimated jump.
