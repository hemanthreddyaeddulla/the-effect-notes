Ep 27Reading Regression Tables

▶ Watch the video 16:00
On this pageThe 30-second versionWhy this existsThe ideasTrapsSay it out loudRecall drillConnectionsGlossary

Every cell in a regression table is the same three things — estimate, standard error in parentheses, stars for the p-value — and the fit statistics underneath (R², adjusted R², F) describe the model as a whole, which for causal work you should mostly ignore.

The 30-second version

  • A regression table is compact and lets you compare models side by side. Each column is one regression.
  • Rows are the predictors, plus an intercept (a.k.a. constant, β₀, α).
  • Each cell: estimate, then in parentheses a standard error — or sometimes a t statistic — then significance stars.
  • Stars encode the p-value against a default null of coefficient = 0, and they stack.
  • What the stars mean varies by field. Look for the table note.
  • Below: number of observations, then model fit statistics — R², adjusted R², F statistic.
  • For causal inference, R² is mostly irrelevant. A clean randomized experiment often has a small R² and a very good model.

Why this exists

After that rant, something straightforward. A regression table is just a table with regression results on it — a very common presentation because it's compact, easy to read, and lets you show several models at once, all read the same way.

The ideas

1

The layout

Plain English. Columns are models. Rows are variables. The stuff at the bottom is about the model, not any one variable.

Worked example. Two regressions predicting restaurant health inspection scores:

(1)(2)
Intercept……
Number of locations−0.019<br>(0.000)***…
Year of inspection−0.065<br>(0.006)***
Observations27,178…
R²0.065…

The top names the dependent variable — what you're predicting, your y. Here it's inspection scores in both columns.

The first row is the intercept, which some tables call the constant; in an equation it's β₀ or α.

Then the predictors — the independent variables, your x and your controls. Here: number of locations (for chain restaurants) and year of inspection. Column 1 uses only number of locations; column 2 includes both.

2

Reading a single cell

Plain English. Three numbers stacked: what you estimated, how precise it is, how surprising it is under a null of zero.

Worked example. Take −0.019 in column 1.

1 · The estimate. The first number is the estimated coefficient. If you wrote out y = β₀ + β₁ × number of locations, this is your β₁-hat — the estimated slope on number of locations is −0.019.

2 · The parentheses. Below sits 0.000, a standard error. Some tables instead show a t statistic — the coefficient divided by the standard error. A table note usually says which; if not, here's the heuristic:

  • Significant coefficients with small values in parentheses → standard errors
  • Significant coefficients with large values in parentheses → t statistics

And 0.000 doesn't mean the standard error is actually zero — it can't be. It means it's smaller than the smallest value the table is willing to display.

3 · The stars. Those asterisks are significance stars, representing the p-value. By default in essentially all regression tables, the null being tested is that the coefficient is zero.

So: assume the slope on number of locations is zero, build a sampling distribution centred on zero with a standard error of (here) roughly zero, and ask how unlikely it would be to see an estimate of −0.019 or further away in either direction. That probability is the p-value, and the stars encode it.

In this table — the standard convention in economics:

StarsMeaning
*p < 0.1
**p < 0.05
***p < 0.01

Other fields commonly use * for 0.05, ** for 0.01, *** for 0.001. Look for the table note.

Three stars therefore means p < 0.01 — significant if you'd chosen a 0.01 cutoff. And you can read it in reverse: had you picked 0.01 as your threshold, this coefficient clears it. Stars stack, because if p < 0.01 then it's also below 0.05 and 0.1.

Column 2's year-of-inspection cell reads the same way: estimate −0.065, standard error 0.006, p < 0.01.

Where it breaks. Depending on how the table is built, standard errors sometimes sit beside the coefficient rather than below. That's fine — just a formatting choice.

3

Model fit statistics

Plain English. Numbers at the bottom describing the whole model rather than any coefficient.

Number of observations. Here, 27,178.

R². The correlation between your model's predicted values and the actual outcome, squared. It represents the proportion of the outcome's variance explained by the model.

With R² = 0.065: doing 1 − 0.065 = 0.935 says that if you split the outcome into the part your model predicted and the part left in the residual, 6.5% of the variation is explained and 93.5% is not. It partitions the outcome into in-the-model and out-of-the-model.

Where R² breaks — and it gets misused a lot.

  • R² measures only how much variance you explained. It does not tell you how good the model is.
  • For causal work you want to estimate one parameter very well. The concern is whether −0.019 reflects the causal effect of number of locations on inspection score. That plenty of other things drive inspection scores simply doesn't matter to you.
  • Never change your model to raise R².
  • The clinching example: a randomized controlled experiment has very clean, very easy causal identification, and often a small R², because a lot is going on in the outcome that the randomly assigned treatment doesn't explain. R² looks bad; the model is excellent.
  • If your only goal is prediction, R² makes more sense — that's what it's about. Even then, there are probably better measures.

Overall, in this book, you can more or less ignore R². It's a nice quick diagnostic with a sharp interpretation — the share of outcome variation inside versus outside your model — but that's all it does, and it isn't answering a question you care about.

Adjusted R². R² will always go up as you add variables, because more predictors means more flexibility to fit the data — and that's true even if the variables you add are complete nonsense. Adjusted R² instead asks whether the variables you included explain more than you'd expect from adding random noise.

Nick's view: he uses adjusted R² less than R², because R² is much easier to interpret as a share while adjusted R² muddles that — and since he isn't especially concerned with R² anyway, and has already done the work of removing nonsense predictors while constructing the model, he doesn't need adjusted R² to do it for him. He flags this as a more contentious opinion.

F statistic. A form of hypothesis test. A model with no predictors at all would just predict the mean inspection score for everybody. Adding predictors will always improve on that, because you have more flexibility. The F statistic asks: is the improvement more than you'd expect by random chance, given how many predictors you added?

The null is that all your predictors have coefficients of zero. The F statistic asks how unlikely the coefficients you observed would be under that null — and if the probability is very small, you reject that they're all zero.

Say it out loud

A regression table looks dense but it's the same three things repeated in every cell.

Columns are models. Rows are variables. Say I'm predicting restaurant health inspection scores, and my first model uses just the number of locations a chain has.

In that cell I see −0.019. That's the estimate — the slope. Then underneath, in parentheses, 0.000 — that's the standard error. And no, it isn't actually zero; it's just smaller than the table will print. Sometimes what's in parentheses is a t statistic instead — check the note. Rough rule: on significant coefficients, small numbers in parentheses are standard errors, big numbers are t statistics.

Then the stars. Those are the p-value, and the null is always the same by default: the coefficient is zero. So you assume zero, build the sampling distribution, and ask how weird −0.019 would be. In economics, one star is p under 0.1, two is under 0.05, three is under 0.01 — but that varies by field, so read the note. And they stack: three stars means you'd also clear the looser thresholds.

Then at the bottom, the model fit stuff. Number of observations — fine, useful. And then R², which is the share of the outcome's variance your model explains. Here it's 0.065, so 6.5% explained, 93.5% unexplained.

And here's the thing I want you to take away: for causal work, basically ignore R². I care whether that −0.019 is the causal effect. I do not care that lots of other things also affect inspection scores.

Best proof of that? A randomized controlled experiment — the cleanest identification you can get — usually has a terrible R², because the randomly assigned treatment doesn't explain much of what's going on in the outcome. Bad R², excellent model. So please never adjust a model to push R² up.

Recall drill

QWhat does each column in a regression table represent?

A separate regression model.

QWhat three things appear in a typical coefficient cell?

The estimated coefficient, a standard error (or sometimes a t statistic) in parentheses, and significance stars.

QThe parentheses show 0.000. What does that mean?

The standard error is smaller than the smallest value the table displays — it isn't literally zero.

QHow do you tell a standard error from a t statistic when there's no table note?

On significant coefficients, small values in parentheses suggest standard errors and large values suggest t statistics.

QWhat is a t statistic?

The coefficient divided by its standard error.

QWhat null hypothesis do significance stars test by default?

That the coefficient is zero.

QIn the economics convention used here, what do one, two, and three stars mean?

p < 0.1, p < 0.05, and p < 0.01 respectively — though conventions differ by field, so check the table note.

QWhy do stars stack?

Because if p < 0.01 it is necessarily also below 0.05 and 0.1, so significance at a tight threshold implies significance at looser ones.

QDefine R².

The squared correlation between the model's predicted values and the actual outcome — equivalently, the proportion of the outcome's variance explained by the model.

QR² is 0.065. State what that partitions.

6.5% of the variation in the outcome is explained by the model; 93.5% remains in the residual.

QWhy is R² largely irrelevant for causal inference?

Because you want to estimate one parameter well. Whether other things also drive the outcome doesn't bear on whether your coefficient is the causal effect.

QWhat's the example showing a low R² can accompany an excellent model?

A randomized controlled experiment — identification is very clean, but the randomly assigned treatment often explains little of the outcome's variation.

QWhat does adjusted R² fix, and why?

R² always rises when you add predictors, even nonsense ones, because more flexibility fits the data better. Adjusted R² asks whether the added variables explain more than you'd expect from random noise.

QWhat is Nick's view on adjusted R²?

He uses it less than R², because R² is easier to interpret as a share, he isn't concerned with R² anyway, and he removes nonsense predictors while building the model rather than relying on a statistic to do it. He flags this as contentious.

QWhat does the F statistic test?

The null that all your predictors have coefficients of zero — asking whether your model's improvement over simply predicting the mean is more than you'd expect by random chance given how many predictors you added.
