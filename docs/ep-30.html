Ep 30Logarithms in Regression

▶ Watch the video 16:30
On this pageThe 30-second versionWhy this existsThe ideasTrapsSay it out loudRecall drillConnectionsGlossary

Logarithms fix two problems at once — they scrunch skewed variables so billionaires don't drag your line around, and they convert additive changes into proportional ones — at the cost of an interpretation step, which you can do exactly or with the approximation that log(1 + small) ≈ small.

The 30-second version

  • Reason 1 — skew. In social science, a few observations are many times bigger than the mass. OLS minimises squared residuals, so it chases the billionaire.
  • Reason 2 — proportional relationships. Some relationships should multiply rather than add. Interest rates don't add dollars to your wealth, they multiply it.
  • Logs keep the big values in the data but scrunch them, so they still count without over-influencing.
  • Logs also give proportional interpretations, because log(x) + log(y) = log(x·y) turns addition into multiplication.
  • The mantra still works — it's just about a one-unit change in the log, so you need a conversion step.
  • Approximation: log(1 + a) ≈ a for small a. Breaks down fast — good to about 10%, useless at 50%.
  • Logs can't take zero or negative values, and zeros are a real problem.

Why this exists

Polynomials let a straight line curve. Logarithms solve two different problems, and they get enormous use in the social sciences because we deal with a lot of highly skewed variables.

Remember the framing: it's fine to fit a straight line between some non-straight things. That's what lets you model non-straight relationships with a procedure that only draws straight lines.

The ideas

1

Reason 1: skew and over-influential outliers

Plain English. A few observations are astronomically bigger than everyone else, and OLS contorts itself to accommodate them.

Precisely. A skewed variable has a mass of relatively similar observations and a few that are way different. Crucially, it isn't just that there are outliers — it's that the outliers are so many times bigger than the mass.

Worked example. Wealth. A typical person has a few thousand dollars, maybe tens of thousands, maybe a hundred thousand depending on the group. Then there's someone with billions and billions.

Why OLS struggles: it minimises the sum of squared residuals. Suppose it's describing the mass of people well — and then there's someone whose wealth it's off by billions on. That residual is enormous, so the model changes to fit that one person well.

Anchor image. A see-saw with a hundred children on one end and a cargo ship on the other. It doesn't matter how many children there are.

Where it breaks — the honest nuance. Outliers aren't necessarily wrong data, and they should affect your analysis — they're part of your data. The problem is only that you don't want them to have undue, over-large influence on your model. That's really what the concern about outliers is.

Also worth noting: in the social sciences the skew is almost always in this direction. There aren't many variables where a lot of people are very large and a few are very, very tiny.

2

Reason 2: relationships that should be proportional

Plain English. Some things multiply rather than add, and a straight line can't express that.

Worked example. Your wealth over time depends on what's in your savings and the interest or investment return on it. Regress wealth on investment return — that shouldn't be a straight-line relationship. If the return rate goes up by a certain percentage, your wealth doesn't rise by a certain number of dollars; it rises by a certain percent. If the interest rate goes up by 0.01, that should multiply your wealth by 1.01, not add a fixed amount.

3

What logs do about both

Plain English. They squash the giants without deleting them, and they turn plus into times.

Precisely.

  • Skew: logs take those very large values and keep them in the data but scrunch them down, so there's less distance between them and the mass. They're still counted; they just don't pull as hard.
  • Proportionality: logs have a convenient proportional interpretation, because log(x) + log(y) = log(x·y).

The mechanism. A regression gives you additive changes — "a one-unit increase in X is associated with a coefficient-sized increase in Y." But adding 1 is the same as adding log(e¹). And log(x) + log(e) is log(x·e). So an additive one-unit increase in the log becomes a multiplication of the original x by e. That's how a linear increase becomes a proportional one.

4

Interpreting: the exact way

Plain English. Push the coefficient back through the log algebra until you get a percentage.

Precisely — two directions.

Log on the left: regress log(y) on x.

a one-unit change in x  →  β₁-unit change in log(y)
β₁  =  log(e^β₁)                    [natural logs, always, in social science]
log(y) + log(e^β₁)  =  log(y · e^β₁)

So y has increased proportionally by e^β₁.

Log on the right: regress y on log(x). What's the effect of a 1% increase in x?

a 1% increase means  x · 1.01
log(x · 1.01)  =  log(x) + log(1.01)
so log(x) rose by log(1.01) units
therefore y rises by  β₁ · log(1.01)

Where it breaks. That's the accurate route: convert the linear change your regression gives you into a proportional change you can actually interpret — in either direction.

5

Interpreting: the approximation

Plain English. For small numbers, log(1.05) is basically 0.05. So percentages and log-units are nearly the same thing.

Precisely. log(1 + a) ≈ a when a is small:

ExpressionApproximately
log(1.01)0.01
log(1.05)0.05
log(1.1)0.1

Where it breaks — quickly. This is an approximation and it degrades fast as the change grows. You would not say a 50% increase in x (a 1.5 proportional increase) is about a 0.5 increase in log(x). That no longer works. Keep the change around 0.1 (10%) or lower; above that, do it properly.

Worked example — three walkthroughs. Start from the mantra and convert one stage at a time.

Regress y on log(x):

  1. A one-unit change in log(x) is associated with a β₁-sized change in y.
  2. Scale everything down by 100 so the approximation applies: a 0.01-unit change in log(x) is associated with a 0.01·β₁ change in y.
  3. A 0.01-unit change in log(x) is approximately a 1% change in x.
  4. → A 1% increase in x is associated with a 0.01·β₁ unit increase in y.

Regress log(y) on x:

  1. A one-unit change in x is associated with a β₁-unit change in log(y).
  2. Scale down if needed — if β₁ is already around 0.01 you're fine; if β₁ is 3, you've got work to do. Divide by 100: a 0.01-unit change in x is associated with a 0.01·β₁ change in log(y).
  3. Convert the log change to a percentage.
  4. → A 0.01-unit change in x is associated with roughly a β₁ percent change in y.

Regress log(y) on log(x): conveniently the approximations cancel — whatever conversion you do gets converted back — so you can work more simply:

→ A 1% change in x is associated with a β₁ percent change in y.

6

The zero problem

Plain English. log(0) doesn't exist, and lots of real variables contain zeros.

Precisely. Logarithms can't accept negative values or zero.

  • Negatives: rarely a problem. It's uncommon to have a variable taking negative values that you'd want to log anyway.
  • Zeros: a genuine problem. Income is highly skewed and plenty of people earn zero, and you'd want them in your model. log(0) is undefined.

The fixes:

  • If the log is on the left (log of y): you can probably drop the logarithm entirely and use something like a Poisson regression.
  • If the log is on the right (log of x): you can't switch to Poisson. So people fudge — add one and take log(1 + x), or use the inverse hyperbolic sine transformation, which does something similar.

Where it breaks. Both fudges let you estimate the model, include the zeros, and solve the skew just like a regular log. But they mess up the percentage interpretation, particularly at smaller values of x — which makes sense, because what is a percentage increase from a base of zero? The calculations get weirder, and you want your variables to take large values, because things are strangest near the bottom.

Which produces a genuinely odd result: your percentage interpretation works better if you report income in cents than in thousands of dollars.

Say it out loud

Logs show up everywhere in social science, and there are exactly two reasons.

First: skew. Think about wealth. Most people have a few thousand, maybe tens of thousands. Then there's someone with billions. And remember what OLS does — it minimises squared residuals. So it's fitting everybody nicely, and then it hits the billionaire and it's off by billions, and it warps the whole line to accommodate that one person.

Worth being careful here: outliers aren't wrong data, and they should count. You just don't want them to have wildly outsized influence. And logs do exactly that — they keep the giants in your data but scrunch them down so they stop yanking the line around.

Second: some relationships should multiply, not add. Regress my wealth on my investment return. If the rate goes up, my wealth doesn't go up by a fixed number of dollars — it goes up by a percent. A straight line can't say that. A log can, because log(x) plus log(y) equals log(x times y) — addition on the log scale is multiplication on the real scale.

Interpreting them is the only annoying part. The mantra still works, it's just about a one-unit change in the log, which you don't care about. So you convert. And there's a beautiful shortcut: log of 1-point-something is basically that something. log(1.01) ≈ 0.01. log(1.05) ≈ 0.05.

Which means for log(y) on log(x), it all collapses to: a 1% change in x is associated with a β₁ percent change in y. Lovely.

Just don't push it — that approximation dies fast. A 50% change is nowhere near 0.5 in logs. Keep it to about 10%.

And the practical headache: log of zero doesn't exist, and lots of people earn zero income. If the log is on your outcome, drop it and use Poisson. If it's on your predictor, people add one and take log(1+x), or use the inverse hyperbolic sine — which works, but quietly wrecks the percentage interpretation near the bottom.

Recall drill

QGive the two reasons to take a logarithm.

To handle a highly skewed variable, and to model a relationship that's proportional rather than additive.

QWhat makes a variable skewed in the sense that matters here?

A mass of relatively similar observations plus a few that are many times bigger — not merely the presence of outliers.

QWhy does skew cause trouble for OLS specifically?

OLS minimises squared residuals, so an observation it's off by billions on generates an enormous residual, and the model shifts to fit that one point.

QAre outliers bad data that should be removed?

No. They aren't necessarily wrong and they should affect your analysis. The problem is only that they can have undue influence.

QGive the proportional-relationship example.

Regressing wealth on investment return. A higher return rate doesn't add a fixed number of dollars — it multiplies your wealth, so the relationship isn't a straight line.

QWhat log identity makes proportional interpretation work?

log(x) + log(y) = log(x·y) — addition on the log scale is multiplication on the original scale.

QYou regress log(y) on x and get β₁. By what factor does y change per one-unit change in x?

y increases proportionally by e^β₁.

QYou regress y on log(x). What's the exact effect of a 1% increase in x?

log(x·1.01) = log(x) + log(1.01), so log(x) rises by log(1.01) units, and y rises by β₁·log(1.01).

QState the approximation and its limits.

log(1 + a) ≈ a for small a — good to roughly 10%. A 50% increase is not approximately 0.5 in logs.

QGive the interpretation for a log-log model.

A 1% change in x is associated with a β₁ percent change in y — the approximations cancel.

QWalk through y on log(x) using the approximation.

A one-unit change in log(x) gives a β₁ change in y; scale down by 100 to get a 0.01-unit change in log(x) giving 0.01·β₁; a 0.01 change in log(x) is about a 1% change in x; so a 1% increase in x is associated with a 0.01·β₁ increase in y.

QWhat values can logarithms not accept, and which is the real problem?

Negative values and zero. Zero is the real problem — negatives rarely come up in variables you'd want to log.

QYour outcome is logged and contains zeros. What can you do?

Drop the logarithm and use something like a Poisson regression.

QYour predictor is logged and contains zeros. What are the options and the cost?

Use log(1 + x) or the inverse hyperbolic sine transformation. Both allow estimation, include the zeros, and solve skew — but they distort the percentage interpretation, especially at small values.

QWhy does the unit of measurement start to matter with those fudges?

Because the interpretation is weirdest near the bottom of the scale, so percentages behave better if income is measured in cents rather than thousands of dollars.

Proportional relationship

Natural logarithm
the log to base e; always what's meant in social science.
