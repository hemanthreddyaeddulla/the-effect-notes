Generate your own data with a random number generator, run your method on it, and check whether you get back the answer you built in — because you chose the truth, which almost never happens with real data.

The 30-second version

  • Simulation = generate data yourself, apply your method, look at the result.
  • Reason 1: find out whether a method works, without writing a proof.
  • Reason 2: find out when it breaks, and how badly.
  • Reason 3 — the big one: you pick the truth. You know what the right answer is, so you know whether the method found it.
  • Method: start with data satisfying every assumption, confirm it works, then break things one at a time.
  • Some broken assumptions barely matter. Some ruin everything. Simulation is how you tell which.
  • The book has code in three languages; the videos stay generic.

Why this exists

Simulation is an important tool to have in the applied statistician's work belt — and it answers a question that's been hanging over this whole series. You've been told a lot about what certain methods do and don't do, when they perform well and when they don't.

How would you check any of that for yourself?

You could write a statistical proof, working out the estimator's properties in mathematical terms. That sounds very difficult, and it isn't especially intuitive — you can reach the answer and still not understand it on a gut level unless you genuinely think in maths. Simulation is something you can do yourself.

The ideas

1

What simulation is

Plain English. Make up data on purpose, run your method on it, see what comes out.

Precisely. Generate your own data, typically using some kind of random number generator, then apply your methods to that data and look at the result.

2

Reason 1 and 2: does it work, and when does it break?

Plain English. Not just "is this method good" but "how wrong does it go when I violate its assumptions?"

Worked example. You've been told regression gives you the causal estimate if you've controlled for everything needed to remove omitted variable bias. But what if you haven't?

  • How bad is the bias going to be?
  • Is it worse or better if the omitted variable is strongly related? Weakly? Negatively? Positively?
  • What if it's more related to the treatment than the outcome? More related to the outcome than the treatment?

Every one of those is a question simulation can answer.

3

Reason 3: you pick the truth

Plain English. You built the data, so you know the right answer. Real data never tells you that.

Precisely. Because you're generating the data yourself, you know what the right answer is — which is rarely the case with actual data. So when you apply your method, you know what it should return if it's working properly. If you get something else, you know either the method is broken or the data you gave it isn't a good setting to apply that method in.

Anchor image. Calibrating a thermometer in ice water. You already know it's 0°C — that's the entire point.

4

The method: start clean, then break things

Plain English. Confirm it works when everything is perfect, then sabotage one thing at a time.

Precisely. Start with a dataset that satisfies all the assumptions the estimator has — the model is linear, you've controlled for everything — and check that it works there. If it does, start breaking things one at a time. Make one of the true model features non-linear, and see whether a linear model is really wrong, or whether it seems to work okay anyway.

Where it breaks — in both directions. There are a lot of cases in statistics where you can break an assumption and it makes things wrong, but only by a little bit. Simulation is a good way to ferret those out. There are also cases where breaking something messes things up a really great deal, and simulation finds those too.

Nick's own practice. He runs a simulation whenever he faces a new method he isn't sure about, or just wants to try something out. Sometimes he has an idea for an estimator and doesn't want to do all the work of writing a proof — so a simulation is a first attempt to see whether it seems to work at all. He does this quite regularly.

5

A note on code

Simulation is inherently something that involves a lot of coding, and these videos have deliberately avoided code. The book contains all the coding examples — in three different languages, which is why the videos don't reproduce them. For the actual implementation, go to Chapter 15 and find your preferred language.

Say it out loud

Here's a question that should have been bothering you this whole time: I keep telling you what methods do and when they work — how would you ever check any of that yourself?

One option is to write a proof. Difficult, and honestly not that enlightening unless you genuinely think in maths. The other option is simulation, and it's something you can just do.

The idea is simple: you generate the data yourself, with a random number generator, then run your method on it and see what comes out.

And the reason that's powerful is the bit people miss. You pick the truth. You built the data, so you know the real answer — which basically never happens with real data. So when your method spits out a number, you can just check it. If it doesn't match what you put in, either the method is broken or that's a bad setting for it.

Which means you can start breaking things on purpose. Build data where every assumption holds, and confirm your method works. Then sabotage one thing — make the true relationship curved, say — and see whether the linear model actually falls apart or whether it holds up fine anyway. Because that's the thing: lots of broken assumptions barely matter, and a few are catastrophic, and there's often no way to know which is which except by trying it.

Recall drill

QWhat is simulation, in this context?

Generating your own data — typically with a random number generator — applying your methods to it, and seeing what result you get.

QWhat's the alternative to simulation for checking an estimator's properties, and why is it worse?

Writing a statistical proof. It's very difficult, and unless you think in maths it can be hard to understand the answer on a gut level.

QGive the three reasons to run a simulation.

To find out whether a method works, to find out when and how badly it breaks, and because you get to pick the truth so you know what the right answer is.

QWhy is knowing the truth so valuable?

Because with real data you almost never know it. Knowing it means you can tell whether your method returned the right answer.

QYour method returns the wrong answer on simulated data. What are the two possible conclusions?

The method is broken, or the data you gave it isn't a good setting in which to apply that method.

QGive examples of questions you could ask about omitted variable bias by simulation.

How bad is the bias when you fail to control for something — and is it worse or better if the omitted variable is strongly or weakly related, negatively or positively related, or more related to the treatment than the outcome?

QWhat's the recommended procedure for building a simulation study?

Start with data satisfying all the estimator's assumptions and confirm it works there, then break things one at a time and see what happens.

QWhat are the two kinds of finding you're hunting for?

Assumptions you can break with only a little damage, and assumptions whose violation ruins the results.

QWhen does Nick personally run a simulation?

Whenever he faces a new method he isn't sure about, or has an idea for an estimator and doesn't want to do the work of writing a proof — as a first check on whether it seems to work.

QWhy do these videos avoid simulation code?

The book contains the coding examples in three different languages, and he wasn't going to record each video three times. Chapter 15 has them.

Glossary

Simulation
generating data yourself with known properties and applying a method to it to test how the method behaves.
Random number generator
the tool used to create simulated data with chosen statistical properties.
The truth
the parameter value you deliberately built into your simulated data, against which the estimate is judged.
