Ep 61Regression Discontinuity

▶ Watch the video 13:40
On this pageThe 30-second versionWhy this existsThe ideasTrapsSay it out loudRecall drillConnectionsGlossary

Where a smooth change is followed by a sudden one at a cutoff, the people just on either side of that cutoff are essentially identical except that one got treated — so comparing within a narrow bandwidth closes every back door, including ones you could never measure.

The 30-second version

  • The setup: a running variable changes smoothly, a cutoff produces a sudden jump, and a bandwidth narrows you to the comparable cases.
  • San Diego to Tijuana: a few miles takes income from $85,000 to $50,000. A few feet takes it from $50,000 to $25,000.
  • Why it works: $12,001 and $11,999 of income are basically the same people, but only one qualifies.
  • This is a front door design, like instrumental variables — not a close-the-back-doors design.
  • You can't control for the running variable — it determines treatment entirely. You control for it everywhere except around the cutoff.
  • Applies to geographic borders, close elections, and any policy with a threshold.
  • Probably the most trusted design after randomized experiments — though Nick suspects future statistical power problems.

Why this exists

Regression discontinuity is all about smooth changes followed by big changes at cutoffs. And the cutoff is where the attention goes.

Worked example — the border. San Diego sits at the very southern end of California, and it's fairly wealthy: median income $85,000, quite a bit above the national average. Downtown is roughly at the northern end, and as you move south toward Mexico, average incomes fall. By the time you reach San Ysidro, the neighbourhood just above the Mexican border, average incomes are more like $50,000.

Then you get out of your car, and there's the border, with all the stands of people guarding it. You walk a few feet — from San Ysidro into Tijuana — and incomes drop precipitously, to something like $25,000.

It took a few miles to fall from 85 to 50. It takes a few feet to fall from 50 to 25.

A continuous change as you go further south, and then a big change all at once when you cross. That is a discontinuity in the relationship between how far south you are and how much income there is.

The ideas

1

The three pieces

Plain English. Something that changes gradually, a line it crosses, and how close you stay to that line.

TermWhat it isIn the border example
Running variableThe continuous variable that changes slowly and determines when you pass the cutoffHow far south you are
CutoffThe point at which something changes abruptlyThe US–Mexico border
BandwidthA narrow range around the cutoff, giving a cleaner comparisonOnly people living very near the border

Why the bandwidth matters. Going further south predicts lower income all by itself. So if you simply compare everyone in Mexico against everyone in the United States, one of those groups is further south than the other, and that influence contaminates the comparison.

But focus on people living just on one side of the border against people just on the other, and they're about as south as each other — while being on different sides. Comparing within a narrow range around the border strips out the influence of "going south" and isolates the influence of "crossing the border."

Anchor image. Two houses on opposite sides of a street that happens to be a county line. Same weather, same soil, same shops — different laws.

2

Where it applies

Plain English. Anywhere a threshold flips something on.

  • Geographic borders — a literal national border, or one determining police jurisdiction, and therefore which kind of policing you're exposed to.
  • Close elections — vote share moves smoothly. Party A is winning at 50-something percent, then 50.1%, then 50.01% — and a tiny change from 50.01% to 49.9% dramatically changes who won the election. So you can see the effect of having one politician rather than another.
  • Threshold policies — say a poverty alleviation policy you qualify for by earning less than a set amount. To find its effect on your future chances of a job: as your income falls, that's related to your future prospects anyway. But right at the cutoff — at $12,001 you don't get it, at $11,999 you do.
3

Why it identifies anything

Plain English. At the cutoff, which side you land on is essentially random.

Precisely. The causal diagram:

Whether you're treated is entirely determined by whether you're above or below the cutoff, which is determined by the running variable. And there are plenty of back door variables related to how far south you are — or, in the policy case, how much income you have. Almost certainly you couldn't control for them all.

But this isn't a design where you control for things. Like instrumental variables, it's a design where you isolate a front door. By looking only at a bandwidth around the cutoff, you focus on the part of the data where there isn't really any variation in the running variable, and it becomes rather like being randomly assigned to one side or the other.

Worked example. Yes, lots of back door stuff relates to income. But compare people earning $12,001 against people earning $11,999. Those are basically the same people — the same on pretty much every characteristic you can imagine — except one got treated and the other didn't. It's almost random which of those you happen to earn in a given year: maybe one of them showed up to work fifteen minutes early one day, got an extra fifteen minutes on their paycheck, and that pushed them over the edge.

So they're comparable, one was treated and one wasn't, and you close all the back doors — even for things you cannot measure.

4

Why you can't just control for the running variable

Plain English. It determines treatment perfectly, so controlling for it leaves nothing.

Precisely. If you tried to control for the running variable directly, that wouldn't work — the running variable is the only thing determining treatment, so there'd be no variation left.

What you're actually doing is controlling for all parts of the running variable except the part just around the cutoff. You're controlling for how "south" affects income as you move through San Diego, and how it affects income as you move through Mexico — but not for how south affects income in that tiny area around the border. Isolating just that part isolates a relationship between going south and the outcome that has a causal interpretation.

5

How much people trust it

Plain English. Highly — arguably more than anything except a randomized experiment.

Precisely. Right now regression discontinuity is probably the causal inference research design that people trust most, aside from randomized experiments. There are a number of literal government policies stating that evidence will only be accepted as high quality if it comes from a randomized experiment or a regression discontinuity design.

Is that justified? Nick isn't sure — he thinks there may be statistical power issues with regression discontinuity in future, which comes up later.

Why it's compelling. It's very intuitive: you can genuinely see how it picks out a part of the variation amounting to random assignment. The assumptions are fairly plausible even if statistical power is a bit weak, and you get nice, intuitive, easy-to-understand results — graphs where a variable changes as it leads up to the cutoff and then, at the cutoff, changes right away.

That's convincing, because when you look at variation in the running variable before the treatment comes into play, there's no real effect; when you look after, no real effect; but right where the treatment changes is exactly where the effect appears. Which tells you the treatment is probably what did it.

Where it breaks. It only applies where treatment is assigned based on a cutoff, which isn't always the case. So the places you can use it are somewhat limited — but when you can, it's very good.

Say it out loud

Here's the picture that makes regression discontinuity obvious.

San Diego is a wealthy city — median income about $85,000. Drive south through it toward Mexico and incomes fall steadily; by San Ysidro, the last neighbourhood before the border, you're at around $50,000. Fine, gradual decline over several miles.

Now get out of the car and walk a few feet across the border into Tijuana. $25,000.

Miles to lose 35 thousand. Feet to lose another 25. That sudden drop is the discontinuity, and it's the whole design.

Three pieces. The running variable — how far south you are. The cutoff — the border. And the bandwidth — how close you stay to it. And the bandwidth is doing the crucial work, because comparing all of Mexico to all of the US is contaminated by the fact that one is further south. But people living a hundred metres either side of the border are equally south. Only one thing differs.

Best version of this intuition: a policy you qualify for by earning under $12,000. Compare someone on $11,999 to someone on $12,001. Those are the same people. Same everything. It's practically random which side you land on — maybe one of them showed up fifteen minutes early one shift and earned two dollars more. But one gets the policy and one doesn't.

And notice what that buys you: you've closed every back door, including ones you couldn't measure and couldn't even name.

One thing that trips people up — you can't just control for the running variable, because it determines treatment perfectly and you'd have nothing left. What you actually do is control for it everywhere except right at the cutoff.

Recall drill

QWhat structure does regression discontinuity require?

A smooth change in some variable followed by a sudden change at a cutoff.

QGive the San Diego to Tijuana figures.

Median income around $85,000 in San Diego, about $50,000 in San Ysidro just north of the border, and about $25,000 in Tijuana just across it.

QWhat makes that a discontinuity rather than just a decline?

It takes a few miles to fall from 85 to 50, but only a few feet to fall from 50 to 25 — a sudden change at a specific point.

QDefine the running variable.

The continuous variable that changes smoothly and determines whether you fall above or below the cutoff.

QDefine the bandwidth and explain why it matters.

A narrow range around the cutoff. It matters because comparing everyone on each side confounds the treatment with the running variable itself — but people just either side of the cutoff have nearly the same value of the running variable.

QName three settings where regression discontinuity applies.

Geographic borders (including policing jurisdictions), close elections, and policies with a qualifying threshold.

QHow do close elections form a discontinuity?

Vote share moves smoothly, but a tiny change from 50.01% to 49.9% dramatically changes who won — so you can compare the effect of having one politician rather than another.

QWhy are people earning $12,001 and $11,999 comparable?

They're essentially the same on every characteristic you can imagine, and which side you land on is almost random — someone arriving fifteen minutes early once could tip it.

QIs this a back door or front door design?

A front door design, like instrumental variables — it isolates variation that's as good as random rather than measuring and controlling confounders.

QWhy can't you control for the running variable directly?

It's the only thing determining treatment, so controlling for it leaves no treatment variation.

QWhat are you actually controlling for instead?

All parts of the running variable except the portion just around the cutoff.

QHow highly is regression discontinuity regarded?

Probably the most trusted design after randomized experiments — some government policies accept evidence as high quality only from randomized experiments or regression discontinuity designs.

QWhat reservation does Nick raise?

He suspects there may be statistical power problems with regression discontinuity.

QWhy are regression discontinuity graphs so convincing?

Because there's no real effect from the running variable before the cutoff, none after, but a clear change exactly at the point treatment changes.

QWhat limits where you can use it?

It only applies where treatment is assigned based on a cutoff.
