Ep 36Five Questions About Matching

▶ Watch the video 17:50
On this pageThe 30-second versionWhy this existsThe ideasTrapsSay it out loudRecall drillConnectionsGlossary

Matching demands five decisions — what counts as close, whether to pick matches or use weights, how many matches, how fast the weights decay, and how bad a match is too bad — and every one of them is the same bias–variance trade-off wearing a different hat.

The 30-second version

  • Q1 — What's the matching criterion? What does "similar" mean? Easy with one variable (just the difference); hard with several, which needs a distance measure.
  • Q2 — Matches or weights? Pick the closest control observations and discard the rest, or keep many and weight them by closeness.
  • Q3 — If matches, how many? Each extra match is a worse match, but grows the comparison group.
  • Q4 — If weights, how do they decay? How fast, and by what kernel — triangular, Epanechnikov, others.
  • Q5 — What's the worst acceptable match? Beyond some distance you drop the match — and possibly drop the treated observation entirely.
  • Every one is bias versus variance. Some procedures let you skip a question or two; usually you face all five.

Why this exists

The goal of matching is a treated group and a control group that are comparable across all the variables you match on. Every time you match on a variable, you close a back door. But how exactly do you do that?

There turn out to be five decisions, and this episode walks each one. To keep it tractable it uses a single matching variable — not how you'd normally run a matching study, where you'd match on several, but much simpler to follow.

The running example. Data on 30,000 credit card holders in Taiwan in 2005: their monthly bills, and whether they repaid, for both April and September. The question is the stickiness of credit problems — is being late on your April payment related to being late in September?

RoleVariable
TreatmentWere you late with your bill in April?
OutcomeWere you late with your bill in September?
Matching variableThe size of your April bill (continuous)

Why that back door: bigger April bills mean you're likely to have bigger September bills too, so big bills make you more likely to be late in both. You want to isolate how late you're willing to be rather than simply having difficulty paying.

The ideas

1

Q1 · What is the matching criterion?

Plain English. You have to define what "similar" means before you can find similar people.

Precisely. You want people with similar April bill sizes. But you can't require they be exactly the same — if a treated person's bill is 47,432, essentially nobody in the control group has that exact figure, and you'd match nobody at all. So for a continuous variable you must decide what it means to be close.

Anchor image. "Nearby" is meaningless until you say whether you're walking or flying.

Worked example. With one variable it's straightforward: take the difference. One person's bill is 43,000, another's is 43,001 — a difference of 1, which is very close. The bigger the difference, the more different they are.

On the graph, treated observation row 10,305 has two nearby candidates: row 27,719 (a bit lower) and row 27,281. All three had April bills close to 91,600, so they're comparable — and you'd pick whichever is closest.

Where it breaks. It gets much harder once you add matching variables. If two candidates are similar on one variable but less similar on another, how do you combine those? You need a proper measure of distance — which is the next episode.

2

Q2 · Select a set of matches, or construct weights?

Plain English. Keep only the best few, or keep lots and let closeness decide how much they count.

Worked example — one-to-one matching. For treated row 10,305, pick the single closest control observation — row 27,281. Those two are treated as basically the same person except one got treated. Everything else is discarded entirely, greyed out. You could pick the two best matches, or three, but the principle is the same: pick the best, throw away the rest.

Worked example — weights. Instead of in-or-out, count the closest ones more. Picture the control observations as circles: the biggest circles are the ones closest to the treated observation — the control bills most similar to it. As you move further away, the circles shrink; they're being counted less. Past a certain distance you stop counting them entirely — too far to be a good comparison at all.

Where it breaks — the trade-off. Picking only the best matches gives the lowest bias — the best possible job of closing that back door — but you may have a single control observation, which makes the control group's average very noisy. Weights let worse comparisons creep in, so possibly a slightly worse job on the back door, but you have far more observations to average over and therefore more precision.

3

Q3 · If selecting matches, how many?

Plain English. One match, or the best five?

Precisely. You could take just one, but that severely limits your control group's sample size. You can take the best two, three, four, five — whatever you want.

Anchor image. Going back to the well. The first bucket is the cleanest.

Where it breaks. As you go back for the next best match, and the next, you are consecutively picking worse and worse matches — inviting more bias with each one. But you're also building a bigger comparison group, which makes your estimate more precise. Same trade-off.

4

Q4 · If using weights, how do they decay with distance?

Plain English. Two sub-decisions: how fast the weights fall off, and what shape the fall-off has.

Precisely — how fast. A very good match gets a lot of weight and worse matches get less, but how quickly does that drop?

  • Fast decay → you're essentially only using the best matches. Fewer effective observations, best comparisons.
  • Slow decay → a wide range of observations included, though with small weights. More precision, more bias.

Precisely — what shape. You have to give it a function so it knows what decay to apply. That function is a kernel: something maximised at the centre, where you're as close to the treated observation as possible, and getting smaller as you move away.

KernelShape
Triangularstraight-line decay
Epanechnikovmore curved — decays more slowly at first, then more quickly

There are plenty of others, each with pros and cons.

5

Q5 · What is the worst acceptable match?

Plain English. At some point the closest available match is so bad you shouldn't use it at all.

Worked example. A treated observation had an April bill of 1,000. You go to the control group to find a match — and nobody has a bill anywhere near 1,000. The single best available match has an April bill of 6,000. Way off.

The whole point of matching is to construct a control group that looks like the treated group on the matching variables. Even the best match available here isn't a good comparison. So you might say 6,000-to-1,000 is simply too far apart to believe you're getting a comparable control group — that match is too bad to count.

In which case you take that treated observation and drop it from the study entirely. No match found, so it can't be used.

How it plays out in each approach:

  • Matched sample. Is the best match within your acceptable range, or outside it? And if you wanted three matches per treated observation, are there enough good ones? Maybe one control at 1,100 is close enough but the next in line is 6,000 — so you stop at one.
  • Weights. The weights decay as matches get worse, and at some point the kernel goes to zero — you don't just give it a low weight, you give it none. How fast does it get there? Quickly, or slowly, accepting some pretty bad matches along the way?

Where it breaks. This is a subjective question. Can you really justify calling 1,000 and 6,000 comparable? Probably not — but where exactly is the cutoff?

And the trade-off again: the worse the matches you accept, the more treated observations find a match and stay in the sample, so more precision — but you're comparing genuinely incomparable things, so you haven't really closed the back door, and bias comes back in. Be strict and that 1,000 gets dropped, shrinking your sample and costing you precision, but every comparison you keep is a real one.

Say it out loud

Matching sounds simple until you actually try to do it. There are five decisions, and every single one is the same trade-off in disguise.

Here's my setup: 30,000 Taiwanese credit card holders. Treatment is being late on your April bill, outcome is being late in September, and I need to match on the size of the April bill — because big bills make you late in both months, and I want to isolate how late you're willing to be.

Question one: what does "close" even mean? I can't demand exact matches — if your bill is 47,432, nobody else has exactly that. With one variable it's easy, just take the difference. 43,000 versus 43,001? Fine. With several variables it gets hard fast, and you need a real distance measure.

Question two: do I pick matches, or use weights? Either I find the single closest control person and bin everyone else, or I keep lots of people and count the close ones more heavily. Pick just the best and my comparison is excellent but I've got one data point — noisy. Use weights and I'm precise, but I've let worse comparisons in.

Question three: if I'm picking, how many? Because the second-best match is worse than the best, and the third is worse still. More data, worse comparisons.

Question four: if I'm weighting, how fast do the weights fall off? And in what shape — that's your kernel, triangular or Epanechnikov or whatever. Fast decay means basically only the best matches. Slow decay means lots of people counting a little.

Question five, and this one's my favourite: how bad is too bad? Say I've got a treated person with a 1,000 bill and the closest control anywhere is 6,000. That's not a comparison, that's a guess. So do I use it anyway — or do I drop that person from the study entirely?

Every one of those five: better matches, or more matches. Never both.

Recall drill

QWhat is the goal that all five questions serve?

Getting a treated group and control group that are comparable across the variables you match on — each matched variable closes a back door.

QDescribe the running example's treatment, outcome, and matching variable.

Treatment: late with your April credit card bill. Outcome: late in September. Matching variable: the size of your April bill.

QWhy is April bill size a back door here?

Bigger April bills mean bigger September bills, so large bills make you more likely to be late in both months — which would look like stickiness when it's really difficulty paying.

QWhy can't your matching criterion be exact equality?

With a continuous variable essentially nobody shares an exact value — if a bill is 47,432, no control observation matches it — so you'd find no matches at all.

QWith a single matching variable, what is the criterion?

Simply the difference between the values. 43,000 versus 43,001 is a difference of 1, so very close; larger differences mean more different.

QWhy does the criterion get harder with more variables?

Two candidates may be similar on one variable and less similar on another, and you need a measure of distance that combines them.

QDescribe one-to-one matching.

For each treated observation, pick the single closest control observation as its comparison and discard everything else.

QDescribe the weighting alternative.

Keep many control observations, giving the closest ones the largest weights and shrinking the weights as distance grows, until beyond some distance they count for nothing.

QGive the bias–variance trade-off between those two.

Picking only the best matches gives lowest bias but a very small, noisy control group. Weighting admits worse comparisons — more bias — but far more observations, so more precision.

QIf you're selecting matches, what happens as you take more of them?

Each successive match is worse than the last, adding bias, but the comparison group grows, adding precision.

QWhat are the two sub-decisions in how weights decay?

How fast they decay, and what function — what kernel — describes the decay.

QWhat is a kernel?

A function maximised at the centre, where you're closest to the treated observation, and getting smaller as you move away — determining the weights.

QName two kernels and how they differ.

The triangular kernel decays in a straight line; the Epanechnikov kernel is more curved, decaying more slowly at first and then more quickly.

QA treated observation has a 1,000 bill and the closest control has 6,000. What are your options?

Accept it as a match despite being incomparable, or declare it too bad and drop that treated observation from the study entirely.

QHow does question 5 show up when using weights rather than matches?

The kernel eventually goes to zero — beyond some distance an observation gets no weight at all — and you decide how quickly it reaches zero.

QState the bias–variance trade-off in accepting worse matches.

Accepting worse matches keeps more treated observations in the sample, giving more precision, but compares incomparable things, so the back door isn't really closed and bias returns.

QDo these five questions apply only to certain matching methods?

No — you generally face all of them for any matching procedure, though some methods let you skip one or two.
