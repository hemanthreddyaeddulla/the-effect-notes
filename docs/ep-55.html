Ep 55Dynamic Difference-in-Differences

▶ Watch the video 13:20
On this pageThe 30-second versionWhy this existsThe ideasTrapsSay it out loudRecall drillConnectionsGlossary

Instead of one effect for "after", estimate a separate difference-in-differences effect for every period — by interacting the treated indicator with time fixed effects — which lets you watch the effect evolve and, crucially, gives you pre-treatment estimates that should all be zero.

The 30-second version

  • Don't lump all the afters together. Compare 2011 to 2013, 2011 to 2014, 2011 to 2015 — separately.
  • Why: treatments often do nothing at first, or do a lot then fade. Forcing one effect across all periods is arbitrary.
  • How: take the two-way fixed effects model and interact the treated indicator with time fixed effects.
  • You must omit one period as the reference — the baseline everything else is compared to.
  • Graph the coefficients to see the effect's trajectory.
  • The pre-treatment coefficients are placebo tests. They should be zero. If they aren't, prior trends is violated.
  • Economists call this an event study — nothing to do with Chapter 17's use of the term.
  • Still requires everyone to be treated at the same time.

Why this exists

So far, if you had multiple periods you added time fixed effects to account for different levels in different periods. You can go further.

A terminology warning first. If you're an economist you may have heard this called an event study — but Chapter 17 used that term for something entirely different, an entire chapter that has nothing to do with this method. Here it's dynamic difference-in-differences.

The ideas

1

The idea

Plain English. Why treat every post-treatment period identically? Estimate one effect per period.

Precisely. You have a control group, a treated group, and a before/after comparison. But rather than comparing all the befores to all the afters, let each after period have its own effect.

Worked example. The teacher training reform goes into effect in 2012.

Basic difference-in-differences compares everything from 2008–2011 against everything from 2012–2020. Instead:

  • Compare 2011 to 2013 — that's one difference-in-differences effect.
  • Compare 2011 to 2014 — another.
  • 2011 to 2015, 2011 to 2016, and so on.
  • And do it with pre-periods too: what's the "effect" in 2010? In 2009, before the policy ever went into place?

What that gives you: the effect of the policy one year after implementation, two years after, three years after, and so on.

Why bother. There's no reason to force a single treatment effect. A lot of treatments — especially those going into effect at a particular time — take a while to do anything, or do a great deal at first and then fade out. Insisting the same effect applies in every period is a choice with nothing behind it.

Anchor image. A photograph versus a film. One tells you it happened; the other tells you how.

2

Estimating it

Plain English. Take the two-way fixed effects model and interact the treatment indicator with every year.

Precisely. Start with the two-way fixed effects model: fixed effects for group, fixed effects for time, and a treated indicator equal to 1 if you're in a treated group in a post-treatment period, and 0 otherwise — either before treatment, or in the untreated group, or both.

To make it dynamic, one change: take that treated indicator and interact it with a set of fixed effects for time periods.

So you build a variable that's 1 if it's 2008 and you're in the treated group. Note the treatment hasn't happened yet — you still want that indicator. Then one for 2009. Then 2010. Skip 2011. Then 2012, 2013, 2014, 2015.

Why you skip one. You need a reference group to compare everything against — a baseline year. That's why there's no β₀; the coefficients run from β₋₁ to β₁. This model is really doing a two-period difference-in-differences many times over, so it needs a baseline before-period — and that's also what the other pre-treatment periods get compared to.

Reading the output. Each β is just like a coefficient from a regular two-way fixed effects difference-in-differences. β₋₁ is the effect of treatment in the last period before treatment occurred; β₁ is the effect in the first period after. Graph them and you can see how the effect changes over time.

3

Worked example: organ donation, dynamically

Worked example. Back to Kessler and Roth — California changing how it asks people to become organ donors in 2011. The static model found a negative effect. But maybe that averaged together several different effects and came out negative when one of them was positive. Estimate the dynamic model and look.

The reference period. The point at quarter 2 of 2011 has no confidence interval at all. That's the reference group — the baseline last pre-treatment period, the rock everything else is compared to.

The post-treatment periods:

PeriodEffect
Q3 2011Large negative, −0.02, statistically significant — the confidence interval doesn't touch zero
Q4 2011About the same, perhaps slightly smaller
Q1 2012Back down again, and much less precise

So an immediate negative drop in organ donation rates, and not much evolution afterwards — the effect went into place and stayed there.

Where it breaks — the case for doing this at all. That isn't always what happens. Some treatments do nothing at first and slowly start to matter.

Worked example. When gas prices jump, people start buying more fuel-efficient cars. Compare a place where the gas price jumped a lot against one where it stayed relatively constant, and you'd expect the fleet to get more efficient — but it takes a while for people to buy new cars. So the effect right after treatment would be very small and would grow over time.

If you forced a single post-treatment effect, you would probably lose the effect that's actually there and miss it entirely.

4

The pre-treatment coefficients are placebo tests

Plain English. You estimated an "effect of treatment" in years before any treatment. It had better be zero.

Precisely. Look at quarter 4 of 2010 and quarter 1 of 2011 — both pre-treatment periods, and yet you're estimating an effect of treatment for them. Why? Placebo tests. There shouldn't be any effect there. If there is, it implies prior trends has been violated, and so parallel trends may not hold.

What it's actually saying: you're comparing two periods in which treatment wasn't in effect in either place, and looking at whether the gap between the two groups changed. If it did, you have a difference-in-differences effect with no treatment behind it — meaning the two groups were changing at different rates. Uh oh.

And in this data, you do see a little of that. In quarter 1 of 2011 the estimated effect is statistically significantly different from zero — an effect of treatment in a period where treatment wasn't occurring. That should make you somewhat concerned about parallel trends.

Where it lands. The deviation isn't very large, even though it's significant, so Nick doesn't look at this graph and get all that concerned about this particular study — though it's not a great look. If you saw a meaningfully large estimated effect in a pre-treatment period, that would make you very concerned about parallel trends.

5

What the model buys you

  • Whether the effect goes into play immediately.
  • Whether it stays at the same level, as with organ donation.
  • Whether it gets stronger over time, as with fuel-efficient cars.
  • Whether it does a lot right away and then fades out — very common in the finance effects from the event study videos.
  • Pre-treatment placebo checks on parallel trends.

Where it breaks. Just like the other difference-in-differences models, this assumes any treatment happened at the same time for everybody. As shown, it cannot handle different treated groups treated at different times.

Say it out loud

Standard difference-in-differences gives you one number: the effect. But why should the effect be the same in every year after the policy? Loads of things take time to work. Loads of things hit hard and then fade.

So instead of comparing all the befores to all the afters, compare 2011 to 2013. Then 2011 to 2014. Then 2011 to 2015. Now you've got the effect one year in, two years in, three years in — and you can watch it move.

Estimating it is easy: take your two-way fixed effects model and interact the treated indicator with time fixed effects. One catch — you have to leave one period out as your reference, because everything is being compared to some baseline.

Look at the organ donation study this way and you see an immediate negative hit right after the policy — about −0.02, clearly significant — and then it just... stays. No evolution. But that's not always the story. Think about gas prices: when petrol gets expensive, people buy more efficient cars — but it takes time to actually buy a car. So the effect starts near zero and grows. Force that into a single average and you'd conclude nothing happened.

Now here's my favourite part, and it's the reason to always run this. Notice you're also estimating an effect of treatment in quarter 4 of 2010 — a year before anything happened. Why? Because it should be zero. It's a placebo test. You're checking whether the gap between your groups was already moving before treatment ever arrived.

And in the organ donation data — quarter 1 of 2011 comes back significantly different from zero. An effect of a treatment that hadn't happened yet. It's small, so I wouldn't throw the study out. But it's not a great look, and if it had been large, that's your parallel trends assumption dying in public.

Recall drill

QWhat do economists call this model, and why is that confusing?

An event study — which is what Chapter 17 called an entirely different method. Here it's dynamic difference-in-differences.

QWhat does dynamic difference-in-differences do differently?

Rather than comparing all pre-treatment periods to all post-treatment periods, it estimates a separate effect for each period.

QWith a 2012 reform, describe the comparisons being made.

2011 against 2013, 2011 against 2014, 2011 against 2015, and so on — plus pre-treatment comparisons like 2011 against 2010 and 2009.

QWhy not force a single treatment effect?

Many treatments take time to do anything, or do a lot at first and fade. There's no reason to assume the same effect applies in every period.

QHow do you estimate it?

Start with the two-way fixed effects model and interact the treated indicator with a set of time period fixed effects.

QWhy must one period be omitted?

You need a reference group — a baseline before-period that every other coefficient, including the other pre-treatment ones, is compared against.

QWhat do β₋₁ and β₁ represent?

The effect in the last period before treatment, and the effect in the first period after treatment.

QWhy does the reference period appear with no confidence interval?

It's the baseline everything is compared to, not an estimate.

QWhat did the dynamic organ donation estimates show?

A large significant negative effect of about −0.02 in Q3 2011, roughly the same in Q4, and a similar but much less precise estimate in Q1 2012 — an immediate drop that then stayed put.

QGive an example of an effect that grows over time.

When gas prices jump, people buy more fuel-efficient cars — but it takes time to buy a car, so the effect starts small and grows.

QWhat would happen to that effect under a static model?

You'd probably lose it entirely — averaging a growing effect with the near-zero early periods can make it disappear.

QWhy are pre-treatment coefficients estimated at all?

They're placebo tests — they should be zero, since no treatment had occurred.

QWhat does a non-zero pre-treatment coefficient mean?

The gap between groups was already changing when no treatment was in effect, so the groups were trending at different rates — prior trends violated, and parallel trends in doubt.

QWhat did the organ donation data show in Q1 2011, and how concerned should you be?

A statistically significant effect in a pre-treatment period. The deviation isn't large, so it isn't alarming — though it isn't a good look. A large one would be seriously concerning.

QList what the dynamic model lets you see.

Whether the effect is immediate, whether it stays constant, whether it grows, whether it fades — and whether the pre-treatment placebo effects are zero.

QWhat limitation does it still share with the other models?

It assumes all treatment happened at the same time for everybody.

Reference period
the omitted pre-treatment period against which all other period effects are measured.
Pre-treatment coefficients
estimated effects in periods before treatment, which serve as placebo tests.
