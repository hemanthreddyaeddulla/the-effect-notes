Ep 8Conditional Conditional Means a.k.a. Control Variables

▶ Watch the video 12:00
On this pageThe 30-second versionWhy this existsThe ideasTrapsSay it out loudRecall drillConnectionsGlossary

Controlling for a variable means explaining away everything Z can account for in both X and Y, throwing that part out, and asking whether X and Y are still related in the leftover — the residual — which is how you answer "isn't that just because of Z?"

The 30-second version

  • Any conditional mean splits an observation into an explained part and an unexplained part, the residual.
  • The residual is the interesting bit: it's the variation that has nothing to do with the variable you conditioned on.
  • Look for a relationship inside the residuals and you get a conditional conditional distribution — the relationship between X and Y that survives after Z is accounted for.
  • That is exactly what "controlling for", "adjusting for", and "matching on" all mean.
  • Mechanically: use any relationship tool to explain X and Y with Z, subtract, then look at what's left. Regression does it automatically — just add Z as a predictor, and the coefficient on X becomes the conditional conditional relationship.
  • It's the standard reply to "isn't that just because of Z?"

Why this exists

You find that people who wear shorts are much more likely to eat ice cream. You announce a causal effect: make people wear shorts and they'll eat more ice cream. Someone immediately says: no, it's just that both happen on hot days.

They might be right. You need a way to check — a way to strip out everything temperature can explain about shorts-wearing and ice-cream-eating, and see whether anything survives. That's what this episode builds, and it's the last piece of description before causal inference proper.

The ideas

1

Explained and unexplained: the residual

Plain English. A conditional mean tells you what to expect. The residual is how far off the actual value is.

Precisely. Given a conditional mean of Y at some value of X, each observation splits into the explained part (the conditional mean) and the residual (actual minus expected). The residual is the variation in Y that the conditioning variable cannot account for.

Anchor image. Weighing a loaded truck and subtracting the truck's known empty weight. What's left is the cargo — and the cargo is the only part you were ever curious about.

Worked example. Nick is a college professor, and he drinks coffee. Suppose professors average 2.3 cups a day. He actually drinks 3.

  • Explained: 2.3 cups. That's what knowing "professor" buys you.
  • Residual: 0.7 cups. The gap between expected and actual.

That 0.7 sounds like failure — you can't predict individuals from a group mean. But notice what it is: the part of his coffee drinking that has nothing to do with being a professor. Some other reason drives it. That leftover is the useful object.

2

Conditional conditional: look for the relationship inside the residual

Plain English. Take out what Z explains, then ask whether X and Y are still related in what's left.

Precisely. A conditional conditional distribution is the conditional distribution of Y given X, computed within the variation remaining after conditioning on Z. If a relationship persists there, Z alone doesn't account for it.

Anchor image. Someone claims the room is only bright because the lamp is on. Turn the lamp off. Still bright? Then something else is contributing.

Worked example. Claim: brown-haired people drink more coffee. Objection: brown-haired people are more likely to be professors, and professors drink a lot of coffee.

So go to the residual. Discard the 2.3 cups that "professor" explains and work only inside the 0.7. Within that leftover, is hair colour still related to coffee drinking?

  • Yes → there's something about brown hair beyond the professor story.
  • No → the objector was right; being a professor was the whole thing.
3

That's all "controlling for" ever meant

Plain English. Controlling for, adjusting for, matching on — same idea, different words.

Precisely. Each names the operation of conditioning on a variable and then examining the relationship that remains within that already-conditioned variation.

Worked example. Shorts and ice cream. You observe they go together. The objection is that both happen on hot days.

Split by hot days versus not — or use all the values of temperature — see what temperature explains about each of shorts and ice cream, and take the residuals. If shorts and ice cream are still related in the leftover, temperature isn't the whole story and there may be something causal. If the relationship vanishes, the objector was right.

Where it breaks. "May be something causal" is the honest ceiling here. Surviving a control for temperature doesn't establish causation — it eliminates one alternative explanation. There are always others, which is what the identification chapters are for.

4

How to actually do it

Plain English. Explain what you can with Z, subtract it out of both variables, then look at the relationship in what's left. Or just add Z to your regression.

Precisely. The manual version uses any relationship tool you already have — line-fitting, means within bins. Explain X with Z and take the residual; explain Y with Z and take the residual; then look at the relationship between those two residuals.

The easy version: instead of regressing Y on X, regress Y on X and Z. The coefficient on X is now the conditional conditional relationship — regression performs the subtraction for you.

Anchor image. Two hills with a valley between them. Flatten both hills by the same amount and whatever separation remains was never about the hills.

Worked example — the geometry. Picture Y, X, and Z. Z is positively related to X and positively related to Y, so part of the X–Y relationship is really Z operating through both. Flatten the Y–Z relationship. Flatten the X–Z relationship. Now Z is flat against both leftovers — it can't be driving anything — and whatever X–Y relationship survives is the part not due to Z.

Worked example — the numbers. From last episode, vitamin E on BMI alone:

proportion taking vitamin E  =  0.110  +  0.002 × BMI

Plenty of reasons that relationship might not be about BMI. Older people may have higher BMI and be more likely to take vitamin E. Gender could matter too. So add age and gender to the regression:

ModelCoefficient on BMI
BMI only0.002
BMI, controlling for age and gender0.001

Read it carefully. The coefficient halved, so part of the original BMI–vitamin E relationship really was age and gender. But it didn't vanish — conditional on age and gender, a one-unit increase in BMI is still associated with a 0.001 increase in the conditional mean of vitamin E taking. Some relationship survives the conditioning.

Say it out loud

You've definitely heard someone say "we controlled for that." Here's what it actually means, and it's simpler than it sounds.

Start here. I'm a professor. Professors drink, say, 2.3 cups of coffee a day. I drink 3. So knowing I'm a professor explains 2.3 cups, and there's 0.7 left over — the residual. That leftover is the part of my coffee drinking that has nothing to do with being a professor.

Now suppose I claim brown-haired people drink more coffee, and you object: brown-haired people are just more likely to be professors, and professors drink a lot. Fair. So I throw out the 2.3 that "professor" explains, and I work only inside that 0.7 residual. If hair colour still predicts coffee in there — your objection didn't cover it. If it doesn't — you were right, it was the professor thing all along.

That's controlling for a variable. Same thing as adjusting for it, same thing as matching on it. Explain away what Z can account for, then look at what's left.

Classic version: people wearing shorts eat more ice cream. Obvious objection — it's just hot days. So take out everything temperature explains about both shorts and ice cream, and check whether they're still related in the leftover. If yes, temperature isn't the whole story.

And the practical shortcut: in a regression you just add the variable. Regress vitamin E use on BMI and you get a slope of 0.002. Add age and gender and it drops to 0.001. That tells you two things at once — part of what you were seeing really was age and gender, and part of it wasn't.

Recall drill

QWhat is a residual?

The difference between an observation's actual value and the conditional mean you'd have expected for it — the part the conditioning variable doesn't explain.

QProfessors average 2.3 cups of coffee; Nick drinks 3. Identify the explained and unexplained parts.

Explained: 2.3 cups, from knowing he's a professor. Residual: 0.7 cups, driven by something other than being a professor.

QWhy is the residual the useful part rather than a nuisance?

Because it's variation guaranteed to have nothing to do with the variable you conditioned on — so a relationship found inside it can't be explained by that variable.

QWhat is a conditional conditional distribution?

The conditional distribution of Y given X, computed within the variation that remains after conditioning on some other variable Z.

QWhich everyday phrases mean the same as "conditioning on Z and looking at what remains"?

Controlling for Z, adjusting for Z, matching on Z.

QShorts-wearers eat more ice cream; someone says it's just hot days. Describe the check.

Take out what temperature explains about both shorts-wearing and ice-cream-eating, then look for a relationship between the residuals. Surviving means temperature isn't the whole story; vanishing means the objection was right.

QHow do you compute a conditional conditional relationship manually?

Use any relationship tool to explain X with Z and Y with Z, take both residuals, then examine the relationship between the residuals.

QWhat's the shortcut in a regression?

Regress Y on X and Z instead of X alone. The coefficient on X becomes the conditional conditional relationship.

QBMI's coefficient goes from 0.002 to 0.001 when age and gender are added. Interpret both facts.

The drop means part of the original BMI–vitamin E relationship was really age and gender. The surviving 0.001 means that conditional on age and gender, a one-unit rise in BMI is still associated with a 0.001 rise in the conditional mean of vitamin E taking.

QYour relationship survives controlling for Z. Have you shown causation?

No. You eliminated one alternative explanation. Others remain — handling that systematically is what identification is for.

Glossary

Residual
the part of an observation not explained by the conditional mean; actual minus expected.
Explained part
the conditional mean itself; what the conditioning variable accounts for.
Conditional conditional distribution
the distribution of Y given X, within variation already conditioned on Z.
Control variable
a variable you condition on in order to remove its explanation from a relationship.
Controlling for / adjusting for / matching on
three names for conditioning on a variable and examining the relationship that remains.
