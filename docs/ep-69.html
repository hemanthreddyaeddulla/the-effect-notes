Ep 69Under the Rug

▶ Watch the video 16:40

Four last assumptions nobody wants to discuss — that treatment doesn't spill over to the untreated, that you picked the right model out of the hundreds you could have, that your variable actually has a mean, and the one that should haunt you: if your treated and control groups are genuinely so comparable, why was one of them treated?

The 30-second version

  • SUTVA: no spillovers. If a tutored child passes tips to friends, the untreated group is treated a little.
  • Fix by redefining treatment broadly enough to contain the spillover — the share of a classroom tutored, rather than one student.
  • Model uncertainty: you had to make decisions on imperfect information. This isn't sampling variation — it's not knowing which of many models is right.
  • Try every combination of controls and build a specification diagram. Colliders and omitted variables you never thought of can't be fixed that way.
  • Nonexistent moments: some fat-tailed distributions have no mean to estimate. Ed Sheeran versus a 1500m runner.
  • The treatment mystery: if they're so similar, why did one get treated? Regression discontinuity can answer this. Twin studies can't.

The ideas

1

SUTVA: the stable unit treatment value assumption

Plain English. Do you actually know what "treated" means? Because if treatment leaks, the untreated are treated too.

Precisely. SUTVA says the treatment you're estimating is the treatment on the individual you're estimating it on. Which sounds circular — so think of it as no spillover effects.

Worked example. You're studying the effect of a tutoring programme on children's grades. You randomize tutoring: completely random, no back doors, everything's fine.

But what if your kid, being tutored, passes tips and tricks along to the friends they study with?

Then tutoring has spillovers. Being treated is being treated — and not being treated might also mean being treated, just a little bit. Look at the effect in your data and you're mixing the two up, because the untreated population is partly treated.

Anchor image. Testing a perfume by having one person wear it in a small room. Everybody's wearing it now.

A way around it — redefine the treatment. Choose a treatment area broad enough that spillovers stay inside it. If tutoring might spill between friends in the same classroom, but probably not between classrooms, then don't study the effect of tutoring on an individual student. Study the effect of the share of students tutored within a classroom.

That way you know what treatment actually is. A single student being treated is ill-defined, because the treatment may reach other people. The share of a classroom being treated really only affects students in that classroom.

Where it breaks. This is a simplified rendering — a more technical treatment is worth reading. And SUTVA is, in Nick's phrase, the terror of many a causal inference researcher.

2

Model uncertainty

Plain English. You made a lot of judgement calls. You could have made them differently.

Precisely. The whole book has said: know your model, write it down, use it to build your research design and your estimate. And that's right — you're theorising, thinking carefully about where your data came from.

But once you actually do it, you find a lot of decisions you can't be 100% certain about. So you shouldn't be perfectly certain that the model you ended up with is the correct one.

And this is not sampling variation. It's that there were many different models you could have run. Maybe one is right — you just don't know which. Some decisions have to be made on imperfect information. You had to go with something.

What you can do — when it's about controls. The simplest form of model uncertainty is not being sure which control variables to include, and that's easy to address: run all the combinations. With this control, without it. With that one, without that one. Ten control variables gives you 2¹⁰ models — prune it a bit if you like, but you can run a few hundred and average things together.

That gives you a specification diagram showing the distribution of your estimated effect across all those control choices — a much better sense of what your estimate might have looked like had you known which set was right.

Where it breaks. Other forms of model uncertainty aren't so tractable. Is this variable a collider or not? Am I leaving something out entirely — is there a control that ought to be in here that I never thought of and that isn't even in my data? You can't simulate your way out of those.

We are never going to be completely certain about our model. And that is going to plague our dreams.

3

Nonexistent moments and power laws

Plain English. Some variables don't have a mean. You can still compute one — it just isn't measuring anything.

Precisely. Almost every method in this book estimates means — or more generally moments, even when it doesn't look like it. A moment is a statistic based on the distribution of your variable, involving a central tendency with deviations raised to some power. The mean is a moment. So are the variance and the skewness.

The problem: some distributions literally do not have those moments. It is possible to have a variable with no expectation.

You can always calculate a mean from a sample — nothing stops you. But that mean isn't estimating any real part of the distribution. And when a distribution has no first moment, the sample mean jumps around sharply from sample to sample. Normally you'd expect the mean to be normally distributed across samples and well-behaved, landing close to the truth, a bit above or below. Here, any given sample tells you very little, and you can't infer much from it.

How it arises: fat tails. A fat-tailed distribution has a lot of data concentrated in one area plus a great many very large outliers.

The distinction from a normal distribution. A normal distribution technically has infinite range — centred at zero with a standard deviation of one, a value of a billion is theoretically possible, just vanishingly rare. With fat tails, those extreme values aren't merely possible: they happen a lot. Regularly.

Worked example — income. Many people cluster around $40,000–$50,000. But there are plenty of millionaires and billionaires making millions every year — nowhere near as rare as a normal distribution would suggest.

Worked example — an even fatter tail: music streaming. Spotify follower counts when Nick checked:

RankArtistFollowers
1Ed Sheeran78.7 million
2Ariana Grande61 million
20Alan Walker27.9 million

First place is 29% bigger than second — a big drop between #1 and #2. And first place is 281% as large as twentieth: you lose more than half your followers going from 1st to 20th.

That's a bigger gap than in income or wealth. The wealthiest person in the world is not three times as wealthy as the second wealthiest. So music popularity is a genuinely big fat tail.

The contrast — a distribution without a fat tail: running 1500 metres.

Fastest ever recorded3:26.00
Second place3:26.34

A difference of 0.16% — against the 29% drop from first to second on Spotify.

And in perspective. Time Nick running a mile. He can run a mile, but he is not a particularly fast man — say 6 minutes. That's not even twice as long as the first-place runner. So going from first place to Nick, who'd be "in like billionth place at best," is only about a 100% difference.

Compare that to going from Ed Sheeran to Alan Walker — merely 1st to 20th — which is a bigger percentage difference than that.

Or: if Nick started a band and got a thousand followers on Spotify, which would be pretty darn successful, he'd be billions of times less successful than Ed Sheeran. Whereas running his mile, he's only half as good as the fastest human in history.

Where it breaks. With fat tails, all your methods work badly. If you're lucky, taking the logarithm of the skewed variable fixes it and you proceed as normal — which is what most people try. But some variables are too skewed even for that: financial returns over long periods may have tails so fat that even a logarithm isn't enough to tame them.

4

The treatment mystery

Plain English. If your two groups really are so alike, why did one of them get treated?

Precisely. A great many causal inference methods are about finding comparable groups — comparing people as similar as possible except that one got treated. You do it explicitly in matching. You do it inherently in regression when you add controls. You do it in difference-in-differences, comparing a treated group to a similar untreated control group.

But then the question arrives: if they're so similar, why was one treated and the other not? That's a mystery.

Sometimes it's obvious. When you genuinely understand why people got treated, you can answer it. Regression discontinuity, for instance: you know exactly why one of two very similar people got treated — one was just barely high enough on the running variable and the other just barely too low. Mystery solved.

But if you're using control variables, or controlling away unobserved variation with fixed effects or difference-in-differences, you have to ask yourself: if I really believe these two groups are that similar, why did one get treated?

Worked example — twin studies. A common back door across social science is genetics: your genes influence a lot of things we'd call treatments and a lot of things we'd call outcomes. Studying the effect of education on earnings, your genetics might make you do better in school and make you earn more later in life.

The standard fix: use twins. Find a pair of twins where one got more education than the other. Their genetics are identical, so whatever difference you see is probably the education.

Then the mystery. If you think they're so similar and comparable — why did one of them get more education than the other?

Maybe they aren't so comparable. Maybe they have the same genetics, but something really different happened to one of them as a kid — and that is what's driving their income, not the education.

The lesson. If you don't really understand where your treatment assignment comes from, and you're just using data to ferret out a good comparison, you always have to wonder: if I think they're so comparable, why aren't they the same?

That's the treatment mystery. And that's the end of the series — "hopefully I have scared you enough to put a nice little bow on all these videos. I am done. I'm going to sleep. Good night."

Say it out loud

Four last things nobody wants to talk about, and the last one should genuinely keep you up.

SUTVA — do you even know what "treated" means? You randomize tutoring, perfectly clean, no back doors. But the tutored kid tells their friends what they learned. So your control group is a bit treated too, and your comparison is mush. Sometimes you can fix it by widening the treatment — study the share of a classroom tutored, so the spillover stays inside the unit.

Model uncertainty — you made dozens of judgement calls you weren't sure about. And that's not sampling variation, that's not knowing which of hundreds of possible models is the right one. If it's just about which controls to include, you can run all 2-to-the-10 combinations and look at the spread. But "is this thing a collider?" or "is there a confounder I never thought of and don't have data on?" — you can't simulate your way out.

Nonexistent moments — and this one is wild. Some distributions don't have a mean. You can compute one from your sample, sure. It just isn't estimating anything.

Compare two worlds. Spotify: Ed Sheeran, 78.7 million followers. Second place, 61 million — a 29% drop. Twentieth place, 27.9 million — you've lost more than half. That's a bigger gap than wealth; the richest person alive isn't three times richer than the second.

Now running. World record for 1500 metres: 3:26.00. Second place: 3:26.34. A 0.16% difference. I could run a mile in maybe six minutes — I'd be roughly billionth place, and I'm still only twice as slow as the greatest of all time. But if I started a band and got a thousand followers, which would be a real success, I'd be billions of times smaller than Ed Sheeran.

And finally — the treatment mystery, which is the one I'd want you to leave with. Every method here is about finding two people who are as alike as possible, except one got treated. Matching does it. Controls do it. Difference-in-differences does it. And then you should stop and ask: if they're so alike, why did only one of them get treated?

Twin studies are the perfect case. Same genetics, one got more education — so the difference must be education, right? But why did one twin get more education? Something happened to one of them. And that something might be the real story.

Recall drill

QWhat does SUTVA assume, informally?

That there are no spillover effects — the treatment you're estimating is the treatment on the individual you're estimating it on.

QGive the tutoring spillover example.

A tutored child passes tips to the friends they study with, so the untreated group is partly treated and the comparison mixes the two.

QHow can you get around a spillover problem?

Redefine treatment broadly enough to contain the spillover — study the share of students tutored within a classroom rather than whether an individual student was tutored.

QWhy does that redefinition help?

Because a single student's treatment is ill-defined when it reaches others, while a classroom-level share only affects students in that classroom.

QWhat is model uncertainty, and what is it not?

Uncertainty about whether the model you chose is the correct one, given the many decisions made on imperfect information. It is not sampling variation.

QHow do you address the control-selection version?

Run every combination of control variables — 2¹⁰ models for ten controls — and average or examine the results.

QWhat's a specification diagram?

A display of the distribution of your estimated effect across all those different control variable choices.

QWhich forms of model uncertainty can't be handled that way?

Whether a variable is a collider, and whether you've omitted something entirely that isn't even in your data.

QWhat is a moment, and why does it matter here?

A statistic based on the distribution of a variable involving a central tendency with deviations to some power — the mean, variance, and skewness are moments, and nearly all the book's methods estimate them.

QWhat does it mean for a distribution to lack a first moment?

It has no expectation to estimate. You can compute a sample mean, but it isn't measuring any real feature of the distribution, and it jumps sharply from sample to sample.

QWhat is a fat-tailed distribution, and how does it differ from a normal one?

One with data concentrated in one area plus many very large outliers. A normal distribution permits huge values but they're vanishingly rare; with fat tails they occur regularly.

QGive the Spotify figures.

Ed Sheeran 78.7 million followers, Ariana Grande 61 million, Alan Walker 27.9 million at twentieth — so first is 29% above second and 281% of twentieth.

QGive the 1500m comparison.

The fastest time ever is 3:26.00 and second place is 3:26.34 — a 0.16% difference, against a 29% drop between first and second on Spotify.

QWhat's the mile-running perspective?

Nick running a six-minute mile would be roughly billionth place and only about twice as slow as the record — whereas a band with a thousand followers would be billions of times smaller than Ed Sheeran.

QWhat's the usual fix for fat tails, and when does it fail?

Taking the logarithm. It fails for variables too skewed even for that, such as financial returns over long periods.

QState the treatment mystery.

If your treated and control groups really are so comparable, why was one treated and the other not?

QWhich design answers it cleanly, and how?

Regression discontinuity — one unit was just barely above the running variable cutoff and the other just barely below.

QWhich designs leave it unanswered?

Ones using control variables, or controlling away unobserved variation via fixed effects or difference-in-differences.

QExplain the twin study version.

Twins share genetics, so a difference in earnings between a more- and less-educated twin looks like the effect of education — but you must ask why one twin got more education, since something different happened to one of them, and that may be what's driving earnings.

Glossary

SUTVA (stable unit treatment value assumption)
the assumption that treatment has no spillover effects onto other units.
Spillover
treatment applied to one unit affecting the outcomes of untreated units.
Model uncertainty
not knowing which of many defensible model specifications is correct.
Specification diagram
a display of estimated effects across many combinations of control variables.
Moment
a distributional statistic such as the mean, variance, or skewness.
Fat-tailed distribution
one in which very large outliers occur regularly rather than rarely.
The treatment mystery
the question of why one of two supposedly comparable units was treated and the other wasn't.
