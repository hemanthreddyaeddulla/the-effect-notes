Ep 50Event Studies in Finance

▶ Watch the video 15:00
On this pageThe 30-second versionWhy this existsThe ideasTrapsSay it out loudRecall drillConnectionsGlossary

An event study only requires comparing what you observed against a predicted counterfactual — no straight line necessary — which is why finance, where effects are immediate and then gone, builds the prediction from the estimation-period mean, from the market as a whole, or from an estimated relationship to the market, and calls the gap an abnormal return.

The 30-second version

  • An event study needs a counterfactual prediction. It does not need a straight-line regression.
  • Finance is full of events, and finance responds to the expectation of change — so effects arrive immediately.
  • Two consequences: short-horizon predictions are far more reliable, and effects fade at once, so fitting a post-period trend line makes no sense.
  • The process: pick an estimation period and an observation period, predict, compare, analyse.
  • The gap between actual and predicted is the abnormal return.
  • Three prediction methods: mean-adjusted, market-adjusted, and risk-adjusted returns.
  • None of it is actually specific to finance.

Why this exists

The last episode estimated an event study by fitting a linear regression on the pre-treatment side and comparing it to one from the post-treatment side. You don't have to do that.

The key component of an event study is that you're comparing what you observe in the post-treatment period against some predicted counterfactual — a prediction of what you'd have seen without the treatment. That doesn't require any sort of straight-line regression. And in finance, it's very common not to use one. Since finance is one of the most common places you'll see event studies applied, it's worth knowing how they do it.

The ideas

1

Why finance suits event studies so well

Plain English. Lots of events, and the effects land instantly.

Precisely. Finance has a lot of events: Google merged with something — what did that do to Google's stock? Apple unveiled the new iPhone — what did that do to Apple's? Something happens at a particular time and you expect an effect on an easily observable outcome.

That applies in lots of fields. What's different about finance is that stock prices respond not to literal physical changes but to the expectation of change — so effects go into play immediately.

Anchor image. A thermometer versus a barometer. One waits for the weather; the other reacts to the forecast.

Worked contrast. Suppose you want the effect of an education programme that gives students better teachers, randomly assigned, on whether they graduate from college. Even if everyone's confident it works, you will not see any change in the number of degrees awarded for about ten years.

Apple unveils the new iPhone and ten seconds later everyone can trade and bid the price up.

2

The two consequences of immediacy

Consequence 1 — short-horizon predictions are much better. All these methods rest on predicting what would have happened absent the event, and those predictions are far stronger over a very short window. Forecasting how many college degrees there'll be in ten years without your policy is very hard. Forecasting Apple's stock price one minute from now, knowing what it is right now, is pretty easy.

So the immediacy of the effect makes the assumptions much more plausible. If you predict Apple's price one minute out and then look one minute later, you can be fairly confident the change came from the announcement and not from some long-term trend you failed to notice — there simply isn't time for other effects to sink in. The time back door gets closed convincingly.

Consequence 2 — the effects fade right away. A corollary of immediacy: everything happens at once and then stops. Almost all of Apple's price growth happens on the first day after the announcement. Everyone thinks that's going to be good for Apple, the price should be up there — so why wait? They bid it up within minutes. You might see a huge effect today and none tomorrow.

Where it breaks — and why this chapter exists. When the effect appears briefly and then goes away, fitting a whole time-series line to the post-treatment period doesn't make sense. You don't want a straight line. You want an effect that's there once and then gone. So you need a method that allows for that.

3

The four-step process

Plain English. Choose your windows, predict, compare, analyse.

Precisely. Throughout, assume you're looking at the effect of an event on the price of a stock — though it applies more broadly.

Step 1 — pick an estimation period and an observation period. Roughly the pre-treatment and post-treatment periods, though not necessarily all your data. You might not use everything before; maybe you give yourself a little lag, or use only a narrow window right next to the treatment. And you might not follow things forever afterwards — maybe just a couple of days. The estimation period is what you use to estimate the general patterns; the observation period is where you make predictions from those patterns.

Step 2 — make the predictions. Take the model estimated from the estimation period and predict what would occur in the observation period.

Step 3 — compare, giving the abnormal return. You have your prediction, and you can see what actually happened.

Worked example. You predict Apple's stock would go up by 3%. It actually went up by 6%. That extra 3 percentage points — beyond what you'd expect from the prior pattern — is the abnormal return. Above and beyond what you'd normally expect, hence abnormal.

Step 4 — analyse the abnormal returns. You may have a different abnormal return for each day of the observation period, or one for each treated group. Calculate standard errors for them and see what the effect of treatment actually was.

Where it breaks. None of this is structurally different from the last episode: an estimation period to fit a model, predictions in the post-treatment period, and a comparison. The only real difference is that you're not using a straight line to do it.

4

Prediction method 1: mean-adjusted returns

Plain English. Take the average from before and use it as your prediction for after.

Precisely. Literally that: take the average in the estimation period and make it your prediction in the observation period. It's an assumption that whatever you saw before would have continued — that there was basically no trend whatsoever.

Where it breaks — better than it sounds. Especially with stock prices. Stock levels might go up and up and up, but the return on a daily basis is pretty flat: you gain about the same percentage each day, give or take. Assuming that general percentage continues into the post period is not the worst assumption in the world — though you'd want to think about it carefully.

5

Prediction method 2: market-adjusted returns

Plain English. Use the whole market as your control group.

Precisely. You have a rather good comparison available: the market as a whole. Apple goes up and down over time; so does the market — and you'd expect them to move in the same direction, because some days are simply good days for the market.

So if Apple goes up 6% after the event, you don't want to confuse Apple going up because Apple did something cool with Apple going up because the entire market went up. That's a back door problem.

Worked example. Don't use pre-treatment Apple prices to build the prediction — use the returns of the market as a whole.

Return
Apple, actual6%
Market as a whole2%
So you'd have expected Apple2%
Abnormal return4%

That extra 4% is your treatment effect.

Anchor image. Everyone on the escalator rises. You want to know who was also walking.

Where it fits. That comparison is like using a control group — you're treating the market as a whole as your control, saying Apple would have been just like everybody else absent treatment. Control-group methods get their own chapter next.

6

Prediction method 3: risk-adjusted returns

Plain English. Same idea, but Apple doesn't move one-for-one with the market — so estimate by how much it does move, and use that.

Precisely. Market-adjusted returns assumed Apple's return would simply be the market's return. Risk-adjusted returns refine that: Apple and the market go in the same direction, but not to the same extent. A market that's 1% higher than normal might be associated with Apple being more than 1% higher than normal.

So estimate a model of the relationship between the market as a whole and Apple's stock price in the estimation period, then apply that relationship in the post-treatment period.

Worked example. Suppose Apple tends to move about twice as much as the market.

Return
Market as a whole2%
Predicted Apple, given it moves ~2× the market4%
Apple, actual6%
Abnormal return2%

Where it goes further. It doesn't have to be as simple as the correlation between the market return and the stock return. There are also the Fama-French factors, which use not just the overall market return but the returns of particular portfolios — ones that do things like buy small stocks and sell big stocks. Predict Apple using those portfolios, make the same prediction in the post-treatment period, and measure the deviation to get your abnormal returns.

7

None of this is really about finance

Plain English. Strip the vocabulary and these are three general strategies.

Say it out loud

An event study just needs a prediction of what would have happened, and a comparison against it. It doesn't need a regression line — and finance, which is where event studies really live, usually doesn't use one.

Why is finance so well suited to this? Because stock prices react to the expectation of change, not to change itself. Apple unveils the iPhone and ten seconds later the price has moved. Compare that to an education programme — even if everyone's certain it works, you won't see the effect on college degrees for ten years.

And that immediacy buys you two things. First, your prediction only has to reach one minute into the future instead of ten years, and short-horizon forecasts are enormously more reliable. There's no time for anything else to sneak in. Second — and this is why the straight-line approach doesn't fit — the effect happens all at once and then stops. Traders don't wait; they bid it up in minutes. Huge effect today, nothing tomorrow. You can't fit a trend line to that.

So instead: pick an estimation period before, an observation period after, predict, and compare. The gap is the abnormal return — you predicted 3%, you got 6%, so 3% is abnormal.

Three ways to build the prediction. Mean-adjusted — just use the average from before, assuming no trend. Sounds crude, but daily returns really are pretty flat even when prices climb.

Market-adjusted — use the whole market as your control. Apple rose 6%, the market rose 2%, so 4% is abnormal. You're not fooled by a good day for everyone.

Risk-adjusted — but Apple doesn't move one-for-one with the market. If it usually moves about twice as much, then a 2% market day should give you 4% from Apple, so 6% actual means only 2% is really abnormal.

And none of that is about finance. Mean-adjusted is statistical process control. Market-adjusted is a control group. Risk-adjusted is just "these things track each other, and I assume they keep doing so."

Recall drill

QWhat does an event study actually require?

A prediction of the counterfactual and a comparison against it — not necessarily a straight-line regression.

QWhy are effects immediate in finance?

Because stock prices respond to the expectation of change rather than to literal physical change.

QGive the contrast used to illustrate that.

An education programme's effect on college graduation wouldn't show up for about ten years; Apple's stock price moves ten seconds after an announcement.

QWhat's the first benefit of immediate effects?

Predictions over a very short horizon are far more reliable, so the counterfactual is more credible and there's no time for other effects to sink in.

QWhat's the second consequence of immediacy, and what problem does it create?

The effect happens all at once and then fades. Fitting a time-series trend line to the post-period makes no sense when you need an effect that appears once and disappears.

QList the four steps of the finance approach.

Pick an estimation period and an observation period; make predictions; compare to get abnormal returns; analyse those abnormal returns.

QWhat is an abnormal return?

The difference between the actual return and the return you predicted from the prior pattern — a predicted 3% against an actual 6% gives a 3-point abnormal return.

QWhat do you do in step four?

Analyse the abnormal returns — possibly one per day or per treated group — including calculating standard errors.

QDescribe the mean-adjusted returns approach.

Take the average from the estimation period and use it as the prediction for the observation period, assuming no trend at all.

QWhy is that assumption more reasonable than it sounds for stocks?

Stock levels may climb steadily, but daily returns are fairly flat — roughly the same percentage each day.

QDescribe the market-adjusted returns approach and the back door it closes.

Use the market's return as the prediction for the stock. It prevents confusing a rise caused by the event with a rise caused by a good day for the entire market.

QApple rose 6% and the market rose 2%. What's the market-adjusted abnormal return?

4%.

QWhat is market-adjusted returns equivalent to?

Using a control group — the market as a whole stands in for what Apple would have done absent treatment.

QHow does the risk-adjusted approach differ?

It doesn't assume the stock moves one-for-one with the market; it estimates the relationship between them and applies that relationship to the post-treatment period.

QApple typically moves twice as much as the market, the market rose 2%, and Apple rose 6%. What's the abnormal return?

You'd have predicted 4%, so the abnormal return is 2%.

QWhat are the Fama-French factors used for here?

Predicting a stock from the returns of particular portfolios — such as ones buying small stocks and selling big ones — not just the overall market return.

QGive the non-finance equivalent of each of the three methods.

Mean-adjusted returns is statistical process control; market-adjusted returns is using a control group; risk-adjusted returns is predicting an outcome from factors it tracks and assuming the relationship persists.

Glossary

Estimation period
the pre-treatment window used to fit the model that generates predictions.
Observation period
the post-treatment window in which predictions are compared to reality.
Abnormal return
the actual return minus the predicted counterfactual return.
Mean-adjusted returns
predicting the post-period using the estimation-period average.
Market-adjusted returns
predicting a stock's return using the market's return, treating the market as a control group.
Risk-adjusted returns
predicting a stock's return from an estimated relationship to the market or other factors.
Fama-French factors
portfolio-based factors used to predict returns beyond the overall market return.
