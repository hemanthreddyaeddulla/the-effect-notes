Ep 16Causal Pathways

▶ Watch the video 11:35
On this pageThe 30-second versionWhy this existsThe ideasTrapsSay it out loudRecall drillConnectionsGlossary

Every way you can walk across a diagram from treatment to outcome is a separate reason those two variables are related — so list all the paths, sort them into the ones your question wants and the ones it doesn't, and identification becomes the concrete job of shutting down the second kind.

The 30-second version

  • A causal diagram's real output is why two variables are related — every path is one reason.
  • A path is any walk along the diagram from one variable to another. You may go backwards up an arrow. You may not visit a variable twice.
  • Front door: all arrows point away from treatment, toward outcome. Back door: at least one arrow somewhere points back at the treatment.
  • Good path: one you want to count for your question. Bad path: one you don't.
  • Good usually means front door — but not always. Your question decides, not the diagram.
  • Identification = close every bad path, leave every good path open. Then the only variation left is the variation you wanted.

Why this exists

You can draw a diagram and simplify it. What is it actually for?

This: it tells you exactly why your treatment and outcome are related. Not vaguely — specifically, as an enumerable list. You already knew a raw correlation between treatment and outcome wouldn't give you the causal effect, because other reasons exist. The diagram turns "other reasons exist" into a list of routes you can point at and deal with one by one.

The ideas

1

What a path is

Plain English. Any way you can walk from one variable to another across the diagram.

Precisely. A path is a sequence of connected variables leading from a starting variable to an ending one. Two rules:

  1. You can walk backwards up an arrow. If the arrow runs B → treatment, "treatment to B" is still a legal step.
  2. No doubling back. You can't hit the same variable twice — so treatment → B → A → treatment → B → C → outcome isn't a path, because it returns to treatment.

Anchor image. A street map where some streets are one-way for traffic but you're on foot. You can walk down a one-way street the wrong way; you just can't loop back through a junction you've already crossed.

Worked example. On a diagram with treatment, outcome, and variables A, B, C — where B causes the treatment — all of these are paths from treatment to outcome:

  • treatment → outcome
  • treatment → A → outcome
  • treatment → B → C → outcome
  • treatment → A → B → C → outcome
  • treatment → B → A → outcome

Note the third one starts by walking against the arrow from B into treatment. Perfectly legal.

Where it breaks. Each path is a separate reason the two variables are related. The direct arrow is only one entry on the list.

2

Every path tells a story

Plain English. For each path you can say out loud why it would make the two variables move together.

Worked example. Does drinking wine affect your lifespan? A simplified diagram:

Income and health affect both how much wine you drink and how long you live. Wine affects drugs — maybe wine substitutes for other drugs, maybe it's a complement and drinking makes you want them — and drugs affect lifespan. U1 is unobserved and relates income to health without sitting directly between them.

Now read off the reasons wine and lifespan are related:

PathThe story
Wine → LifespanWine directly affects how long you live
Wine → Drugs → LifespanWine changes how many other drugs you take, which affects lifespan
Wine ← Income → LifespanRicher people can afford more wine, and live longer for unrelated reasons
Wine ← Health → LifespanHealthier people feel able to drink; if you're really sick you stay off alcohol — and better health means a longer life
Wine ← Income ← U1 → Health → LifespanRicher people drink more wine; income is related to background health; health affects lifespan

Every one of those would produce a correlation between wine and lifespan. Only some are the effect of wine.

3

Front doors and back doors

Plain English. Front doors run forward from treatment. Back doors have an arrow pointing back at it.

Precisely. A front door is a path where all arrows point away from the treatment and toward the outcome. A back door is a path with at least one arrow somewhere along it pointing back at the treatment.

Anchor image. Front doors are the treatment's own influence flowing outward. Back doors are something upstream pushing the treatment around, and that something has its own route to the outcome.

Worked example. In the wine diagram:

  • Front doors: Wine → Lifespan, and Wine → Drugs → Lifespan.
  • Back doors: the three paths that begin by walking backwards into wine, via income or health.

Where it breaks. A back door means something other than random variation is driving your treatment. That's precisely why back doors are usually the alternative explanations you have to deal with.

4

Good and bad paths — your question decides

Plain English. A good path is one you want to count. That's a fact about your research question, not about the diagram.

Precisely. Good paths are the ones that should count toward your answer; bad paths are everything else. Often good = all front doors. Not always.

Anchor image. Front door versus back door is architecture. Good versus bad is which door you meant when you asked how people get in.

Worked example — the same diagram, two different questions.

Question A: what's the effect of wine on lifespan, all in? Then both front doors are good. If wine extends your life because it means you take fewer drugs — sure, that counts as an effect of wine.

Question B: what's the effect of wine on lifespan, not counting its effect on drug taking? Now the only good path is Wine → Lifespan. Wine → Drugs → Lifespan is a front door that is a bad path, and you'd want it shut.

Meanwhile Wine ← Income → Lifespan is bad under either question. Rich people drinking more wine and living longer for other reasons is not an effect of wine.

Where it breaks. This is why you can't mechanically read identification off a diagram. The diagram gives you the paths; you supply which ones your question wants.

5

What identification is, concretely

Plain English. Close every bad path, keep every good path open, and what's left is your answer.

Precisely. Once all the bad paths are shut and the good ones remain, the only variation left in your data is variation you wanted — which is what it means to have identified your effect.

Anchor image. A building with many doors. Lock all of them except the one you're measuring traffic through, and every person you count came through that door.

Say it out loud

Here's what a causal diagram is actually for. It tells you every reason your two variables are related — as a list you can point at.

The unit is a path: any way you can walk across the diagram from treatment to outcome. Two rules. You're allowed to walk backwards up an arrow — that's crucial. And you can't visit the same variable twice.

Say I want to know whether drinking wine affects how long you live. Wine affects lifespan directly — that's one path. Wine changes how many other drugs you take, and those affect lifespan — second path. But now walk backwards: richer people can afford more wine, and they also live longer for completely unrelated reasons. That's a path too. Same with health — healthy people feel fine drinking, sick people stay off alcohol, and healthy people live longer anyway.

Every one of those would make wine and lifespan correlate. Only some are wine doing something.

So you sort them. Front doors run forward, away from the treatment. Back doors have an arrow pointing back into the treatment — something upstream is pushing your treatment around, and that something reaches the outcome by its own route.

And then, separately: good and bad. A good path is one you want to count. Usually that's the front doors — but not always, and this is the bit worth remembering. If I want the effect of wine on lifespan not counting the fact that it changes your drug taking, then wine → drugs → lifespan is a front door that I want to shut. The diagram gives you the paths. Only your question can say which ones you meant.

And that's identification, finally made concrete: close every bad path, leave every good path open. Then whatever's left in your data is the thing you were asking about.

Recall drill

QDefine a path on a causal diagram.

Any walk from one variable to another along the diagram. You may travel backwards up an arrow, and you may not visit a variable twice.

QThe arrow runs B → treatment. Is "treatment to B" a legal step in a path?

Yes. Paths can be walked against the direction of an arrow.

QWhy is treatment → B → A → treatment → B → C → outcome not a path?

It doubles back, visiting treatment twice.

QWhat does each path represent?

A separate reason the two endpoint variables would be related to each other in your data.

QDefine front door and back door.

A front door is a path where all arrows point away from the treatment toward the outcome. A back door has at least one arrow somewhere along it pointing back at the treatment.

QIn the wine diagram, give the two front door paths.

Wine → Lifespan, and Wine → Drugs → Lifespan.

QTell the story for the path Wine ← Health → Lifespan.

Healthier people feel able to drink more (if you're really sick you avoid alcohol), and being healthier also means living longer — so wine and lifespan correlate without wine doing anything.

QWhat's the difference between a front door path and a good path?

Front door is structural — it's about arrow directions. Good is about your research question — whether you want that path counted.

QGive an example of a front door path that is a bad path.

If your question is the effect of wine on lifespan excluding its effect on drug taking, then Wine → Drugs → Lifespan is a front door you want to shut.

QWhat does a back door path indicate about your treatment?

That something other than random variation is driving it — something upstream is influencing who gets treated, and that something reaches the outcome by its own route.

QState what identification means in path terms.

Close all the bad paths and leave all the good paths open, so the only variation remaining in the data is the variation you wanted to measure.
