Ep 18Testing Causal Diagrams, and Placebo Tests

▶ Watch the video 10:20
On this pageThe 30-second versionWhy this existsThe ideasTrapsSay it out loudRecall drillConnectionsGlossary

All of this assumes your diagram is right — so test it: pick any two variables, close every path between them, and the relationship in the data should be zero; if it isn't, your diagram is missing something.

The 30-second version

  • Closing bad paths identifies your effect only if the diagram is correct. It's a set of assumptions, and modelling a complex world means you left something out.
  • A placebo test takes your assumptions and derives a relationship that should be zero. A non-zero result means you've missed something.
  • Simple version: your ad this month can't have raised sales last month. If your design says it did, the design is broken.
  • Every diagram is full of placebo tests. Pick any two variables, list every path, close them all — the observed relationship should be zero.
  • Colliders do a lot of work here, because between non-treatment variables many paths are pre-closed and need no action.
  • Failing isn't fatal. The goal was never 100% correct assumptions — it's assumptions close enough not to mess you up too much.

Why this exists

Everything so far has one enormous condition attached. You list the paths, sort them, close the bad ones — and you've identified your effect if the diagram is a reasonable representation of the actual data generating process.

That's hard. The world is very complex, you're modelling it, and you are definitely leaving out detail. So the natural question is whether there's any way to check — and thankfully there is, at least for finding out whether you got it particularly wrong.

The ideas

1

What a placebo test is

Plain English. Use your own assumptions to predict something that must be zero. Then go look.

Precisely. A placebo test takes the assumptions you've made — and writing down a causal diagram is a set of assumptions — and derives from them a relationship or effect that should be zero if those assumptions hold. Run your actual research design on it. A non-zero answer means an assumption is wrong: something is missing, some control unaccounted for.

Anchor image. Calibrating a scale by weighing nothing. If it doesn't read zero, every number it has ever given you is suspect.

Worked example. Does a television advertisement increase your sales? You design a study — you'll control for a certain set of variables, and you believe that identifies the effect.

Now check your own reasoning. If the assumptions hold, this month's advertising should improve next month's sales. It should have no effect whatsoever on last month's sales — that's not possible.

So run the identical design, same controls, on last month's sales. If this month's advertising appears to have raised last month's sales, that effect should have been zero and isn't. You've missed something. Back to the drawing board.

2

Every diagram is packed with placebo tests

Plain English. Take any two variables at all, shut every route between them, and they should be unrelated.

Precisely. Pick any two variables on the diagram — not just treatment and outcome. Write out every path between them. Close all of them. The relationship between those two variables in your data should now be zero, because paths aren't only causal effects — they're any reason two variables could be related. Remove all of them and nothing is left.

If they're still related, there's a path you left off the diagram, or a variable you should have been conditioning on. Which means you don't yet know what to control for to identify your real effect.

Anchor image. Seal every door and window in a room and the air should stop moving. A draught means a gap you haven't found.

3

Worked example: income and drugs

Plain English. In the wine diagram, pick two variables that aren't the treatment or the outcome and test the implication.

Worked example. Take income and drugs from the Ep 16 wine diagram.

Step 1 — list the paths. Income → Wine → Drugs. Income ← U1 → Health → Wine → Drugs. Then a batch running through lifespan: Income → Lifespan ← Drugs, Income → Wine → Lifespan ← Drugs, and more.

Step 2 — throw out the pre-closed ones. Every path through lifespan has both arrows pointing at lifespan — income → lifespan, and drugs → lifespan. Lifespan is a collider on all of them, so they're pre-closed. You don't need to worry about them, and you emphatically don't want to control for lifespan. Colliders come into play a lot once you look at pairs of variables that aren't the treatment and outcome.

Step 3 — close what's left. The remaining paths both run through wine. So control for wine — look only at wine drinkers, or only at non-drinkers, or condition on how much wine you drink statistically.

Step 4 — the prediction. Having done that, the relationship between income and drugs should be zero. If it isn't, there's a route from income to drugs going through neither wine nor lifespan, and your diagram is incomplete.

Where it breaks. Writing the paths out already tells you it's deficient. What the diagram claims is that the only reason your income relates to your drug taking is that wine affects drug taking. Obviously insufficient — there's a lot missing from the drug-taking picture. You can predict this test fails before running it.

4

What to do when you fail

Plain English. Failing isn't a verdict, it's information. Sometimes you fix it, sometimes you note it and move on.

Precisely. The goal in causal inference was never assumptions that are 100% correct — that's impossible. The goal is assumptions close enough to correct that they don't mess you up too much.

So look at the size of what you found. If income and drugs are still related but only slightly, maybe that's acceptable: you're leaving a little out and it won't be a huge problem.

Then do the reflection regardless: why would income and drugs be related other than through wine? You'll come up with a few reasons. If any look important, put them on the diagram, redo the path analysis, work out what to close, and re-run the placebo tests.

Anchor image. A smoke alarm. It doesn't tell you the house is lost — it tells you to go look, and sometimes the answer is "the toast."

Where it breaks. The third option is legitimate: decide it isn't much of a problem — but now you know there's a problem, and you can tell the readers of your study that it's there.

Say it out loud

Everything we've done — list the paths, close the bad ones — comes with a giant asterisk. It only identifies your effect if the diagram is right. And the diagram is a set of assumptions about a very complicated world, so you've definitely left something out.

So how do you check? Placebo tests. You take your own assumptions and use them to predict something that should be zero, then go look.

Simplest possible version. I want to know whether TV advertising raises my sales. I set up a design, control for some things, and I think that identifies it. Fine — but if my assumptions hold, my advertising this month should raise next month's sales and have absolutely no effect on last month's sales. That's impossible. So I run the exact same design on last month's sales. If it says this month's ads worked backwards in time, something in my design is broken.

And here's the lovely part: every causal diagram is stuffed with these. Take any two variables — they don't have to be your treatment and outcome. List every path between them. Close all of them. Now the relationship in your data should be zero, because paths are any reason two things are related. If they're still related, there's a route you didn't draw.

Let me do it. In the wine diagram, take income and drugs. There are paths through wine, and paths through lifespan. But look at lifespan — income points into it, drugs points into it. That's a collider, so those paths are already closed. Ignore them. So all I have to do is control for wine, and then income and drugs should be unrelated.

They won't be. Because what my diagram is claiming is that the only reason your income relates to your drug taking is that wine affects drug taking — which is obviously nonsense.

But failing isn't a disaster. The goal was never assumptions that are perfectly correct; that's impossible. It's assumptions close enough that they don't mess you up too much. So you look at how big the leftover is, you think about what you missed, and either you fix the diagram — or you decide it's small, and you tell your readers it's there.

Recall drill

QWhat assumption underlies the whole close-the-bad-paths procedure?

That the diagram is a reasonable representation of the actual data generating process.

QDefine a placebo test.

Taking your assumptions, deriving from them a relationship or effect that should be zero, and checking. A non-zero result means an assumption is wrong.

QGive the advertising placebo test.

Run the same design on last month's sales. This month's advertising cannot have raised them, so the effect should be zero; if it isn't, the design is missing something.

QHow does any causal diagram generate placebo tests?

Pick any two variables, list every path between them, close them all. Since paths are any reason two variables are related, the observed relationship should then be zero.

QWhat does a non-zero result on such a test tell you?

There's a path you left off the diagram, or a variable you should be conditioning on — meaning you don't yet know what to control for to identify your real effect.

QIn the wine diagram, why can you ignore every income–drugs path running through lifespan?

Income points into lifespan and drugs points into lifespan, so lifespan is a collider on those paths and they're pre-closed.

QWhat single variable closes the remaining income–drugs paths?

Wine — look only at wine drinkers, only at non-drinkers, or condition on wine statistically.

QHow can you tell this particular test will fail before running it?

Because the diagram claims the only reason income relates to drug taking is via wine's effect on drugs, which is plainly insufficient.

QWhat is the actual goal for your assumptions, if not being correct?

Being close enough to correct that they don't mess you up too much — perfect assumptions are impossible.

QName three legitimate responses to failing a placebo test.

Add the missing variables to the diagram and redo the path analysis; judge the violation small enough not to matter; or accept the problem and disclose it to readers of your study.

QWhy are colliders especially relevant when testing pairs that aren't the treatment and outcome?

Because many paths between two arbitrary variables run into a common effect, making that variable a collider and the path pre-closed — so there's much less to do than the path count suggests.
