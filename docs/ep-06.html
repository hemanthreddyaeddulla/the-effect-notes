Ep 6Conditional Distributions

▶ Watch the video 11:10
On this pageThe 30-second versionWhy this existsThe ideasTrapsSay it out loudRecall drillConnectionsGlossary

Two variables are related exactly when learning the value of one changes the distribution of the other — so describing a relationship is nothing more than describing how one variable's distribution shifts as you move across values of another.

The 30-second version

  • Conditioning means holding one variable at a particular value and asking what the other variable's distribution looks like there.
  • If the conditional distribution changes as you move across values, the variables are related. If it doesn't change at all, they're independent.
  • This works for every kind of variable: categorical distributions shift, density curves shift.
  • Since you already know how to summarize a distribution, you get conditional means — and conditional medians, standard deviations, skews, whatever you like — for free.
  • Conditioning on a continuous variable is awkward, because almost nobody shares an exact value. Binning is the crude fix; line-fitting is the good one, next episode.
  • None of this is causal yet. It's description only.

Why this exists

Causal inference is about the effect of one variable on another — which means, before anything else, it's about the relationship between two variables. You can't ask which part of a relationship is causal until you can say what the relationship is.

And the good news is you already have the machinery. Everything from the last three episodes — distributions, densities, means, medians, spread — carries over untouched. You just apply it one slice at a time.

The ideas

1

Conditioning

Plain English. Hold one variable fixed at some value, and look at what the other variable does there.

Precisely. To condition on a variable is to restrict attention to observations at a particular value of it, and examine the distribution of a second variable within that restriction. That's a conditional distribution.

Anchor image. A deck of cards face down: any card is equally likely. Someone tells you it's a red card. You haven't seen the card — but the distribution over what it could be just collapsed to half of what it was. That collapse is conditioning.

Worked example. The distribution of babies' sex is roughly 50/50. You're told you'll meet a stranger; your best guess about their sex is 50/50.

Now someone mentions the stranger is named Sarah. Your distribution shifts hard — maybe 95% female, maybe higher. Conditional on the name being Sarah, the distribution of sex is nothing like 50/50.

That shift is the entire definition. Learning about one variable told you something about another. That is what it means for two variables to be related.

2

Independence is the case where nothing shifts

Plain English. If learning one variable's value leaves the other's distribution exactly as it was, they're unrelated.

Precisely. Two variables are independent when conditioning on one produces no change in the distribution of the other.

Worked example. A six-sided die: each face has a 1/6 chance. You roll and get a 1. What does that tell you about the next roll? Nothing. It's still 1/6 for every face. The conditional distribution equals the unconditional one, so the rolls are independent.

Contrast: names and sex. "Sarah" gives you one distribution, "Chris" gives you a different one. Different conditional distributions across values means the variables are related.

3

It works the same for every variable type

Plain English. Categorical or continuous, you're doing the identical thing — look at the distribution, then look at it again inside each slice.

Worked example. Vitamin E supplements. Vitamin E wasn't recommended, then was recommended by health professionals for a while, then wasn't again. That history makes it interesting: while it was being recommended, the people who took it up were plausibly the people who follow health advice in general — so vitamin E would look related to all sorts of healthy outcomes, when really it's healthy people choosing to take it. This is the setup behind a study by Emily Oster.

Start with the unconditional distribution: about 15% of people take vitamin E, 85% don't.

Now condition on smoking. Among smokers, the share taking vitamin E is lower than among non-smokers. Two different conditional distributions — one for smokers, one for non-smokers — so vitamin E use is related to smoking.

Now the continuous version: instead of whether you took vitamin E, look at how much, which is continuous, and condition on whether you exercised vigorously in the last month. The density curve shifts a little left or right depending on which group you're in. Same conclusion, reached by comparing densities rather than proportions.

Where it breaks. All of this is description. You've established that vitamin E use and smoking are statistically related. You have said nothing about which causes which, or whether either does.

4

Conditional means (and everything else)

Plain English. Once you have a distribution per slice, summarize each slice the way you'd summarize any distribution.

Precisely. Apply any summary statistic within each conditional distribution and compare across slices. Conditional means are by far the most common — most of causal inference is comparing conditional means — but conditional medians, standard deviations, skews, and interquartile ranges are all equally available.

Anchor image. You already own the measuring tape. Conditioning just means measuring each room instead of the whole house.

Worked example. "Is the average amount of vitamin E people take different depending on whether they exercise vigorously?" is a comparison of two conditional means. Useful detail: for a binary variable, the proportion is the mean — so "15% take vitamin E" is already a conditional mean when you compute it within a group.

5

Conditioning on a continuous variable

Plain English. You can't hold a continuous variable at an exact value, because essentially nobody shares one.

Precisely. Conditioning on, say, exactly 0.1376 grams of vitamin E gives you a slice containing maybe one person, maybe none. The crude fix is to bin the continuous variable — the same trick that turned a continuous variable into a histogram — and compute a conditional mean within each bin.

Worked example. Bin people by BMI, and within each bin compute the proportion taking vitamin E. Tabulate it, then graph it: conditional mean on the y-axis, BMI bin on the x-axis. You can now see how vitamin E use moves with BMI.

Where it breaks. Binning is arbitrary. Where do the cuts go? How many bins? Different choices give different pictures, and nothing in the data tells you which is right. That arbitrariness is exactly what line-fitting exists to replace.

Say it out loud

Here's a definition that made relationships between variables click for me.

Think about the sex of a baby — roughly fifty-fifty. Now suppose I tell you you're about to meet someone, and their name is Sarah. Your fifty-fifty just became something like ninety-five percent female. You haven't met them. You learned one thing, and the distribution of a completely different variable moved.

That's it. That's what it means for two variables to be related: learning about one changes the distribution of the other. It's called conditioning — you condition on the name, and you get a conditional distribution of sex.

And the opposite tells you when things are unrelated. Roll a die, get a one. What does that tell you about the next roll? Nothing at all — still one in six for every face. The conditional distribution is identical to the unconditional one, so the rolls are independent.

The nice part is you already know how to summarise a distribution — mean, median, spread. So you just do that inside each slice and compare. About fifteen percent of people take vitamin E overall; condition on smoking and the rate among smokers is lower than among non-smokers. Different conditional distributions, therefore related.

One catch. Conditioning on something continuous is awkward, because nobody shares an exact value — you can't find the group of people who take exactly 0.1376 grams. You can chop it into bins, but where you put the cuts is arbitrary. That's the problem line-fitting solves.

And the thing to hold onto: none of this is causal. We've shown vitamin E use and smoking are related. We have not said a word about what causes what.

Recall drill

QWhat does it mean to condition on a variable?

To hold it at a particular value and look at the distribution of another variable within that restriction.

QState the definition of two variables being related.

Learning the value of one changes the distribution of the other.

QWhat's the definition of independence?

Conditioning on one variable produces no change at all in the other's distribution — the conditional and unconditional distributions are the same.

QSex is ~50/50. You learn a stranger's name is Sarah. What has happened, in these terms?

You conditioned on name, and the conditional distribution of sex shifted to roughly 95% female. The shift is what makes name and sex related.

QYou roll a die and get a 1. Why does this demonstrate independence?

Because the distribution of the next roll is still 1/6 for every face — conditioning changed nothing.

QAbout 15% take vitamin E. Among smokers the share is lower. What have you established, and what haven't you?

Established: vitamin E use and smoking are statistically related. Not established: anything about causation or direction.

QWhy is vitamin E a suggestive example in the first place?

It was recommended by health professionals only for a stretch, so uptake plausibly tracked people who follow health advice generally — making it look related to healthy outcomes because healthy people chose to take it.

QHow do you condition on a continuous variable, and what's wrong with that?

Bin it and compute a conditional mean within each bin — as with BMI bins versus share taking vitamin E. The problem is the bins are arbitrary in both placement and number.

QWhy is "proportion" already a conditional mean for a binary variable?

Because the mean of a binary variable is its proportion, so computing a share within a group is computing a conditional mean.

QName three conditional summaries besides the conditional mean.

Conditional median, conditional standard deviation, conditional skew, conditional interquartile range — any summary statistic works within a slice.

Glossary

Conditioning
restricting attention to observations at a particular value of a variable.
Conditional distribution
the distribution of one variable among observations sharing a value of another.
Independence
the case where conditioning on one variable leaves the other's distribution unchanged.
Conditional mean
the mean of one variable computed within a value or bin of another.
Binning
grouping a continuous variable into ranges so it can be conditioned on.
