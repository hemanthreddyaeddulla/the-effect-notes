Ep 14Drawing Causal Diagrams

▶ Watch the video 15:20

Drawing a causal diagram is two steps — list the variables that explain why you'd see your data, then use prior knowledge to decide which causes which — and the variables you must not miss are the ones that cause both your treatment and your outcome, plus anything at all that causes the treatment.

The 30-second version

  • Nobody hands you a diagram. You have to draw your own.
  • Step 1: list the variables. Anything that helps explain why you'd see the data you see — measured or not.
  • Step 2: draw the arrows. This is where prior knowledge and prior research come in. You cannot draw a diagram while refusing to assume anything.
  • Highest priority: variables causing both treatment and outcome. Second: anything causing the treatment, because how well you understand why people got treated determines how well you can do causal inference at all.
  • Don't include everything. In social science the list of faintly relevant things is infinite. Trivial causes can be left off.
  • Variables cause each other too, not just the treatment and outcome.
  • Your first attempt will be an absolute mess. That's normal, and simplifying it is the next episode.

Why this exists

You know what a causal diagram is and what an arrow means. But being handed one is not a thing that happens — if you want to use this to identify an effect, you have to be able to build one from your own understanding of a setting.

The honest framing: you're trying to write down what you think the data generating process looks like. You observed some relationship between a treatment and an outcome. What set of underlying laws would produce that relationship, in that direction, at that strength?

The ideas

1

Step 1: list the variables

Plain English. Write down everything that helps explain why the data looks the way it does.

Precisely. Enumerate the measurements or concepts that plausibly bear on your data generating process. Some will be measured variables you can get hold of. Others will be abstract concepts you can't measure — include them anyway.

Worked example. Nick's own study: does taking online courses affect your chances of staying in college? Maybe it changes how much you learn, or your affiliation with the college.

Two variables to start with — the ones the question names:

  • Treatment: was the class online or face-to-face?
  • Outcome: did you stay in school or drop out?

Then ask what determines each of those:

2

The two categories you must not miss

Plain English. Anything causing both treatment and outcome. And anything causing the treatment.

Precisely. Variables that cause both are the ones that will produce a relationship between treatment and outcome that isn't your effect. Separately, and more broadly, anything that causes the treatment matters enormously, because a great deal of causal inference comes down to how well you understand why different people got different levels of treatment.

Anchor image. Before you can say the medicine worked, you have to know who got it and why. If you don't know how the pills were handed out, nothing you observe afterwards is interpretable.

Worked example. Work hours, in the online-courses study. Someone working a lot:

  • is more likely to want an online course, so it doesn't clash with the job, and
  • is more likely to drop out — it's too much time on top of work, or they got a promotion and no longer need the degree.

Related to both. That one absolutely has to be on the diagram.

Where it breaks. The difficulty in causal inference is usually that you don't fully understand why some people got treated and others didn't. The less you know about that, the more back-door trouble you invite in — which is exactly what the next chapter is about.

3

Don't put the whole world on it

Plain English. You could keep going until you'd drawn all of society. Don't.

Precisely. Something being technically a cause doesn't earn it a place. If a variable has only a very minor effect or relationship, you can generally leave it off. In the social sciences the list of things that might be slightly relevant is effectively infinite, and trying to include all of it is a trap.

Worked example. Whether you live near a quiet café might affect whether you take an online course — somewhere pleasant to work. For a handful of people, that's genuinely true. It's not a major determinant for most. Leave it off.

Where it breaks. Judgement call, and you can get it wrong in both directions. Ep 15 gives you a way to check.

4

Step 2: draw the arrows

Plain English. Go pair by pair. Does this cause that? Why do you think so? Which direction?

Precisely. This is where you lean on prior knowledge — things you've learned from previous research, or things you simply know about how the world works. It's essentially impossible to draw a causal diagram while refusing to make assumptions or stand on any existing knowledge. You can't learn more without staking something you already believe.

Worked example — the reasoning, one arrow at a time.

  • Almost everything → dropout. Demographic background characteristics all relate to dropping out; rates differ across those groups. Not necessarily causing dropout, but related somehow, which you might shorthand as an arrow.
  • Internet access → online classes, but not dropout. Bad internet and you won't take an online course. Little direct effect on whether you drop out.
  • Variables cause each other, too. Socioeconomic status → preferences: how you were raised shaped your feelings about online courses. Socioeconomic status → internet access: grow up with money and a decent connection is probably available.

That last chain is a theory Nick holds about the world. He came up with it himself — but if you're doing the best job you can, you go looking for research that backs a given arrow up. Basing arrows on evidence rather than invention is also how a field ends up agreeing on diagrams.

5

The result is a mess, and that's normal

Plain English. Your first diagram will be unusable. Everyone's is.

Precisely. A long list of variables, arrows ping-ponging in every direction, plus unlabelled unobserved variables U1 through U5 explaining why things are related when neither causes the other. This is the social sciences — everything is related to everything.

Worked example. U5 causes both socioeconomic status and location. Why not an arrow? You wouldn't confidently say socioeconomic status causes your location, and you wouldn't confidently say location causes socioeconomic status — at least not strongly enough to stake a claim. So you say they're related and give them a common cause.

Someone with a different theory might draw it differently — they might say socioeconomic status causes location. Your theory determines your diagram, which is another reason to ground arrows in sources.

Where it breaks. You can't do much with a mess. Simplification is next.

Say it out loud

Nobody's ever going to hand you a causal diagram, so you have to draw your own. Two steps.

List the variables, then draw the arrows.

Say I want to know whether taking online courses makes you more or less likely to stay in college. Start with the two the question names — online versus face-to-face, and stayed versus dropped out. Then ask what causes each. Your preferences about education, which I can't measure but which obviously matter. Demographics. Socioeconomic status. How much freedom you've got in your schedule. Whether you've got internet at all.

Now — which of these matter most? Two categories. First, anything that causes both the treatment and the outcome. Work hours is the perfect example: if you work a lot you're more likely to want an online course so it doesn't clash with your job, and more likely to drop out because it's too much, or because you got promoted and don't need the degree anymore. That one has to be on there.

Second — and this is the one people underrate — anything that causes the treatment at all. Because so much of causal inference comes down to understanding why some people got treated and others didn't. The less you know about that, the more trouble sneaks in.

But don't put the whole world on it. Living near a quiet café probably nudges a few people toward online classes. It's a real cause. Leave it off — in social science the list of faintly relevant things is infinite.

Then you draw arrows, pair by pair, from what you know and what previous research shows. And here's the thing people resist: you cannot do this without assumptions. If you're not willing to stand on something you already believe, you can't draw anything.

And when you're done it'll be an absolute mess. Arrows everywhere, unobserved variables labelled U1 through U5. That's normal. Cleaning it up is the next step.

Recall drill

QWhat are the two steps in drawing a causal diagram?

List the variables relevant to the data generating process, then use prior knowledge and research to decide which variables cause which, and draw the arrows.

QWhy can't you draw a causal diagram without making assumptions?

The arrows come from prior knowledge and previous research. If you refuse to rest on anything you already believe, there's nothing to draw arrows from — and you can't learn more without staking something.

QWhich variables are the highest priority to include?

Ones that cause both the treatment and the outcome, and — more broadly — anything at all that causes the treatment.

QWhy does anything causing the treatment matter so much?

Because much of causal inference depends on understanding why different people got different levels of treatment. The less you understand that, the more back-door problems you let in.

QIn the online-courses study, why do work hours have to be on the diagram?

They cause both. Working a lot makes an online course more attractive (it doesn't clash with the job) and makes dropping out more likely (too much time, or a promotion removes the need for the degree).

QYou realise living near a quiet café slightly affects whether people take online courses. Include it?

No. It's a genuine cause for a handful of people but not a major determinant, and in social science the list of faintly relevant causes is infinite.

QShould unmeasurable variables like "preferences about education" be included?

Yes. Whether you can measure something has nothing to do with whether it causes things.

QDo arrows only run into the treatment and outcome?

No — variables cause each other. Socioeconomic status causes preferences, and it causes internet access.

QWhy draw U5 causing both socioeconomic status and location instead of an arrow between them?

Because you wouldn't confidently claim either direction. They're related, so you give them a common cause rather than assert a causal direction you can't defend.

QYour first diagram is a chaotic mess. What does that mean?

Nothing has gone wrong — that's the normal first result, because in social science everything relates to everything. Simplification is the next step.

QWhy is it better to base arrows on previous research than on your own reasoning?

It makes the diagram defensible and helps a field converge on the same diagram, rather than everyone drawing from private intuition.

Glossary

Treatment
the variable whose effect you want to measure.
Outcome
the variable you want to see the effect on.
Prior knowledge
what you already believe about a setting, from research or experience; the source of every arrow.
Unobserved variable (U1, U2, …)
an unmeasured cause, often used to explain why two variables are related without committing to a direction between them.
