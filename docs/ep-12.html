Ep 12Causality

▶ Watch the video 11:00
On this pageThe 30-second versionWhy this existsThe ideasTrapsSay it out loudRecall drillConnectionsGlossary

X causes Y if reaching in and changing X would change the probability distribution of Y — which means causality is about intervention, not about two things happening together, and it's a claim about distributions rather than about what actually happened.

The 30-second version

  • Causal diagrams, developed by Judea Pearl, are a way of writing a data generating process down. Before drawing one you need to be precise about what an arrow means.
  • The working definition: intervene on X, and the distribution of Y changes as a result.
  • This is not the same as a conditional relationship. Names and sex are related; renaming someone doesn't change their sex.
  • "A cause" is not "the only cause". Opening your hand causes the pen to drop, alongside gravity and there being nothing underneath.
  • It's about distributions, not events. A wet floor nobody slips on still caused slipping — it raised the probability.
  • The tell in language is direction. "X causes Y" ≠ "Y causes X", but "X is related to Y" = "Y is related to X".

Why this exists

You've got identification as a goal and the data generating process as the thing you're trying to recover. What you don't have is a way to write a DGP down so you can work with it.

Causal diagrams are that notation. They're lists of variables with arrows between them, and each arrow asserts that one variable causes another. So before drawing a single one, you'd better be able to say exactly what you're asserting when you draw an arrow.

Worth noting up front: the definition of causality varies by field. A physicist means something different by it and would object to what follows. This is the definition that works when you're inferring causal relationships from statistical data.

The ideas

1

Causality is intervention

Plain English. If you could reach in and change X, would Y change as a result?

Precisely. X causes Y when intervening to change the value of X changes the probability distribution of Y. The intervention is the whole point — it's what distinguishes causality from any amount of things merely co-occurring.

Anchor image. A light switch versus a rooster. Both reliably precede the arrival of light. Flip one and the light comes on; silence the other and the sun still rises.

Worked example. From Ep 6: the proportion of people who are women is much higher among people named Susan than among people named Chris. That's a genuine conditional relationship — learning the name shifts the distribution of sex.

Now intervene. Convince someone named Susan to change their name to Denise. Does their probability of being a man or a woman shift? No. You changed the name and nothing happened to the other distribution.

So: related, yes. Causal, no.

Where it breaks. The two variables really are related, and that relationship is real and useful — it's just not the thing an arrow asserts.

2

A cause, not the cause

Plain English. Saying X causes Y doesn't claim X is the only thing that matters.

Precisely. A variable can have many causes at once. Being one of them is enough to draw the arrow.

Anchor image. A recipe. Flour caused the cake. So did the eggs, the oven, and someone turning it on.

Worked example. Nick holds a pen, opens his hand, and the pen drops. Two variables: whether the hand is open or closed, and where the pen is. Intervene on the hand and the pen's position changes — causal.

But opening the hand wasn't the whole story. There was also nothing underneath to stop it, and the earth was down there pulling it. Many factors caused the drop. That doesn't make the hand not a cause; it makes it a partial cause, and that's what the arrow claims.

3

Distributions, not events

Plain English. Something can cause an outcome that never actually occurred.

Precisely. The definition is about changing the distribution of a variable, not about producing a particular realisation. Raise the probability of Y and you've caused Y, in this sense, whether or not Y happened.

Anchor image. Loading extra faces of a die with sixes. You caused the sixes even on a roll that came up two.

Worked example. A lawyer thinks about causality in terms of things that actually happened. There's a wet spot in front of Nick's store. You walk past and think, someone could really slip on that. Nobody does. You can't sue — no event occurred that he could have caused.

From a causal inference point of view, though: making the floor wet changed the distribution of people falling. It raised the probability. So he is a cause of slipping even though nobody slipped.

Second worked example. You give someone a scholarship they'd never have had, so they go to college. They graduate and then do exactly what they would have done anyway. Nothing observable changed. But sending them to college changed the distribution of occupations available to them — it raised the probability they'd become a lawyer, even though they didn't. So the scholarship caused their occupation, even though they ended up in the same place.

Where it breaks. The reverse case too: causes don't have to work every time. Flipping a light switch causes the lamp to come on. Sometimes the bulb is burned out or the lamp isn't plugged in and nothing happens. We'd still say switches cause lamps to light — it changes the distribution, even when it doesn't change the outcome you observe.

4

Direction is the tell in language

Plain English. Causal words point one way. Non-causal words read the same in both directions.

Precisely. To say two variables are related without implying causality: associated, correlated, related, tend to occur together, tend not to occur together, go together. To assert causality: X causes Y, X affects Y, the effect of X on Y, X increases or decreases Y, X changes Y, X leads to Y, X determines Y.

Anchor image. "Next to" versus "pushed". Two things can be next to each other in either order. Only one of them did the pushing.

Worked example. "X causes Y" and "Y causes X" are different statements. "X is related to Y" and "Y is related to X" are the same statement. That asymmetry is the signature of a causal claim.

Where it breaks. Watch for words that smuggle direction in without earning it: X is linked to Y, X is followed by Y, X has ramifications for Y. Technically these say nothing causal. They're built to put a causal idea in your head when the evidence can't.

Say it out loud

Before you can draw a causal diagram you need to know what an arrow actually claims. And the definition that works here is about intervention: X causes Y if reaching in and changing X would change the distribution of Y.

Here's the cleanest way to see why that's different from things just going together. People named Susan are overwhelmingly likely to be women; people named Chris, much less so. Real relationship. Now intervene — talk Susan into changing her name to Denise. Did her probability of being a woman move? Obviously not. Related, but not causal.

Two things people get wrong about this. First, causing something doesn't mean being the only cause. I drop a pen — my hand opening caused that. So did gravity, so did there being nothing underneath it. My hand was a cause, and that's all an arrow ever claims.

Second — and this one's genuinely strange — it's about distributions, not events. Say there's a wet patch outside my shop and nobody slips on it. A lawyer would say I caused nothing; no event, no lawsuit. But I changed the probability someone falls, and in causal inference that means I caused falling, even though nobody fell.

Same in reverse. A light switch causes the lamp to come on — even when the bulb's burned out and nothing happens. Causes shift distributions; they don't guarantee outcomes.

And there's a giveaway in the language. Causal words have a direction. "X causes Y" and "Y causes X" are different claims. But "X is related to Y" and "Y is related to X" are the same claim. So when you see "linked to" or "followed by" — those sneak the direction in without earning it. Watch for them.

Recall drill

QState the working definition of causality used in causal inference.

X causes Y if intervening to change the value of X would change the probability distribution of Y.

QWho developed causal diagrams, and what are they for?

Judea Pearl. They're a way of writing a data generating process down so you can use it to identify an effect.

QNames and sex are strongly related. Why isn't that causal?

Because intervening — persuading someone named Susan to become Denise — doesn't change the distribution of their sex. Conditional relationship, no causal one.

QNick opens his hand and the pen falls. Was his hand the cause?

No — a partial cause. Gravity and there being nothing underneath were causes too. An arrow only ever claims "a cause".

QThere's a wet spot outside a store and nobody slips. Did the wet spot cause slipping?

In causal inference terms, yes — it raised the probability of someone falling, which is what causing means. A lawyer would say no, because no event occurred.

QYou flip a light switch and nothing happens because the bulb is burned out. Does the switch cause the light?

Yes. Causes change distributions rather than guaranteeing outcomes, so a failure on one occasion doesn't revoke the causal relationship.

QSomeone gets a scholarship, goes to college, then does exactly what they'd have done anyway. Did college cause their occupation?

Yes — it changed the distribution of occupations available to them, raising the probability of outcomes like becoming a lawyer, even though they landed in the same place.

QList some words that assert a relationship without asserting causality.

Associated, correlated, related, tend to occur together, tend not to occur together, go together.

QList some words that assert causality.

Causes, affects, the effect of, increases, decreases, changes, leads to, determines.

QWhat structural feature distinguishes causal language from non-causal language?

Direction. "X causes Y" and "Y causes X" are different statements; "X is related to Y" and "Y is related to X" are the same one.

QWhy are "linked to" and "followed by" worth being suspicious of?

They imply a direction without technically claiming causality — planting a causal idea where the evidence doesn't support one.

Glossary

Causal diagram
a graph of variables and arrows representing a data generating process, developed by Judea Pearl.
Intervention
reaching in and setting the value of a variable, as opposed to observing it.
Cause
X causes Y if intervening on X changes the probability distribution of Y.
Partial cause
one of several causes of a variable; what an arrow in a causal diagram asserts.
