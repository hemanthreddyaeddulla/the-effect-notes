Ep 53Estimating Difference-in-Differences

▶ Watch the video 13:20
On this pageThe 30-second versionWhy this existsThe ideasTrapsSay it out loudRecall drillConnectionsGlossary

Compute four means and subtract twice, or run a regression with a treated × after interaction where β₃ is the answer, or — for many groups and many periods — use a two-way fixed effects model; and all of these break the moment different groups get treated at different times.

The 30-second version

  • Four means: treated after minus treated before, minus (control after minus control before). Done.
  • Limitations: no standard errors, and it doesn't extend past two groups and two periods.
  • Regression version: regress the outcome on treated group, after treatment, and their interaction. The four coefficients recover the four means, and β₃ is the effect.
  • Two-way fixed effects: a fixed effect for group, a fixed effect for time, and a variable for "currently being treated." Handles many groups and periods.
  • Cluster standard errors by group.
  • Critical: all of these require every treatment to begin at the same time. Staggered treatment breaks them.

Why this exists

The concept is settled. Now you have to actually estimate it — which means comparing the treated group's before-to-after change against the control group's, and subtracting.

The ideas

1

The four means approach

Plain English. Just literally do it. Four averages, two subtractions.

Precisely. Calculate the average outcome after treatment for the treated group and the average before, and subtract. Do the same for the control group. Now you have a before-to-after difference for each, and you subtract one from the other.

2

Worked example: organ donation and forced choice

Plain English. California made you answer the organ donor question instead of letting you skip it, and donation rates fell.

Precisely. In the United States, organ donation is an opt-in system: when you get your driver's licence they ask whether you want to be an organ donor, and you check a box for yes or leave it. That produces much lower donation rates than countries with opt-out systems, where you're assumed to be a donor unless you say otherwise.

Forced choice is a variant in between. You still have to opt in, but you're required to respond — yes for yes, no for no. You can't simply ignore it.

The question: does forced choice increase organ donation rates? Kessler and Roth looked at California, which adopted forced choice in July 2011, and compared it against a set of other, comparable states that didn't.

The graph. Four lines: a dashed line before and after the July 2011 cutoff for the control states, and a solid line before and after for California. There are three quarters of California donation data before treatment and three quarters after; average each to get the two solid segments. The estimation is: see how much the dashed line jumped at the cutoff, declare that's how much California would have jumped anyway, and push the lines down so the dashed segments meet — moving California's line down by the same amount.

The four means, in organ donation rates:

GroupBeforeAfterChange
Control (other states)44.545.9+1.4
California (treated)27.126.3−0.8
Difference-in-differences−2.2

The reasoning. Control-group donation rates were going up over time — so absent any policy, California's probably would have gone up too, by around 1.4 percentage points. Instead they went down by 0.8. So the policy appears to have made things worse by 2.2 percentage points.

The arithmetic is just the four means in the right order: (26.3 − 27.1) − (45.9 − 44.5) = (−0.8) − (1.4) = −2.2.

Where it breaks. Two obvious limitations. It's hard to get a standard error this way. And it's very difficult to go beyond two groups and two time periods — what if you have data from more periods, or more groups?

3

The regression version

Plain English. Outcome on treated, on after, and on their interaction. The interaction is the answer.

Precisely. Set up a regression that estimates those four means for you. Regress the outcome on:

  • an indicator for being in the treated group
  • an indicator for being after treatment
  • the interaction between the two — which is also just asking, are you currently being treated?

The four coefficients map onto the four means:

treated groupafter treatmentCombinationWhich mean
00β₀Untreated group, before
10β₀ + β₁Treated group, before — so β₁ is the baseline difference
01β₀ + β₂Untreated group, after
11β₀ + β₁ + β₂ + β₃Treated group, after

β₃ is your difference-in-differences effect — the regression does the calculation for you.

Why. Ask what happens when after goes from 0 to 1:

  • Untreated group: from β₀ to β₀ + β₂ — an increase of β₂. That's the time effect.
  • Treated group: from β₀ + β₁ to all four terms — an increase of β₂ + β₃. That's the time effect and the treatment effect.

Subtract the time effect β₂ and you're left with β₃.

Anchor image. Two receipts with a shared surcharge. Subtract the common line item and only the thing you actually bought remains.

Where it breaks. That's a lot to hold at once — it's a fairly standard application of an interaction term, so reviewing Ep 32 helps as much as re-reading this.

4

Beyond two groups and two periods: two-way fixed effects

Plain English. Fixed effects for group and time, plus one variable saying "treated right now."

Precisely. The organ donation study actually had six time periods — three quarters before and three after — and a number of different states. You might not want to lump them all into a single control and treatment, before and after; you might want their individual variation to show.

And you already know the tool. You're looking at within variation — the before-to-after change for the treated group, and the before-to-after change for the control group. Within variation means fixed effects.

So regress the outcome on:

TermWhat it does
α_g — fixed effect for groupEnsures you're working within within-variation
α_t — fixed effect for timeSubtracts out the shared time effects, estimated using the control group
treated — 1 if you're in a treated group in a post-treatment periodIts coefficient β₁ is the effect of treatment

That leaves β₁ as the treatment effect from before to after. This is the two-way fixed effects model, and it's probably the easiest way to estimate difference-in-differences. It gives you nice standard errors, and you can cluster them by group — a common choice, since you'd expect observations within a group to be more similar than across groups.

5

The warning that matters most

Plain English. Everything above assumes everyone got treated at the same moment.

Precisely. All of these methods — the four means approach and all the regression methods — only work if all of the treatments went into effect at the same time.

Worked example. California is fine: there's only one California, treated once, in July 2011. But suppose Wyoming also switched to forced choice — California in July 2011, Wyoming in July 2012.

That's staggered treatment. You can still work with it, but you cannot use these regression methods to do it. It doesn't work properly.

Say it out loud

Three ways to actually estimate a difference-in-differences, and they're the same thing getting progressively more general.

Four means. Average the treated group before and after, subtract. Average the control before and after, subtract. Subtract those two differences. Done.

Great example: organ donation. In the US it's opt-in — you tick a box for your driver's licence, or don't. California switched to forced choice in July 2011, meaning you now have to answer: yes for yes, no for no. Question is whether that raises donation rates.

Control states went from 44.5 to 45.9 — up 1.4. California went from 27.1 to 26.3 — down 0.8. So everybody else was drifting up, California drifted down, and the estimate is minus 2.2 percentage points. The policy appears to have made things worse.

Four means gets you the number but no standard errors, and it can't handle more than two groups. So regression: outcome on treated, on after, and on the interaction. Four coefficients for four means — and β₃, the interaction, is your answer. Because when you go from before to after, the untreated group moves by β₂ — that's just time — and the treated group moves by β₂ plus β₃. Subtract time and you're left with β₃.

Then for many groups and many periods: two-way fixed effects. Fixed effect for group, fixed effect for time, and one variable that says "are you being treated right now." That last coefficient is your effect. Cluster your standard errors by group.

And now the warning, and it's a big one. Every single method here assumes everyone got treated at the same time. California in July 2011 — fine. But if Wyoming switches in July 2012, you have staggered treatment, and none of this works. That's a whole separate problem.

Recall drill

QDescribe the four means approach.

Compute the treated group's average before and after and subtract; do the same for the control group; then subtract the control group's difference from the treated group's.

QWhat is forced choice, and how does it differ from opt-in and opt-out?

You still have to opt in, but you're required to answer the question — yes for yes, no for no — rather than being able to ignore it. It sits between opt-in and opt-out.

QWhat did Kessler and Roth study, and what was the treatment?

Whether forced choice raises organ donation rates. California adopted forced choice in July 2011, compared against other comparable states.

QGive the four means.

Control states 44.5 before and 45.9 after; California 27.1 before and 26.3 after.

QCompute the difference-in-differences and interpret it.

(26.3 − 27.1) − (45.9 − 44.5) = −0.8 − 1.4 = −2.2 percentage points — the policy appears to have reduced donation rates by 2.2 points.

QWhy isn't California's 0.8-point drop the answer on its own?

Because the control states rose by 1.4, so California would likely have risen too. The shortfall is 2.2, not 0.8.

QWhat are the two limitations of the four means approach?

It's hard to obtain standard errors, and it doesn't extend beyond two groups and two time periods.

QWhat three terms go in the regression version?

An indicator for being in the treated group, an indicator for being after treatment, and their interaction.

QWhat does each coefficient represent?

β₀ is the untreated group before; β₀ + β₁ the treated group before, so β₁ is the baseline difference; β₀ + β₂ the untreated group after, so β₂ is the time effect; and all four terms give the treated group after.

QWhich coefficient is the difference-in-differences effect, and why?

β₃ — because going from before to after, the untreated group rises by β₂ while the treated group rises by β₂ + β₃, so subtracting the time effect leaves β₃.

QWhat three terms make up the two-way fixed effects model?

A fixed effect for group, a fixed effect for time, and a variable equal to one if you're in a treated group during a post-treatment period.

QWhat does each of the two fixed effects accomplish?

The group fixed effect keeps you working within within-variation; the time fixed effect subtracts out the shared time effects estimated from the control group.

QHow should standard errors usually be handled here?

Clustered by group, since observations within a group are likely more similar than across groups.

QWhat is the crucial limitation shared by every method in this episode?

They only work if all treatments went into effect at the same time.

QWhat is staggered treatment, and what can you do about it?

When different groups get treated at different times — California in July 2011 and Wyoming in July
