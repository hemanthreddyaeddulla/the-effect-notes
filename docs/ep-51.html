Ep 51Difference-in-Differences

▶ Watch the video 14:00
On this pageThe 30-second versionWhy this existsThe ideasTrapsSay it out loudRecall drillConnectionsGlossary

Add a group that never got treated, watch how they changed over the same period, and treat that change as what would have happened to the treated group anyway — subtract it out, and what's left is the effect.

The 30-second version

  • One of the most commonly applied designs, because the conditions are so easy to find.
  • All you need: a treatment that goes into effect at a particular time and affects some people and not others.
  • The problem it solves: you have a before/after comparison but don't trust any event-study way of closing the time back door — and you can't control for time, because that removes the treatment entirely.
  • The fix: bring in a control group that was never treated.
  • This makes the diagram worse — a second back door through group — but it lets you close the time one.
  • Logic: the control group's change over time is what would have happened to the treated group anyway. Subtract it.
  • John Snow's 1850s cholera study is a difference-in-differences before the name existed.

Why this exists

Difference-in-differences is commonly applied because it's very easy to find conditions in which it seems to apply. You don't need much: a treatment that goes into effect at a particular time, and a treatment that affects some people and not others. That's it. There are further assumptions required for it to work properly — but with that basic structure in place, you can probably get a difference-in-differences going, which is a large part of why it's so popular.

A policy enacted in one country but not another? Difference-in-differences. A school makes a reform and a different school doesn't? Difference-in-differences.

The ideas

1

The problem being solved

Plain English. You have a before and an after, and no way to trust the before-and-after comparison.

Precisely. You have a treatment that went into effect at a particular time — an event, a change, a new policy implemented, an old one cancelled — with a before period and an after period. Event studies handle exactly this, comparing before to after with some adjustment, perhaps for a trend.

But what if you don't believe any of those ways of closing the time back door?

Worked example. A school introduces a teaching reform and you want to know whether it improves students' maths scores. You can look at how they were doing before and how they were doing after — but how do you tell it's the effect of the new policy and not that things would have changed anyway? Maybe there's no real way to use a time trend to account for that.

And you can't just control for time. Control for the differences in time that determine treatment and there is literally no treatment variation left. Time and treatment go hand in hand — you can't control for one without controlling away the entire treatment.

2

The fix: make the problem more complex

Plain English. Bring in a second group that never got treated.

Precisely. So far there's only the group that gets treated, observed before and after. Now add a group that did not get the treatment in either period — your control group, or comparison group.

And this makes the causal diagram look worse:

You still have the back door through time, and now there's an additional back door because which group you're in clearly determines whether you're treated — one group got treated, the other didn't — and there are likely baseline differences between those groups anyway.

Things seem to have gotten worse. Except that the addition of this group is what lets you close the time back door.

3

How the control group closes the time back door

Plain English. Whatever happened to the untreated group is what would have happened to the treated group anyway.

Precisely. The before/after comparison for the treated group contains two things at once: the effect of treatment, and whatever would have changed over time anyway. In fixed effects language, you're looking at that group's within variation — which solves some problems, but does not control for anything that changes over time. And that's exactly what you're worried about.

Now look at the control group's within variation — how they changed from before to after.

The placebo-test intuition. If there were no time variation going on — if the time back door didn't matter, and only the treatment changed things — you'd expect the control group not to change at all. So if you look at the control group and see no change, that's a decent indication you can simply compare before and after for the treated group.

And if the control group did change? Say maths scores at the school with no reform went up. That tells you something would have changed over time regardless. And it tells you roughly how much — that's probably the amount of change you'd have expected in the treated group too.

Worked example.

GroupChange from before to after
Control school (no reform)+5 points
Treated school (reform)+8 points
Difference of the differences+3 points ← the treatment effect

Five of those eight points are probably what would have happened anyway, because that's what you saw in the untreated school. The extra three points is your effect.

Anchor image. Two boats rising with the tide. One also has an engine. To find out what the engine did, measure how much more it rose than the other boat.

That's the design. Take the difference in the treated group from before to after. Take the difference in the control group from before to after. Treat the second as what would have happened anyway, subtract it from the first — a difference between the differences — and whatever remains is the treatment effect.

4

Worked example: John Snow and the London cholera epidemic

Plain English. A water company moved its intake upstream, and Snow compared its customers' death rates against everyone else's — before the name "difference-in-differences" existed.

Precisely. In 1850s London — and quite often in European cities — cholera outbreaks were common and extremely deadly. Nobody knew how cholera spread, which made it very hard to stop.

The leading theory was miasma theory: that bad smells transmitted disease from one person to another. That's why old pictures show people in big robes with the large beaked nose-masks — they packed herbs inside so it would smell nice and ward off the bad smells.

Snow thought cholera travelled through water. And he had a way to test it.

The setting. One of the main ways to get water in London was to go to a pump and pump your own. Different companies supplied different pumps, and those companies drew their water from different sources.

The treatment. The Lambeth company moved its water source from the middle of the Thames to a point before the city — upstream, before the waste went in. Clean water flows toward London, London dumps a lot of sewage into the river, and anyone drawing water from that downstream portion should be getting cholera spread to them, if cholera spreads through water.

The design. Treated group: areas supplied by Lambeth, whose water source changed. Control group: areas supplied by the other pumps, which didn't change.

Death rates from cholera, per 10,000 people:

BeforeAfter (1854)Change
Non-Lambeth (control)134.9146.6+11.7
Lambeth (treated)130.184.9−45.2
Difference-in-differences−56.9

Note there were already baseline differences before treatment — 134.9 against 130.1 — though not a huge gap.

The reasoning. Look at Lambeth alone: they moved their pump upstream and death rates dropped. So maybe water does transmit the disease. But maybe death rates would have dropped anyway — maybe the epidemic simply burned out on its own, which is how it had always gone before. Cholera comes, cholera goes.

If it had gone away on its own, you'd have expected death rates to drop for the non-Lambeth companies too. They didn't. They got higher. So it was the treatment that lowered death rates for Lambeth's customers.

Where it landed. They ended up changing how the water system worked in response to Snow's findings — although they didn't quite buy his theory, they were willing to go along with the results — and it saved a great many lives. So all this observational data business does seem to have at least some purpose.

Say it out loud

Difference-in-differences is probably the most used design in the book, and the reason is that the conditions are almost embarrassingly easy to meet. You need a treatment that starts at some point in time, and it has to hit some people and not others. That's the whole requirement.

Here's the problem it fixes. A school runs a teaching reform, and you compare maths scores before and after. But how do you know that's the reform and not just... things changing? And you can't control for time, because time is the treatment — before, nobody's treated; after, everybody is. Control for it and there's nothing left.

So you bring in a second school that didn't do the reform. And notice this actually makes your diagram worse — now there's a back door through which group you're in, on top of the time one. But it's worth it.

Because now: the control school's maths scores went up 5 points. Nobody treated them, so that 5 points is just "what happens over time." And the treated school went up 8. So 5 of those points would have happened anyway, and the extra 3 is your effect. Difference between the differences.

And the best example of this is 170 years old. London, 1850s, cholera everywhere. The going theory was miasma — that bad smells carried disease, which is why the plague doctors had those beaked masks stuffed with herbs. John Snow thought it was the water.

And he got lucky: the Lambeth water company moved its intake upstream, above where London dumps its sewage. Everyone else's stayed put. So — Lambeth's death rate fell from 130 to 85. Great, water theory confirmed?

Not so fast. Cholera epidemics always burned out on their own. Maybe it would have fallen anyway. So check the control group: their rate went from 135 to 147. It went up. So not only did Lambeth's fall — it fell while everyone else's was rising. That's about 57 fewer deaths per ten thousand, and it changed how London built its water system.

Recall drill

QWhat are the minimum conditions for a difference-in-differences design?

A treatment that goes into effect at a particular time, and that treatment affecting some people and not others.

QWhat problem is it solving that event studies couldn't?

A before/after comparison where you don't trust any of the event-study methods for closing the time back door.

QWhy can't you close the time back door by controlling for time?

Time determines treatment completely — nobody treated before, everybody after — so controlling for time removes all treatment variation.

QWhat does adding a control group do to the causal diagram?

It makes it worse — adding a second back door through which group you're in, on top of the time back door.

QWhat two things does the treated group's before/after change contain?

The effect of treatment, and whatever would have changed over time anyway.

QWhy doesn't fixed effects logic solve this on its own?

Within variation controls for things constant over time but doesn't control for anything that changes over time — which is exactly the concern.

QWhat's the placebo-test intuition for the control group?

If the time back door didn't matter, the untreated group shouldn't change at all. If they don't, you can compare the treated group before and after directly.

QThe control school gains 5 points and the treated school gains 8. What's the effect?

3 points — five of the eight would have happened anyway, judging by the control school.

QState the design in one sentence.

Take the treated group's before-to-after difference, subtract the control group's before-to-after difference, and what remains is the treatment effect.

QWhat was miasma theory?

The prevailing belief that bad smells transmitted disease — hence the beaked masks packed with herbs.

QWhat was the treatment in John Snow's study?

The Lambeth water company moving its water source from the middle of the Thames to a point upstream, before the city's sewage entered the river.

QWho were the treated and control groups?

Treated: areas supplied by Lambeth, whose source changed. Control: areas supplied by other pumps, whose sources didn't.

QGive the four death rates per 10,000.

Non-Lambeth: 134.9 before, 146.6 after. Lambeth: 130.1 before, 84.9 after.

QCompute the difference-in-differences.

Lambeth fell by 45.2, non-Lambeth rose by 11.7, so the estimate is about 56.9 fewer deaths per 10,000.

QWhy wasn't Lambeth's drop alone convincing?

Cholera epidemics had always burned out on their own, so the rate might have fallen regardless.

QWhat ruled that out?

The non-Lambeth death rate didn't fall — it rose. So the epidemic wasn't burning out on its own.

subtracting a control group's before-to-after change from the treated group's, to remove what would have happened anyway.
Control group / comparison group
a group untreated in both periods, used to estimate the time effect.
Miasma theory
the 19th-century belief that disease spread through bad smells.
