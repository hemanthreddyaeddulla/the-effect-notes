Ep 2Research Questions

▶ Watch the video 13:10

You care about why the world works the way it does, but data can only show you what happened — so a research question is the bridge you build from a why to an observable what and back again, and most research fails because that bridge doesn't actually hold.

The 30-second version

  • Research starts with a theory — a claim about why something is the way it is.
  • Data can't see why. It only reports what. So you need a what you can observe that genuinely informs your why.
  • The test of a good question: imagine every result you could get, and ask whether each would change your mind. If you'd explain away the inconvenient one, your question isn't testing your theory.
  • Then check the practical filters: is it feasible, is it affordable at scale, does a setting exist where the design works?
  • And keep it small. One study answers one narrow question, not "why are some countries rich?"

Why this exists

You don't do research because you want a number. You do it because you have an idea about how the world fits together and you want to know if it's right. But the thing you can measure is never the idea itself — it's some observable trace of it.

The gap between those two is where research goes wrong, and it goes wrong before any data is collected. Get too deep in before checking, and you finish the analysis only to realise the number you produced never had the power to change your mind about the theory in the first place.

Everything here is quantitative empirical research. The advice doesn't transfer to questions that aren't answered with data.

The ideas

1

Theory is "why", data is "what"

Plain English. Your theory says because. Your data says this is what we saw. Data is very good at what and very bad at why.

Precisely. A theory is any claim containing an explanation — a why, a because, a mechanism. A research question names something observable that, if you saw it, would update your belief in that theory. The job is: go from the why to a what you can measure, then reason from that what back to the why.

Anchor image. You want to know whether the neighbour is home. You can't see inside. You can see whether the lights are on. "Lights" isn't "home" — but if you picked the right observable and you know how lights and people relate, the lights genuinely tell you something. Pick the wrong observable and you're staring at a timer switch, learning nothing.

Worked example. Theory: being exposed to lots of different cultures makes you smarter — one reason some people are more intelligent than others.

The obvious what: look in data, check whether intelligence and cultural exposure move together. They do. Does that inform the why?

No — because the correlation is equally consistent with smarter people choosing more cultural exposure. The what you picked can't distinguish the two, so it can't move your belief.

Now change the what: randomly assign some people to cultural exposure and follow up on their intelligence later. Same two variables, same kind of relationship measured — but now the arrow from intelligence back to exposure is broken by the randomisation, so the result actually bears on the theory.

Where it breaks. Note what changed. Not the theory, not the variables, not the statistic. Only the question, and with it the design.

2

Imagine the results before you have them

Plain English. Before running anything, picture each result you might get and ask honestly: would that change what I believe?

Precisely. Take every plausible outcome, including the ones that contradict you, and write down your reaction to each. If a contradicting result would make you start generating excuses, then a confirming result shouldn't have moved you either. That asymmetry is the tell: the question isn't connected to the theory.

Anchor image. A bet you can't lose is a bet that isn't measuring anything. If the coin comes up tails and you say "well, that one didn't count", you weren't betting.

Worked example. Correlational version: you find exposure and intelligence are negatively related. Your reaction: "maybe smart people are too busy to travel", or "maybe cultural exposure was measured badly". You've explained it away. So the positive result you were hoping for wouldn't have been evidence either.

Experimental version: you randomly assigned people to more cultural exposure, and they came out less intelligent. Much harder to wriggle out of. That's the point — you want a question where the data can genuinely shoot your theory down.

Where it breaks. This is a check on the question, and it only works if you do it before you see results. Afterwards you'll know which outcome you got, and you can't un-know it.

3

The three practical filters

Plain English. Even a question that would settle your theory is useless if you can't actually run it.

Precisely.

FilterThe question to askFailure
FeasibilityDoes the data exist, and would anyone let me do this?Nobody will let you randomly assign the minimum wage across states.
ScaleDo I have the money, the time, the people?The answer needs every home transaction: hundreds of thousands of dollars and a year of several people's time.
DesignDoes a setting exist where my approach applies?Your plan needs students randomly assigned to a global studies course. Did that ever happen anywhere?

Anchor image. A recipe you can't source the ingredients for isn't a dinner plan, however good it would have tasted.

Where it breaks. Failing a filter doesn't kill your theory — it kills that question. You go looking for a different observable what that reaches the same why.

4

Keep it small

Plain English. One study answers one small, well-defined question. It does not explain the world.

Precisely. Big questions — why are some countries rich, what's the best form of government — can't be resolved by a single study. The common failure is the "determinants of X" paper: thirty predictors of school dropout, none of them identified, and no way to act on any of it.

Anchor image. Thirty half-answers stacked together aren't one whole answer. They're a pile.

Worked example. Instead of "what are the determinants of school dropout?", carve off a chunk: does a free pre-school lunch help you stay in school? Narrow, well defined, and you'll know when you've answered it — and it still feeds the big theory you actually care about.

Where it breaks. Small isn't the same as trivial. The chunk still has to bear on the theory, or you've just made a well-identified answer to something nobody asked.

Say it out loud

Here's the thing about research that took me a while to get. What you actually care about is a why — some claim about how the world works. Say: being exposed to lots of different cultures makes people smarter.

But data can't see why. Data only sees what. So you have to pick something observable that, if you saw it, would tell you about the why. And that step is where nearly everything goes wrong.

Watch. The obvious move is to check whether intelligence and cultural exposure go together in some dataset. They do. Feels like a win. Except — smart people might just be the ones who go seek out different cultures. Both stories predict exactly the same data, so seeing it doesn't move you at all.

There's a test for this that I really like. Before you run anything, imagine getting the opposite result and ask yourself what you'd say. If your honest answer is "well, that measure was probably bad" — then finding what you expected shouldn't have convinced you either. You weren't really testing anything.

Now compare: randomly assign some people to more cultural exposure, follow up later, and they come out less intelligent. That one you can't talk your way out of. Same variables, same theory — but now the question can actually kill the theory, and that's what makes it a real question.

Recall drill

QWhat's the difference between a theory and a research question?

A theory is a claim about why the world works some way. A research question names an observable what that, if you measured it, would inform the theory.

QWhy can't data answer a "why" question directly?

Data reports what was observed. Multiple different whys can produce the same what, so the observation alone doesn't pick between them.

QTheory: cultural exposure makes people smarter. Why does finding a positive correlation fail to support it?

The correlation is equally consistent with the reverse — more intelligent people choosing more cultural exposure. The observation can't distinguish the two.

QWhat's the pre-analysis test for whether a research question is any good?

Imagine each result you could get, including contradictory ones. If a contradictory result would make you make excuses rather than change your mind, the question isn't testing your theory — and the confirming result wouldn't be evidence either.

QYou want to know the effect of the minimum wage and plan to randomly assign it across states. Which filter kills this?

Feasibility. Nobody will let you do it. The theory survives; you need a different question that reaches the same why.

QName the three practical filters on a research question.

Feasibility (does the data exist, would anyone permit it), scale (money, time, people), and design (does a real-world setting exist where your approach applies).

QWhat's wrong with a paper on "the determinants of school dropout" listing 30 determinants?

None of them are answered well, so the results are a mush you can't act on. One narrow question answered properly is worth more.

QHow should you handle a question that's too big?

Carve off a small, well-defined chunk that still bears on the theory — "does a free pre-school lunch help you stay in school?" rather than "what causes dropout?"

Glossary

Theory
a claim about why the world works a certain way; contains a because, not just a description.
Research question
an answerable question about something observable, chosen so that answering it informs a theory.
Feasibility
whether the data can be obtained and the study permitted at all.
Scale
whether you have the money, time, and people the question would require.
Setting
a real-world situation in which your intended design can actually be applied.
