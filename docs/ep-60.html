Ep 60Testing Instrumental Variables Assumptions

Relevance you can genuinely check — via the first stage F statistic, though the "F above 10" rule is wrong and the honest reading is Stock and Yogo's tables — while validity is inherently theoretical, and the two tests that claim to check it both amount to treating a failure to reject a null as proof, which Nick doesn't buy.

The 30-second version

  • Conditions: relevance, validity, and also monotonicity — the instrument must push everyone's treatment probability in the same direction.
  • Weak instruments are disastrous: little variation in predicted treatment means wildly noisy estimates.
  • Worse, instrumental variables is biased in finite samples, and weak instruments let that bias dominate.
  • First stage F statistic tests joint significance of the instruments. The bigger, the smaller the IV bias relative to OLS bias — but it never vanishes until F is enormous.
  • F > 10 is not how it works. Use the Stock and Yogo tables.
  • Abandoning studies on a failed F test creates publication bias. Better: proceed and use Anderson–Rubin confidence intervals.
  • Validity is theoretical. The Durbin–Wu–Hausman and over-identification tests both rest on failing to reject a null.

Why this exists

For instrumental variables to work you need relevance — the instrument genuinely predicts the treatment, because if it doesn't, isolating the random part of treatment leaves nothing to study — and validity — no open back door between instrument and outcome, and no front door from instrument to outcome except through the treatment you care about. Your instrument can't be causing two different treatments; that gets confusing.

There are other assumptions too, such as monotonicity: the instrument must affect the probability of being treated in the same direction for everybody. That one isn't covered much here; the chapter has more.

So: what can you actually check in the data?

The ideas

1

Relevance, and why weak instruments are so bad

Plain English. If your instrument barely moves treatment, your estimate is noise.

Precisely. Relevance is the easy one — just look at how closely related the instrument and treatment are. Strongly related, good. Weakly related, and you have a weak instrument problem, which can be very bad because it's very noisy.

Why. Recall the components of an OLS standard error: the more variation there is in the predictor, the more precisely you can estimate. If x moves around a lot, it's easy to see whether y moves with it; if x barely moves, it's hard to tell. And in instrumental variables you're using only the part of x predicted by z. So if z predicts only a little variation in x, there's very little variation in your predicted values — very little movement down in the first stage — which makes it very hard to see what's happening up in the second.

The result is extremely noisy estimates — estimates that can change by thousands of percentage points from sample to sample, which makes the whole approach worthless.

Anchor image. Steering by a compass needle that barely moves. Every tiny wobble sends you somewhere completely different.

2

Small sample bias

Plain English. In any finite sample, "unrelated to the error term" isn't exactly true — and that introduces bias.

Precisely. Instrumental variables has small sample bias: as an estimator it is genuinely biased in a finite sample. The reason is the assumption that your instrument is unrelated to the second-stage error term.

In the population that might literally be true — you may be able to say theoretically, with real confidence, that the true model has no back door from your instrument to the outcome. But in a small sample, by random chance, that relationship won't be exactly zero. And because it isn't exactly zero, a little bias creeps into the estimation.

Where it breaks. Not a huge problem unless you have a really small sample or a really weak instrument. But the weaker the instrument, the more that bias takes up of what's going on in your estimate, and the less of it is the actual effect.

3

The first stage F statistic

Plain English. Test whether your instruments jointly predict treatment. Bigger is better.

Precisely. The most common check. Look at the first stage and perform an F test of the joint significance of your instruments — whether the coefficient on your instrument is zero, or, with multiple instruments, whether all their coefficients are zero simultaneously. The bigger the F statistic, the better a job your instruments do of predicting treatment.

Where the rule of thumb goes wrong. You may have heard that an F statistic of 10 or above means you're fine. That's not really how it works. What's actually true is that the bigger your F statistic, the smaller the instrumental variables bias is relative to the bias you'd get from plain ordinary least squares if you didn't correct for endogeneity at all.

So a bigger F means instrumental variables looks better relative to that comparison — but the bias is still there. It doesn't simply go away, at least not until your F statistic is positively enormous, something like 105.

What to use instead. The Stock and Yogo tables, which show how big your F statistic needs to be to keep the small sample bias small enough relative to the OLS bias. At least a slightly more detailed version of the number you're looking for.

4

The problem with the F statistic approach itself

Plain English. Abandoning studies when the F test fails quietly selects for flukes.

Precisely. What happens when you fail? You might conclude your instrument is too weak, drop the study, and go away. That introduces publication bias.

Worked example. Suppose you have an instrument that's genuinely somewhat weak. Not the strongest — but you're fairly confident it's valid. Because it's weak, its measured strength varies a lot from sample to sample.

Now two different researchers both use this instrument, and both apply an F statistic cutoff. One happens to draw a sample where the instrument looks stronger; the other draws one where it looks weaker. The first proceeds with their study; the second doesn't.

But the first one just happened to get a strong first stage by random fluke. They have the worse data, and they're the ones going ahead. That's bad.

The better approach. Go ahead with the study anyway, but account for the fact that you might have a weak instrument. Use Anderson–Rubin confidence intervals — a different way of calculating the confidence interval for your instrumental variables estimate that allows for the instrument being weak.

So rather than our instrument's too weak, call it all off, you say our instrument's somewhat weak, so we'll change the confidence intervals to account for it — making them wider, and in fact not even symmetric: you might get a larger interval on one side of your estimate than the other. The chapter's "how the pros do it" section has the detail and the code.

5

Validity: inherently theoretical

Plain English. There's no real empirical test, and the two that exist rest on accepting a null.

Precisely. Validity is trickier because it's inherently a theoretical assumption — about the relationship between your instrument and something you can't necessarily observe: whether your model is correct, whether you're omitting unobservables related to your instrument. In general, any empirical test for validity kind of misses the point. You want to be able to say theoretically, with a lot of confidence, that your instrument is valid.

There are two things you can check, both of which Nick is grumpy about.

6

Test 1: Durbin–Wu–Hausman

Plain English. Run it with and without the instrument and see whether the answers differ.

Precisely. Estimate your results twice — once with instrumental variables as normal, once with ordinary least squares, forgetting the instrumental variables part entirely — and check how different the two estimates are.

The idea: instrumental variables should be correcting for endogeneity in your OLS model. Your OLS model is biased because of some relationship between treatment and error term, and that's what instrumental variables fixes. So if instrumental variables is doing anything, the coefficients should differ.

  • Different → instrumental variables is correcting for something, so use it, despite its own problems with weak instruments and validity.
  • Indistinguishable → you might as well use ordinary least squares, which has better statistical properties and is more precise.

Nick's objection. Think about what this actually tells you. If you don't get a big difference, you're concluding you don't have an endogeneity problem. Do you really believe that failing to reject a null hypothesis is proof that you don't have an endogeneity problem? He rarely would, and would be very sceptical of anyone saying I wasn't sure about my instrument, but I ran this test and it said I was fine.

An important clarification. This is not testing the validity of your instrument. It's testing for the presence of endogeneity in your OLS that would require instrumental variables to correct — so it's about the exogeneity of your treatment, which isn't quite the same thing. Even so, he wouldn't believe your treatment is exogenous just because you failed to find a difference.

7

Test 2: the over-identification test

Plain English. With two instruments, trust one and use it to audit the other.

Precisely. This one does test the validity of an instrument — but it needs more than one. Assume at least one of your instruments is genuinely valid, and use it as ground truth to check the other.

Compare what happens using only the instrument you're confident about, against what happens using both. If the results differ, the second instrument may be introducing invalidity. If they're the same, you can use multiple instruments and improve your precision.

Nick's first objection. Same problem. If you fail to reject the statistical null, does that really mean the new instrument is valid? He isn't going to use a statistical test to determine whether a validity condition holds. That just doesn't feel right — though he notes it's a personal view.

Nick's second objection — the local average treatment effect. Remember instrumental variables tells you about the effect among people whose treatment is driven by the instrument, which differs depending on which instrument you use, since some people respond more to one than another.

So if two instruments give different results, that could mean one of them is invalid — or it could just mean they're isolating different parts of the variation and different kinds of responses. You might reject for that reason, and end up discarding an instrument that is in fact perfectly valid but simply captures a different part of the treatment effect.

Say it out loud

Two assumptions to worry about, and one of them you can actually check.

Relevance is easy — does your instrument really predict treatment? And if it barely does, you have a weak instrument, which is genuinely catastrophic. Remember: in IV you only use the part of treatment your instrument explains. If that's a sliver, you're trying to detect an effect using almost no variation, and your estimate can swing by thousands of percent from sample to sample.

It's worse than that, actually, because IV is biased in finite samples. In the population, your instrument might be perfectly uncorrelated with the error term — but in your sample, by chance, it won't be exactly zero. And the weaker your instrument, the more that little bias dominates everything.

You test it with the first stage F statistic. And you've probably heard "F above 10 and you're fine." That's not how it works. What's true is that a bigger F means IV's bias is smaller relative to the OLS bias you were trying to escape. It doesn't disappear until F is something like 105. Use the Stock and Yogo tables instead of a folk rule.

But here's a subtle problem with the whole approach. If your F fails and you abandon the study — that's publication bias. Two researchers with the same slightly-weak instrument: one gets a lucky sample where it looks strong and publishes, the other doesn't. The person publishing is the one who got the fluke. So instead, run the study and use Anderson–Rubin confidence intervals, which widen — and can even be asymmetric — to account for weakness.

Validity, though? That's a theoretical question. There are two tests that claim to help, and I'm suspicious of both — because they both come down to: I failed to reject a null, therefore I'm fine. Do you really believe failing to find a problem proves there isn't one? I generally don't.

Recall drill

QName the three assumptions mentioned, including the one not covered in detail.

Relevance, validity, and monotonicity — the instrument must affect everyone's probability of treatment in the same direction.

QHow do you check relevance?

Look at how closely related the instrument and treatment are.

QWhy are weak instruments so damaging?

You use only the part of treatment predicted by the instrument, so weak prediction leaves very little variation to work with, giving estimates that can vary by thousands of percentage points across samples.

QWhat is small sample bias in instrumental variables?

Instrumental variables is a biased estimator in finite samples, because the instrument's relationship with the error term won't be exactly zero in a given sample even if it is in the population.

QWhen does small sample bias become a serious problem?

With a really small sample or a really weak instrument — the weaker the instrument, the more of your estimate is bias rather than effect.

QWhat does the first stage F statistic test?

The joint significance of your instruments in the first stage — whether all their coefficients are zero.

QWhy is "F above 10" the wrong way to think about it?

Because a bigger F means smaller instrumental variables bias relative to the ordinary least squares bias — the bias doesn't disappear until F is enormous, on the order of 105.

QWhat should you consult instead?

The Stock and Yogo tables, which show how large F must be to keep small sample bias small relative to the OLS bias.

QHow does using an F cutoff create publication bias?

With a genuinely weak instrument, whichever researcher happens to draw a sample where it looks strong proceeds, while others don't — so the study that gets published is the fluke.

QWhat's the recommended alternative?

Proceed with the study but use Anderson–Rubin confidence intervals, which account for a possibly weak instrument.

QWhat's unusual about those intervals?

They're wider, and not necessarily symmetric — the interval may be larger on one side of the estimate than the other.

QWhy is validity hard to test empirically?

It's inherently theoretical — an assumption about the relationship between your instrument and things you can't necessarily observe. Empirical tests for it rather miss the point.

QDescribe the Durbin–Wu–Hausman test.

Estimate twice — once with instrumental variables and once with plain ordinary least squares — and compare. Differing coefficients suggest instrumental variables is correcting something; similar ones suggest you may as well use OLS.

QWhat is Nick's objection to it?

It amounts to treating a failure to reject a null hypothesis as proof that you don't have an endogeneity problem.

QWhat is that test actually testing?

The presence of endogeneity in your OLS — the exogeneity of your treatment — not the validity of your instrument.

QDescribe the over-identification test.

With more than one instrument, assume one is valid and use it as ground truth: compare results using only that instrument against results using both. Differences suggest the second may be invalid.

QGive Nick's two objections to it.

It again treats failure to reject a null as evidence of validity; and differing results may simply reflect that the two instruments isolate different local average treatment effects, so you might discard a perfectly valid instrument.

Glossary

Monotonicity
the requirement that the instrument moves everyone's treatment probability in the same direction.
Weak instrument problem
an instrument that predicts treatment poorly, yielding extremely noisy and bias-dominated estimates.
Small sample bias
the finite-sample bias of instrumental variables, arising because the instrument's correlation with the error term isn't exactly zero in any given sample.
First stage F statistic
a test of the joint significance of the instruments in predicting treatment.
Stock and Yogo tables
reference tables giving the F statistic needed to keep small sample bias acceptably small.
Anderson–Rubin confidence intervals
confidence intervals valid under weak instruments, wider and possibly asymmetric.
Durbin–Wu–Hausman test
a comparison of instrumental variables and OLS estimates, testing for endogeneity in the treatment.
Over-identification test
with multiple instruments, using one assumed-valid instrument to assess another.
