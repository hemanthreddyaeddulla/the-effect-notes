Ep 31Categorical Predictors

▶ Watch the video 12:30
On this pageThe 30-second versionWhy this existsThe ideasTrapsSay it out loudRecall drillConnectionsGlossary

A binary predictor in a regression is just a comparison of means, and a categorical variable is a set of binary indicators with one left out as the reference group — which means every coefficient, and every significance test on it, is relative to whichever category you happened to omit.

The 30-second version

  • A binary predictor compares means. Regress income on treatment, get 5,000: mean income is 5,000 higher among the treated. Same as a two-sample t-test.
  • Why bother with regression? Because you can add control variables.
  • It follows from the mantra: with a binary variable the only sensible one-unit change is 0 → 1.
  • The intercept becomes the mean of the group coded zero.
  • A categorical variable becomes one binary indicator per category — and you must leave one out.
  • The omitted category is the reference group. Every other coefficient is a difference from it.
  • Change the reference group and every coefficient and every significance test changes.
  • To test the variable as a whole, use a joint F test.

Why this exists

You've been working on the right-hand side of a regression — transformations, non-linear terms. Now something you'll see constantly: predictors that are categories.

Lots of variables are like this. Did you get the treatment or not — you can't half get it. Which continent are you on — seven mutually exclusive options, and you can't be on two at once (barring something odd at a border). So how do you put those on the right-hand side, whether as your treatment or as a control?

The ideas

1

A binary predictor compares means

Plain English. The coefficient is the gap between the group averages.

Precisely. Including a binary predictor gives you a comparison of means — exactly the same comparison as a two-sample t-test.

Anchor image. A step, not a slope. There's only one place to put your foot down.

Worked example. Regress income on whether you got the treatment, and the coefficient comes back 5,000. That says the mean income is 5,000 higher for the group that got the treatment than for the group that didn't.

Why use regression instead of a t-test? Because you can add control variables. Then you can say: controlling for everything I need to identify my effect, the effect of the treatment on the outcome is [coefficient] — the mean of the outcome rises by that much going from 0 to 1. You also get a standard error and a significance test on the coefficient for free, letting you check whether the average outcome differs statistically between the yes group and the no group.

Why it works. Return to the mantra: a one-unit increase in X is associated with a coefficient-sized change in Y. Now make X binary. There's only one one-unit change that makes any sense at all — going from 0 (not in the group) to 1 (in the group). So the coefficient gives the jump in the outcome you'd see, on average, going from a no to a yes.

2

The intercept becomes the reference mean

Plain English. Set the binary variable to zero and only the intercept is left — so the intercept is the mean of that group.

Worked example. Regress sales on whether it's currently winter. Average sales are 10 in winter and 15 in not-winter.

TermCoefficientWhy
Intercept15Set winter = 0 and only the intercept remains, so it must be the not-winter average
Winter−5Winter sales are 5 lower than not-winter

Your model should give the best prediction it can — the winter average for winter, the not-winter average for not-winter. That's exactly what these two numbers deliver.

Where it breaks — the key idea. The coefficient on winter is relative to something: winter versus not-winter. A binary coefficient is always about being relative to something else. That's the bridge to categorical variables with more than two categories.

3

Categorical variables: one indicator per category, minus one

Plain English. Split the categories into yes/no variables, then throw one away.

Precisely. Take each category and turn it into its own binary indicator. For continents: are you in Asia, yes or no? In Europe, yes or no? In South America, yes or no? And so on — seven categories, seven binary indicators.

Then, when you run the regression, you have to leave one out. The reason is statistical (the book has the full explanation), but you do have to. Whichever you leave out becomes your reference group.

A note on vocabulary. You may have heard binary indicators called dummies. Nick avoids the term — if you're talking to someone who doesn't know econometrics and you say "I put the female dummy in the model," that may not go over well. Binary indicator is the better term.

Where it breaks. People in the omitted group are still in your data. They just don't get their own coefficient — they become the intercept, and everything else is measured relative to them.

Leave out Asia, and the coefficient on Europe is not "the effect of being in Europe." It's the difference in means between Europe and Asia. And South America's coefficient is South America versus Asia. And Africa's is Africa versus Asia. Everything is relative to the same reference group.

Because of this, the individual coefficients don't mean much by themselves. They only mean something relative to the reference group you selected.

4

The reference group is arbitrary — and so are your significance tests

Plain English. Pick a different omitted category and every number in the table changes.

Worked example. Income regressed on country, with three countries in the data:

CountryAverage income
France30
Gambia30.5
New Zealand33

Reference group: France.

TermCoefficientWhy
Intercept30France's average — the prediction when both other indicators are 0
Gambia0.530.5 − 30
New Zealand333 − 30

Now switch the reference group to Gambia. France re-enters the model with its own term:

TermCoefficientWhy
France−0.530 − 30.5
New Zealand2.533 − 30.5

Where it breaks. All the coefficients — and therefore all the statistical significance tests — are completely arbitrary, determined by whichever reference group you happened to choose.

So if you want to test something, make sure it's the test you actually want. Asking whether Gambia's coefficient is significant asks whether the difference from France is significant. If that isn't your question, pick a different reference group. And you can't look at Gambia's effect on its own at all — it's always relative to something else.

5

Testing the variable as a whole: the joint F test

Plain English. To ask "does country matter at all?", test all the indicators at once.

Precisely. If you want to know whether country overall is a decent predictor of income, run a joint F test on the indicators — a test of the joint significance of all the coefficients together. The null hypothesis is that all the country coefficients are zero, meaning every country is exactly the same as the reference group. An F test tells you whether you can reject that.

Say it out loud

Here's the thing that makes binary predictors click: a binary variable in a regression is just comparing means. That's it. Regress income on whether someone got the treatment, get a coefficient of 5,000 — that means the average income is 5,000 higher among the treated. Same answer a two-sample t-test would give you. The reason you'd use regression instead is that you can add control variables.

And it follows straight from the mantra. A one-unit change in X — but X is binary, so the only one-unit change that even exists is going from no to yes. So the coefficient is the jump from no to yes.

Quick example. Sales are 10 in winter, 15 the rest of the year. Regress sales on "is it winter", and you get a coefficient of −5 and an intercept of 15. Why 15? Because if you set winter to zero, all that's left is the intercept — so the intercept has to be the not-winter average. The coefficient is winter relative to not-winter.

Now scale that up. Got a categorical variable — continents, countries, whatever? Make each category its own yes/no indicator, and then leave one out. The one you leave out is your reference group, and every other coefficient is a difference from it. Leave out Asia and the Europe coefficient isn't "the effect of Europe" — it's Europe minus Asia.

And here's the part I want you to really hold onto. France 30, Gambia 30.5, New Zealand 33. Use France as reference: intercept 30, Gambia 0.5, New Zealand 3. Now switch to Gambia as reference: France becomes −0.5, New Zealand becomes 2.5. Every number changed. And so did every significance test.

Which means those tests are arbitrary — they're testing against whatever category you happened to drop. If you want to ask whether country matters at all, don't look at individual stars. Run a joint F test with the null that all the country coefficients are zero.

Recall drill

QWhat does a binary predictor in a regression actually do?

It compares means — the coefficient is the difference in the outcome's mean between the yes group and the no group.

QWhich classical test is that equivalent to?

A two-sample t-test.

QSo why use a regression instead?

Because you can add control variables and interpret the coefficient as the effect adjusting for them — plus you get a standard error and significance test.

QHow does the mantra explain the binary case?

A one-unit change in X is associated with a coefficient-sized change in Y, and for a binary variable the only sensible one-unit change is going from 0 to 1.

QSales are 10 in winter and 15 otherwise. Give the intercept and the winter coefficient.

Intercept 15 (the not-winter average) and −5 on winter.

QWhy must the intercept equal the not-winter average?

Setting winter to zero leaves only the intercept, so it has to deliver the best prediction for not-winter, which is that group's mean.

QHow do you include a categorical variable with seven categories?

Create a binary indicator for each category, then include all but one in the regression.

QWhat is the omitted category called, and what happens to the observations in it?

The reference group. Those observations remain in the data — they become the intercept.

QWhy does Nick prefer "binary indicator" to "dummy"?

Because saying something like "I put the female dummy in the model" to someone who doesn't know econometrics may not go over well.

QWith Asia omitted, what does the coefficient on Europe mean?

The difference in means between Europe and Asia — not the effect of being in Europe.

QFrance 30, Gambia 30.5, New Zealand 33, with France as reference. Give all three terms.

Intercept 30, Gambia 0.5, New Zealand 3.

QNow switch the reference group to Gambia. What do France and New Zealand become?

France −0.5 and New Zealand 2.5.

QWhat's the implication of that for significance tests?

They're arbitrary — they test against whichever reference group you picked. A coefficient's significance answers "is this different from the reference?", which may not be your question.

QHow do you test whether a categorical variable matters overall?

A joint F test on all its indicators, with the null that every one of those coefficients is zero — i.e. every category is identical to the reference group.

Glossary

Binary predictor
a 0/1 variable; its coefficient is a difference in means.
Binary indicator
a 0/1 variable marking membership of one category; preferred term over "dummy".
Reference group
the omitted category, against which all other coefficients are measured.
Joint F test
a test of the null that a set of coefficients are all zero simultaneously.
