Ep 32Interaction Terms

▶ Watch the video 14:10
On this pageThe 30-second versionWhy this existsThe ideasTrapsSay it out loudRecall drillConnectionsGlossary

An interaction term lets the effect of X differ across values of another variable Z — so the effect of X is no longer β₁ but β₁ + β₃Z, meaning β₁ is the effect for the Z = 0 group, β₁ + β₃ is the effect for the Z = 1 group, and β₃ is the difference between those two effects.

The 30-second version

  • Polynomials let the effect of X vary across values of X. Interactions let it vary across values of Z.
  • Most common use: treatment effects differing across groups — a conditional average treatment effect.
  • Adding Z as a control does not do this. Without the interaction, changing Z shifts the line up and down; the slope on X never changes.
  • Always include Z on its own alongside X×Z, or the interaction coefficient absorbs Z's effect from the error term.
  • The effect of X is β₁ + β₃Z. You must plug in a value of Z.
  • β₁ = effect when Z = 0. β₁ + β₃ = effect when Z = 1. β₃ = the difference between them.
  • Don't go fishing. Interactions are noisy — a binary interaction estimates each effect on roughly half your sample.

Why this exists

Fair warning: interaction terms are fantastic and they show up everywhere in causal inference — a lot of research designs are built around them — and they are also genuinely hard to get your head around. This is a page to bookmark and come back to when you meet one in the wild. Take it slowly, one step at a time, and you'll get the right interpretation.

The idea itself is a small step from what you've already done. Polynomials and logarithms fit a curvy line, which is really saying the effect of X on Y should be different at different values of X. But what if the effect of X changes as the value of a different variable changes? That can obviously happen, and interaction terms are how you study it.

The commonest version: seeing how a treatment effect differs across groups. One effect for women, another for men; one for tall people, another for short. That's a conditional average treatment effect — you're letting the effect of X on Y differ across values of another variable.

The ideas

1

The model, and what does not work

Plain English. You need the product term. A plain control won't do it.

Precisely. The model with an interaction:

y  =  β₀  +  β₁·x  +  β₂·z  +  β₃·(x·z)  +  ε

You're interested in the effect of x, and you're allowing it to vary across values of z.

The common misconception: that adding z as a control by itself lets the effect of x differ across values of z. It doesn't. Drop the interaction and keep only x and z: as z changes, the line moves up and down, but the slope on x stays the same. For the effect of x to differ between groups, the slope has to differ between groups — and without the interaction term that simply isn't allowed to happen.

Anchor image. A control variable is an elevator: it moves the whole floor up or down. An interaction is a hinge: it changes the tilt.

2

Always keep Z in the model

Plain English. Never include X×Z without also including Z on its own.

Precisely. You pretty much always want z in the model, even when you have no interest in it. The reason is omitted variable bias — back doors. Leave z out and it goes into the error term, because everything determining y that isn't in the model goes there. And the interaction term x×z is definitely related to z. So the coefficient on the interaction would be mixed up, combining the effect of the interaction and the effect of the z now sitting in the error term.

3

Getting the effect of X: take the derivative

Plain English. X appears twice, so β₁ isn't the answer. Differentiate.

Precisely. Because x shows up in two places, the change in y as x changes is reflected in two parts of the model. Write out the equation and take the derivative — the first thing to do every time, especially when you're starting out:

dy/dx  =  β₁  +  β₃·z

β₁ is the coefficient on x; β₃ is the coefficient on the interaction. This whole expression is the effect of x, and it's conditional on z — so you have to plug in a value of z.

Everything below works if z is continuous too; it just takes an extra mental step. Assume z is binary, which is both the most common case and much easier to read.

4

Reading the three coefficients

QuantityValueMeaning
Effect of x when z = 0β₁Plug z = 0 and the β₃·z term drops out
Effect of x when z = 1β₁ + β₃Plug z = 1
β₃the differenceHow much the effect of x changes going from z = 0 to z = 1

Why β₃ means that. Look at the derivative again — β₁ + β₃·z — and compare it to a regression on a binary indicator, y = β₀ + β₁·(binary). There, the coefficient on the binary variable gave you the difference in means between the two groups. Same idea here, except the left-hand side is now the derivative of y with respect to x rather than y itself.

So β₃ is still the difference across groups — it's just the difference in the effect of x on y rather than in the mean of y. You can also see it directly: the z = 1 group's effect is β₁ + β₃, the z = 0 group's is β₁, and the difference is β₃.

Which means: the coefficient on the interaction term gives you a test of whether the effect differs between the two groups.

5

Worked example: restaurant inspections on weekends

Worked example. Regress health inspection scores on the number of locations a chain has, whether it's currently the weekend, the interaction of those two, and a control for year of inspection:

TermCoefficient
Number of locations−0.019
Weekend1.759
Number of locations × weekend−0.010

The effect of number of locations is the derivative:

dScore/dLocations  =  −0.019  +  (−0.010 · weekend)
WhenEffect of one more location
Not the weekend (weekend = 0)−0.019
The weekend (weekend = 1)−0.019 + (−0.010) = −0.029
Difference between them−0.010 — the interaction coefficient

So the effect is 0.010 different on the weekend than during the week. And note it isn't a particularly strong effect — there's no statistical significance here, so you don't have enough evidence to reject that the effects are the same on the weekend and during the week.

6

The warning: don't go fishing

Plain English. Interactions are tempting, addictive, and noisy. Have a reason before adding one.

Precisely. You could always wonder whether a treatment effect differs by gender, or where people live, or their height, or hair colour, or eye colour. Any of those could be true — why not? But try all of them and you will get false positives.

Why they're especially prone to it: interaction terms tend to be relatively noisy. Think about what you're doing with a binary interaction — you're essentially estimating each effect using half the sample size you had. Halve the sample and things get noisy fast. Noisy estimates, made in bulk, will produce statistical significance even when there's nothing there.

Anchor image. Enough lottery tickets and somebody wins. That doesn't make the winner good at lotteries.

Where it breaks. So before you add an interaction, make sure you have a reason for it. Don't add it just to try it out and see what's there. You don't want to run your model, find little effect for the treatment, and then start wondering whether maybe the effect only exists for blonde women who live in Arkansas. You could try it — and if you try enough combinations like that, you'll find something.

Say it out loud

Interaction terms are everywhere in causal inference, and they're the thing people find hardest to interpret. So go slowly — it's fine to work through it step by step every time.

Here's the idea. A polynomial lets the effect of X change at different values of X. An interaction lets the effect of X change at different values of something else. Usually that means: does my treatment work differently for different groups? Different effect for men and women, say.

First thing people get wrong: they think adding Z as a control does this. It doesn't. Adding Z moves the line up and down — it doesn't change the slope. And the effect of X is the slope. You need the actual product term, X times Z.

Second thing: always keep Z in the model on its own too. If you drop it, it falls into the error term, and X-times-Z is obviously related to Z, so your interaction coefficient ends up absorbing Z's effect.

Now, interpretation. X shows up in two places, so β₁ is not the effect of X. Take the derivative and you get β₁ + β₃Z. That whole thing is the effect — and it depends on Z, so you have to pick a value.

Say Z is binary. Plug in zero: the effect is β₁. Plug in one: the effect is β₁ + β₃. And β₃? β₃ is the difference between those two effects — how much the effect of X changes when you move from one group to the other. Which is why the interaction coefficient is a test of whether the effect differs by group.

Real example. Inspection scores on chain size: −0.019 on number of locations, −0.010 on the interaction with weekend. So during the week, one more location is worth −0.019. On the weekend, −0.029. Difference: −0.010. Though that one isn't significant, so I can't claim the weekend matters.

Last thing — a warning. Interactions are addictive. You can always wonder whether the effect is different for tall people, or blondes, or people in Arkansas. Try enough and you'll find "something." And they're noisy: a binary interaction is basically estimating each effect on half your sample. Have a reason before you add one.

Recall drill

QWhat does an interaction term let you do that a polynomial doesn't?

Let the effect of X vary across values of a different variable Z, rather than across values of X itself.

QWhat's the most common application in causal inference?

Estimating how a treatment effect differs across groups — a conditional average treatment effect.

QDoes adding Z as a control let the effect of X differ across values of Z?

No. It moves the line up and down but leaves the slope on X unchanged. You need the interaction term.

QWhy must you include Z on its own when including X×Z?

Otherwise Z goes into the error term, and since X×Z is related to Z, the interaction coefficient combines the interaction effect with Z's effect — omitted variable bias.

QWrite the derivative of Y with respect to X for the model y = β₀ + β₁x + β₂z + β₃(x·z).

dy/dx = β₁ + β₃z.

QWhat is β₁ in that model?

The effect of x on y among the group where z = 0 — plugging in z = 0 makes the β₃z term drop out.

QWhat is the effect of x when z = 1?

β₁ + β₃.

QWhat does β₃ mean?

The difference in the effect of x on y between the z = 1 group and the z = 0 group — how much the effect changes going from one group to the other.

QHow does β₃ relate to the binary-indicator interpretation from Ep 31?

It's the same "difference between groups" idea, except the left-hand side is the derivative of y with respect to x rather than y itself — so it's a difference in effects, not a difference in means.

QLocations coefficient −0.019, interaction with weekend −0.010. Give the effect on weekdays and on weekends.

Weekdays −0.019; weekends −0.019 + (−0.010) = −0.029.

QThat interaction wasn't statistically significant. What can you conclude?

Not that the effects are the same — only that you lack sufficient evidence to reject that they're the same on weekends and weekdays.

QWhy are interaction terms especially noisy?

With a binary interaction you're effectively estimating each effect using half the sample size, and halving the sample makes estimates noisy quickly.

QWhat's the practical rule before adding an interaction?

Have a reason for it. Don't add interactions to try them out and see what turns up — enough combinations will always produce something significant.

Glossary

Interaction term
a predictor formed by multiplying two variables, allowing one's effect to vary with the other.
Main effect
the coefficient on a variable entered on its own, e.g. β₁ or β₂.
Fishing
trying many interactions without prior reason until one comes out significant.
