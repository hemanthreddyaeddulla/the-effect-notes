Ep 15Simplifying Causal Diagrams

▶ Watch the video 14:00
On this pageThe 30-second versionWhy this existsThe ideasTrapsSay it out loudRecall drillConnectionsGlossary

Four moves turn an unusable diagram into a working one — drop the unimportant, bucket the redundant, remove mediators you're not studying, and cut the irrelevant — and the skill being trained is figuring out what you're allowed to ignore, because refusing to ignore anything means never learning anything.

The 30-second version

  • The world is complex and your diagram should reflect it — but a diagram with everything on it is a diagram you can't use.
  • Unimportance: drop trivial causes. Statistics models the world, it doesn't reproduce it, and every assumption you make is at least a little false.
  • Redundancy: variables whose arrows go to and from the same places play the same role. Bucket them — race, gender, SES → demographics.
  • Mediators: the variable explaining why X causes Y. If you're not studying the mechanism, remove it and draw X → Y directly.
  • Irrelevance: a variable that isn't on any pathway between treatment and outcome can go, even though it's genuinely part of the DGP.
  • Don't oversimplify. Placebo tests catch you: if the simplified diagram implies a correlation is zero and you know it isn't, you cut too much.

Why this exists

You ended the last episode with a mess, and there's an obvious objection to cleaning it up: the world is complex, so shouldn't the diagram be? Yes, genuinely — and you have to be careful not to simplify into something that no longer represents reality.

But there's a counter-argument. Sometimes the diagram is a mess because you're being hard on yourself, admitting every conceivable form of complexity. The simplifications below are the kind that help you see the setting and design a study — they clarify rather than cheat. Some of them don't reduce the complexity at all; they just put it in a labelled bucket so you can see what the actual problem is.

The ideas

1

1 · Unimportance

Plain English. Trivial causes come off.

Precisely. There are infinitely many things related in some obscure way to your treatment or outcome. If something is unimportant — relevant occasionally, a tiny bit — you can drop it without much worry, even though it technically causes something on your diagram.

Anchor image. A map of a city that marks every drain cover is not a better map.

Where it breaks. The justification matters: statistics is about modelling the world, not perfectly representing it. Almost every assumption you make is at least slightly false already. There will always be some gap between your model and a reality too complex to capture — so a little less is often a little more, if losing an unimportant distraction lets you see the important structure.

2

2 · Redundancy

Plain English. If several variables do the same job on your diagram, treat them as one.

Precisely. If a set of variables all have their arrows going into and out of the same places, then from the point of view of your research question they occupy the same role. Bucket them into a single node.

Anchor image. Four people carrying one end of the same sofa. For the purpose of moving the sofa, they're one force.

Worked example. In the online-courses diagram: race, gender, and socioeconomic status all cause taking an online course, all cause dropping out, and don't do much else. Three variables collapse into one:

Where it breaks. Remember the bucket is a bucket. When you actually go to use the diagram, demographics is still three separate things and you'll need to handle it as such. This is a presentational simplification for thinking about identification.

Note this doesn't cheat — it doesn't pretend the world is simple. It puts the problem variables in one place so you can see what the problem is.

3

3 · Remove mediators you aren't studying

Plain English. A mediator is the reason X causes Y. If you're not studying the reason, cut it out and connect X to Y directly.

Precisely. A mediator sits between two variables and explains the mechanism. Whether it stays depends entirely on your research question, not on the world.

Anchor image. A recipe that says "bake at 180°C" rather than describing protein denaturation. Both are true; only one is what you're cooking from.

Worked example — keep it or cut it. Price of cigarettes → lung cancer. Raise the price and fewer people smoke, so cancer rates fall:

Cigarettes smoked is the mediator.

  • If your question is the effect of smoking on lung cancer, and you're only noting that smoking is also driven by price — keep it. It's your treatment. You don't remove a mediator just because it's a mediator.
  • If your question is the effect of price on lung cancer — cut it. Draw price → cancer directly, and hold the reason in the back of your mind without studying it.

Second worked example. Why would online schooling affect staying in school? Maybe it changes how affiliated you feel with the college — never set foot on campus and you don't really think of yourself as one of the Fighting Lions, so you don't come back. Or maybe it's a difference in education quality.

Interesting questions. But if you're not able to study them, or you're humbly leaving them for another paper, and all they do on your diagram is sit between treatment and outcome — pop them out. Draw online classes → stay in school.

4

4 · Irrelevance

Plain English. If a variable isn't on any pathway between treatment and outcome, it can go.

Precisely. There's more nuance here, arriving in later videos, but the basic idea holds: a variable genuinely part of the data generating process may still have nothing to do with your research question.

Worked example. A programme at the college is structured so students tend to reach the end of a four-year degree still needing one more class — pushing them into an extra term. That causes plenty of dropout. It is a real determinant of your outcome and it belongs in the DGP.

But it has nothing to do with taking online courses. It's not on any pathway between treatment and outcome, so for your purposes it comes off.

Where it breaks. "Not on a pathway" is doing precise work, and the precision comes in the next chapter.

5

Ignoring things is the actual skill

Plain English. Deciding what you can safely ignore is where the leverage is.

Precisely. You cannot take the full complexity of the world into account. If you're unwilling to put your foot down and ignore some wrinkle, the only conclusion available to you is "the world sure is complex, isn't it" — and you'll never learn anything more specific than that. Anything you can justifiably ignore is leverage.

6

Checking you didn't cut too far

Plain English. Ask what your simplified diagram claims, and check whether the data agrees.

Precisely. A simplified diagram bakes in assumptions you may not have noticed. If it implies some relationship must be zero, and you know that relationship isn't zero, you've probably cut too much. A placebo test is one way to find this.

Worked example. In the simplified online-schooling diagram, demographics and location end up with no pathway between them that doesn't run through a collider (next chapter). That implies the correlation between demographics and location should be zero in the data.

That is almost certainly false. Different locations have different racial mixes, different age distributions. So either you simplified too far, or you left out something important — and either way it's worth reconsidering. Occasionally you'll decide the simplification is justified and the violation isn't important enough to matter. That's a judgement, but it should be a conscious one.

Say it out loud

So you've drawn a causal diagram and it's a disaster — arrows everywhere. Now you simplify. And there's an obvious objection: the world is complicated, shouldn't the diagram be? Yes, but a diagram with everything on it is a diagram you can't do anything with.

Four moves.

Unimportance — cut the trivial stuff. Statistics is about modelling the world, not reproducing it. Every assumption you make is already a little bit false.

Redundancy — if race, gender, and socioeconomic status all point into the same places and all point out to the same places, they're doing one job. Bundle them into demographics. And notice this doesn't cheat — you haven't pretended the world is simple, you've just put the problem in one labelled box so you can see it.

Mediators — a mediator is the reason one thing causes another. Cigarette prices affect lung cancer because fewer people smoke. Now: if my question is the effect of smoking on cancer, smoking stays — it's my treatment. But if my question is the effect of price on cancer, I cut it out and draw price straight to cancer. Same diagram, different question, different answer.

Irrelevance — there's a programme at the college that accidentally leaves students needing one extra term, and that genuinely causes dropout. Real part of the world. But it has nothing to do with online courses, so off it comes.

And here's the bigger point. Working out what you're allowed to ignore is the whole skill. You cannot hold the full complexity of the world in your head — and if you refuse to ignore anything, the only thing you'll ever conclude is "wow, the world is complicated."

Just don't overdo it. In my simplified diagram, demographics and location ended up implying zero correlation with each other. Which is obviously wrong — different places have different demographics. That's the diagram telling me I cut too much.

Recall drill

QName the four ways to simplify a causal diagram.

Unimportance, redundancy, removing mediators, and irrelevance.

QWhat justifies dropping unimportant variables?

Statistics is about modelling the world, not perfectly representing it. Every assumption is at least slightly false, so losing a trivial distraction to see the important structure is a good trade.

QWhen can several variables be bucketed into one?

When their arrows go into and out of the same places, so from the point of view of your research question they play the same role — like race, gender, and SES becoming demographics.

QDoes bucketing variables pretend the world is simple?

No. It packages the complexity into one labelled node so you can see what the problem is. You'll unpack it when you actually use the diagram.

QWhat is a mediator?

A variable sitting between two others that explains why one causes the other — like cigarettes smoked between cigarette price and lung cancer.

QWhen do you keep a mediator and when do you cut it?

Keep it if the mechanism is your research question — if smoking is your treatment, it stays. Cut it if you're studying price → cancer and aren't investigating the mechanism.

QGive the online-courses mediators Nick removes.

How affiliated you feel with the college (never being on campus, so you don't think of yourself as one of the Fighting Lions) and differences in education quality.

QWhat is the irrelevance criterion?

A variable not on any pathway between your treatment and outcome can be dropped, even though it is genuinely part of the data generating process.

QGive the irrelevance example.

A college programme structured so students end up needing one extra term to graduate. It really does cause dropout, but has nothing to do with taking online courses, so it comes off.

QWhy is deciding what to ignore described as the actual skill?

Because you can't account for the full complexity of the world. Refuse to ignore anything and the only conclusion available is "the world sure is complex" — anything you can justifiably ignore is leverage.

QHow did the simplified online diagram reveal it had gone too far?

It left no pathway between demographics and location except through a collider, implying their correlation should be zero. Different locations plainly have different racial mixes and age distributions, so that's false.

Glossary

Mediator
a variable that sits between two others and explains why one causes the other.
Redundancy
several variables playing the same role on a diagram, collapsible into one node.
Irrelevance
a variable that is part of the data generating process but sits on no pathway between treatment and outcome.
Collider
a variable caused by two others; introduced properly in Ep 17.
Placebo test
checking a relationship your diagram implies should be zero; a non-zero result signals oversimplification or an omission.
