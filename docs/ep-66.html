Ep 66Estimating Heterogeneous Treatment Effects

▶ Watch the video 13:20

Rather than asking what a single estimate represents, get a different estimate for everybody — with a causal forest, which splits the data to maximise differences in effect rather than prediction, or with sorted effects, which piles in interactions and then graphs the whole distribution — remembering that neither makes anything causal that wasn't already.

The 30-second version

  • Chapter 10 asked what a single estimate represents. Why get a single estimate at all?
  • Already covered: interaction terms (great for a specific group, gets busy fast) and hierarchical linear modeling (smoother, shows the distribution).
  • Causal forest: a random forest that splits on differences in the causal effect rather than predictive power.
  • Its big advantage: individual effects come with standard errors, which most machine learning methods can't give you.
  • Sorted effects: throw in many interactions and non-linearities, compute everyone's individual effect, then graph the distribution — and see who sits where in it.
  • Neither is magic. Both assume you've already identified the causal effect. A causal forest doesn't make a correlation causal.

Why this exists

Back in Chapter 10 came heterogeneous treatment effects — different individuals affected differently by the treatment. The question then became what your single estimate represents: an average treatment effect over everybody? An average treatment on the treated? A local average treatment effect weighted toward people responsive to assignment?

But why do you have to get a single estimate? Why not just get a different estimate for everybody — for you, and me, and my friend, and my neighbour?

Two approaches already covered:

  • Interaction terms — interact the treatment with predictors to see how the effect varies across groups. Excellent if you're particularly interested in one specific group, but limited: you can't allow the full range of treatment effects, and it gets too busy too quickly.
  • Hierarchical linear modeling — random effects letting the effect vary across characteristics in a much smoother way than interactions, so you can see the distribution of the effect.

Now two methods from machine learning for estimating the full range of individual estimates — so you can say your estimate is 0.352678.

The ideas

1

First, how a random forest works

Plain English. Split the data in two, over and over, on whichever split predicts best.

Precisely. A random forest is a predictive model, not concerned with causal inference — though you could use one for double machine learning. Here's the procedure:

  1. Start with all your data and a set of variables predicting an outcome — say income.
  2. Run through all your variables and all their values, using each to split the data in two: men against women; above and below age 24; above and below 25; above and below 26; this state against that state — every possible two-group split.
  3. Pick the split that best maximises predictive power. If men versus women is the biggest difference, split there. You've turned a single node into a tree with two branches.
  4. Repeat within each branch. Among the men: above versus below 25, above versus below 26, this race versus that. Split again. Then again on the women. Keep going, chopping the data into tiny chunks.
  5. Predict each individual based on where they fall in the tree.
  6. Do the whole thing over and over, randomly including and excluding different predictors.

To predict you, follow your splits: a man, above 25, hair five inches long — down the branches until you reach your spot. That's your prediction.

2

Causal forest

Plain English. Identical machinery — but split on where the effect differs most, not where the prediction improves most.

Precisely. A causal forest is the exact same idea. Still splits, still runs through all the variables looking for the best split, still repeats to chop the data into ever-tinier chunks. The only difference is that instead of basing the split on improving predictive power, you base it on the difference in the causal effect you estimate.

Worked example. You're not predicting income — you're estimating the effect of getting a college degree on income. So look at a candidate split, men versus women:

  • Among men: what's the relationship between getting a college degree and income?
  • Among women: same question.
  • Compare those two. How different are they?

Do that comparison across many candidate splits, and pick the split that maximises the difference in those effects.

Having done that, you've packed together lots of tiny groups that each have very similar treatment effects — so you can estimate the treatment effect just for them. And by seeing where you fall in the tree, you get your individual treatment effect.

Anchor image. A random forest sorts people into boxes where they look alike. A causal forest sorts them into boxes where the treatment works alike.

Why it's popular. It's very easy to use, has a lot of power, and handles many predictors — though you don't want to go too overboard. And it has a very nice property: the individual treatment effects it estimates come with standard errors you can calculate. A lot of the time machine learning methods give you no standard errors at all, which makes proper statistical inference difficult. Causal forests do.

3

Sorted effects

Plain English. Add every interaction you can think of, compute everyone's effect, and plot the distribution.

Precisely. Interaction terms were dismissed earlier as something you can't simply pile up — it gets noisy and hard to interpret. Sorted effects says: find any way of estimating heterogeneous effects in your regression model. Throw in a whole pile of interaction terms. Add non-linearities, polynomials, whatever.

Do that, and you can estimate each individual person's treatment effect. Interact treatment with gender and race and state and area and height and hair length and everything else, then get the effect of treatment by taking the derivative of the regression model — exactly as in the interaction terms episode — and plug in your variables to get your individual effect.

That's still hard to interpret. So the method predicts the individual effect for every person and graphs the distribution of effects.

Worked example — reading the distribution. You might get a graph where the average effect is about 0.07, while individual effects vary wildly from about 0.02 up to 0.1. You also get a standard error at every quantile of the effect.

And better: you can see who is in each part of the distribution. That's rather harder to do with hierarchical linear modeling, though possible.

Worked example — vitamin E. Looking at the effect of being recommended vitamin E on whether you take vitamin E, interacted with things like how much exercise you get, whether you smoke, and a "vitamin behaviour score" — Nick doesn't know exactly what that last one is.

Look at who sits where in the distribution of effects:

GroupSmoking rate
People who don't respond to the vitamin E recommendation100% smoke
People who respond stronglyNone smoke

That's a big difference. It lets you describe the kinds of people who respond strongly to treatment and the kinds who don't — which matters enormously if you want to know not just what a policy's effect is, but how it differs across groups of people.

4

The caveat that matters most

Plain English. Neither method makes anything causal. They only describe variation in an effect you already identified.

Precisely. Neither of these is magic. Both assume you already have some way of identifying the causal effect of interest — the thing the rest of the book has been about. Once you're past that hurdle, they let you model the heterogeneity.

Running a causal forest does not guarantee your results are causal. They're only causal if the relationship you're splitting over is genuinely a causal effect. When you split the data and compare the correlation here against the correlation there — if that correlation isn't a causal effect, you aren't getting a causal effect out.

So be sure you're actually getting the causal effect before you try to estimate heterogeneity in it.

Nick's view. That said, this is genuinely exciting. Modelling heterogeneous treatment effects is one of the things machine learning can really contribute to causal inference, because it's very good at picking up fine detail and producing specific individual-level predictions and effects. He expects a lot of growth there as machine learning influences causal inference further.

Say it out loud

Chapter 10 spent ages asking what a single estimate represents — average treatment effect, effect on the treated, local average. And there's an obvious question hiding in that: why get a single estimate at all? Why not one for every person?

Two machine learning methods do exactly that.

First, the causal forest. Start with a random forest, which is a prediction machine — it splits your data in two on whichever variable predicts best, then splits each half again, and again, chopping into tiny groups, then predicts you by following your branches down.

A causal forest is the identical machinery with one change. Instead of splitting where prediction improves most, it splits where the treatment effect differs most. So it looks at men and women and asks — is the effect of a college degree on income different between them? Then tries every other split and picks the one where the effects diverge most. End up with little pockets of people for whom the treatment works about the same, and now you can read off your effect by finding your pocket.

And it has a property that's rarer than it sounds in machine learning: you get standard errors.

Second — sorted effects, which is cheerfully brutal. You can't normally just dump fifty interaction terms into a regression, because it's uninterpretable. Sorted effects says: do it anyway. Then compute everyone's individual effect and graph the whole distribution. Average might be 0.07 while people range from 0.02 to 0.1.

And you can look at who's where. In a study of vitamin E recommendations: of the people who ignored the recommendation entirely, 100% were smokers. Of the people who responded most strongly, none were.

One warning though, and it's a big one. Neither of these makes anything causal. A causal forest splits on whatever relationship you hand it. If that relationship wasn't causal to begin with, you now have beautifully personalised nonsense.

Recall drill

QWhat question does this episode ask that Chapter 10 didn't?

Why get a single estimate at all, rather than a different estimate for every individual.

QWhat are the two previously covered ways of estimating heterogeneous effects, and their limits?

Interaction terms — good for a specific group but they get too busy too quickly and can't cover the full range — and hierarchical linear modeling, which varies effects more smoothly and shows the distribution.

QDescribe the random forest procedure.

Try every variable and value as a two-way split, pick the split that best improves prediction, repeat within each branch to chop the data into tiny chunks, predict each person by where they land, and repeat the whole process randomly including and excluding predictors.

QWhat single thing changes to make it a causal forest?

The splits are chosen to maximise the difference in the estimated causal effect rather than predictive power.

QWalk through a causal forest split.

Considering men versus women, estimate the relationship between college degree and income within each, compare how different those effects are, do the same across many candidate splits, and take the split with the largest difference.

QWhat do you end up with?

Small groups whose members have very similar treatment effects — so locating yourself in the tree gives your individual treatment effect.

QWhat's the causal forest's notable statistical advantage?

Its individual treatment effects come with calculable standard errors, which most machine learning methods don't provide.

QWhat does sorted effects do?

Adds many interaction terms and non-linearities, computes each individual's treatment effect by taking the derivative of the model, then graphs the distribution of those effects.

QWhat does the distribution graph show you?

The spread of individual effects — for example an average of about 0.07 with individuals ranging from about 0.02 to 0.1 — plus a standard error at each quantile.

QWhat extra thing can sorted effects tell you?

Who sits in each part of the distribution — which kinds of people respond strongly and which don't.

QGive the vitamin E finding.

Among people who don't respond to the vitamin E recommendation, 100% smoke; among those who respond strongly, none do.

QWhat do both methods assume you've already done?

Identified the causal effect of interest.

QDoes running a causal forest make your results causal?

No. It splits on whatever relationship you give it — if that relationship isn't causal, the output isn't either.

QWhat's Nick's view of machine learning's role here?

That modelling heterogeneous treatment effects is one of the things machine learning can genuinely contribute to causal inference, and an area he expects to grow.

Glossary

Causal forest
a tree-based method splitting the data to maximise differences in estimated treatment effects rather than predictive accuracy.
Random forest
a predictive tree ensemble splitting on whatever most improves prediction.
Sorted effects
estimating individual treatment effects from a heavily interacted model and graphing their distribution.
Individual treatment effect
a treatment effect estimated for a specific person rather than a group average.
