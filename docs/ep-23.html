Ep 23Weighted Average Treatment Effects

▶ Watch the video 12:20
On this pageThe 30-second versionWhy this existsThe ideasTrapsSay it out loudRecall drillConnectionsGlossary

Some averages count everybody but not equally — controlling for variables makes people with more leftover variation in treatment (or who are easier to match) count more, and isolating random variation in treatment makes people who respond to it count more, which is the local average treatment effect.

The 30-second version

  • A weighted average treatment effect includes everyone, but some people count more than others.
  • You don't pick the weights. The method imposes them, for a reason.
  • Variance weighting (regression): after controlling for Z, whoever has more treatment variation left gets more weight — because that's all there is to look at.
  • Matching / sample selection: whoever is easier to find a match for counts more, so common types dominate.
  • Rule of thumb 3: controlling for variables in any way generally introduces a weighted average treatment effect.
  • Local average treatment effect (LATE): when you isolate treatment variation driven by a random source, weight is proportional to how much you respond to it. Don't respond at all, weight zero.
  • Rule of thumb 5: random variation in treatment + using only the predicted part → LATE.
  • Which one you want depends entirely on what you'll do with it. None is better.

Why this exists

You've got equally-weighted averages — over everyone (ATE) or over a group (CATE). There's a third shape: everybody counts, at different levels.

And the important thing is that this isn't a choice you make. You don't walk in and declare that this person matters more than that one. The method hands you the weights, and it has a reason.

The ideas

1

What a weighted average treatment effect is

Plain English. Everyone's in the average, but with different amounts of pull.

Precisely. Instead of averaging individual effects equally, or restricting to a group, each person enters with a weight. Nick gets 0.1, this person 0.2, that person 0.3 — and it works like any weighted average.

Anchor image. A shareholder vote. Everybody votes. Not everybody has the same number of shares.

2

Variance weighting

Plain English. After controlling for things, some people have no treatment variation left — and you can't learn anything from someone who doesn't vary.

Precisely. Adding a control variable means predicting what you can with it, subtracting that off, and working with the residuals — exactly the Chapter 4 operation. So you're analysing the variation in X and Y that remains after Z is removed. If there's no variation left for someone, there's nothing to study about them.

Someone whose treatment is well explained by the controls has little residual variation, so a regression estimator weights their effect less. Someone with more leftover variation gets weighted more, simply because there's more to look at.

Anchor image. Trying to learn what a dial does by watching people who never touch it. The ones who fiddle constantly teach you everything; the ones who never move it teach you nothing — so your conclusion is really about the fiddlers.

Worked example. What's the effect of which city you live in on your income? Regress income on city. But there's a back door: a really effective worker might be more likely to move to a certain kind of city, and that's also related to their income. So control for individual characteristics.

Now what variation is left?

PersonAfter controlling for who they areWeight
Never moves — lives in one city their whole lifeNo variation in city left. You can't see whether city affects their outcomes at allLow
Moves a lotYou can clearly see their income is higher in this city and lower in that oneHigh

And here's the sting: if moving cities is enormously effective for the person who never moves, and irrelevant for the person who moves constantly, you only see the mover's effect.

Where it breaks. How this shakes out mathematically is fairly complex and doesn't come across well in a video — the chapter has it. The idea is what matters: statistics can only study things that vary, so less variation means less weight.

3

Weighting from matching and sample selection

Plain English. If you find people's doppelgängers instead of adjusting statistically, the easy-to-match people dominate.

Precisely. Rather than statistical adjustment, you pick a dataset with no variation in the control variable — find someone very similar in every characteristic and compare. People who are easier to find a match for count more.

Anchor image. A dating app that only shows you people with a match. If your profile is unusual, you never appear in anyone's results — and the aggregate statistics quietly become about everyone else.

Worked example. Effect of city on income again, but by matching. Take a person and find someone with the same hair colour, height, gender, race, birthplace — two doppelgängers who happen to live in different cities.

Who gets a match easily? People with the most common race, hair colour, height, and so on. Their treatment effects count for more. Unusual people rarely match and contribute little.

Where it breaks. So regression gives you variance weighting and matching gives you something more like representativeness weighting. Different weights, same underlying fact.

4

Rule of thumb 3

Precisely. Whenever you control for variables in some way — adjusting for them in a regression, or picking a sample — you are in general introducing some sort of weighted average treatment effect. The exact weights depend on how you incorporated the controls.

5

Local average treatment effect

Plain English. When you use random variation to drive treatment, people who ignore that random push don't count at all.

Precisely. The local average treatment effect arises when you have a variable that randomly influences treatment and you identify the effect by isolating only the variation in treatment explained by that source — the front-door strategy from Chapter 9. Different people respond differently to that random variation, and weight is proportional to responsiveness.

Anchor image. Measuring how much a nudge moves people by watching only the ones who can be nudged. The immovable are invisible.

Worked example. A genuine randomized experiment: you tell some people to exercise and others not to.

  • Some do what you say.
  • Some say "I always exercise, I don't care what you randomly assigned" — and carry on regardless.

That second person isn't affected by your random variation at all. When you isolate the part of treatment driven by the randomization, they fall away entirely. Their weight is zero. Respond a little and you get a little weight. Respond strongly and you're weighted heavily.

That's what "local" means: how local you are — how responsive to the assignment.

Rule of thumb 5. A random source of variation in treatment, plus using only the part of treatment you can predict with it, tends to produce a local average treatment effect.

6

Which average do you actually want?

Plain English. It depends entirely on what you plan to do with the number.

What you want to doThe average you want
Apply the treatment to everybodyATE — everyone will be affected, so you want everyone's effect
Apply it only to a particular groupCATE for that group — the effect for the people you'll actually treat
Know what a policy actually did in the past, not what it will doATT — what happened to the people who were treated
Implement a policy by asking people to participateLATE — the effect among people most likely to listen to the assignment

Where it breaks. None of these is wrong, and none is inherently better. They do different things. What matters is thinking about which one you'll get — and if it isn't the one you want, either the research design is leading you somewhere bad, or you need an estimator that produces the average you're after. In that case, go read the papers discussing that estimator to find out what kind of average it actually gives you.

Say it out loud

Two more ways an estimate can be an average — and the key thing is that you don't pick the weights, the method does.

First: variance weighting. Say I want the effect of which city you live in on your income, and I control for individual characteristics to close the back door. Fine. But think about what controlling actually does — it subtracts out what those characteristics explain and leaves the residuals. So now, someone who has lived in one city their entire life: after I control for who they are, there's no variation in city left for them. I literally cannot see what city does to their income. Whereas someone who moves constantly — I can watch their income rise here and fall there.

So the mover counts a lot and the stayer counts almost nothing. And if it happens that moving cities would be transformative for the stayer and irrelevant for the mover — I only ever see the mover's effect.

Same thing with matching, different weights. If I pair people up with their doppelgängers, the people who count are the ones who are easy to match — the common heights, common hair colours. Unusual people barely appear.

That's rule three: controlling for anything, in any way, generally gives you a weighted average.

Then there's the local average treatment effect. Say I randomly tell some people to exercise and others not to. Some comply — and some say "I exercise every day, I don't care what you assigned me." That person is completely unaffected by my randomization, so when I isolate the part of treatment that my random assignment drove, they get a weight of zero. The more you respond to the nudge, the more you count. That's what "local" means.

And which one do you want? Depends entirely on the use. Treating everybody — you want the ATE. Evaluating what a policy did in the past — the effect on the treated. Running a program people have to opt into — you want the local effect, because those are exactly the people who'll show up. None of them is wrong. They just answer different questions.

Recall drill

QWhat is a weighted average treatment effect?

An average of individual effects where everyone is included but people count at different levels, according to weights.

QWho decides the weights?

The method does, for a reason inherent to how it works — not the researcher deciding who matters more.

QExplain variance weighting.

Controlling for a variable leaves you working with residuals. Someone whose treatment is well explained by the controls has little variation left, so they get less weight; someone with more leftover variation gets more, because there's more to look at.

QIn the city-and-income example, why does someone who never moves get almost no weight?

After controlling for who they are, there's no variation in their city, so you can't observe whether city affects their income at all.

QWhat's the danger in that example?

If moving cities would be highly effective for the never-mover and irrelevant for the frequent mover, you only see the mover's effect.

QHow does matching weight people, and toward whom?

Toward people who are easy to find a match for — those with the most common characteristics like race, hair colour, and height.

QState rule of thumb 3.

Controlling for variables in any way — regression adjustment or picking a sample — generally introduces a weighted average treatment effect.

QWhat is a local average treatment effect?

The average effect you get when you isolate the treatment variation driven by a random source, weighting each person by how strongly they respond to that random variation.

QYou randomly tell people to exercise. Someone says "I always exercise anyway." What's their weight?

Zero. They don't respond to the random variation, so they fall away entirely when you isolate the part of treatment it drove.

QWhat does "local" refer to in the local average treatment effect?

How local you are — that is, how responsive you are to the assignment to treatment.

QState rule of thumb 5.

A random source of variation in treatment, combined with using only the part of treatment predicted by it, tends to give a local average treatment effect.

QYou want to know what a policy actually did in the past. Which average?

The average treatment on the treated — what happened to the people who were actually treated.

QYou'll implement a policy by asking people to participate. Which average, and why?

The local average treatment effect, because it weights toward the people most likely to respond to the assignment — which is who will actually take part.

QIs one of these averages better than the others?

No. They do different things. What matters is knowing which one your design gives you and whether it's the one you need.

Glossary

Weighted average treatment effect
an average of individual effects in which people contribute at different weights.
Variance weighting
weighting by how much treatment variation remains after controlling, as regression does.
Local average treatment effect (LATE)
the average effect weighted by how strongly each person responds to a random source of variation in treatment.
Responsiveness
how much a person's treatment status changes with the random assignment; determines their weight in a LATE.
