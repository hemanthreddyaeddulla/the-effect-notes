Ep 62Estimating Regression Discontinuity

▶ Watch the video 15:20
On this pageThe 30-second versionWhy this existsThe ideasTrapsSay it out loudRecall drillConnectionsGlossary

Fit one line on each side of the cutoff, centre the running variable so it equals zero there, and the jump between the two intercepts — β₂ — is your effect; then wrestle with bandwidth, functional form, and the fact that a fuzzy cutoff turns the whole thing into instrumental variables.

The 30-second version

  • Regress the outcome on (running variable − cutoff), a treated indicator, and their interaction. Two lines, one per side.
  • Subtracting the cutoff makes the running variable zero there, so the intercepts become the predictions at the cutoff.
  • Untreated side: intercept β₀, slope β₁. Treated side: intercept β₀ + β₂, slope β₁ + β₃. The effect is β₂.
  • Mathematically identical to interrupted time series — but a very different design, because "randomly on one side" is far more plausible than "randomly before or after."
  • Bandwidth is a bias–variance trade-off: narrow means credible but noisy, wide means precise but less comparable.
  • Functional form matters enormously, because you're extrapolating to where each line hits the cutoff. A quadratic is usually as far as you go.
  • Fuzzy regression discontinuity — where the cutoff only partly determines treatment — is solved with instrumental variables.

Why this exists

You have the design. Now estimate it. And as ever, the research design and the estimation method are not the same thing — there are often many ways to estimate a given design, and the same estimation method can apply to different designs depending on context.

The ideas

1

The basic regression

Plain English. One line each side, and the gap where they meet is the answer.

Precisely. Regress your outcome y on:

  • (running variable − cutoff)
  • an indicator for being treated — being on the correct side of the cutoff
  • the interaction between the two

You're effectively estimating two lines: one linear regression to the left of the cutoff, a different one to the right.

SidetreatedInterceptSlope
Untreated0β₀β₁
Treated1β₀ + β₂β₁ + β₃
2

Why you subtract the cutoff

Plain English. So that "zero" means "at the cutoff" — which makes the intercepts do the work for you.

Precisely. Take your running variable — say the income that qualifies you for some relief programme — and subtract the cutoff. If the cutoff is $12,000 and you earn $12,005, your centred running variable is 5, because you're five above the cutoff.

Now think back to the interpretation of an intercept: it's your prediction of the outcome when all other predictors are zero. So set the running variable to zero and the intercept is your prediction at zero — and by centring, zero is the cutoff. The intercept becomes your prediction at the cutoff, which is exactly the point where the two lines meet.

Reading the graph. Fit one regression line on the left and a different one on the right. Where the left-hand line hits the dashed cutoff line, that's the left intercept — β₀. Where the right-hand line hits it, that's the right intercept — β₀ + β₂.

So the jump at the cutoff, where the treatment goes into effect, is the change from one intercept to the other: β₂ is your regression discontinuity effect.

Anchor image. Two roads approaching a river from opposite banks. What you're measuring is the height difference where each one reaches the water.

3

It's mathematically the same as interrupted time series

Plain English. Same maths, completely different assumption.

Precisely. If you watched the event study videos, this is mathematically the exact same thing as the interrupted time series approach estimated with linear regression. And there's a reason: event studies and regression discontinuity are basically the same thing mathematically. In event studies the running variable is time; in regression discontinuity it's something else.

So why treat them as entirely separate designs? Because the assumptions you need are very different.

It is a much different assumption to say that people just before and just after an event, within a narrow window, are basically comparable — versus people on one side of a cutoff or the other. The idea that you're randomly falling on one side or the other makes a lot more sense for running variables that aren't time. You can't really randomly be before or after.

In general people consider regression discontinuity a lot more plausible than event studies for exactly that reason.

4

Bandwidth and the power trade-off

Plain English. The whole design says only the cases near the cutoff are comparable — and then you went and used all the data.

Precisely. The basic regression above used the entire range of the data to estimate its lines, which may not be what you want. So a common step is to narrow your range, dropping data far from the cutoff.

Worked example. With the income cutoff at $12,000, you might decide people earning between $11,900 and $12,100 have incomes close enough to be comparable, limit the data to that, and fit your regression lines within it.

The trade-off — and this is the statistical power issue.

BandwidthBiasPrecision
NarrowLow — you genuinely believe in the identificationPoor — you drop a lot of data, the sample gets very small, the estimate gets very noisy
WideHigher — you're comparing someone on $13,000 to someone on $10,000, and back door variables may returnBetter — more observations

Widening asks whether $13,000 and $10,000 of income are really comparable. Maybe, maybe not — and at some point you start to lose belief in the identification.

Choosing one. There are procedures called optimal bandwidth selection. Alternatively, just try a bunch of different bandwidths and see whether your results are stable as they widen and your estimates get more precise. If the estimate itself doesn't change much as you bring in more data, you might as well bring it in — it doesn't seem to be biasing you much.

5

Functional form matters more than usual

Plain English. You're extrapolating each line to the cutoff, so getting the shape wrong moves your answer directly.

Precisely. You don't have to use a linear regression on either side. In the example graph the fit is clearly not good. You can use a curve or polynomial on either side to better match the shape of the data — in particular a squared term, a quadratic. You probably don't want to go beyond that, for reasons given in the chapter.

Why this matters so much here. You want a functional form that fits your data well, because otherwise you won't do a good job of telling where the data is going to hit that dashed line — and that is the entire goal. In the original graph, the data hits the dashed line at a very different point than the fitted line does. Adding a polynomial term would substantially improve the estimate.

How to add it. Before, you had (running − cutoff), interacted with treated. For a polynomial: add (running − cutoff) squared, and interact that with treated as well. That's it.

6

Fuzzy regression discontinuity

Plain English. If crossing the cutoff only raises the chance of treatment, use the cutoff as an instrument.

Precisely. What if the cutoff doesn't perfectly determine your treatment? Fuzzy regression discontinuity is when the running variable only partially determines treatment.

Worked example. Back to the income relief. If you earn below $12,000 a year you qualify — but you also have to apply, and not everybody does. So instead of going from 0% treated above $12,000 to 100% treated below it, you might get 0% treated above and 50% treated below.

The fix — and it's the thread from the last chapter. You still have a source of random-ish assignment: being just above or just below the cutoff. And that still predicts your treatment. So use being on one side of the cutoff as an instrument for actually being treated.

Your income being just below $12,000 increases the probability that you're treated; use that predicted increase in treatment probability to see the effect of treatment on your outcomes. That's taking all the instrumental variables machinery from the previous chapter and applying it here, with side of the cutoff as the instrument. Technical details are in the chapter.

Say it out loud

Estimating a regression discontinuity is beautifully simple: fit a line on each side of the cutoff and measure the gap where they meet.

In a regression that's the outcome on the running variable, a treated indicator, and their interaction. But there's one move that makes it clean — subtract the cutoff from your running variable, so it equals zero exactly at the threshold.

Why? Because the intercept is your prediction when everything else is zero. Centre it, and the intercept becomes your prediction at the cutoff — the very point where the two lines meet. So the left line's intercept is β₀, the right line's is β₀ + β₂, and the jump is β₂. One coefficient.

Now — if that feels familiar, it should. This is mathematically identical to interrupted time series from the event studies chapter. Same equations. The only difference is that there the running variable was time.

And yet they're treated as completely different designs, and rightly so. Because saying "people just either side of an income cutoff are basically the same" is very believable. Saying "people just before and just after a date are basically the same" is much shakier. You can't randomly be before or after.

Two things to get right. Bandwidth: the whole logic says only cases near the cutoff are comparable — but narrow it too far and you've thrown away your sample and your estimate is noise. Wide it too far and you're comparing $13,000 to $10,000. Try several and see whether the answer is stable.

And functional form, which matters more here than almost anywhere, because you're extrapolating each line to the cutoff. Get the curve wrong and you get the jump wrong. A quadratic is usually far enough.

Last thing. What if crossing the cutoff doesn't guarantee treatment — you qualify below $12,000, but you also have to apply, and only half do? That's fuzzy regression discontinuity. And the fix is last chapter's tool: use which side of the cutoff you're on as an instrument for actually being treated.

Recall drill

QWhat three terms go into the basic regression discontinuity regression?

The running variable minus the cutoff, an indicator for being treated, and their interaction.

QWhy subtract the cutoff from the running variable?

So the running variable equals zero at the cutoff, which makes the intercepts the predicted outcomes at the cutoff — the point where the two lines meet.

QCutoff is $12,000 and you earn $12,005. What's your centred running variable?

5.

QGive the intercept and slope on each side.

Untreated side: intercept β₀, slope β₁. Treated side: intercept β₀ + β₂, slope β₁ + β₃.

QWhich coefficient is the regression discontinuity effect?

β₂ — the jump between the two intercepts at the cutoff.

QWhat other method is this mathematically identical to?

The interrupted time series approach from the event studies chapter, estimated with linear regression.

QWhat distinguishes the two designs, given identical mathematics?

The running variable. Event studies use time, and the assumption that people just before and just after an event are comparable is much weaker than the assumption for a non-time cutoff.

QWhy is "randomly on one side" more plausible than "randomly before or after"?

You can't really randomly be before or after a date, whereas landing just above or below a threshold genuinely can be near-random.

QWhat's the problem with the basic regression as first specified?

It uses the entire range of the data, when the design's logic only holds near the cutoff.

QState the bandwidth trade-off.

Narrow bandwidths give more credible identification but drop so much data that estimates get very noisy; wide bandwidths give precision but bring back less comparable observations and possible bias.

QGive two ways to choose a bandwidth.

Optimal bandwidth selection procedures, or trying several bandwidths and checking whether the estimate is stable as they widen.

QWhat does stability across bandwidths tell you?

That widening isn't biasing you much, so you might as well use the extra data for precision.

QWhy does functional form matter so much in regression discontinuity?

Because you're extrapolating each line to the cutoff, and a poorly fitting shape puts the line in the wrong place exactly where you're measuring the jump.

QHow do you add a quadratic to the model?

Include the squared centred running variable and interact that with the treated indicator as well.

QHow far up the polynomial orders should you go?

Usually no further than a quadratic.

QWhat is fuzzy regression discontinuity?

When the running variable only partially determines treatment — for instance, qualifying below $12,000 but also having to apply, so 0% are treated above and only 50% below.

QHow do you estimate a fuzzy design?

Use being on one side of the cutoff as an instrument for actually being treated, and apply instrumental variables.
