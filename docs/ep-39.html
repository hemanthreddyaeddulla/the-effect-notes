Ep 39Coarsened Exact Matching and Entropy Balancing

▶ Watch the video 11:00
On this pageThe 30-second versionWhy this existsThe ideasTrapsSay it out loudRecall drillConnectionsGlossary

Distance and propensity score matching can leave you with groups that still don't look alike, sending you back to try again — so two non-iterative methods guarantee the result up front: coarsened exact matching allows zero distance after binning continuous variables, and entropy balancing finds weights that force the moments you asked for to be equal.

The 30-second version

  • Distance and propensity score matching tend to fail — you run them and the matched groups still don't resemble each other, so you iterate.
  • Non-iterative methods avoid the guess-and-check loop entirely.
  • Coarsened exact matching: distance matching where the worst acceptable match has zero distance. Exactly the same on every matching variable.
  • Coarsened because continuous variables are first cut into bins, so exact matching is possible at all.
  • It works without checking — but it drops people ruthlessly, and the curse of dimensionality hits extra hard.
  • Entropy balancing: you name variables and moments (means, variances…) you want equal across groups, and it finds weights that satisfy them.
  • Uncommon and relatively new, but Nick thinks it has a promising future.

Why this exists

Both methods so far share a flaw: they have a tendency to fail. You perform the matching and end up with a set of matched observations that doesn't actually look all that much alike. Then you go back, change something about how you matched, or change your propensity score estimation model, and do it again — over and over until you get a decent set of matches.

The goal, remember, is a set of treated and control observations that look pretty similar, whether after matching or after applying weights. Thankfully some methods don't require the guess-and-check loop.

The ideas

1

Coarsened exact matching

Plain English. Distance matching where the only acceptable distance is zero.

Precisely. Exact matching allows no differences at all between observations on a matching variable. Coarsened exact matching is a form of distance matching where the worst acceptable match has zero distance — exactly the same on all matching variables.

Anchor image. A key that either turns or doesn't. There's no "nearly fits."

Worked example. You're studying a job training program, matching on state, sex, and education level. Nick is 35, lives in Washington, and has a PhD. He'll only accept as comparable someone who is also 35, also lives in Washington, and also has a PhD. Anyone else isn't a match at all. And if nobody on the other side of treatment is like that, he gets dropped — he simply doesn't get to be in the data.

Where exact matching also shows up. You can incorporate it into other methods. In distance matching you might say: you can be a bit further apart on the size of your bill, I want the shortest distance there — but you have to match exactly on which state you live in. Coarsened exact matching just says you must match exactly on everything.

2

The "coarsened" part

Plain English. Continuous variables get chopped into bins first, or nobody would ever match.

Precisely. Exact matching on a continuous variable is impossible — if your bill is $9,132, nobody else has exactly that. So before exact matching, continuous variables are coarsened into bins. Instead of a bill of 9,132 you have a bill in the range 9,000 to 10,000, and you can match with anyone else in that bin even though the exact numbers differ.

Why it needs no checking. You know before you run it that you'll get treated and control sets that are exactly the same on average, because you aren't allowing any differences. Any difference at all and you're chucked from the sample.

3

The cost: it drops people ruthlessly

Plain English. Being slightly unusual on any single variable gets you deleted.

Precisely. The curse of dimensionality works extra hard here. How many people in your data are 35, in Washington, with a PhD, and on the other side of treatment from you? There might be nobody. And it might be very common for there to be nobody — not just for 35-year-olds, but even once you bin to 30-to-40, and even once you coarsen education to "bachelor's degree or higher."

It gets worse with every matching variable you add. Imagine matching on nine things — finding someone exactly the same as you on nine different aspects of your life would be pretty amazing.

Consequences: you drop a lot of people, giving very imprecise results — and possibly biased ones, because you're dropping people who are merely slightly unusual on even one aspect.

Practice. When using coarsened exact matching, check how many observations got dropped. If it's a lot, either use a different method or do some iterating after all — make bigger bins, or remove some matching variables.

Where it works well. Coarsened exact matching simply doesn't work when you have to match on many variables; there's no way to match someone on nine, ten, fifteen, twenty elements, so if you need to close a lot of back doors it may not do it for you. But if the variables you're matching on are broad enough that you'll find plenty of matches, it can do a very good job — or if you simply have a dataset with millions and millions of observations, you may find matches on all nine elements anyway.

4

Entropy balancing

Plain English. State what you want to be equal, and it solves for weights that make it so.

Precisely. The least common of the four approaches — distance matching with Mahalanobis, propensity score matching, coarsened exact matching, and this. It's relatively new and relatively underused.

You pick a set of variables and a set of moments — means, variances, skewness, kurtosis, and so on down the list — and declare that you want those moments to be the same across your two groups.

Worked example. Your list of requirements might be:

  • The mean age should be the same between treatment and control.
  • The variance of age should be the same between treatment and control.
  • The proportion of people living in Washington should be the same.
  • The proportion with a PhD should be the same.

You can enforce that averages match across matching variables, add requirements that variances match, and add or remove conditions as you like.

What it does with that list. Entropy balancing sets about finding a set of weights that makes all your requirements true. The process is mathematically complex and isn't covered, but if a possible set of weights exists, it does a pretty good job of finding them.

Anchor image. You don't adjust the recipe and taste. You state the target flavour and let the solver work out the quantities.

Where it breaks. You could request too many things — it may simply not be possible to satisfy all your demands at once. But if it is, you get weights and all your conditions are satisfied, again without having to go back and check balance, because balance has been enforced for you.

Nick's verdict. Coarsened exact matching is quite popular these days, with the downsides that you drop a lot of observations and can't match on many variables. Entropy balancing isn't common, but he thinks it has a promising future and may be worth learning about. More detail is in the chapter.

Say it out loud

Both the matching methods so far have the same annoying habit: you run them, and the groups still don't look alike. So you go back, tweak something, run it again. Over and over. There are two methods that skip that loop entirely.

The first is coarsened exact matching, and it's beautifully blunt. It's distance matching where the only acceptable distance is zero. If I'm 35, live in Washington, and have a PhD, then my match has to be 35, in Washington, with a PhD. Anything else isn't a match. And if there's nobody like that — I get dropped from the study.

The "coarsened" bit handles continuous variables. Nobody else has a credit card bill of exactly $9,132, so first you bin it — call it "between nine and ten thousand" — and then match exactly on the bin.

And the nice thing is you never have to check whether it worked, because by construction the groups are identical. You didn't allow any differences.

The catch is how savagely it drops people. Think about how many people in your data are 35, in Washington, with a PhD, on the other side of treatment. Maybe zero. And every variable you add makes it worse — imagine finding someone identical to you on nine different things. So always check how many observations you lost, because dropping everyone slightly unusual makes your results imprecise and possibly biased.

The second method is entropy balancing, and it works backwards. Instead of picking matches and hoping, you just declare what you want: I want mean age equal across groups. And the variance of age. And the proportion with a PhD. Then it goes and solves for the weights that make all of that true. If a solution exists, it finds it — and you never check balance, because balance was the input.

Recall drill

QWhat flaw do distance and propensity score matching share?

They tend to fail — you can run them and still end up with matched groups that don't look alike, forcing you to go back and change something and try again.

QWhat is coarsened exact matching?

A form of distance matching where the worst acceptable match has zero distance — observations must be exactly the same on all matching variables.

QNick is 35, lives in Washington, and has a PhD. Who counts as a match, and what if nobody does?

Only someone who is also 35, also lives in Washington, and also has a PhD. If nobody on the other side matches, he's dropped from the data.

QCan exact matching be combined with other methods?

Yes — you might allow distance on bill size while requiring an exact match on which state someone lives in.

QWhy is "coarsened" necessary?

Because exact matching on a continuous variable is impossible — nobody else has a bill of exactly $9,132 — so you bin the variable first and match exactly on the bin.

QWhy does coarsened exact matching need no iterative checking?

Because by construction the treated and control groups are the same on average — any difference at all and the observation is dropped.

QWhat's the main downside of coarsened exact matching?

It drops a great many observations, giving imprecise and possibly biased results, since anyone slightly unusual on even one variable gets removed.

QWhy does the curse of dimensionality hit CEM extra hard?

Each additional variable must be matched exactly, and finding someone identical to you on nine or ten aspects of life is essentially impossible.

QWhat should you check after running coarsened exact matching?

How many observations were dropped. If it's a lot, use another method or iterate — bigger bins, or fewer matching variables.

QWhen does coarsened exact matching work well?

When the matching variables are broad enough to yield plenty of matches, or when you have millions of observations.

QWhat do you specify in entropy balancing?

A set of variables and a set of moments — means, variances, skewness, kurtosis — that you want to be equal across the treated and control groups.

QGive an example set of entropy balancing requirements.

Mean age equal across groups; variance of age equal; proportion living in Washington equal; proportion with a PhD equal.

QWhat does entropy balancing then do?

Finds a set of weights that makes all those requirements true — and if a possible set exists, it does a good job of finding it.

QWhy does entropy balancing need no balance check?

Because balance was the input — the conditions you specified have been enforced by construction.

QWhat can go wrong with entropy balancing?

You may request too many conditions for any set of weights to satisfy simultaneously.

QHow common is entropy balancing, and what's Nick's view?

It's the least common of the four approaches and relatively new, but he thinks it has a promising future and may be worth learning.

Glossary

Non-iterative matching
a method that guarantees comparable groups without a check-and-revise loop.
Exact matching
allowing no difference at all between matched observations on a variable.
Coarsened exact matching
exact matching after continuous variables have been binned.
Coarsening
cutting a continuous variable into bins so exact matching becomes possible.
Entropy balancing
finding weights that force specified moments of specified variables to be equal across groups.
Moment
a distributional summary such as the mean, variance, skewness, or kurtosis.
