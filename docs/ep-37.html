Ep 37Distance Matching

▶ Watch the video 13:00
On this pageThe 30-second versionWhy this existsThe ideasTrapsSay it out loudRecall drillConnectionsGlossary

With several matching variables you need to boil many separate distances down to one, which you do by treating observations as points in space and taking the straight line between them — usually the Mahalanobis distance, which first divides out the correlations so shared traits aren't counted twice — and the catch is that every dimension you add pushes everyone further apart.

The 30-second version

  • One matching variable: distance is trivially the difference between values.
  • Several variables: you need to combine several distances into a single distance to pick a closest match.
  • The trick is a spatial metaphor — distance on each axis collapses into one straight line, and it generalises past three dimensions even though the picture doesn't.
  • Euclidean distance is literally Pythagoras. Mahalanobis distance is more common, because it divides out the correlations between variables so a shared underlying trait isn't double-counted.
  • Once you have one distance you can do anything — best match, best three, or weights.
  • Curse of dimensionality: each variable you add is another way to be far apart, so all matches start to look bad.

Why this exists

The goal hasn't changed: pick a group of control observations very similar to the treated group across your matching variables, so that when someone asks whether a back door is driving the difference between the groups, you can say no — you matched on it, there's no variation left in it between the groups, so the back door is closed.

Distance matching does this by asking, literally, how far apart are two observations on the matching variables. Trivial with one variable. Not trivial with several.

The ideas

1

The problem: multiple distances

Plain English. Two candidates, each closer on a different variable. Which one is closer overall?

Worked example — one variable, easy. Match on April credit card bill size. If your bill is 6,000 and mine is 5,900, the difference is 100. To pick the best match for me, find whoever in the control group is closest to 6,000, or build weights around it. Simple.

Worked example — two variables, not easy. Now match on bill size and age. Say I'm 35 with a 6,000 bill. Two candidates:

CandidateBillAge
A6,000 — identical60 — 25 years off
B1,000 — 5,000 off35 — identical

Which is closer to me? You have one distance based on how far apart our bills are and a different distance based on how far apart our ages are — and to pick "whoever is closest", you need a way to combine multiple distances into a single distance, then take whoever has the smallest one.

2

The spatial metaphor

Plain English. Treat each observation as a point in space and measure the straight line between them.

Precisely. Most methods for boiling multiple distances into one are based on a metaphor with literal distance in the real world.

Anchor image. Hold up two fists. There's a distance between them left-to-right. There's another up-and-down. And one is further forward than the other. Three separate distances — and yet there is obviously one distance between your fists: the straight line.

Worked example. With two matching variables, define the distance along one variable and the distance along the other. Draw a straight line between the two points on that 2D plane and you have a single distance value — and you pick matches by minimising it.

With three variables you have three separate distances and still one straight line between the points. And it generalises beyond three dimensions into four, five, six — the visuals break down there, but the maths stays relatively easy.

3

Euclidean and Mahalanobis distance

Plain English. Euclidean is plain Pythagoras. Mahalanobis first removes the overlap between your variables.

Precisely.

  • Euclidean distance — literally what was just described: take this distance and that distance, apply the Pythagorean theorem, get the distance between the points.
  • Mahalanobis distance — more common in matching. It makes a statistical adjustment that makes more sense in statistical applications: some of those distances are double-counting the same thing, so it divides out the correlations between the variables.

Worked example. Suppose gender is a back door you want to match on and close — but you can't actually measure gender. What you do have is how much arm hair someone has and whether they have a beard. Both are more common, or take higher values, among men than women.

Use both as matching variables and you're treating them as two separate distances — but you're also counting "being a man" twice, because it shows up in both variables.

Mahalanobis divides out the correlation between them. Both variables stay in as matching variables, but the shared "man" component only counts once; the double-count gets subtracted out.

What that leaves you. You've rescaled all the axes to take out the shared elements, so every distance is now orthogonal to the others: here's how much arm hair you have not counting the overlap with facial hair, here's how much facial hair not counting the overlap with arm hair. And once you've subtracted that out, you're back to exactly the same geometric idea — points in space, shortest line between them.

4

What you do with it

Plain English. One number per pair, and now every matching option is open.

Precisely. For every treated observation you can now calculate the distance to every single control observation. Then do whatever you like:

  • Pick the best match — the shortest distance.
  • Pick the three best matches — the three shortest.
  • Construct weights where the shortest distances get the biggest weights, decaying as distance grows.

The key is that you now have a single measure of distance, and that's what makes any of it possible.

5

The curse of dimensionality

Plain English. Every variable you add is another way for two people to be far apart.

Precisely. The curse of dimensionality afflicts methods that are very sensitive to added dimensions — and here they're literal dimensions. One matching variable, one dimension. Two variables, two dimensions. Three, four, five. As the number of dimensions grows, things tend to get further and further apart.

Anchor image / worked example. Two people in the same building. Look straight down from above: they're both in the northeast corner, in the exact same spot. Matching in two dimensions, they look extremely close.

Now zoom out to the side and add the third dimension. One of them is on the fifth floor and the other is on the first. By adding a dimension you added a new way for them to be far apart — and now they're much further apart than they looked.

Where it breaks. So the more variables you match on, the harder it gets to find a decent match. Distance matching gets into trouble as you add matching variables, because all the matches start to look bad. Add enough dimensions and it becomes genuinely hard to find anyone who's similar to you across all those axes.

That's a particular problem for Q5 from the last episode — the worst allowable match — because once you've added a lot of dimensions, every match looks poor. Some people recommend getting more lax about what counts as an acceptable match the more matching variables you have. But it remains something to think about, and it's an issue if you need to match on many variables to close many back doors.

Say it out loud

Matching on one variable is easy — your bill's 6,000, mine's 5,900, the distance is 100. Done.

But what if I'm matching on bill size and age? I'm 35 with a 6,000 bill. One candidate has the exact same bill but is 60. Another is exactly 35 but has a 1,000 bill. Which one is closer to me? You genuinely can't answer until you decide how to combine two different distances into one number.

And the trick for that is lovely: pretend they're points in space. Hold up your two fists — there's a left-right distance, an up-down distance, and one's further forward. Three distances. But obviously there's just one distance between your fists: the straight line. Same idea, and it keeps working in four or five dimensions even though you can't picture it.

The plain version is Euclidean distance — straight Pythagoras. But the one people actually use is Mahalanobis, and here's why. Suppose I want to match on gender but I can't measure it. What I can measure is arm hair and whether you've got a beard. Both track being a man. So if I use both, I'm accidentally counting "man" twice. Mahalanobis divides out the correlation between the variables, so the shared bit only counts once.

Now the catch, and it's a big one: the curse of dimensionality. Picture two people in the same building. From directly overhead they're both in the northeast corner — identical, perfect match. Then you look from the side and realise one's on the fifth floor and the other's on the first.

Adding a dimension added a way to be far apart. So the more variables you match on, the worse every match gets — which is awkward, because matching on more variables is exactly how you close more back doors.

Recall drill

QWhat does distance matching ask?

How far apart two observations are on the matching variables — then pick the closest ones.

QWhy is one matching variable easy?

The distance is simply the difference between the two values.

QI'm 35 with a 6,000 bill. Candidate A has a 6,000 bill and is 60; candidate B is 35 with a 1,000 bill. What's the problem?

You have two separate distances — one on bill, one on age — and no way to say who's closer until you combine them into a single distance.

QWhat metaphor is used to combine multiple distances?

Physical distance in space — separate distances along each axis collapse into one straight line between two points, and it generalises beyond three dimensions.

QWhat is Euclidean distance?

The straight-line distance computed with the Pythagorean theorem from the distances along each axis.

QWhat does Mahalanobis distance add, and why?

It divides out the correlations between the matching variables, so a shared underlying component isn't double-counted.

QGive the arm hair and beard example.

If you want to match on gender but can't measure it, arm hair and having a beard both track being a man. Using both counts "being a man" twice, so Mahalanobis removes the correlation and lets the shared component count once.

QDoes Mahalanobis drop one of the correlated variables?

No. Both remain as matching variables; only the double-counted overlap is subtracted out.

QOnce you have a single distance measure, what can you do with it?

Pick the single best match, pick the several best matches, or construct weights where the shortest distances get the largest weights.

QDefine the curse of dimensionality in this context.

As you add matching variables — and therefore dimensions — observations tend to get further and further apart, so decent matches become harder to find.

QGive the building illustration.

Two people look identically placed when viewed from directly above — both in the northeast corner — but adding the vertical dimension reveals one is on the fifth floor and the other on the first.

QWhat practical problem does this create for the "worst acceptable match" question?

With many dimensions, every match looks bad — so you may reject nearly all of them unless you loosen your standard.

QWhat's the usual mitigation, and what's its limitation?

Being more lax about the worst acceptable match as you add matching variables. It's a mitigation rather than a fix — the matches genuinely are worse.
