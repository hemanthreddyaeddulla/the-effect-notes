Ep 10Alternative Explanations

▶ Watch the video 10:15
On this pageThe 30-second versionWhy this existsThe ideasTrapsSay it out loudRecall drillConnectionsGlossary

You can never see causation in data — only that two variables are related — so identification works by listing every other reason they might be related and killing those reasons one at a time, until what survives can only be the effect you're after.

The 30-second version

  • Causality here means: if you reached in and changed X, would Y change as a result?
  • Data doesn't show you that. It shows you conditional distributions — that Y's average differs across values of X.
  • So the working question becomes: what are all the reasons these two could be related? Then isolate the one you want.
  • Randomization is the cleanest tool, because assignment by coin flip can't be caused by anything else in the system.
  • Conditioning on a variable is another, and later episodes add many more.
  • You've identified the effect when every alternative explanation is either ruled out or removed, and a relationship still remains.
  • The alternatives don't disprove your effect. They just have to be cleared away before you can see it.

Why this exists

The DGP is out there, and any set of observations is consistent with lots of different ones. That's a problem, because the thing you can actually see — that X and Y move together — is exactly the thing that every one of those processes predicts.

So identification can't work by staring harder at the relationship. It has to work by elimination. Enumerate the other stories, dispose of them, and see what's left standing.

The ideas

1

What data shows you versus what you want

Plain English. You want to know whether changing X changes Y. What you observe is that X and Y tend to go together.

Precisely. The causal question is interventional: reach in, change the value of X, does Y change as a result? What data gives you is a conditional distribution — at different values of X you get different conditional means of Y. That, by itself, doesn't say why the relationship exists.

Anchor image. Two people always leaving a building at the same time. You can see the co-timing. You cannot see whether one is waiting for the other.

2

The list of alternatives

Plain English. Before you claim an effect, write down every other reason the relationship could show up.

Precisely. Enumerating alternative explanations converts a vague worry into a checklist. Each entry is a data generating process that would produce your observed relationship without the causal effect you care about — and each one tells you something specific your analysis has to handle.

Worked example. The classic econometrics question: does more schooling raise your earnings? In the data, people with more education do earn more on average. Reasons that could be true other than education causing earnings:

  • People who were going to be better workers anyway choose to get more education.
  • People who live in richer areas tend to get more education and earn more because of where they are.
  • People with a natural aptitude for a highly-paid field — you'd have made a good engineer regardless — think "engineering school sounds great" and go, so the schooling tags along with the aptitude.

Where it breaks. Crucially, none of this means education doesn't raise earnings. The effect may be entirely real. It just means it's buried among other things, and you have to dig through them to pull out the part you want.

3

Randomization removes alternatives by construction

Plain English. If a coin decided who got the treatment, then nothing about the people could have decided it.

Precisely. Explicit randomization severs the link between treatment assignment and everything else. Whatever else varies across people cannot have influenced who got treated, because a random device did. That's the entire purpose of randomizing.

Anchor image. Dealing cards from a shuffled deck rather than letting players pick. Nobody can claim the good hands went to the people who wanted them.

Worked example. Suppose you could randomly assign how much education people get. Now revisit the alternatives: could people who'd have earned more anyway have chosen more education? No — that never happened in your data. The only reason anyone got more education is that you assigned it, and you assigned it randomly, which has nothing to do with what their earnings would have been. The alternative is gone, not adjusted for.

Where it breaks. Randomization is one route, not the only one. Conditioning on a variable — the conditional conditional distributions of Ep 8 — closes off alternatives too, and much of the rest of the series is other methods for doing the same job when you can't randomize.

4

Worked all the way through: the lamp

Plain English. Take a relationship you're sure about, and see how many alternatives still need killing.

Worked example. Does pulling the string cause the lamp to turn on? Collect data: pull, light on. Pull, off. Pull, on again. Strong relationship.

Identification means not taking that at face value. So:

Alternative 1 — the pulls are timed. Maybe Nick isn't causing anything; maybe he's timing his pulls to moments the light was going to change anyway, using some other knowledge about the lamp. That would produce this exact data with no causal effect at all.

How to kill it: hand the decision to a coin. Heads, pull; tails, don't. Flip — heads, pull, light changes. Flip again — heads, pull, light changes. Now the timing wasn't Nick's choice, it was the coin's, and the coin has no knowledge of the lamp. The relationship survives, which is evidence the string is doing the work.

Alternative 2 — wrong mechanism. Maybe the string controls all the electricity in the house. The string genuinely causes the lamp to change, but by cutting power to everything, which is a different process than the one you claimed.

How to kill it: pull the string and check whether other things in the house switch on and off at the same time. They don't. Alternative pushed aside.

Where it breaks. This is a lamp. If the alternatives run to two for a lamp, imagine the list for education and earnings — you could sit and generate them all day. The list gets longer as the setting gets more complex, and that's the actual job.

5

When you're done

Plain English. Alternatives all cleared, relationship still there — now you've got something.

Precisely. You've identified the causal effect when you can show that in the data you're analysing, every alternative explanation has either been ruled out as not happening or had its influence removed by conditioning or design — and a relationship remains. At that point you know your analysis answers your research question.

Anchor image. A police lineup. You don't prove who did it by pointing. You clear everyone who couldn't have.

Where it breaks. "Every alternative explanation" is doing enormous work in that sentence — you can only eliminate the ones you thought of. Most of the remaining videos in the series exist because doing this properly, in real settings, is hard and situation-specific.

Say it out loud

Here's the thing that makes causal inference hard. You want to know: if I reached in and changed this thing, would that other thing change? But data never shows you that. Data only ever shows you that two things tend to go together.

So you flip the problem around. Instead of trying to see the effect, you ask: what are all the other reasons these two could be related? And then you kill them off one by one.

Take education and earnings. People with more schooling earn more — sure. But maybe people who were going to be great workers anyway are the ones who go get more education. Maybe people in rich areas both get more schooling and earn more because of where they live. Maybe someone with natural engineering talent thinks "engineering school sounds good", so the schooling is just riding along with the talent.

Now — that doesn't mean education doesn't raise earnings. It probably does. It just means the effect is buried in there with everything else, and you have to dig it out.

The cleanest tool is randomization. If a coin decided who got more education, then "people who'd earn more anyway chose more schooling" literally can't have happened in your data. The alternative doesn't get adjusted for, it gets erased.

Let me show you with something dumb. Does pulling this string turn on the lamp? I pull, it goes on. Off. On. Strong relationship. But maybe I'm just timing my pulls to when I know it'll change anyway. So — coin flip. Heads I pull, tails I don't. Still works. That explanation's dead. Or maybe the string controls all the electricity in the house; so I check whether anything else switches at the same time. It doesn't. Dead too.

That's a lamp and it took two. Now imagine the list for education and earnings.

Recall drill

QState the definition of causality being used here.

If you reached in and changed the value of one variable, would the other change as a result?

QWhat can you actually observe in data, as opposed to what you want?

You observe a relationship — that the conditional mean of Y differs across values of X. You want to know whether changing X changes Y, which the relationship alone doesn't tell you.

QGive three alternative explanations for the education–earnings relationship.

People who'd be better workers anyway choose more education; people in richer areas both get more education and earn more; people with natural aptitude for a high-paying field go to school for it, so schooling rides along with aptitude.

QDoes an alternative explanation mean your hypothesised effect is absent?

No. The effect may be entirely real — it's just mixed in with other things, and you have to isolate it.

QWhy does randomization eliminate alternatives rather than merely adjust for them?

Because assignment was made by a random device, so nothing about the people could have influenced who got treated. The alternative never occurred in your data.

QPulling a string is strongly related to a lamp turning on. Give an alternative explanation and the fix.

Nick might be timing his pulls to when the light was going to change anyway. Fix: let a coin decide whether to pull — the coin has no knowledge of the lamp — and see if the relationship survives.

QGive the second lamp alternative and its fix.

The string might control all the electricity in the house, so it changes the lamp via a different mechanism. Fix: pull the string and check whether other things in the house switch on and off at the same time.

QWhat besides randomization can close off an alternative explanation?

Conditioning on a variable — the conditional conditional distributions from Ep 8 — plus the many other designs covered later in the series.

QWhen have you identified a causal effect?

When every alternative explanation has been ruled out or had its influence removed, and a relationship still remains — so you know the analysis answers your research question.

QWhy isn't a very strong relationship evidence of causality?

Because alternative explanations can produce near-perfect relationships. The timed-pulls story generates a strong relationship with no causal effect at all.

Glossary

Alternative explanation
a data generating process that would produce the relationship you observed without the causal effect you're claiming.
Randomization
assigning treatment by a random device, so nothing about the observations can influence who is treated.
Identified
the state in which all alternative explanations have been eliminated or removed, so a remaining relationship answers your research question.
