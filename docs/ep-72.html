Ep 72Partial Identification in Matching with Rosenbaum Bounds

▶ Watch the video 14:00
On this pageThe 30-second versionWhy this existsThe ideasTrapsSay it out loudRecall drillConnectionsGlossary

If your matching worked, you shouldn't be able to tell which member of a matched pair got treated — so instead of assuming it's a perfect coin flip, assume it's no worse than 60/40, and ask how badly mismatched your pairs would have to be before your result disappears.

The 30-second version

  • Matching's assumption: your matched pair is so alike that you couldn't guess which one was treated.
  • Rosenbaum bounds weaken that: it needn't be exactly 50/50, just no worse than some bound — for every pair in your data.
  • Gamma (Γ) is that bound, the worst mismatch you'll tolerate. It brackets the treatment probability between Γ/(1+Γ) and 1/(1+Γ).
  • Procedure: raise Γ until you can no longer reject zero. Then ask whether you believe the mismatch could really be that bad.
  • UK sentencing: the gender effect survives until Γ ≈ 1.5 — meaning you'd have to distinguish a matched man and woman more than 60% of the time using everything you never observed.
  • Caveat: Rosenbaum bounds only fit propensity score matching with matched pairs — which Chapter 14 told you not to do. Take the intuition; find another method to actually use.

Why this exists

Matching rests on a strong assumption. You pick matching variables, find weights or matched pairs, and then assume you've closed all the back doors — that you matched on the full set of variables needed, so your matched pair is perfectly matched. If someone asked you to guess which of the two was treated, you wouldn't be able to tell.

Worked example — the propensity score version. You have a treated observation with a propensity score of 0.25: a 25% chance of being treated, and they happened to be treated. You match them with someone else whose propensity score is also 0.25. Each had a 25% chance of treatment. So if you look at the pair without knowing, you genuinely can't tell which one it was.

That's exactly what you want — it's essentially random which of the two is treated, and they're comparable otherwise.

But that's a strong assumption. Probably you've left something out. And you don't want to respond by saying well, there's some bias left, I give up, I'm going home. So go somewhere in the middle: accept that you haven't perfectly removed all the back doors, then work out a reasonable range of assumptions about how much bias remains — and get a range of estimates instead of a point estimate.

The ideas

1

A caveat before starting

Plain English. This method only fits a setup the matching chapter told you to avoid.

Precisely. By far the most common way to do partial identification in matching is Rosenbaum bounds — and there's a problem with them: they're very much designed for one specific context. You're doing propensity score matching and you're picking sets of matches rather than doing inverse probability weighting.

Which is exactly what Chapter 14 said not to do. If you're going to use propensity scores, use inverse probability weighting; if you're going to pick matched pairs, don't use propensity scores to do it.

But Rosenbaum bounds are definitely the most common approach, they're really intuitively good for conveying how this works, and a lot of the concepts carry through to other matching approaches. So: take the intuition from here, and if you're actually going to do it, go to the chapter and look at other sources for approaches that don't rely on picking sets of matches with propensity scores.

2

The idea: how well could you guess?

Plain English. If matching worked, guessing which one was treated is a coin flip. If you can do better than a coin flip, you have bias.

Precisely. You're working with matched pairs, and the idea is they're perfectly matched: no other relevant information would tell you which one is treated. Someone could tell you everything else about these two people and it wouldn't give you a clue.

But maybe it would. You matched on a bunch of things — but if someone told you one new thing about them, perhaps that would tell you which one was treated. You might guess right more than half the time. It's not a coin flip anymore.

If two people truly have the same propensity — 25% each — you shouldn't be able to tell. It should be a complete coin flip.

And if you can tell, you have bias. Because if one of them really is more likely to be treated than the other, you haven't actually matched well on propensity score. There's something left over — especially damaging if the omitted thing is also related to the outcome, meaning it sits on a back door.

Anchor image. A blind taste test that works only if you genuinely can't tell the glasses apart. The moment one has a chip in the rim, it isn't blind anymore.

3

What Rosenbaum bounds do about it

Plain English. Don't assume it's exactly 50/50. Assume it's no worse than 60/40.

Precisely. Rosenbaum bounds say: I know it may not be exactly 50/50 — but I'll put a bound on how bad the mismatch might get.

Where typical matching assumes no bias at all — zero deviation in the true propensity between the two members of a pair — you instead say: I don't think it's exactly 50/50, but I'm going to say it's no worse than 60/40. Or 55/45, or 70/30 — whatever bound you consider reasonable.

If it's no worse than that, you can still narrow down your treatment effect estimate. Not to a single number — to a range of reasonable values, spanning the potential bias from zero up to the maximum amount you're willing to consider.

And note how demanding this is. You're making that assumption for every single pair in your data. If you say no worse than 60/40, you're claiming that for literally every matched pair in your sample there is no additional information that would let anyone beat 60/40 at identifying which one was treated.

4

Gamma

Plain English. One number for the worst mismatch you'll allow anywhere.

Precisely. The range of estimates is bounded by gamma (Γ) — the worst level of mismatch you're going to allow among any pair in your data.

Set it up and you get a bound. The middle term involves π_t, the probability of treatment for the member of the pair who actually is treated, and π_u, the probability of treatment for the member who is actually untreated.

  • If those two probabilities are exactly the same, that middle term works out to exactly 50%.
  • If the treated one was in fact somewhat more likely to be treated, it comes out above 50%.
  • If the treated one was actually less likely to be treated, it comes out below 50%.

And you bound it between:

    Γ / (1 + Γ)      on the high end
    1 / (1 + Γ)      on the low end

Assume no mismatch worse than that, and you can bound your estimate. You're effectively saying: there's this much bias in there, I don't know which direction it runs, but it's no worse than this — which means my estimate has to lie in this range.

5

How you actually use it

Plain English. Turn Γ up until your result dies, then ask whether things could really be that bad.

Precisely. Try a number of different gamma values and see at what level the mismatch becomes so bad that you can no longer reject zero for your treatment effect.

You estimated an effect. You want to know how bad the mismatch would have to be to get rid of it. And then ask yourself: am I willing to believe the mismatch is no worse than that?

6

Worked example: gender and sentencing again

Precisely. The same setting as the previous episode — data on people accused of crimes, typically drug crimes, in the United Kingdom. The question: is there a gender bias in the rate at which people accused of crimes actually go to prison?

The matching variables include socioeconomic status, the kind of drug you're accused of working with, and whether you have children at home to take care of.

Why bias is plausible. Some of these things might be related to gender. Within a matched pair, some unobserved variable might tell you which one is the man and which the woman — which is a mismatch, since you're trying to match men to similar women. And it might also relate to whether you go to prison. That produces a biased result.

The finding. There is a gender bias: men are more likely to go to prison for the same crime than a similar woman with similar levels of all those matching variables.

But something might be left out — and maybe the whole effect is just bias. How much bias would there need to be for it all to go away?

The answer: the effect goes away at roughly Γ = 1.5. (The exact value depends on the matching procedure you use; this is with the defaults set here.)

What that actually means. Run it through the equation and Γ = 1.5 works out to not being able to tell them apart more than 60% of the time.

So: take a man matched with a woman who is similar on every characteristic you do observe. Then magically learn about all the stuff you never saw.

If that new information lets you tell them apart…Then
More than 60% of the timeThat's enough bias to explain the entire effect
Only 55% of the timeThat would reduce your estimated effect, but not take it to zero

That's the question being answered: at what point does my effect go away? How robust is my result?

7

The closing reframing

Plain English. You don't have to claim perfection, and you don't have to give up. You just have to say what you'd defend.

Precisely. With partial identification you ask what assumptions you actually believe. You don't have to assume the pairs are exactly perfectly matched. You also don't have to say they're not perfectly matched, there's bias here, so I'm giving up and going home.

Instead: how well matched am I willing to claim they are?

  • Believe you couldn't tell them apart more than 55% of the time, even knowing everything unobserved → your original estimate is probably good to go, and there probably is gender bias in this sentencing procedure.
  • Believe you could tell them apart more often than that → you can't demonstrate gender bias. There's simply too much bias in the estimate to distinguish it. You can't see it.

Is that a reasonable assumption? It depends — and you still have to argue for it. But you at least know what you're arguing about. You no longer have to give up because some bias exists, and you no longer have to make the probably-false assumption that you matched everybody perfectly with nothing left over.

It's just: what's the strongest argument you can make? What's the reasonable claim you can reach, based on the things you think are actually true about the world? And that is the bonus of partial identification.

Say it out loud

Matching makes one big promise: if you handed me a matched pair and asked which one got treated, I couldn't tell you. Both had a 25% chance. It's a coin flip. That's what "well matched" means.

But of course you didn't match on everything. So imagine I now whisper to you every single thing about those two people that you never measured. Could you guess better than a coin flip? Probably a bit. And that's your bias, measured in guesses.

Rosenbaum bounds turn that into the analysis. Instead of assuming it's exactly 50/50 — which is almost certainly false — you assume it's no worse than 60/40. Or 55/45. Whatever you'd defend. And crucially, you're assuming that for every single pair in your data.

That bound is called gamma. And the way you use it is lovely: turn gamma up — 1.1, 1.2, 1.3 — and watch your result. At some point the possible bias gets big enough that you can't rule out zero anymore. That's your answer.

Back to the UK sentencing question — are men more likely to be imprisoned than similar women? The effect survives until gamma of about 1.5. And when you convert that, here's what it means: if learning everything you never observed would let you tell the man from the woman more than 60% of the time, that's enough hidden bias to explain the whole result. If it'd only get you to 55%, your finding shrinks but survives.

So the question stops being statistical and becomes something you can actually reason about: do I believe my matched pairs are that badly mismatched?

And that's the whole gift of partial identification, and a fine place to end. You never had to claim you'd matched perfectly. You never had to walk away because you hadn't. You just had to say what you honestly believe about the world — and see how far it takes you.

Recall drill

QWhat does matching assume about a matched pair?

That they're so well matched on everything relevant that you couldn't guess which one was treated.

QGive the propensity score version of that.

Two people each with a propensity score of 0.25 — each had a 25% chance of treatment, so which one actually got treated should be indistinguishable.

QWhat's the caveat about Rosenbaum bounds?

They're designed for propensity score matching where you pick matched pairs — which the matching chapter advises against. Take the intuition, but use other methods in practice.

QWhy does Nick cover them anyway?

They're by far the most common approach, they're very intuitive for conveying the idea, and the concepts carry over to other matching approaches.

QHow does the method detect bias?

By asking whether additional, unobserved information would let you guess which member of a pair was treated better than a coin flip.

QWhy does being able to guess indicate bias?

Because it means one member really was more likely to be treated, so you didn't match well — and if that omitted thing also relates to the outcome, it sits on an open back door.

QWhat does a Rosenbaum bound assume instead of perfect matching?

That the mismatch is no worse than some specified bound — for instance no worse than 60/40 — rather than exactly 50/50.

QHow broadly does that assumption apply?

To every single matched pair in the sample, not on average.

QWhat is gamma?

The bound on the worst level of mismatch you're willing to allow among any pair in your data.

QWhat do π_t and π_u represent?

The probability of treatment for the pair member who actually is treated, and for the one who actually is untreated.

QWhat happens to the middle term when those probabilities are equal?

It works out to exactly 50%.

QGive the upper and lower bounds in terms of gamma.

Γ/(1+Γ) on the high end and 1/(1+Γ) on the low end.

QDescribe the practical procedure.

Try a range of gamma values and find the level at which the possible mismatch is bad enough that you can no longer reject zero — then judge whether mismatch that severe is believable.

QIn the UK sentencing study, at what gamma does the effect disappear?

Roughly 1.5, though the exact value depends on the matching procedure used.

QTranslate that gamma into plain terms.

It corresponds to being able to tell a matched man and woman apart more than 60% of the time using everything you never observed.

QWhat if you could only tell them apart 55% of the time?

The estimated effect would shrink but wouldn't be reduced to zero.

QState the closing reframing of partial identification.

You needn't claim perfect matching, and you needn't give up because bias exists. You state how well matched you're willing to claim they are, and see what follows.

QWhat does partial identification leave you still having to do?

Argue for whether your assumption is reasonable — but now you know exactly what you're arguing about.

Glossary

Rosenbaum bounds
a sensitivity analysis for matched-pair designs bounding how badly matched pairs could be before a result disappears.
Gamma (Γ)
the parameter bounding the worst permitted mismatch between members of a matched pair.
Matched pair
two observations, one treated and one not, selected as comparable.
Sensitivity analysis
determining how strong an unobserved problem would need to be to overturn a finding.
