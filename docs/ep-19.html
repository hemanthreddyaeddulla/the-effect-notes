Ep 19Finding Front Doors

▶ Watch the video 9:00
On this pageThe 30-second versionWhy this existsThe ideasTrapsSay it out loudRecall drillConnectionsGlossary

Instead of hunting down and closing every back door — which requires a diagram everyone agrees on and the ability to measure everything on it — find some source of random or random-ish exogenous variation driving your treatment, isolate the part of treatment it drives, and you've built yourself an experiment without running one.

The 30-second version

  • Closing back doors has three hard requirements: build the whole diagram, get others to agree it's right, and measure everything you need to control for.
  • The alternative: pick out the front door directly rather than eliminating every back door.
  • A randomized experiment does this. Randomize the treatment and no other factor can be driving it, so every back door is gone without measuring anything.
  • Crucially, you then use only the randomized data — not everyone in the world.
  • Can't randomize? Look for exogenous variation: something that drives treatment and has no back doors to the outcome. That's exactly the structure randomization creates.
  • Sometimes someone else already randomized for you — the Vietnam draft lottery.
  • You then isolate, statistically, the part of treatment driven by that variation.

Why this exists

The last few chapters gave you a complete procedure: list the alternative explanations, draw them as paths, close the bad ones by controlling for things. It works. It's also very hard to actually pull off.

Three problems, in order:

  1. Constructing the entire causal diagram is difficult.
  2. Even if you construct one, getting other people to agree it's the correct diagram — and therefore that you really do know the full set of back doors — is another matter.
  3. Even if everyone agreed, can you measure everything you'd need to control for? Often not.

Picking the right controls is easy once you have the diagram. The trouble is knowing the diagram is right and being able to measure what it demands. So it's worth having another route entirely.

The ideas

1

Grab the front door instead of closing every back door

Plain English. You've got front door paths you want and back door paths you don't. Rather than shutting each unwanted one, pick out the wanted one directly.

Precisely. Instead of enumerating and closing every back door from treatment to outcome, isolate the portion of the treatment–outcome relationship that runs through front doors.

Anchor image. A noisy room. One approach is to find and silence every other conversation. The other is a directional microphone pointed at the one you want.

2

Why randomized experiments work

Plain English. A back door exists because something else is pushing your treatment around. Randomize the treatment and nothing else is.

Precisely. A back door path exists because some factor drives both treatment and outcome, producing a relationship in the data that isn't the effect. Randomize the treatment and that factor is no longer driving it — so its route to the outcome no longer matters. All back doors close at once, and you didn't measure a thing.

Worked example. Does wearing shorts make you eat more ice cream? In observed data, temperature drives both — on a hot day you wear shorts and you eat ice cream.

Now randomly assign people to wear shorts. Shorts-wearing is no longer determined by temperature. It doesn't matter that temperature still causes ice cream eating, because temperature has lost its grip on the treatment — at least in this data.

Where it breaks — and this is easy to miss: you must then limit yourself to the randomized data. You wouldn't randomize the treatment and then analyse your entire dataset. You wouldn't randomize shorts-wearing and then include both the people in your experiment and everyone outside it. You use the experiment.

Experiments have their own problems, which the book doesn't dig into. But they do solve this particular identification problem cleanly.

3

Exogenous variation: the generalisation

Plain English. You don't need actual randomization. You need something that behaves like it.

Precisely. Exogenous variation is variation that's essentially random with respect to your outcome. The requirement: something drives your treatment, and there are no back doors between that source and your outcome.

Anchor image. A coin doesn't have to be your coin. It just has to be a coin.

Worked example — why randomization satisfies this. In a randomized experiment, the only thing driving treatment is your randomization. Since you assigned it randomly, it's unrelated to the outcome in any other way. There's no back door from the coin flip to the outcome:

Anything that mimics that structure works just as well. You don't have to have done the randomizing yourself.

4

Someone else's randomization: the draft lottery

Plain English. The world sometimes randomizes things for its own reasons, and you can use it.

Worked example. A famous economics paper asks: does being in the military improve your later labour-market prospects? It could go either way. Maybe the military teaches you valuable skills and makes you a more effective worker, so you earn more. Or maybe it takes you away from years you'd have spent building a civilian career, so it hurts your prospects afterwards.

You can't randomize people into the military. Nobody would let you.

But during the Vietnam War the United States had a draft, and the way it worked was that the order of birth dates was randomized. Born on June 2nd? That lands early or late in the draft order, decided at random, and whether you got drafted depended on where you fell.

That's randomization — not done for research, but real. It pushes people into treatment, and your birth date should be largely unrelated to your later earnings. Maybe some minor things sneak in, and you can probably control for those. It doesn't have to be purely random; you just need to be able to close whatever other back doors exist.

5

Isolating the part of treatment the randomization drove

Plain English. Don't study everyone who served. Study the people who served because of the lottery.

Precisely. Exactly as with a real experiment, you restrict attention to the randomized variation. Most people who did or didn't go into the military weren't really "in the experiment" — they'd have made the same choice regardless.

Anchor image. A crowd, some pushed forward by one specific shove. You want the people who moved because of the shove, not everyone standing in the same direction.

Worked example. You want the people driven into service by their randomly assigned birth date. You can't point at them — you don't know who they are. But you can do it statistically: use statistics to identify who was driven into service by the random assignment, then look at the relationship between that random portion of treatment and earnings.

Do that and you've effectively found a little group inside your data for which a random experiment was running, even though you never controlled it. That erases the back doors and leaves the front doors — identification, without ever measuring the confounders.

Where it breaks. There are many specific ways to do this and many contexts where it comes up, which is most of the remaining series. The general shape is what matters here: find exogenous, random-ish variation with either no back doors or back doors you can easily shut.

Say it out loud

Everything so far said: find all the back doors, close them. And it works — but look at what it actually demands. You have to build the entire causal diagram. You have to convince everyone else it's the right one. And then you have to be able to measure every single thing you need to control for. That last one alone kills most studies.

So here's the other approach. Instead of shutting every back door, just grab the front door directly.

The version you already know is a randomized experiment. And think about why it works. A back door exists because something else is pushing your treatment around. Does wearing shorts make you eat ice cream? In the data, temperature drives both. But if I randomly assign who wears shorts, temperature isn't driving shorts anymore. It doesn't matter that it still causes ice cream — it's lost its grip on my treatment. Every back door closes at once and I measured nothing.

One catch people miss: you then use only the experiment. You don't randomize some people and then analyse the whole world.

Now — what if you can't randomize? You look for exogenous variation: something that drives your treatment and has no back door to your outcome. That's the exact structure randomization creates, so anything with the same shape works.

Best example. Does military service help or hurt your later earnings? Nobody's letting you randomize that. But during Vietnam the US draft randomized the order of birth dates — where your birthday landed determined whether you got drafted. Nobody did that for research, and your birthday shouldn't have much to do with your later earnings.

So you don't compare everyone who served to everyone who didn't — most of them were never in the experiment. You find the people who ended up serving because of that random birth date. You can't point at them, but you can pull them out statistically. And now you've got an experiment running inside your data that nobody ever ran.

Recall drill

QName the three difficulties with the close-all-back-doors approach.

Constructing the entire causal diagram is hard; getting others to agree it's correct is hard; and you may not be able to measure everything you'd need to control for.

QWhat's the alternative strategy?

Isolate the front door paths directly rather than enumerating and closing every back door.

QExplain why randomizing the treatment closes all back doors.

A back door exists because some factor drives both treatment and outcome. Randomize the treatment and that factor no longer drives it, so its route to the outcome no longer produces a relationship.

QYou randomize shorts-wearing to study ice cream eating. What must you do with your data?

Use only the randomized data — the people in your experiment — not everyone in the dataset.

QDefine exogenous variation.

Variation that drives your treatment and has no back doors to your outcome — essentially random with respect to the outcome.

QWhy does a randomized experiment satisfy that definition?

The only thing driving treatment is your randomization, which you did at random, so there's no back door from the coin flip to the outcome.

QWhat question does the Vietnam draft lottery study answer, and why can't it be done directly?

Whether military service improves later labour-market earnings. You can't randomly assign people to the military — nobody would permit it.

QHow does the draft lottery supply exogenous variation?

The order of birth dates was randomized, and where your birthday fell in that order determined whether you were drafted — a real randomization, done for non-research reasons, and largely unrelated to later earnings.

QDoes exogenous variation have to be perfectly random?

No. It needs no back doors to the outcome, or back doors you can close. Minor imperfections you can control for are acceptable.

QWhy not compare everyone who served in the military to everyone who didn't?

Most of them weren't in the experiment — they'd have made the same choice regardless. You want the people driven into service by the random assignment.

QHow do you identify those people?

Not by hand — you don't know who they are. You use statistics to isolate the portion of treatment driven by the random assignment, then relate that portion to the outcome.

QGive both arguments for how military service might affect earnings.

It might teach valuable skills making you a more effective worker, raising earnings. Or it might take you away from years you'd have spent developing a civilian career, hurting your prospects.

variation in the treatment that has no back doors to the outcome; essentially random with respect to it.
Randomized controlled experiment
a design where the researcher assigns treatment at random, closing all back doors by construction.
Draft lottery
the Vietnam-era randomization of birth-date order, used as exogenous variation in military service.
