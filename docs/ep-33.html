Ep 33Your Standard Errors are Wrong

▶ Watch the video 12:00
On this pageThe 30-second versionWhy this existsThe ideasTrapsSay it out loudRecall drillConnectionsGlossary

The standard error formula isn't a definition — it's a calculation resting on assumptions about the error term, and when those fail (heteroskedasticity, autocorrelation, clustering) your standard errors stop measuring the sampling distribution, which is the only thing they were for.

The 30-second version

  • Good news first: when these assumptions fail, your coefficient is usually fine. It's the standard error that breaks.
  • Normality of errors is the least of your worries — and it's about the error term, not your variables.
  • The real assumption is that errors are i.i.d.: identically distributed and independent.
  • Not identically distributed → heteroskedasticity. The variance of the error is related to X.
  • Not independent → autocorrelation (repeated measures over time) or clustering (hierarchical data).
  • Why it breaks the formula: σ² is meant to capture error variance, and you measure it badly if you ignore these patterns.
  • Fix with sandwich estimators — same formula, σ² and the variance of X computed to allow for the problem.
  • Many economists just always use heteroskedasticity-robust errors. Nick: seems okay to him.

Why this exists

Bad news: your standard errors are probably wrong and you need to fix them. This is a scourge of the applied econometrician — or anyone doing causal inference — and you will have to deal with it.

Here's the issue. A standard error is the standard deviation of the sampling distribution of your coefficient, and you need it because it tells you how much you actually know — how precise a claim you can make. Ep 26 gave you a formula for it. But that formula is not the definition of the standard deviation of an OLS coefficient. It's a calculation based on assumptions. If the assumptions are wrong, the calculation no longer reflects the standard deviation of the sampling distribution — which is the only reason you computed it.

Reassuringly: when these assumptions fail, the main casualty is your estimate of the standard error. Your OLS coefficient will usually be fine. But you still have to get the standard errors right.

The ideas

1

Assumption 1: normally distributed errors — mostly ignorable

Plain English. It's about the errors, not your variables. And even then it barely matters.

Precisely. In y = β₀ + β₁x + ε, one assumption is that ε follows a normal distribution.

The widespread misunderstanding: many people take this to mean your variables — your X's and Y's — must be normally distributed. They don't. The assumption is only about the error term.

And even past that, it doesn't matter much. If your errors aren't normal, what breaks is that you can no longer prove the OLS coefficient is normally distributed. It might follow some other distribution — but it's usually pretty close to normal anyway, unless your errors are really, really weird, in which case you have bigger problems than non-normality.

Anchor image. A warning light for a fault that almost never grounds the plane.

2

Assumption 2: i.i.d. errors — the one that matters

Plain English. Every error comes from the same distribution, and knowing one tells you nothing about another.

Precisely. Independent and identically distributed.

  • Identically distributed — your error term, my error term, everyone's error term follows the same distribution.
  • Independent — they're uncorrelated. Knowing my error term tells you nothing about that person's.

Three common ways this fails.

3

Failure 1: heteroskedasticity

Plain English. The spread of the errors changes as X changes.

Precisely. Heteroskedasticity is when the variance of the error term is related to your predictors X. Note the subtlety: you already assumed X is unrelated to the error term itself — but that doesn't mean its variance can't still be related to X.

Anchor image. A river that's narrow and calm at the source and wide and choppy at the mouth. Same river, different behaviour along its length.

Worked example. The classic "fan" shape: at low values of X there's very little spread around the line — the variance of the residuals, and so probably of the errors, is small. As you move to high values of X, things spread out; the variance grows.

Where it breaks. Not all heteroskedastic data looks like a fan. That's one example; other shapes occur too.

4

Failure 2: autocorrelation

Plain English. Anything measured repeatedly over time correlates with itself nearby in time.

Precisely. With time series data (one observation tracked over time) or panel data (many individuals each measured repeatedly), observations are likely correlated with their nearest periods in time.

Anchor image. Today's weather is a decent guess for tomorrow's. Errors behave the same way.

Worked example. Fit an OLS line to the unemployment rate over time and it looks bad immediately — partly because there's shape you haven't accounted for, but also because of autocorrelation. Pick any point on the graph: the points on either side of it are likely closer to it than other observations in the data are. If you're above the line this period, you're likely above it next period too.

And being above the line is the residual — how far above you are is the residual, which is your representation of the error term. So the error terms are correlated with each other across time.

Where it breaks. This shows up essentially any time you have a measure that is repeatedly measured.

5

Failure 3: clustering

Plain English. Data collected in groups has errors that come in groups.

Precisely. When data is collected hierarchically, observations within a group resemble each other more than they resemble observations in other groups — and so do their residuals, and so do their errors.

Anchor image. Families at a restaurant. Knowing what one person ordered tells you a lot about their table and nothing about the next one.

Worked example. Students taking a test. Students at the same school probably have more similar scores than students at different schools. And within a school, students in the same classroom — sharing a teacher — are likely more similar still. Once you fit a model and look at the residuals, those cluster too: this classroom's errors are closer to each other than to another classroom's.

So if I learn about your error term, that tells me something about the error terms of everyone in your class. The errors are correlated.

6

Why any of this breaks the formula

Plain English. The formula's top term is supposed to measure error variance, and you'll measure it badly if you ignore the structure.

Precisely. Look back at the standard error equation. σ² on top is a measure of prediction error — the variance of the error terms. You won't do a good job measuring that if you don't account for heteroskedasticity, clustering, or autocorrelation. Just taking the overall variance of the errors while ignoring those relationships leaves you ignorant about how sampling variation is actually going to behave. You may also get weirdness in the variance of X term.

So the calculation needs adjusting to be aware that these assumptions have failed.

7

The fix: sandwich estimators

Plain English. Same formula, different arithmetic for the pieces that broke.

Precisely. A sandwich estimator performs the same calculation you already have, except it computes σ² in a slightly different way that accounts for the problem, and adjusts the variance of X term similarly.

There's one for each failure, named unsurprisingly:

ProblemFix
HeteroskedasticityHeteroskedasticity-robust standard errors
Autocorrelation (with heteroskedasticity)Heteroskedasticity- and autocorrelation-consistent (HAC) standard errors
ClusteringCluster-robust standard errors

They don't do anything wild — they take the calculation you already have and compute σ² and the variance of X in ways that allow for the problem. The mechanics are in the book, or on Wikipedia.

Worked example — what the heteroskedasticity-robust version is doing. Go back to the fan graph. At low X there's little variance in the error; at high X there's a lot. When computing the standard error, the estimator inflates the influence of those high-X observations, because it knows there's a lot of variance over there, and shrinks the influence of the low-X ones. The result is an estimate of error variance that better matches the sampling variation you'd actually get from a graph shaped like that.

Where it breaks. A lot of economists pretty much always use heteroskedasticity-robust standard errors, just assuming it's always going to be a problem. Nick's verdict: he doesn't know that that's necessarily wrong — it seems okay to him.

Say it out loud

I have bad news: your standard errors are probably wrong.

Here's why. A standard error is the standard deviation of your coefficient's sampling distribution — it's what tells you how much you actually know. But the formula for it isn't a definition. It's a calculation that rests on assumptions. Break the assumptions and it stops measuring the thing it exists to measure.

Small relief first: when this happens, your coefficient is usually fine. It's the standard error that's broken.

One assumption you can mostly ignore: that the errors are normally distributed. And note — the errors, not your variables. People constantly think their X's and Y's need to be normal. They don't. And even if the errors aren't normal, you just lose the proof that your coefficient is normal; it's usually close anyway.

The one that matters is that errors are i.i.d. — same distribution, and uncorrelated with each other. Three ways that fails.

Heteroskedasticity: the variance of the error is related to X. Classic fan shape — tight around the line at low X, spraying out at high X. And note the subtlety: you assumed X is unrelated to the error term, but that doesn't stop it being related to the error's variance.

Autocorrelation: anything measured over time. Look at the unemployment rate — if you're above the line this period, you're probably above it next period too. And "above the line" is your residual. So your errors are correlated across time.

Clustering: students in the same classroom have similar test scores, so they have similar residuals. Tell me your error term and I know something about your classmates'.

Why does this wreck the formula? Because σ² on top is supposed to measure the variance of the errors — and you'll measure that badly if you compute it as though there's no structure there.

The fix is a sandwich estimator, which sounds exotic and isn't: it's the same formula, just computing σ² and the variance of X in a way that allows for the problem. There's a version for each. And honestly a lot of economists just always use heteroskedasticity-robust errors on the assumption it's always a problem — which seems reasonable enough.

Recall drill

QWhy can the standard error formula be wrong?

It isn't the definition of the sampling distribution's standard deviation — it's a calculation resting on assumptions about the error term. If those fail, it stops reflecting the sampling distribution.

QWhen these assumptions fail, what breaks and what survives?

The estimate of the standard error breaks. The OLS coefficient is usually fine.

QWhat exactly does the normality assumption apply to?

The error term ε — not your X and Y variables, which is the common misunderstanding.

QWhat actually goes wrong if errors aren't normally distributed?

You can no longer prove the OLS coefficient is normally distributed. It may follow another distribution, but usually one close to normal unless the errors are very strange.

QWhat does i.i.d. stand for, and what does each part mean?

Independent and identically distributed. Identically distributed: every error follows the same distribution. Independent: they're uncorrelated, so knowing one tells you nothing about another.

QDefine heteroskedasticity.

When the variance of the error term is related to your predictors X.

QYou assumed X is unrelated to the error term. Why doesn't that rule out heteroskedasticity?

Because X being unrelated to the error term itself doesn't prevent it from being related to the error term's variance.

QDescribe the fan shape.

At low values of X the residuals have little spread; as X increases the spread grows. It's one example of heteroskedasticity, not the only shape.

QWhat is autocorrelation and where does it arise?

Errors correlated with each other across time — arising in time series data or panel data, and essentially any time you have a repeatedly measured value.

QUse the unemployment example to explain why errors are autocorrelated.

If you're above the fitted line this period, you're likely above it next period too. Being above the line is the residual, which represents the error term — so errors correlate across time.

QWhat is clustering, and give the example.

Hierarchically collected data producing grouped errors. Students at the same school have more similar scores than students at different schools, and within a school, students sharing a classroom and teacher are more similar still — so their residuals and errors cluster.

QWhy do these failures break the standard error calculation?

σ² is supposed to measure the variance of the error terms, and computing it while ignoring heteroskedasticity, autocorrelation, or clustering measures it badly — leaving you ignorant of how sampling variation actually behaves. The variance of X term can also misbehave.

QWhat is a sandwich estimator doing?

The same standard error calculation, but computing σ² and the variance of X in ways that account for the specific problem.

QName the three fixes.

Heteroskedasticity-robust standard errors; heteroskedasticity- and autocorrelation-consistent (HAC) standard errors; and cluster-robust standard errors.

QHow does the heteroskedasticity-robust version handle the fan graph?

It inflates the influence of the high-X observations, where variance is large, and shrinks the influence of the low-X ones — giving an error-variance estimate closer to the sampling variation you'd really get.

QWhat do many economists do by default?

Always use heteroskedasticity-robust standard errors, assuming it's always a problem — which Nick considers reasonable.
