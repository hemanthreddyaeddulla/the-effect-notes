Ep 21Treatment Effects

▶ Watch the video 12:00
On this pageThe 30-second versionWhy this existsThe ideasTrapsSay it out loudRecall drillConnectionsGlossary

There is no such thing as the effect — every individual has their own treatment effect, so any single number you estimate is an average of individual effects, and the whole question becomes which average, over whom.

The 30-second version

  • The title of the book is a lie. Effects vary by person, context, and time.
  • A cervical cancer drug can't reduce Nick's risk by 1%. He doesn't have a cervix — his risk is 0% with or without.
  • Even among people who can get cervical cancer, effects differ by age, race, background, and baseline risk. That's heterogeneous treatment effects, and it's true essentially everywhere.
  • Two responses: estimate the full distribution of effects (personalized medicine — Chapter 21), or estimate one number and work out what it represents.
  • Any single estimate is an average of individual effects. Over everybody? Over a group? Weighted?
  • Average treatment effect (ATE) = the average over everybody. Often described as the one you want — often it isn't.
  • Rule of thumb 1: full randomization in a representative sample → ATE.

Why this exists

Every chapter so far has been about identifying "the causal effect of X on Y." Time to admit that phrase doesn't refer to anything.

Effects vary. The same treatment does different things to different people, in different contexts, at different times. Which means once you've done all the identification work and produced a number, you have a second question waiting: whose effect is this? And the answer determines what you're allowed to do with it.

The ideas

1

There is no the effect

Plain English. A drug doesn't have one effect. It has a different effect for every person it's given to.

Precisely. Individual treatment effects vary across people. A single reported effect is some aggregation of them.

Anchor image. "How long does the commute take?" There's no such thing. There's your commute, and mine, and the one at 3am.

Worked example. A drug reduces the rate of cervical cancer by, say, 1% on average.

Nick doesn't have a cervix. His chance of cervical cancer is 0%, and it will still be 0% after taking the drug. So the drug cannot possibly reduce his risk by 1%. His individual treatment effect is exactly zero.

So already there are at least two effects: one for people who have a cervix to get cancer in, and a different one for people who don't.

But why stop there? Among people who do have a cervix, why would the effect be identical? Older versus younger, different races, different backgrounds, different baseline risk levels. For one person it reduces risk by nothing, for another by 1%, another by half a percent, another by 2%.

Every individual person has a different causal effect.

Where it breaks. This is one of the things making social science and causal inference harder than the physical sciences — you're chasing a different effect for every person.

2

Heterogeneous treatment effects

Plain English. The name for "the treatment does different things to different people."

Precisely. Heterogeneous treatment effects exist when a treatment has different effects for different individuals. There is essentially always at least a little heterogeneity in any effect, anywhere.

Where it breaks. Sometimes you can ignore it — if you think it doesn't differ much across people. Sometimes you need to think hard about what it means.

3

Two ways to deal with it

Plain English. Map the whole distribution of effects, or report one number and be honest about what it is.

Precisely.

  1. Estimate the full heterogeneity. Work out who the zeros are, who the 1%s are, who the 2%s are, and describe the whole distribution — enabling statements like "here is this pill's effect for you." That's what personalized medicine is after. Methods for it wait until Chapter 21.
  2. Estimate a single value, then determine what that value represents.

Worked example — why the second question matters. Your estimate for the cervical cancer drug comes back at 1%. What is that?

  • If it's the average over everybody, it's probably understating how good the drug is, because it's dragged down by all the Nicks whose effect is zero — and you'd never give them the pill anyway, so who cares that their effect is zero.
  • If it's the average among people with a cervix, that's a different and more useful number. And even then, is averaging over everyone with a cervix what you want?

If it's the overall figure, you should be more impressed by 1%, because it's being weighed down by all those zeros. Working out who you're getting the effect for is essential to knowing what you can do with it.

4

Every single estimate is an average

Plain English. Whatever number comes out, it took the true individual effects and averaged them somehow.

Precisely. If you produce one value, it aggregates the true individual effects — possibly over everyone, possibly over a particular group, possibly as a weighted average. So the question is always: what kind of average, and for whom?

Anchor image. A photograph of a crowd taken with a long exposure. It's a real image, and it's not any one person.

Worked example — individual effects you can't see.

PersonUntreated riskTreated riskIndividual effect
Nick0%0%0
Second person5%3%−2 percentage points
Third person0.8%0.3%−0.5 percentage points

Where it breaks. You can't observe any of these directly, because you can't observe the same person both getting and not getting the treatment. But a method can still tell you that your estimate averages Nick's effect with the second person's and the third's — even though you never see any of them individually.

5

The average treatment effect, and rule of thumb 1

Plain English. The ATE is the effect if you gave the treatment to everybody.

Precisely. The average treatment effect is the simplest and most straightforward aggregate: the average effect across everybody. It discards the heterogeneity and gives you one value, and that value is often well worth knowing.

Anchor image. The average temperature of a country. Genuinely informative. Also useless for deciding what to wear anywhere in particular.

Rule of thumb 1. A representative sample plus full randomization of the treatment within it — a randomized controlled trial, say — gives you an average treatment effect. You have randomization, and the group you randomized within is representative of the group you want to generalise to.

Where it breaks — two important caveats.

First, people will tell you the ATE is the kind of average you want. Not always true — it depends how you plan to use the result. For the cervical cancer drug you wouldn't want it, because it includes a lot of people you're certain you'll never give the pill to. You'd want the average effect among people who can actually get cervical cancer.

Second, the ATE is genuinely hard to come by. You need randomization within a representative group, which is hard even when running an RCT. A randomized trial of a selected group won't give you the overall ATE. Psychology has run many experiments meant to say something about human psychology in general, and recruited only college students. It's a real randomized experiment — but randomized only within that group, so it isn't the average treatment effect for the human population.

Note also that design is not destiny. For any given research design there are usually several estimators available that produce different kinds of treatment effect averages. These are rules of thumb.

Say it out loud

Time for the confession: the title of this book is a lie. There's no such thing as the effect.

Here's the cleanest way to see it. Say a drug reduces cervical cancer risk by one percent on average. I don't have a cervix. My risk is zero, and after the pill it's still zero. So the drug's effect on me is exactly nothing — it cannot be one percent.

And it doesn't stop there. Among people who do have a cervix, why would it be the same for everyone? Different ages, different backgrounds, different baseline risk. For one person it's zero, for another one percent, another half a percent, another two. Every single person has their own treatment effect. That's called heterogeneous treatment effects, and it's true of basically every effect, everywhere.

So you've got two options. Either try to estimate the whole distribution — that's what personalized medicine is chasing — or estimate one number and be very clear about what that number is.

Because any single number you produce is an average of individual effects. And which average changes everything. If that one percent averages over everybody, it's being dragged down by all the people like me whose effect is zero — people you'd never give the pill to anyway. So actually you should be more impressed by one percent, not less.

The one people know is the average treatment effect — the effect if you gave it to everyone. And you'll hear that's the one you want. Often it isn't! For this drug you'd rather have the effect among people who can actually get cervical cancer.

Rule of thumb: you get the ATE when you fully randomize in a representative sample. Which is harder than it sounds — psychology has run a lot of genuine randomized experiments about human nature using nothing but college students. Real randomization, real experiment. Just not the effect for humans.

Recall drill

QWhy is there no such thing as "the effect"?

Because individual treatment effects vary across people, contexts, and times. Any single number is an aggregation of many different effects.

QWhat is Nick's individual treatment effect from a cervical cancer drug, and why?

Zero. He doesn't have a cervix, so his risk is 0% both with and without the drug.

QDefine heterogeneous treatment effects.

When a treatment has different effects for different individuals. There's essentially always at least some heterogeneity in any effect.

QWhat are the two ways to handle heterogeneity?

Estimate the full distribution of individual effects (personalized medicine, Chapter 21), or estimate a single value and determine what it represents.

QA drug's estimated effect is 1%. Why might that understate how good it is?

If it averages over everybody, it includes all the people whose effect is zero — people you'd never treat anyway — dragging the average down.

QWhat does any single treatment effect estimate actually consist of?

An average of the true individual treatment effects, aggregated in some way — over everyone, over a group, or weighted.

QWhy can't you observe an individual treatment effect directly?

You can't observe the same person both getting and not getting the treatment.

QDefine the average treatment effect.

The average effect among everybody — what the average effect would be if the treatment were given to everyone.

QState rule of thumb 1.

A representative sample with full randomization of the treatment — such as a randomized controlled trial — gives you an average treatment effect.

QWhy is the ATE hard to come by even with a randomized trial?

You need randomization within a representative group. A trial run on a selected group gives the effect for that group, not the population.

QWhat's wrong with a randomized psychology experiment run on college students that claims to describe human psychology?

The randomization only happened within college students, so you get the effect for that group, not the average treatment effect for the human population.

QIs the ATE always the average you want?

No. It depends how you'll use the result. For the cervical cancer drug you'd want the effect among people who can actually get cervical cancer.

QWhat does "design is not destiny" mean here?

For any given research design there are usually several estimators available that produce different kinds of treatment effect average. These are rules of thumb, not laws.

Glossary

Treatment effect
the change in an individual's outcome caused by the treatment.
Individual treatment effect
one person's own effect; never directly observable.
Heterogeneous treatment effects
different individuals having different effects from the same treatment.
Average treatment effect (ATE)
the average effect across everybody.
Personalized medicine
the project of estimating an individual's own treatment effect.
