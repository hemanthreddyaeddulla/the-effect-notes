Ep 13Causal Diagrams

▶ Watch the video 14:45
On this pageThe 30-second versionWhy this existsThe ideasTrapsSay it out loudRecall drillConnectionsGlossary

A causal diagram is nothing more than a list of the variables relevant to your question plus arrows showing which causes which — and its value is that once it's drawn you can see the pathways connecting your two variables, sort them into the ones you want and the ones you don't, and go get rid of the rest.

The 30-second version

  • A causal diagram is a graph: variables as nodes, arrows meaning "this causes that". Literally that's all it is.
  • The variable is hair color, not brown hair. Brown is one of its values.
  • Arrows come from theory, not from the data.
  • One variable can cause many; one variable can be caused by many. Both are fine.
  • Include variables whether or not you can measure them — unmeasured causes still cause things. Grey them out, or use a placeholder like U1.
  • The payoff: a diagram shows you pathways. Some answer your question, some don't. Identification is keeping the first kind and killing the second.

Why this exists

You know you need the data generating process, and you know what an arrow means. Now you need to actually write one down.

Once it's on paper something useful happens that couldn't happen before: you stop seeing "X and Y are related" as one undifferentiated fact and start seeing the specific routes by which they're related. Some of those routes are your research question. Some are the alternative explanations from Ep 10. Now they're distinguishable objects you can point at.

The ideas

1

What a causal diagram is

Plain English. Dots for variables, arrows for causes. Done.

Precisely. A causal diagram is a set of variables relevant to what you're studying, plus arrows linking them, each arrow meaning that one variable causes the other in the Ep 12 sense. There's plenty of complexity hidden underneath — what counts as a variable, how to analyse the diagram, how to know you've got the right one — but the object itself is that simple.

2

Variables, precisely

Plain English. A node is the thing being measured, not one of its answers.

Precisely. A variable is a measurement taken across observations, which takes different values for different observations. The node is the measurement.

Anchor image. The node is the question on the form. The values are the boxes you could tick.

Worked example. Take a thousand people and record hair colour: brown, blonde, red, black. Hair color is the variable. Brown hair is not a variable — it's one value the variable can take. Getting this backwards will wreck a diagram.

For "does hair colour affect labour-market performance?", the relevant variables might be hair colour, earnings, intelligence, get-up-and-go, network connections, parents' social standing. Earnings takes values like 30,000, 40,000, 100,000.

3

Building one up: the cake

Plain English. Think about your setting, list what varies, ask what determines what.

Worked example. You have cake in the office kitchen. Brad comes in and offers to flip a coin for it.

Step 1 — two variables. The coin flip (heads or tails) and who has the cake (Brad or you). The flip determines who gets the cake, so:

That's a complete causal diagram.

Step 2 — one cause, two effects. Say the winner gets the cake but the loser keeps the coin. Now the flip determines your money too. One variable causing two things is fine:

Step 3 — one effect, two causes. Your friend Terry is sometimes in the room. If Terry is there, they see the flip, and if you lost your coin they feel bad and give you one. So money now has two causes — also fine:

The rule that generates arrows: anything that changes the distribution of a variable should be drawn as a cause of it. The coin flip changes the distribution of your money, therefore coin flip → money.

4

Unmeasured variables go on the diagram anyway

Plain English. Draw the causes that are really there, not the ones you happen to have data for.

Precisely. The diagram represents the underlying laws producing the data. Whether you can measure a variable is irrelevant to whether it belongs. Mark unmeasurable ones — grey them out — so a reader knows, but include them.

Anchor image. A map that only shows roads you've personally driven is not a map.

Worked example. Whether Terry is in the room might depend on Terry's mood that day. You can't measure Terry's mood. It goes on the diagram anyway, greyed out, because it genuinely causes Terry's presence.

Sometimes you know an unmeasured cause exists but can't name it. Use a placeholder — U1 — for "some unknown factor causing both of these":

Whatever U1 is — maybe temperature — when it's high or low it induces a relationship between shorts and ice cream, even though there's no arrow directly between them.

Where it breaks. This matters enormously for identification. The alternative explanations from Ep 10 show up whether or not you can measure them. Leave them off the diagram and you won't see the problem; you'll just get the wrong answer.

5

The payoff: pathways

Plain English. A diagram lets you trace the specific routes connecting your two variables, and decide which ones you actually asked about.

Worked example. Does police presence reduce crime? A simplified diagram — you could reasonably object that poverty is missing:

Now read the routes from police to crime:

PathwayWhat it meansWant it?
Police → CrimePolice directly stop a crime in progressYes
Police → Expected crime payout → CrimeDeterrence: criminals think they're less likely to get away with it, so commit lessYes
Police ← Lagged crime → CrimeHigh-crime areas get more police assigned, and past crime predicts current crimeNo

The first two are the effect of police on crime. The third is police and crime moving together because both trace back to how much crime there already was — nothing to do with police reducing anything.

Where it breaks. That third pathway is exactly an Ep 10 alternative explanation, now drawn rather than merely worried about. And that's the real value of these diagrams: look at one, mark which pathways you want and which you don't, then work out how to shut down the ones you don't. That's identification, and it's the next several episodes.

Say it out loud

A causal diagram sounds fancy and it really isn't. It's dots for variables, arrows for "this causes that." That's the whole object.

One thing people get wrong immediately: the variable is hair colour, not brown hair. Brown is a value. The node is the measurement.

Let me build one. You've got cake, Brad wants it, he offers to flip a coin. Two variables — the flip, and who ends up with the cake. Flip causes cake. Done, that's a causal diagram. Now say the loser keeps the coin, so the flip also determines your money — one cause, two effects, totally fine. And your friend Terry, if she's in the room, feels bad and gives you a coin when you lose — so money has two causes now. Also fine.

Here's the rule that generates every arrow: anything that changes the distribution of a variable is a cause of it.

And this bit is important — you include variables even if you can't measure them. Whether Terry's around might depend on her mood, which you'll never observe. Draw it anyway, greyed out. Sometimes you don't even know what the thing is, so you write U1 — "something causes both of these." Whatever it is, it makes them related with no arrow between them at all.

Now the payoff. Do police reduce crime? Draw it, and you see three routes. Police stop crimes directly — want that. Police make criminals think they'll get caught, so they commit less — want that too. But also: high-crime areas get assigned more police, and past crime predicts current crime. That route makes police and crime move together for reasons that have nothing to do with policing.

Before the diagram, that was a vague worry. Now it's a line you can point at — and deciding how to shut it down is exactly what identification is.

Recall drill

QWhat is a causal diagram, precisely?

A set of variables relevant to your question, plus arrows between them, each arrow meaning one variable causes the other.

QIs red hair a variable? Explain.

No. Hair color is the variable; red is one of the values it can take.

QWhere do the arrows in a causal diagram come from?

From theory — your understanding of the setting. Not from the data.

QState the rule for when to draw an arrow.

Anything that changes the distribution of a variable should be drawn as a cause of it.

QCan one variable cause two things? Can one variable have two causes?

Both, and both are ordinary. The coin flip causes cake and money; money is caused by both the coin flip and Terry being in the room.

QShould unmeasurable variables appear on the diagram?

Yes. Terry's mood can't be measured but genuinely causes whether Terry is in the room, so it goes on — usually greyed out to signal it's unobserved.

QWhat is U1 doing on a diagram?

Standing in for an unknown unmeasured factor causing two variables — for example something causing both shorts-wearing and ice-cream-eating, which makes them related with no direct arrow between them.

QGive the two pathways from police to crime that you want to count.

Police → Crime (directly stopping crimes in progress) and Police → Expected crime payout → Crime (deterrence — criminals think they're less likely to get away with it).

QGive the pathway from police to crime that you don't want, and why.

Police ← Lagged crime → Crime. High-crime areas get assigned more police, and past crime predicts current crime, so the two move together for reasons unrelated to policing.

QWhat's the real value of drawing a causal diagram?

It makes the pathways between two variables visible, so you can sort them into the ones that answer your question and the ones that don't, then work out how to eliminate the latter.

QTwo variables have no arrow between them. Can they still be related?

Yes — a shared cause such as U1 will make them move together without any direct causal link.

Glossary

Node
a variable on a causal diagram.
Arrow
an assertion that one variable causes another.
Unmeasured variable
a real cause you have no data on; still belongs on the diagram, usually greyed out.
Placeholder variable (U1)
a stand-in for an unknown unmeasured common cause.
Pathway
a route through the diagram connecting two variables, traced along arrows.
