Ep 68Missing Data

▶ Watch the video 14:40
On this pageThe 30-second versionWhy this existsThe ideasTrapsSay it out loudRecall drillConnectionsGlossary

Everyone deals with missing values by deleting those observations, and whether that's fine depends entirely on why the data is missing — harmless if the question didn't apply or the loss was truly random, salvageable if you can explain the missingness with other variables, and close to hopeless if the reason is the missing value itself.

The 30-second version

  • Listwise deletion — dropping observations with missing values — is by far the most common approach.
  • Whether it works depends on why the data is missing. Four reasons:
  • Not in universe: the question doesn't apply. Deletion is fine.
  • Missing completely at random: no rhyme or reason. Deletion is fine.
  • Missing at random: explained by variables you can measure. Deletion doesn't quite work; other methods do.
  • Missing not at random: the missingness depends on the value itself. Deletion can really mess you up, and there's not much you can do.
  • Multiple imputation predicts missing values from your other data, repeatedly, with noise.
  • Limited information maximum likelihood and the expectation maximization algorithm use the model itself rather than imputing first.

Why this exists

Missing data is when you have a variable and a bunch of observations, but some observations don't have all the values filled in.

Worked example. In a census study you know how many people live in your household, your income, your race, and your gender — but for some reason the married variable isn't there. It doesn't say you're married. It doesn't say you're not. It says I don't know.

That's a problem: if you want to study the effects of being married, or what predicts being married, what do you do with this person? They're not married, they're not unmarried — and you can't do statistics on a value you don't have.

The ideas

1

Listwise deletion, and why the reason matters

Plain English. Everyone just drops those people. Whether that's okay depends on why they're missing.

Precisely. The common approach is to drop anybody with missing values on the relevant variables from the data — I'm studying marriage, I don't know whether you're married, so I'll ignore you entirely. That's listwise deletion, and it's the most common way of dealing with missing data.

Whether it works depends on a number of factors — importantly, why the data is missing. Which means you have to think theoretically about why this variable might not be recorded for this particular individual.

2

Reason 1: not in universe

Plain English. The question doesn't apply to them.

Worked example. Your census data has an occupation variable — carpenter, plumber, teacher. There's another variable asking whether you have a job at all. If you answered no, why would anyone ask what your occupation is? You're not in universe for that question.

Listwise deletion works perfectly fine here. You're simply not the kind of person to whom the question applies.

3

Reason 2: missing completely at random

Plain English. No rhyme or reason. It just wasn't written down.

Precisely. A value is missing completely at random if it's missing for entirely random reasons, completely unrelated to everything else. There's no explanation — it just happens to be missing.

Worked example. The data is stored in a filing cabinet and some of the files got wet. Or you knocked on the door that day and didn't write it down. Or the entry got smudged.

Listwise deletion also works fine.

Anchor image. A few pages torn out of a book at random. Annoying, but the remaining pages still tell the same story.

4

Reason 3: missing at random

Plain English. There is a reason, but it's a reason you can measure.

Precisely. Confusingly, the name is very similar to missing completely at random and it's an entirely different concept. Missing at random means the reason the value is missing is related to some variables — but variables you can measure and control for. You can predict why somebody is missing.

Worked example. Return to that marriage variable. Suppose there's a religion that forbids telling anyone whether you're married — that's for the religion to know, not the government. You wouldn't observe a marriage value for those individuals — but you can say exactly why: they're in that religion. If you recorded religion elsewhere, you can explain the missingness. So it's random once you account for the variables that explain it.

Listwise deletion doesn't quite work here, but other methods do.

5

Reason 4: missing not at random

Plain English. The value is missing because of what the value is.

Precisely. The scariest version. A value is missing not at random if the reason it's missing is related to the value of the variable itself.

Worked example. Suppose people are embarrassed to tell a survey taker that they're not married. Then the people with missing values are far more likely to be unmarried — if you're married you'll say so, and if you're not, some will tell you and some will decline out of embarrassment.

So the value of the variable is related to whether it's missing at all. That's the hardest case to deal with. Listwise deletion could really mess you up — and there's also not a whole lot you can do.

Anchor image. Asking a room who's uncomfortable, and only the comfortable people answer.

6

Imputation, building up

Plain English. Guess the missing value from what else you know about that person.

Precisely. The most common alternative approach is multiple imputation: use what else you know about someone to predict the missing value. Build up to it:

Mean imputation. Your income is missing, so take the average of everybody else's income and give you that. That doesn't work very well, but it's the starter idea — making a prediction and filling in your value from other information.

Single imputation. Rather than the overall average, predict your value using your other data. Build a model predicting income from all the other variables, estimated using everybody else's data, then plug in your values and predict your income.

Multiple imputation. The same idea, repeated many times with noise added, which tends to improve the predictive quality of the results. You end up with, say, 10 or 20 different predicted values for your income — and when you estimate your model, you run it 10 or 20 times and average the models together to get your results.

Where it works and doesn't.

  • It works when data is missing at random, because it uses your other values to make the prediction — so to the extent those variables explain the missingness, it incorporates that.
  • It works less well when missing not at random, because it can't incorporate the unobserved value itself. If the value is likely to be different for people who are missing, the model can't reflect that — it has no data on the missing people.
  • And it imputes without recognising the model it will eventually be used for.
7

Methods that use the model itself

Plain English. Instead of filling in the blanks first, estimate the model with whatever each person has.

Limited information maximum likelihood. Maximum likelihood hasn't featured much in this book, though it underlies some methods already used. The basic idea: you have a model, and that model would generate data if you ran it. So pick the parameters that make the randomly generated data as much like the actual data you have as possible — maximising the likelihood of seeing the data you do in fact see.

Applied to missing data: not everybody can fully contribute to the estimation, but you can still contribute based on the variables you do have. So take what you can from each individual, leave out the parts that are missing, and estimate the model that way. It doesn't impute the missing values — it estimates the model while accounting for the fact that you don't have full data on everyone.

Expectation maximization algorithm. Another approach using the model to do the imputation. It goes back and forth between two estimation processes:

  1. Estimate a model. That model can generate likely values — based on this model, here's your most likely income.
  2. Now you've predicted an income and filled it into the data. Re-estimate the model using the filled-in values.
  3. Go back and forth — using the data to estimate the model, and using the model to predict what the missing values should be — a few times, to get the best version of the model you can.

Where it breaks. All of these work best in the missing at random or missing completely at random cases. For missing not at random there are methods, but they're rather complex and aren't covered.

Say it out loud

Missing data is the mundane problem that quietly wrecks studies. Your census data has someone's income, race, household size — and then for married, it just says nothing. Not married, not unmarried. Nothing.

And what does everyone do? Delete that person. That's listwise deletion, and it's overwhelmingly the most common approach.

Whether that's fine depends entirely on why the value is missing, and there are four answers.

Not in universe — the question didn't apply. You said you don't have a job, so nobody asked your occupation. Delete away, no problem.

Missing completely at random — no reason at all. The filing cabinet leaked. Someone forgot to write it down. Also fine to delete.

Missing at random — and I'm sorry about the naming, these are genuinely different things. This means there is a reason, but it's one you can measure. Say there's a religion that forbids disclosing marital status. You'll have missing values — but you know exactly who and why, because you recorded religion. Now deletion isn't right, but other methods work.

Missing not at random — the nightmare. The value is missing because of what the value is. People are embarrassed to admit they're not married, so the missing people are disproportionately unmarried. Delete them and you've systematically deleted the unmarried. And honestly? There's not much you can do.

For the middle cases, the standard tool is multiple imputation — predict someone's missing income from everything else you know about them, do it twenty times with noise, run your model on each version, and average. Or use methods like maximum likelihood that don't fill anything in at all — they just let each person contribute whatever variables they do have.

But notice: none of that saves you from missing not at random. Because to predict the missing people, you'd need data on the missing people.

Recall drill

QWhat is missing data?

When some observations don't have all their values filled in — the variable is neither one value nor another, just unknown.

QWhat is listwise deletion?

Dropping any observation with missing values on the relevant variables — the most common approach.

QWhat determines whether listwise deletion is acceptable?

Why the data is missing — which requires thinking theoretically about why the value wasn't recorded.

QExplain "not in universe" with an example.

The question doesn't apply — someone who answered that they have no job has no occupation to record. Deletion is fine.

QDefine missing completely at random and give examples.

Missing for entirely random reasons unrelated to anything — files got wet, an entry got smudged, someone forgot to write it down. Deletion is fine.

QDefine missing at random.

The missingness is related to variables you can measure and control for, so you can predict why somebody's value is missing.

QGive the missing at random example.

A religion forbidding members from disclosing marital status — you don't observe marriage for them, but you know why, because you recorded religion.

QDefine missing not at random.

The reason a value is missing is related to the value of the variable itself.

QGive the missing not at random example and why it's dangerous.

People embarrassed to say they aren't married decline to answer, so missing values are disproportionately unmarried people — and deleting them systematically removes them.

QIn which cases does listwise deletion work well?

Not in universe, and missing completely at random.

QDescribe mean imputation and Nick's assessment of it.

Replace a missing value with the average of everyone else's. It doesn't work very well — it's just the starting idea.

QDescribe single imputation.

Build a model predicting the variable from someone's other data, estimated on everybody else, then plug in their values to predict theirs.

QHow does multiple imputation extend that?

It repeats the process many times with noise added, producing perhaps 10 or 20 predicted values, then runs the model on each and averages the results.

QWhen does multiple imputation work, and when doesn't it?

It works under missing at random, since it uses the other variables that explain the missingness. It works poorly under missing not at random, because it has no data on the missing people to learn how they differ.

QWhat's the basic idea of maximum likelihood?

Choose model parameters that make the data the model would generate as much like the data you actually observe as possible.

QHow does limited information maximum likelihood handle missing data?

It doesn't impute — each individual contributes based on the variables they do have, and the model is estimated from whatever information is available.

QDescribe the expectation maximization algorithm.

Estimate a model, use it to predict likely values for the missing entries, re-estimate the model using those filled-in values, and go back and forth several times.

QWhich missingness types do all these methods suit best?

Missing at random and missing completely at random.
