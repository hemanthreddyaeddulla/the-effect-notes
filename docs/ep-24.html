Ep 24Causality with Less Modeling

If drawing a full causal diagram is hopeless — and that objection is a serious one — you have four escapes: list only the joint determinants of treatment and outcome, use front-door methods and skip the diagram, draw a simplified diagram and test it, or accept you didn't close everything and report a range instead of a point.

The 30-second version

  • The objection: this is all too hard, and it depends entirely on having the right diagram. Plenty of people are sceptical you can model social settings this precisely.
  • In a sceptic's view the real diagram is treatment → outcome, plus a back door running through a thousand variables you'll never measure.
  • Approach 1 — joint determinants. Skip the full diagram; list the variables that cause both treatment and outcome. Much easier, gets you most of the way.
  • Approach 2 — front doors. Don't model the confounding at all; find exogenous variation instead.
  • Approach 3 — draw it anyway, then test it. Your diagram implies certain variables are unrelated. Check with placebo tests.
  • Approach 4 — partial identification. Assume a range of severities for what you couldn't control, get a range of estimates, report the range.
  • This closes Part 1 of the book.

Why this exists

Everything so far demands a lot: think carefully about your model, draw the causal diagram, find every pathway from X to Y, close them all, make sure you can actually measure everything you need — or set up a process isolating just the front doors.

That's difficult, and it all rests on having a very good idea of what your causal diagram is. You don't have to look hard to find people sceptical that you can model things this precisely. Every diagram in this series came prefaced with "assume this is right for a minute," and you can probably think of things left off even the more complex ones. People will disagree about what a diagram should look like.

For a lot of social science, the objection is: this is simply too complex to write down in a neat compact diagram, so why bother? In that view the honest diagram is:

This is not a trivial objection. Things really are complex, and you really do have to simplify to use this process at all — and you could make the case that any such simplification is too simple. So: are you out of luck? Four answers.

The ideas

1

1 · Focus on the joint determinants

Plain English. Don't draw the whole diagram. Just list what causes both the treatment and the outcome.

Precisely. You may not be able to write out the full complex diagram, but you can think very carefully about which variables are likely to sit directly on a simple back door path — the variables that cause the treatment and cause the outcome. Writing down that list of likely confounders is a far easier task than a complete diagram, and honestly it gets you most of the way there.

Anchor image. You can't map the whole river system. You can find the places where two streams feed the same pond.

Where it breaks. You'll miss things. You won't catch collider variables, and you might get yourself into trouble with post-treatment bias. But at minimum you'll be thinking carefully about what has to be controlled for, even if not in the fully proper way.

This is how a great many researchers actually operate: rather than a full diagram, they ask what variables plausibly cause both the treatment and the outcome, deal with those, and flag anything they can't measure as a problem to address.

2

2 · Use front-door methods and skip the diagram

Plain English. Don't model the confounding. Find variation that doesn't have any.

Precisely. Plenty of researchers reason that there's no point drawing a big causal diagram and finding all the pathways, because at the end there'll be something they can't measure or something they forgot. The way around it is to isolate front door paths rather than close back doors — which is what the Chapter 9 methods are for.

Where it breaks — Nick's own position, worth having. There are researchers who think you can only get a causal effect from a randomized controlled experiment. He thinks that's a bit harsh. There are others who think you can only get one from an experiment or by isolating front doors with exogenous variation. He thinks that's a bit too strong, but closer to his own view.

The honest summary: it is simply a lot easier to identify a causal effect when you have a source of exogenous variation, because it lets you ignore a great deal of stuff that's very hard to figure out otherwise.

3

3 · Draw a simplified diagram, then test it

Plain English. Admit it's simplified, then check you didn't get it egregiously wrong.

Precisely. Draw the diagram knowing it's a simplification, then use the fact that a causal diagram implies certain conditional independences — pairs of variables that should be unrelated once you've closed the paths between them. Test those with placebo tests.

Anchor image. You can't prove the bridge is perfect. You can drive a truck over it.

Worked example. The humble version: "I know I'm leaving something out. I don't have the full picture of all the thousands of interlocking parts that should be here. But my diagram says these variables should be unrelated — let me check whether they are." You might get lucky and pass, and conclude that although something's missing, it's probably not that important.

4

4 · Partial identification and sensitivity analysis

Plain English. You couldn't control for everything. So assume a range of severities for what you missed, and report the range of answers that follow.

Precisely. Partial identification is the broader idea, most commonly applied as sensitivity analysis. Rather than stopping when you can't close every back door, you make a range of plausible assumptions about the leftover and compute what your estimate would be under each — arriving at a range rather than a point. You've identified an answer; you just haven't narrowed it to a single value.

Anchor image. You can't weigh the parcel, but you know it's between two and five kilos. That is genuinely more than nothing.

Worked example. You're studying the effect of the average income of the zip code you grew up in on your adult income. There's a variable you can't control for — say your parents' intelligence. You don't have that data, but it's likely related both to where you grew up and to your income as an adult. Leaving it out biases your result in some direction, because the correlation you observe won't represent only the causal effect.

So:

  1. Assume the worst. Suppose that relationship is super, super strong. Calculate how badly biased your estimate would be, and what estimate you'd get.
  2. Assume it's weak. Do it again. What estimate would you get then?
  3. Report the range. If it's very strong, the effect of where you grew up on adult earnings is this number. If it's very weak, it's that number. The answer is somewhere in between.

Where it breaks. Nick thinks this one is a little underutilised. More on sensitivity analysis in the very last chapters of the book.

Say it out loud

Time for the objection everything so far has been dodging: what if this is all just too hard?

Because look at what's being asked. Draw the entire causal diagram. Find every pathway. Close them all. And measure everything you need to close them with. Every diagram I've shown you came with "just assume this is right for a minute" — and you could probably poke holes in all of them. A lot of people look at social science and say the real diagram is: treatment, outcome, and a back door running through a thousand variables nobody will ever measure. Why bother?

That's a fair objection. Here are four ways out.

One: just find the joint determinants. Forget the full diagram — what causes both the treatment and the outcome? That list is way easier to write, and it gets you most of the way. You'll miss colliders, and you might wander into post-treatment bias, but you'll be thinking about the right things. Honestly, this is what most researchers actually do.

Two: skip the modelling entirely and go find a front door. Don't try to close a hundred back doors — find exogenous variation that doesn't have any. Some people say only randomized trials count, which I think is a bit harsh. Some say experiments or front doors, which is a bit strong but closer to where I land. It really is just easier with exogenous variation, because it lets you ignore all the stuff you were never going to figure out.

Three: draw it anyway, then test it. Be humble — say you know it's incomplete. But your diagram claims certain variables should be unrelated to each other. Go check! If they are, maybe what you left out doesn't matter much.

Four — and I think this one's underused — partial identification. You couldn't control for your parents' intelligence. Fine. Assume it's an incredibly strong influence, and calculate what your estimate becomes. Then assume it's weak and do it again. Now you've got a range, and the truth is somewhere in it. You haven't identified a number — you've identified an interval. That's still a real answer.

Recall drill

QWhat's the core objection this chapter responds to?

That drawing a full causal diagram is too hard, especially in social science, and everything depends on getting it right — so why bother.

QWhat does a sceptic's version of the diagram look like?

Treatment → outcome, plus a back door running through "all sorts of stuff" — perhaps a hundred or a thousand variables you could never measure and control for.

QIs that objection dismissed?

No. Settings really are complex, simplification really is required to use the method, and you can argue any such simplification is too simple.

QDescribe approach 1.

Focus only on the joint determinants — the variables likely to cause both the treatment and the outcome, sitting on simple back door paths. Far easier than a full diagram and gets you most of the way.

QWhat does approach 1 cost you?

You won't catch collider variables and you may run into post-treatment bias, since you're not doing it in the fully proper way.

QDescribe approach 2.

Skip the diagram and use front-door methods — find exogenous variation and isolate the front door rather than closing every back door.

QWhat's Nick's view on "only RCTs give causal effects"?

He thinks that's a bit harsh. The stronger-but-closer view he holds is that experiments or front-door isolation with exogenous variation are what make it work — though even that he calls a bit too strong.

QDescribe approach 3.

Draw a simplified diagram, acknowledge it's incomplete, then use the conditional independences it implies to run placebo tests and check you haven't made egregious errors.

QDescribe approach 4.

Partial identification — accept you couldn't close everything, assume a range of severities for what you missed, and compute the corresponding range of estimates.

QWalk through the parents' intelligence example.

You're estimating the effect of your childhood zip code's average income on your adult income, but can't control for parents' intelligence, which likely affects both. Assume that influence is extremely strong and compute an estimate; assume it's weak and compute another; report the range between them.

QWhy is it called partial identification?

Because you have identified an answer — you just haven't narrowed it to a single value.

QWhat does Nick say about how often partial identification is used?

He thinks it's a little underutilised.

Glossary

Joint determinants
variables that cause both the treatment and the outcome; the likely confounders on simple back door paths.
Conditional independence
a pair of variables a causal diagram implies should be unrelated once paths between them are closed.
Partial identification
identifying a range of possible values for an effect rather than a single point.
Sensitivity analysis
the most common application of partial identification: varying assumptions about what you couldn't control for and reporting how the estimate moves.
