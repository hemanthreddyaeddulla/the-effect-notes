Ep 48Event Studies

▶ Watch the video 15:20

Something happened at a moment in time; compare after to before — but you can't just control for time, because time perfectly determines treatment and controlling for it leaves no variation at all, so instead you use the pre-treatment period to predict what the after would have looked like and measure the deviation.

The 30-second version

  • Event study: an event happens at a time, there's a before and an after, compare them. That's the whole design.
  • These are canned designs — structures where, if your setting fits, you can expect to get a causal effect.
  • One back door: time. Time determines whether you're before or after, and time also affects the outcome.
  • You cannot control for time. Doing so removes all the variation in treatment — 0% treated before, 100% after — leaving nothing to study.
  • Instead: predict what the post-treatment period would have been, and compare the actual to that prediction.
  • Three ways to predict: ignore the problem, extend a time trend, or use another observable variable the outcome used to track.
  • Terminology is a mess — the same design has many names, and "event study" means different things in economics and finance.

Why this exists

If you picked up a causal inference book based on what you'd heard about the field, you might be surprised this isn't Chapter 1. This is where the canned designs start — structures that, if your setting applies, let you expect to get a causal effect of interest.

Fixed effects got partway there, but it's pretty rare that anyone thinks fixed effects alone identifies a causal effect. Controlling for variables works but is difficult — you have to figure out the whole causal diagram.

Event studies are the very simplest of the canned designs. So simple you've probably done several without knowing. Dogs do event studies: whine, get fed, and learn that whining causes food.

Simple doesn't mean it always works. The setting has to apply, and you have to do it correctly — which is what the chapter is about.

The ideas

1

A note on names

Plain English. This design has many names, and its name means several things.

Precisely. Because it's so simple, the same design goes by:

  • Statistical process control
  • Interrupted time series
  • Before-and-after comparison
  • Sometimes case control, though that usually means something else entirely

And in the other direction, "event study" means different things in different fields:

FieldWhat "event study" usually means
EconomicsMany events occurring to different people at different times, possibly with a control group
FinanceA single event at a single time, comparing before to after

This chapter uses the finance sense.

2

The diagram, and why you can't control for time

Plain English. There's exactly one back door and it's time — and it's the one confounder you're forbidden from controlling for.

Precisely. The treatment goes into effect at a particular time. Before that time you're untreated; after it you're treated. Whether you're treated is entirely determined by whether you're before or after the event. Time changes, so time affects whether you're currently before or after — and things also just change over time in general, so time affects the outcome too.

You want the arrow from treatment to outcome. There's a single back door, through time. Time is measurable. So why not just control for it?

Because time is far too good at determining treatment. Control for time and you predict the level of treatment before the event — which is zero, nobody was treated — and the level after, which is 100%, everyone was. Subtract those predictions out and there is no difference in treatment left to compare. You've removed all the variation in treatment, and there's nothing left to study.

Anchor image. Adjusting for the light switch to study the lamp. The switch explains the lamp perfectly, so once you've adjusted for it there is no lamp left.

3

The actual approach: predict the counterfactual

Plain English. Use the before to guess what the after would have looked like, then see how far off reality was.

Precisely. Use something about the before-treatment period to make a prediction about what the post-treatment period would have been like. Then look at how things actually turned out and compare:

Actual vs predictionConclusion
Higher than predictedPositive treatment effect
Lower than predictedNegative treatment effect
Same as predictedZero treatment effect

This is different from controlling for time, because you are not removing all the variation in treatment — you're using pre-treatment data to build a counterfactual for the post-treatment period, and making your comparison against that.

Worked example — two graphs.

Graph one: a time series that jumps around but has no real trend — flat as you go along. The treatment goes into effect. What would you have expected without it? No trend before, so probably flat continuing. What you actually see: at the point of treatment the series jumps up, then fades back down toward what you'd have expected anyway. So: a strongly positive treatment effect right at the moment of treatment, fading out over time.

Graph two: an upward trend — jumps day to day, but clearly climbing. You'd have expected the climb to continue. What you actually see: the outcome drops, against the trend. So: a negative treatment effect.

In both cases you're comparing what you see to a counterfactual prediction.

4

The three ways to build that prediction

Plain English. Assume nothing changed, extend the trend, or borrow another variable's path.

Approach 1 — ignore the problem. Simply compare after outcomes to before outcomes. Better now → positive effect. Worse now → negative effect.

You didn't close the back door; you assumed there wasn't one. Sometimes that works. If you're in a situation where you're confident nothing would have changed from before to after — you look at the data and there's no trend at all, like graph one — then you might confidently say you're willing to believe things would have stayed the same on average, and compare the outcome against a baseline of no change. Won't work in all scenarios, but lucky you if you can justify it in yours.

Approach 2 — extend a time trend. This is what the graphs did. You see a trend in the pre-treatment data — flat or upward-sloping — and assume it would have continued. Whatever you observe relative to that trend is your positive or negative treatment effect.

There are many ways to predict a trend: linear regression, advanced time series modelling, whatever. This works best when there's good reason to believe the trends you saw beforehand would have continued.

**Approach 3 — use other after-treatment data. Wait — how is that a prediction, if you can see the after period? Because you're not using the outcome to make the prediction. You're using other observable variables**.

Worked example. In the pre-treatment data, notice your outcome is highly correlated with some other variable. Say you want the effect of a law banning beer — maybe you can't have a beer garden anymore — on the price of beer. Beforehand, beer and wine prices are at very different levels but tightly interlocked: beer up, wine up; beer down, wine down.

Then the ban happens. Wine keeps going on its merry way. Beer shifts. You'd have expected beer to keep tracking wine, and instead it deviates — and that deviation is your treatment effect.

Where this is most used. Financial markets: a particular stock tracks the market as a whole pretty well, and then right when the event occurs it deviates from what the market is doing. That deviation is the treatment effect.

Say it out loud

Event studies are the simplest design in the whole book. Something happened at a particular moment, there's a before and an after, and you compare them. That's it. Dogs do event studies — whine, get fed, conclude that whining causes food.

But there's one wrinkle, and it's a good one. Draw the diagram and there's exactly one back door: time. Time determines whether you're before or after the event, and time also affects your outcome directly.

So control for time, right? You can't. And the reason is beautiful. Time determines your treatment perfectly — zero percent treated before the event, one hundred percent after. So if you control for time, you've removed all the variation in treatment. There's literally nothing left to compare.

So instead you do something cleverer: you use the before period to predict what the after would have looked like, and you measure the gap. Higher than predicted, positive effect. Lower, negative.

Three ways to build that prediction. You can ignore the problem — just compare before to after — which sounds lazy but is genuinely fine if the data was dead flat beforehand and you believe it would have stayed flat.

You can extend the trend. If things were climbing steadily and then dropped, that drop against the trend is your effect.

Or — my favourite — you can borrow another variable. Say you're studying a beer ban's effect on beer prices. Before the ban, beer and wine prices move in lockstep. Then the ban hits: wine carries on as normal, beer deviates. That deviation is your effect. This is exactly how finance does it — a stock tracks the market until the event, then breaks away.

Recall drill

QWhat is an event study?

A design where an event occurs at a particular time, and you compare the period after to the period before to find the effect of what happened.

QWhat are "canned designs"?

Structures that, if your particular setting applies to them, let you expect to get a causal effect of interest.

QGive some other names for this design.

Statistical process control, interrupted time series, before-and-after comparison, and sometimes case control — though that usually means something else.

QHow do economists and finance researchers use "event study" differently?

Economists usually mean many events happening to different people at different times, possibly with a control group. Finance means a single event at a single time, comparing before to after.

QWhat is the single back door in an event study?

Time — it determines whether you're before or after the event, and it also affects the outcome.

QWhy can't you close that back door by controlling for time?

Time determines treatment perfectly — 0% treated before, 100% after — so controlling for it removes all the variation in treatment and leaves nothing to study.

QWhat do you do instead?

Use the pre-treatment period to predict what the post-treatment period would have looked like, then compare the actual outcome to that prediction.

QHow do you read the comparison?

Actual above the prediction means a positive effect; below means negative; equal means zero.

QDescribe the first graph and its conclusion.

A flat time series with no trend; after treatment it jumps up and then fades back toward the expected level — a strong positive effect at the moment of treatment, fading over time.

QDescribe the second graph and its conclusion.

An upward-trending series; after treatment the outcome drops, contrary to the trend — a negative treatment effect.

QDescribe approach 1 and when it's acceptable.

Simply compare after outcomes to before outcomes. It's defensible when the pre-treatment data shows no trend at all and you're confident nothing would have changed.

QDescribe approach 2.

Fit a time trend in the pre-treatment data and assume it would have continued; the deviation from that trend is your treatment effect.

QWhen does approach 2 work best?

When there's good reason to believe the pre-existing trend would have continued.

QDescribe approach 3, and why it isn't circular.

Use post-treatment data on other observable variables — not the outcome — that the outcome tracked closely before treatment, and see whether the outcome deviates from them.

QGive the beer and wine example.

Before a beer ban, beer and wine prices move in lockstep. After it, wine continues as normal and beer deviates — and that deviation from the expected tracking is the treatment effect.

QWhat's the most common application of approach 3?

Financial markets — a stock that tracks the market as a whole and then deviates when the event occurs.

Glossary

Event study
comparing outcomes after an event to a prediction of what they'd have been without it.
Canned design
a reusable research design structure that identifies an effect whenever its setting applies.
Counterfactual prediction
the estimate of what the post-treatment outcome would have been absent treatment.
Time trend
a fitted path over time, extended from the pre-treatment period to build the counterfactual.
