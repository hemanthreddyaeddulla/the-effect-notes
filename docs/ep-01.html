Ep 1Designing Research

▶ Watch the video 9:20
On this pageThe 30-second versionWhy this existsThe ideasTrapsSay it out loudRecall drillConnectionsGlossary

Data never answers your question by itself — you can have perfectly good data, run a perfectly correct analysis, and still end up with a precise answer to a question you never asked.

The 30-second version

  • Causal inference is figuring out, from data, whether one thing causes another — usually when you can't run the experiment that would settle it.
  • Things go wrong in two places: the data can be bad, or the data can be fine and your design still answers the wrong question.
  • The second failure is the dangerous one, because nothing about it looks like a mistake. The numbers are correct.
  • A research design is what connects your question to your analysis, so that the number you compute is the number you wanted.
  • The rest of the series: pick a good question → describe data → identify the right analysis → learn the specific tools.

Why this exists

You look at the world, you get a question — does this drug cure the disease, does this policy reduce crime. The tempting move is to find data that's about your topic, run something on it, and treat the output as the answer. That move fails constantly, and it fails quietly.

Sometimes the data is simply bad: wrong source, unreliable, poorly measured, inconsistent, or about something adjacent to what you care about. That failure is at least visible if you look. The other failure isn't. You can have data you fully trust, do the analysis competently, and land on a real answer to a different question — and because every step was defensible, you'll believe it.

The ideas

1

The car insurance ad

Plain English. Every car insurance company advertises that people who switch to them save about $500 a year. All of them. They can't all be the cheapest — so what are those ads actually measuring?

Precisely. The advertised statistic is the mean saving among people who chose to switch. Switching is voluntary, so a person only switches when this company happens to quote them less than they currently pay. The sample is selected on the outcome. The number is an honest average over a group defined by having saved money.

Anchor image. A gym posts a sign: "members who joined lost 15 pounds on average." It's true. It's also true at every gym on the street — because the only people who finish the year as members are the ones it was working for. The sign measures who stayed, not what the gym does.

Worked example. Two questions you might think the ad answers:

QuestionWho's in the comparisonWhat you'd learn
Which company is cheapest?Everyone, priced at every companyWhich company actually has the lowest rates
What do switchers save?Only people who switchedAn average over a self-selected group

Same data on prices. Different design. Only the second is what the ad computed, and only the first is what you had in your head when you watched it.

Where it breaks. Nothing here is a data problem or a math problem. The company plausibly has real, well-measured data on its switchers, and the average is correctly calculated. The failure is entirely in the gap between the question and the design — which is exactly the failure no amount of better data or better statistics will catch.

2

Two ways to get a wrong answer

Plain English. Bad data, or good data used to answer something other than what you asked.

Precisely. Failure one is a measurement and sourcing problem — the numbers don't faithfully represent the thing. Failure two is an identification problem — the numbers are faithful, but the comparison you built out of them isolates a different quantity than the one your question names.

Anchor image. A telescope doesn't create stars, it decides which light reaches your eye. Bad data is a dirty lens. A bad design is a perfectly clean lens pointed at the wrong part of the sky — and you will describe what you see with total confidence.

Where it breaks. You can't fix a design problem with more data. A bigger sample of switchers gives you a more precise estimate of switcher savings, and moves you no closer at all to knowing which company is cheapest.

3

What a research design is for

Plain English. A research design is the plan that makes your analysis answer your question instead of some neighbouring question.

Precisely. Given the same dataset, different questions demand different analyses. The design is the reasoning that picks the analysis matching the question — and lets you say what your number means once you have it.

Say it out loud

You've seen those car insurance ads — "switch to us and save five hundred dollars a year." Every company runs that ad. They obviously can't all be the cheapest, so what's going on?

Here's the trick. They only surveyed the people who switched to them. And think about who does that: you only switch if this company quoted you less than you're already paying. So the group they measured is, by definition, the group that saved money. Everyone who got quoted a higher price just walked away and never showed up in the data.

And the part I find genuinely unsettling is that nothing there is a lie. The data is real. The average is calculated correctly. It's a completely accurate answer — to the question "what do switchers save?" It's just that the question in your head was "which company is cheapest?", and those are different questions that need different comparisons.

That gap is what research design is. It's not about having better data or fancier statistics — it's the reasoning that makes sure the number you compute is the number you actually wanted. And the reason it matters so much is that when you get it wrong, there's no error message. Everything looks fine.

Recall drill

QWhat is causal inference trying to do?

Use data to work out whether one thing actually causes another — typically in settings where you'd like to run an experiment but can't.

QEvery car insurance company claims switchers save ~$500. Why can that be true for all of them?

Because each only surveys its own switchers, and people switch only when that company quotes them less than they currently pay. The sample is selected on having saved.

QName the two distinct ways an analysis can give you a wrong answer.

The data is bad (badly sourced, unreliable, weakly measured, or about the wrong thing), or the data is fine but the design answers a different question than the one you asked.

QWhich of those two failures is more dangerous, and why?

The second. Bad data can be spotted by inspecting it. A design mismatch leaves every step looking correct, so there's nothing to notice.

QYou have the insurance company's switcher data and want to know which company is cheapest. Would ten times more data help?

No. More data makes the estimate of switcher savings more precise. It doesn't change which quantity you're estimating. Design problems don't shrink with sample size.

QWhat job does a research design do?

It connects the question to the analysis — it's the reasoning that makes the number you compute the number your question actually asked for.

QWhat does it mean that data is good at telling you "what" but not "why"?

Data reports what was observed. Turning that into a claim about how the world works takes a design that rules out the other explanations for what you observed.

Glossary

Causal inference
using data to determine whether one thing causes another, especially without a controlled experiment.
Research design
the plan connecting a research question to an analysis, such that the analysis answers that question.
Identification
establishing that a particular analysis of your data recovers the specific quantity your question asks about.
Selection
when who ends up in your data depends on the outcome you're measuring, as with insurance switchers.
