Ep 29Polynomials in Regression

▶ Watch the video 13:00
On this pageThe 30-second versionWhy this existsThe ideasTrapsSay it out loudRecall drillConnectionsGlossary

Regression gives you the best version of the shape you chose — and the best version of a bad shape is still bad — so when a relationship curves, add powers of X to let the line bend, then accept that the effect of X is now different at every value of X and read it off with a derivative.

The 30-second version

  • Regression fits the best version of the shape you picked. Pick badly and you get the best version of something bad.
  • Default OLS shape: a straight line. Plenty of relationships aren't straight.
  • Fix by transforming variables so the transformed relationship is straight. The two big ones in causal inference: polynomials and logarithms.
  • A polynomial adds powers of X. Linear = first order. Quadratic = X and X². Cubic = X, X², X³. Keep the lower terms.
  • Diagnose the need by plotting residuals against X. A visible pattern is a warning sign.
  • Stop at third order. More gets weird — don't add them willy-nilly.
  • Interpretation: there is no single effect of X, and there shouldn't be. Take the derivative and plug in a value of X.

Why this exists

Remember what regression is: a shape-fitting procedure. You supply a shape and it finds the best version of that shape for your data. Getting the best version of a shape sounds great — but you still had to pick the shape, and if the shape doesn't make sense for your data you get back a bad result. Even the best version of a bad thing is still a bad thing.

By default, ordinary least squares fits a straight line. Look at data that clearly curves: fit a straight line and you'll get the best possible line, which is still a poor fit, because the relationship simply isn't well described by a straight line.

The ideas

1

Transformations

Plain English. You can't bend the line, so bend the variables instead.

Precisely. If the relationship between your variables isn't a straight line, you can often still get a good fit from regression by transforming the variables — so that while the original data has no straight-line relationship, the transformed data does.

Several transformations are popular in statistical analysis; the book covers a few, and there are more it doesn't. The two you'll meet most in causal inference and regression analysis are polynomials and logarithms.

2

Polynomials

Plain English. Add X squared to let the line curve. Add X cubed to let it curve back.

Precisely. A polynomial adds additional powers of the variable to your model.

ModelTermsWhat it allows
Linear — first-order polynomialXA straight line. Slope never changes: increase X by one and Y always moves by β₁.
Quadratic — second orderX and X²One curve. Parabola-shaped, though zoomed in it may not curve back on itself.
Cubic — third orderX, X², X³A curve, then a curve back the other direction.
Quartic, quintic, …X⁴, X⁵, …More wiggle than you want.

Keep the lower-order terms. A quadratic includes X and X². You don't leave the X out.

Anchor image. A garden hose. Straight by default. One bend, two bends — after about three you're not shaping it, you're just tangling it.

Where it breaks. You're pretty much always fine with the first three orders. Beyond that, don't get wild: too many polynomial terms makes the model behave strangely, and you shouldn't add them willy-nilly.

3

Diagnosing with residuals

Plain English. Plot your residuals against X. If they make a shape, your line has the wrong shape.

Precisely. With a straight-line fit to curved data, the residuals sit consistently above the line at low X, dip below in the middle, then come back above at high X. That's a relationship between the residuals and X, which you don't want.

Why it matters: one of the assumptions you need about the error term is that it's unrelated to X. What you're really worried about is the error term, not the residuals — but a visible pattern in the residuals is still not a good sign.

Worked example. Plot the residuals from both fits of the same curved data:

  • Straight-line model: a clear pattern remains — residuals shift systematically as you move along the X axis.
  • Quadratic model: no relationship whatsoever between residuals and X. Exactly what you want.

Where it breaks. So the rule is: plot residuals against X, and if you see a pattern, that may indicate you want a polynomial term to get rid of it.

4

Interpreting polynomial terms

Plain English. You can't read one coefficient anymore — and there is no single effect of X.

Precisely. Once X appears in more than one place, you must consider all the places it appears to interpret its effect. And stranger still: there is no single effect of X, and there shouldn't be. The effect of a one-unit increase in X is the slope of the fitted line, and in a polynomial model the slope is changing all the time. So the effect differs at low, middle, and high values of X.

How to get it: write out the equation and take the derivative.

For a cubic model, the derivative of Y with respect to X is:

dY/dX  =  β₁  +  2·β₂·X  +  3·β₃·X²

where β₁ is the coefficient on X, β₂ on X², and β₃ on X³.

Notice X is in that expression. The whole expression is the effect of X — so to state an effect you must pick a specific value of X to evaluate it at.

  • At X = 0: everything else drops out, and the effect is just β₁.
  • At X = 1: the effect is β₁ + 2β₂ + 3β₃.
5

Worked example: chain restaurants

Worked example. Regress restaurant health inspection scores on the number of locations the chain has, with a quadratic term:

PredictorCoefficient
Number of locations−0.08
Number of locations²0.0001

You want: comparing two chain restaurants where one has one more location than the other, what's the association with the inspection score?

Write out the model and take the derivative:

Score      =  −0.08 · locations  +  0.0001 · locations²  + …
dScore/dLocations  =  −0.08  +  2 · 0.0001 · locations

Now plug in whatever value of X you care about:

At this many locationsThe effect of one more location
1−0.08 + 2(0.0001)(1) = −0.0798
1,000−0.08 + 2(0.0001)(1,000) = +0.12

Going from one location to two, and going from a thousand locations to a thousand and one, give completely different answers — including different signs. That's exactly what a curved relationship is telling you: the effect differs across the range of the X axis, as it should.

Say it out loud

Remember what regression actually is: you pick a shape, and it finds the best version of that shape. Which sounds great until you realise — the best version of a bad shape is still bad. And by default, the shape OLS picks is a straight line.

So what if the relationship curves? You can't bend the line — so you bend the variables. That's a transformation, and the simplest one is a polynomial: you just add powers of X. Add X² and the line can curve once. Add X³ and it can curve and then curve back. And keep the lower terms — a quadratic is X and X squared, you don't drop the X. Stop around third order; beyond that things get weird.

How do you know you need one? Plot your residuals against X. If you fit a straight line through curved data, you'll see the residuals sit high at the start, dip below in the middle, and come back high at the end. That pattern is the data telling you the shape is wrong. Add the quadratic and the pattern vanishes.

Now here's the part that trips people up. Once X is in your model twice, there is no single effect of X — and there shouldn't be! The effect is the slope, and the slope is changing the whole way along. So you take the derivative, and you plug in a value.

Watch this. Restaurant inspection scores against how many locations the chain has. Coefficient on locations is −0.08, on locations-squared is 0.0001. Take the derivative: −0.08 plus 2 times 0.0001 times locations.

At one location, one more location is worth about −0.08. At a thousand locations, one more is worth +0.12. Different size, different sign. And that's not a problem with the model — that's the curve telling you something real.

Recall drill

QWhat does "regression gives you the best version of the shape you picked" imply?

That if the shape is wrong for your data, you get the best version of a bad thing — which is still bad.

QWhat's the default shape ordinary least squares fits?

A straight line.

QWhat's the general fix when a relationship isn't a straight line?

Transform the variables so that while the original data has no straight-line relationship, the transformed data does.

QName the two transformations most common in causal inference.

Polynomials and logarithms.

QWhat terms does a quadratic model include, and what's the common mistake?

X and X². The mistake is dropping the X and keeping only the squared term.

QWhat does a cubic allow that a quadratic doesn't?

A curve and then a curve back in the other direction. A quadratic allows only one curve.

QHow far up the polynomial orders should you go?

You're pretty much always fine through third order. Beyond that models get strange and you shouldn't add terms willy-nilly.

QHow do you diagnose that you need a polynomial?

Plot the residuals against X. If they show a pattern — high, then low, then high — the shape is wrong.

QWhy do patterned residuals matter?

Because one assumption you need is that the error term is unrelated to X. The residuals aren't the error term, but a pattern in them is a bad sign.

QIn a polynomial model, why is there no single effect of X?

The effect is the slope, and the slope changes continuously — so the effect differs at low, middle, and high values of X.

QGive the derivative of Y with respect to X for a cubic model.

dY/dX = β₁ + 2β₂X + 3β₃X².

QIn that expression, what is the effect at X = 0?

Just β₁ — all the other terms drop out.

QInspection scores on locations: coefficients −0.08 and 0.0001 on the squared term. Write the derivative.

dScore/dLocations = −0.08 + 2(0.0001)(locations).

QEvaluate that at 1 location and at 1,000 locations.

At 1: −0.08 + 0.0002 = −0.0798. At 1,000: −0.08 + 0.2 = +0.12 — different magnitude and different sign.
