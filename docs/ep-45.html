Ep 45Fixed Effects

▶ Watch the video 15:40
On this pageThe 30-second versionWhy this existsThe ideasTrapsSay it out loudRecall drillConnectionsGlossary

If you can't measure a confounder but it sits inside a broader category you can identify — like your birthplace sitting inside you — then controlling for that category controls for everything inside it, which is why controlling for the individual strips out all the between variation and leaves only the within.

The 30-second version

  • New problem: the diagram tells you what to control for, and you don't have the data.
  • The insight: if an unmeasured variable is constant within a broader category you do observe, control for the category and you've controlled for the variable.
  • Requires panel data — the same individuals measured repeatedly over time. People, companies, countries.
  • Controlling for the individual controls for everything unique to them that doesn't change over time.
  • This splits variation in two: between (differences across individuals) and within (changes for one individual over time).
  • Fixed effects removes all the between variation, leaving only within.
  • So back doors made of time-constant things close — even ones you could never measure. Time-varying back doors stay open.

Why this exists

This starts a whole section of the book on research designs that solve one particular problem.

So far it's been ways to control for variables — regression, matching, drawing causal diagrams that tell you what needs controlling. And there's a very, very common problem with all of it, especially in social science: you draw the diagram, it tells you what to control for, you go to set up your regression or your matching estimator — and you realise you don't actually have data on it.

Thankfully there are methods that let you estimate a causal effect even when you can't measure everything you need to control for. They only work in particular settings, so you have to get a little lucky and be clever about applying them. But if you can apply them, they work — and depending on who you ask, some people think they're the only good way to get causal effects from observational data when you can't run an experiment.

The ideas

1

The key insight

Plain English. You can't measure the thing — but you can measure the box it lives in.

Precisely. If you need to control for a variable, and you can't measure it, but you do have data on a broader category that the variable sits inside — and that category explains all the variation in that variable — then you can control for the broader category and it controls for everything inside it.

Anchor image. You can't weigh the individual ingredients after they're baked in. You can weigh the cake.

Worked example. You have panel data: the same measurement on the same people over time — you this year, last year, the year before. It works for individual people, companies, countries: New Zealand this year, New Zealand last year, and so on.

Say the variable you need to control for is your birthplace. Your birthplace both affects whether you were treated and affects your outcome — a back door. And you don't have data on it.

But you do have data on you. And your birthplace is fixed within the category of you. So if you could somehow control for your identity, you'd be controlling for everything unique to you that doesn't change over time — including your birthplace, even though you can't measure it.

That's fixed effects: add a control for the individual, and in doing so control for all the things unique to that individual that are constant over time.

2

Worked example: chancellor visits and trade

Plain English. Bundle everything unchanging about a country into one variable called "country."

Worked example. Does a recent visit from the Chancellor of Germany make your country trade more with Germany? Plenty of other things determine how much you trade:

VariableWhy it's a back doorCan you measure it?
GeographyCloser to Germany, more tradeSort of
History with GermanyPrior trading history affects both whether the chancellor visits and future tradeHow do you put a number on it?
Culture of the populationA German-speaking country, or shared heritage, trades moreHow do you measure a population's culture?
Current politicsIs your country angry at Germany or friendly with it?Maybe

Several of those are genuinely hard to measure. But notice something: some of them are constant within country over time.

Geography isn't changing much — Austria has had the same geography for decades. History with Germany is an ever-evolving thing, but go back far enough and the history you had a hundred years ago isn't changing from year to year.

So take all of those and bundle them into a single variable: country.

Move between countries and these things change — different countries have different geographies, different histories with Germany. But stick with a single country and there's no variation left in them. Austria's geography doesn't vary from year to year, at least not on the time scale of your data.

That diagram is much easier to work with. Control for which country you are and you close all those back doors at once.

Where it breaks. It doesn't close everything. Current politics does change from year to year — Austria's relationship with Germany shifts — so that back door stays open and you'd need to control for it separately. Fixed effects gets a lot of your work done; it doesn't finish the job.

3

Between and within variation

Plain English. Differences across people versus changes within one person over time.

Precisely. Controlling for the individual separates your variation into two forms:

  • Between variation — variation between individuals. Austria has different geography than England does.
  • Within variation — variation within an individual over time. Austria's political relationship with Germany changes from year to year.

A variable can have both. Politics has a between component (Austria differs from England) and a within component (Austria changes year to year).

The key of fixed effects: when you control for the individual, you control away all the between variation, and only the within variation is left.

So if your back doors are made of between variation, they close. If they're within, they don't.

4

Worked example: the exercise table

Plain English. Split each person's numbers into "their average" and "how they varied around it."

Worked example. Exercise ratings on a 1–10 scale:

20192020Average
You576
Me433.5

The between variation is the difference in averages: your 6 against my 3.5. You got more exercise on average than I did.

The within variation is each year's value minus that person's own mean:

20192020
You5 − 6 = −17 − 6 = +1
Me4 − 3.5 = +0.53 − 3.5 = −0.5

Fixed effects gets rid of the difference between 6 and 3.5 entirely. It looks only at the within variation — only at the fact that in 2020 you were exercising more than you typically do, and I was exercising less than I typically do.

5

The graphical version, and the punchline

Plain English. Give every person their own origin, then look only at movement around it.

Worked example. Data on you and me across several years: exercise hours per week, and colds per year. Now give each person their own set of axes, centred on their own average exercise hours and average colds — a personal "home location."

Every point is now read relative to that person's axes. Some years you exercised more than you typically do, some years less; some years you had more colds than usual, some fewer.

Take a point that's a fairly middling number of exercise hours overall — it might be a particularly low number for you. It's a completely different comparison depending on whether you're looking at the overall variation (between plus within) or relative to your own average (within only).

  • Strip out the individual points and compare only your average against mine → that isolates the between variation.
  • Look at everything relative to the individual axes → that isolates the within variation.

The punchline. In the overall graph the relationship has an upward slope. Look just at Nick's own data, relative to his own average, and there's a vague downward slope. Between and within can point in opposite directions.

Say it out loud

Here's a problem you hit constantly: you draw your causal diagram, it tells you exactly what to control for — and you don't have that data. So what now?

Fixed effects has a genuinely lovely answer. Suppose I need to control for where you were born, and I don't have it. But I do have data on you — measured over several years. And here's the thing: your birthplace never changes. It's fixed inside the category "you." So if I control for your identity, I've automatically controlled for your birthplace — and your childhood, and your parents, and everything else about you that doesn't change. Without measuring any of it.

Real example. Does a visit from the German chancellor boost your country's trade with Germany? Loads of things confound that — geography, your history with Germany, your culture, your current politics. Try measuring "the culture of a population." You can't.

But geography doesn't change. Austria's had the same geography for decades. Neither does deep history. So bundle all of it into one variable — country — and control for that. All those back doors close at once.

Not all of them, mind. Current politics changes year to year, so that one's still open and needs its own control.

The way to hold this is between versus within. Between variation is how you differ from me. Within variation is how you differ from yourself, year to year. Fixed effects deletes all the between variation and keeps only the within.

And here's what made it click for me. Take exercise and colds. Overall, the graph slopes up. But look at just my data, compared to my own average — it slopes down. Between and within can genuinely tell opposite stories.

Recall drill

QWhat problem does this section of the book address?

That your causal diagram tells you to control for something you don't have data on.

QState the key insight of fixed effects.

If an unmeasured variable is constant within a broader category you do observe, controlling for that category controls for everything inside it.

QWhat kind of data does fixed effects require?

Panel data — the same individuals measured repeatedly over time, whether people, companies, or countries.

QHow does controlling for your identity control for your birthplace?

Because your birthplace is fixed within the category of you, so a control for you absorbs everything unique to you that doesn't change over time.

QIn the chancellor-visit example, which confounders get bundled into "country"?

Geography, history with Germany, and the culture of the population — all effectively constant within a country over the timescale of the data.

QWhich confounder in that example is not closed by country fixed effects, and why?

Current politics, because a country's relationship with Germany changes from year to year, so it has within variation.

QDefine between variation.

Variation between individuals — Austria having different geography than England.

QDefine within variation.

Variation within an individual over time — Austria's political relationship with Germany changing year to year.

QCan a variable have both?

Yes. Politics differs across countries and changes within a country over time.

QWhat does controlling for the individual do to these two components?

It removes all the between variation, leaving only the within variation.

QYou: 5 in 2019 and 7 in 2020. Me: 4 and 3. Give the averages and the between variation.

Your average is 6, mine is 3.5. The between variation is that difference — you exercise more on average.

QGive the within variation for both people.

You: −1 in 2019 and +1 in 2020. Me: +0.5 in 2019 and −0.5 in 2020.

QWhat does fixed effects do with the 6-versus-3.5 difference?

Discards it entirely, looking only at how each person varied around their own average.

QIn the graphical version, what does giving each person their own axes accomplish?

Every point is measured relative to that person's own averages, so you're seeing only within variation.

QWhat's the punchline about slopes in the exercise-and-colds graph?

The overall relationship slopes upward, but within Nick's own data it slopes vaguely downward — between and within can point in opposite directions.

QDoes fixed effects require you to know what the unmeasured confounders are?

No. Anything constant within the individual is absorbed whether or not you can name or measure it.

Glossary

Fixed effects
controlling for the individual, thereby absorbing everything constant within that individual over time.
Panel data
repeated observations of the same individuals over time.
Between variation
differences across individuals.
Within variation
changes within a single individual over time.
Time-constant variable
a characteristic that doesn't change within an individual, and is therefore absorbed by fixed effects.
