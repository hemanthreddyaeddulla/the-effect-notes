Ep 44Power Analysis

▶ Watch the video 12:20

Power analysis asks whether you have enough firepower to detect what you're looking for — you fix four of true effect size, variation in treatment, other variation in the outcome, desired precision, and sample size, and solve for the fifth — and if the answer says you need 100,000 observations and you have 5,000, don't run the study.

The 30-second version

  • Statistics is not magical. Tiny effects, little variation in treatment, lots of noise, and high evidence standards all make its job harder.
  • An underpowered study gives imprecise estimates, probably misses a real effect — and if it does find one, that's likely a fluke.
  • Power analysis takes five quantities, holds four fixed, and solves for the fifth.
  • Most common: solve for minimum necessary sample size, or for minimum detectable effect size.
  • Calculators handle simple cases like a two-group randomized trial. Simulation handles everything else.
  • Simulation method: the usual loop, wrapped in another loop over different sample sizes or effect sizes.

Why this exists

Statistics is not magical, and it's bad at some things. Four situations make its job harder:

Tiny effects. Want to know whether a parachute saves your life if you jump out of a plane? One or two observations will do it. Want to know whether a pill makes your hair grow a fraction of a centimetre longer? You'll need an enormous number. Tiny effects are simply very difficult to spot.

Not much variation. Statistics is all about studying variation — if there isn't any, there's nothing to figure out. Does going to the moon make you smarter? Only a couple of people have been. There's just not enough variation in that treatment to study, at least not statistically.

Noise relative to signal. Does the kind of pencil you use affect your exam score? Suppose it genuinely does — a nicer pencil helps a little. But an awful lot of other things determine exam performance, and all that other stuff makes the real effect hard to see.

High evidence standards. If you just want a general idea of how much the pencil helps, a certain sample might do. If you want to be really precise, you'll need a much bigger one.

The ideas

1

The question: is it too hard?

Plain English. Do you have enough data to answer this at all — or should you go home?

Precisely. You don't want to perform a study if you simply don't have the ability to estimate the thing you want to estimate. Trying with too small a sample is a bad idea:

  • You'll get very imprecise estimates.
  • There's a decent chance you won't find an effect even if one truly exists.
  • And here's the nasty one: if you do find an effect, there's a decent chance it's a fluke. With a very tiny sample, managing to reject the null pretty much only happens when you happen to get weird data.

Anchor image. Fishing with a net whose holes are bigger than the fish. Catching one tells you less than you'd think.

Where it breaks. So it's a good idea to know you have enough sample size before you start.

2

The five quantities

Plain English. Fix four, solve for the fifth.

Precisely. Power analysis looks at five things:

QuantityThe question
True effect sizeHow big is the actual effect you're trying to study?
Variation in treatmentDo you have plenty of treated and untreated people, or only a couple treated? The more treatment moves around, the easier it is to see whether it does anything.
Other variation in the outcomeHow much else is going on — all the determinants of the test score other than the pencil?
Statistical precisionIf there truly is an effect, do you want to reject the null 80% of the time? 90%? Do you want standard errors below a certain size?
Sample sizeHow much data do you have to work with?

Set four constant, and it tells you how big the fifth needs to be.

The two common uses:

  • Minimum necessary sample size. How many observations do you need to hit your precision goal, given a certain true effect size and the variation involved?
  • Minimum detectable effect size. You know your sample size — how big does the true effect have to be for you to detect it? With 30 observations you can only detect genuinely huge effects; with a million, you can pick up much smaller ones with decent precision.

You could solve for the other three, but these two are far and away the most common.

3

Why do it with simulation

Plain English. Calculators handle the textbook case. Simulation handles yours.

Precisely. Power analysis calculators exist and will do the work for you — but only in the settings whose designers thought about them ahead of time. A simple randomized controlled experiment with two groups and random assignment to one or the other? The calculator will handle that.

But in observational data, where you want to incorporate complex features — the hierarchical structure of your data, or the complex ways treatment gets assigned — you'll want a simulation. Simulation lets you incorporate all the features of your actual data generating process.

4

The procedure

Plain English. The normal simulation loop, with a second loop wrapped around it.

Precisely. Steps 1–4 are the same as any simulation:

  1. Create data with your setting in place — how you think the data is generated, the true effect size you picked, the variation in treatment, the other variation in the outcome, how much noise you have to deal with.
  2. Apply the estimation method you're planning to use.
  3. Store the estimate — and if you're looking at statistical power, meaning how often you reject a false null, store whether you rejected the null.
  4. Repeat many times to get the distribution of estimates, or the rejection rate.

Step 5 is what makes it a power analysis: take that entire iteration procedure and wrap another iteration around it, running the whole thing again with different sample sizes, or different true effect sizes, and see what changes.

Worked example. Suppose the true effect is that changing your pencil raises your test score by 5 points.

Sample sizeTrue effectResult
100 students+5 pointsReject the null 70% of the time
1,000 students+5 points?
10,000 students+5 points?
100,000 students+5 points?

Run each and you learn your statistical power at that effect size. Or hold the sample size constant and vary the effect size instead.

Either way you end up with your minimum detectable effect size or your minimum necessary sample size — because you tried a bunch of settings and saw which ones give a reasonable level of statistical precision or power.

And the kicker. If it tells you that you need 100,000 students to estimate the effect of the pencil, and you only have access to 5,000 — then you probably shouldn't do that study. It is not a good idea to run poorly powered studies.

Say it out loud

Power analysis is asking one question: do I have enough firepower to find what I'm looking for?

Because statistics isn't magic. Some things are just hard. Tiny effects — if I want to know whether a parachute helps when you jump out of a plane, one observation will do; if I want to know whether a pill grows your hair a fraction of a centimetre, I need a fortune's worth of data. Little variation in treatment — does going to the moon make you smarter? Only a handful of people have been, so there's nothing to study. And noise — maybe your pencil really does help your exam score a bit, but so does everything else in your life, and that drowns it.

Now here's why this matters, and it's not what people expect. Everyone knows a small sample might miss a real effect. The sneakier problem is that if a tiny sample does show you something significant — it's probably a fluke. You only clear the bar with weird data.

So power analysis takes five things: the true effect size, how much your treatment varies, how much other noise is in the outcome, how precise you want to be, and your sample size. Fix four, solve for the fifth. Usually you're asking "how many observations do I need?" or "given what I've got, how big would an effect have to be for me to see it?"

And you can do it with simulation — it's the same loop as before, just wrapped in a second loop. Run your simulation at 100 students, then 1,000, then 10,000, and watch how often you correctly reject the null.

Then the punchline. If it tells you that you need a hundred thousand students and you can get five thousand — don't run the study. That's the whole point of asking first.

Recall drill

QWhat is power analysis asking?

Whether you have enough observations and sample size to actually estimate the thing you want to estimate.

QName the four situations that make statistics' job harder.

Tiny true effects; little variation in the treatment; a lot of noise relative to signal; and high standards for evidence.

QGive the parachute and hair-growth contrast.

Whether a parachute saves your life needs one or two observations. Whether a pill grows your hair a fraction of a centimetre needs an enormous number — tiny effects are hard to spot.

QWhy can't you study whether going to the moon makes you smarter?

Only a couple of people have been, so there's almost no variation in the treatment, and statistics studies variation.

QGive the three problems with running an underpowered study.

Very imprecise estimates; a decent chance of missing a real effect; and a decent chance that anything you do find is a fluke.

QWhy is a significant result from a tiny sample suspicious?

Because with very little data you generally only clear the significance bar when you happen to draw unusually weird data.

QList the five quantities in a power analysis.

True effect size, variation in treatment, other variation in the outcome, statistical precision, and sample size.

QHow does a power analysis work mechanically?

You hold four of the five quantities constant and solve for how big the fifth needs to be.

QWhat are the two most common questions asked?

What's the minimum necessary sample size, and what's the minimum detectable effect size?

QExplain minimum detectable effect size.

Given a fixed sample size, how big the true effect must be for you to detect it — 30 observations can only detect very large effects, while a million can detect much smaller ones.

QWhen is a power calculator sufficient, and when do you need simulation?

A calculator handles simple settings like a two-group randomized experiment. Simulation is needed for complex features like hierarchical data structure or complicated treatment assignment.

QWhat extra step makes a simulation a power analysis?

Wrapping the whole simulation loop in another iteration that reruns it at different sample sizes or different true effect sizes.

QWhat do you store on each iteration if you're measuring statistical power?

Whether or not you rejected the null hypothesis.

QYour power analysis says you need 100,000 students and you can get 5,000. What should you do?

Not run the study. Running poorly powered studies is a bad idea.

Glossary

Power analysis
determining whether a study design has enough data to detect the effect it's looking for.
Statistical power
how often you correctly reject a false null hypothesis.
Minimum detectable effect size
the smallest true effect a given sample size can reliably detect.
Minimum necessary sample size
the number of observations needed to reach a target level of precision.
Underpowered study
one whose sample size is too small to estimate the effect of interest reliably.
