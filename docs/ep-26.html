Ep 26Regression and Sampling Variation

▶ Watch the video 21:00
On this pageThe 30-second versionWhy this existsThe ideasTrapsSay it out loudRecall drillConnectionsGlossary

An OLS coefficient is normally distributed across samples, centred on the population parameter, with a spread — the standard error — driven by how well your model predicts, how much your X varies, and how many observations you have; and hypothesis testing uses that distribution to ask whether your starting assumption survives, which is a much narrower question than people treat it as.

The 30-second version

  • Set identification aside. You don't have all the universe's data, so your estimate carries sampling variation.
  • Across samples, an OLS coefficient follows a normal distribution, centred on the population parameter.
  • Its standard deviation is the standard error, and three things drive it: model prediction error σ (up top), the variance of X, and the sample size n (both below).
  • All three are about information. More information, more precision.
  • Hypothesis testing: assume a null, build the sampling distribution it implies, see how unlikely your result was. Either something unlikely happened, or the assumption was wrong.
  • It does not tell you the probability your estimate is correct.
  • Nick's rant: the null is arbitrary, significance depends on sample size, insignificance can be the right answer, and never change your model to chase stars.

Why this exists

You're doing statistics, which means you don't have all of the universe's data. The estimate you got isn't a perfect representation of the truth — ideally it's a representation of the truth plus some sampling variation and noise built in.

So you've run a regression and got a coefficient. What does that estimate actually mean? To answer that you need to know how it would have bounced around if you'd drawn different data.

The ideas

1

The sampling distribution of an OLS coefficient

Plain English. Collect data, estimate, repeat forever, and plot all the estimates. You get a bell curve centred on the truth.

Precisely. An OLS coefficient follows a normal distribution across samples — provable, though not proved here. Its mean is the population parameter: what you'd get if you did have all the data in the universe and estimated your model. So on average, OLS gives you the population parameter.

Anchor image. A well-calibrated archer. Individual arrows scatter; the centre of the scatter is where they aimed.

Worked example. You estimate 2.5. You don't know the population parameter — maybe it's 2.5, maybe 3, maybe 2. But you can say the truth is probably not 10. That's the whole game: using the distribution to make claims about the population parameter from your single estimate.

Where it breaks. "On average OLS gives you the population parameter" is not the same as giving you what you want. That population parameter can itself be biased if you haven't got causal identification. This episode is only about sampling variation.

2

The standard error and its three ingredients

Plain English. The standard error is how wide the bell curve is. Three things make it narrow, and all three are versions of "you have more information."

Precisely. A standard error is the standard deviation of a sampling distribution. For an OLS coefficient it has three components:

ComponentWhereWhat it means
σ — prediction errornumeratorThe variance of the error term, estimated by the variance of the residuals. How closely your model can predict the outcome.
Variance of XdenominatorHow much your predictor moves around.
Sample size ndenominatorHow many observations you have.

σ — model precision. If your model predicts the outcome very accurately, you know a lot about what's going on, so you can state your parameter with real confidence. Small residuals → small σ → small standard error. Knowing a lot about the outcome means your coefficient won't vary much from sample to sample.

Variance of X. You can say more about a variable's effect if it moves around a lot — and the intuition is straightforward. If X moves a lot and Y stays still, you know nothing is happening. But if X barely moves and Y doesn't change much either, maybe you just can't see it. It's simply hard to study things with statistics that don't vary. More movement along X makes it far easier to see whether there's movement along Y.

Sample size. More observations, more information, more precision. A large sample gives a small standard error because you have a lot to build inferences from.

Anchor image. Reading a sign in fog. It gets clearer if the fog thins (σ), if you can walk back and forth to see it from angles (variance of X), or if a hundred people read it and compare (n).

3

Why statisticians care about distributions at all

Plain English. Knowing how a thing varies lets you rule out claims about the truth.

Worked example. You estimate an OLS coefficient of 2. Could that have come from a distribution with population parameter 100 and standard deviation 1? Essentially never — you'd basically never see a value that low. And yet you did. So that's the wrong sampling distribution.

4

Hypothesis testing, carefully

Plain English. Assume something, work out what data you'd expect, and see whether what you got is absurd under that assumption.

Precisely. The procedure:

  1. Make an assumption about the world — the null hypothesis. Say the true population parameter is 100.
  2. Build the sampling distribution that assumption implies. You know it's normal, you know it's centred on the assumed parameter, and you know the standard deviation — so you know exactly what the sampling distribution would be, and how likely any particular estimate is under it.
  3. Compare your actual estimate to it.
  4. If your outcome was very unlikely under that distribution, exactly two things could have happened: something very unlikely just occurred, or your starting assumption was wrong.

Worked example. Null hypothesis: the average person is 5 feet 7 inches tall. That gives you a sampling distribution of sample averages centred on 5'7", varying a bit above and below from sample to sample.

Now you draw a sample and it averages 3 feet. Under the null, that is wildly unlikely from random chance alone. So: either something extraordinary happened, or 5'7" was wrong.

Where it breaks — the part people get wrong. Hypothesis testing does not tell you how likely it is that the outcome you got is correct. All it tells you is whether the assumption you started with is unlikely to still be true.

The p-value. The probability, under the null's sampling distribution, of getting your result or something even weirder — in either direction for a two-sided test. Null of 5'7", result of 3 feet: the probability of getting 3 feet or lower, plus something similarly strange in the other direction.

The cutoff. Choose a threshold; if the p-value falls below it, reject the null. The logic: at under 5%, the trade-off between "something weird happened" and "the assumption was wrong" tilts toward the assumption being wrong. At p = 0.25, that distribution produces such results a quarter of the time — still not likely, but not unlikely enough to abandon your starting assumption.

5

The rant: why not to take significance too seriously

Plain English. Nick keeps using significance testing because it's ubiquitous and it's a good way to think about sampling variation. He'd rather you didn't take it too seriously.

1 · The null you chose is arbitrary. The most common null is zero. Often that isn't an interesting question — in social science pretty much all relationships are non-zero at least a little, so rejecting a null of zero rejects an assumption nobody expected to be true. Big whoop.

More usefully: ask which nulls you can't reject. Sure, you rejected zero — but can you reject 0.01? 0.02? 0.03? If the truth were 0.03, would you still say "well, at least it's not zero"? For that, use a confidence interval, which shows the range of nulls you can't reject. Different question entirely.

2 · Significance isn't only about whether an effect is real. It also depends on sample size and everything else determining precision. Plenty of effects are statistically significant with a large sample and insignificant with a small one — the true effect didn't change, the sample size did. Significance is about whether you have evidence the null is wrong, not about whether it is wrong.

3 · An insignificant result can be the correct result. We talk about a significant finding as a "positive result", as though it were a success. But if there is no effect, then finding an insignificant result is the success — you found the truth.

4 · Never change your model to get significance. Two reasons:

  • It makes the test wrong. If you got p = 0.15, changed the model, and got p = 0.04 — you have not found statistical significance, because every one of those calculations assumes you didn't do the thing you just did. Your p-value is wrong, and you may not be significant at your chosen level at all.
  • With enough choices, you'll always find one. Many decisions go into an analysis, and if you try enough you'll find a way to make results significant. Those choices don't change the truth — but they change your results. You want to reflect the truth, and to let the data prove you wrong.

Statistics professors always say this, and everyone remembers the opposite.

5 · Cutoffs are strange in general. Isn't it odd that p = 0.049 is significant and p = 0.051 is not? That isn't a real difference between two results, and yet you'd make substantially different choices on the basis of it. It's artificially induced by having a cutoff at all — and it doesn't matter what the cutoff is, only that there is one. Statistics works on sliding scales, mores and lesses, not yeses and noes.

The recommendation: think about the precision of your estimate. What's your standard error? What does your confidence interval look like? That gives a far more nuanced view of what you can learn about the world than worrying about whether you're above or below some arbitrarily chosen cutoff.

Say it out loud

Forget identification for a minute. You ran a regression, you got a number. What does that number mean, given you only had one sample?

Here's the useful fact: if you gathered data, estimated, gathered different data, estimated again, forever — those estimates form a normal distribution, centred on the population parameter, the thing you'd get with all the world's data. So if I estimate 2.5, I don't know the truth — but I can say it's probably not 10.

How confidently I can say that depends on the standard error, which is just the standard deviation of that distribution. Three things drive it, and they're all "how much do I know?"

How well my model predicts — small residuals mean I understand what's going on. How much X varies — and this one's intuitive: if X barely moves and Y doesn't move either, maybe I just couldn't see it. You can't study something that doesn't vary. And sample size — more data, more precision.

Then hypothesis testing. You assume something — the null — build the exact sampling distribution it implies, and check whether your result is absurd under it. Null: average height is 5'7". You sample and get 3 feet. Either something extraordinary happened, or 5'7" was wrong. That's the whole logic.

And note what it does not say. It doesn't tell you the probability your estimate is right. It only tells you whether your starting assumption survived.

Now — my rant. The null you pick is almost always zero, and in social science essentially nothing is exactly zero, so you've rejected something nobody believed. Big whoop. Ask instead: can you reject 0.01? 0.02? That's a confidence interval, and it's the better question.

Significance also depends on your sample size. The same true effect is significant in a big sample and not in a small one — the world didn't change, your data did.

And please: if your result comes out insignificant, do not go change the model until it isn't. First, that breaks the test — every p-value calculation assumes you didn't do that. Second, make enough choices and you'll always find one that works, and none of those choices changed the truth. If there's really no effect, finding nothing is the right answer.

Recall drill

QWhat distribution does an OLS coefficient follow across samples, and where is it centred?

A normal distribution, centred on the population parameter — what you'd get with all the data in the universe.

QWhat is a standard error?

The standard deviation of a sampling distribution.

QName the three components of an OLS standard error and where each sits.

σ, the model's prediction error (numerator); the variance of X (denominator); the sample size n (denominator).

QWhy does more variance in X reduce the standard error?

Because if X moves a lot it's easy to see whether Y moves with it. If X barely varies, you may simply be unable to detect an effect — statistics can't study things that don't vary.

Q"On average, OLS gives you the population parameter." Why isn't that enough?

Because the population parameter itself can be biased if you haven't achieved causal identification. Unbiasedness for the population parameter is not the same as getting the causal effect.

QDescribe the logic of hypothesis testing in four steps.

Assume a null; build the sampling distribution it implies; see how likely your result is under it; conclude that either something very unlikely happened or the assumption was wrong.

QWhat does hypothesis testing not tell you?

How likely it is that the estimate you got is correct. It only speaks to whether your starting assumption is still tenable.

QDefine a p-value.

The probability, under the null's sampling distribution, of getting your result or something even more extreme — in either direction, for a two-sided test.

QWhy is a null of zero often uninteresting?

In social science almost all relationships are non-zero at least a little, so rejecting zero rejects an assumption nobody expected to be true.

QWhat should you look at instead of a single sharp test?

A confidence interval — the range of nulls you can't reject — and more generally the precision of your estimate.

QWhy can the same true effect be significant in one study and not another?

Because significance depends on sample size and other determinants of precision, not only on whether an effect exists.

QGive the two reasons not to modify your model to obtain significance.

It invalidates the test — the p-value calculation assumes you didn't do that — and with enough analytical choices you can always find one that works, changing your results without changing the truth.

QWhy is a statistically insignificant result sometimes a success?

If there is genuinely no effect, finding insignificance means you found the truth.

QWhat's odd about a 0.05 cutoff?

p = 0.049 is significant and p = 0.051 is not, though there's no real difference between them. The sharp boundary is an artefact of having a cutoff at all.

Glossary

Sampling variation
the variation in an estimate arising from having drawn one particular sample.
Population parameter
the value you'd estimate if you had all the data in the universe.
Standard error
the standard deviation of a sampling distribution.
Null hypothesis
the assumption about the world used to build the sampling distribution you test against.
p-value
the probability of your result or something more extreme, given the null's sampling distribution.
Confidence interval
the range of null values you would not reject.
