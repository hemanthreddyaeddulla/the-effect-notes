Ep 58Instrumental Variable Validity

▶ Watch the video 13:00
On this pageThe 30-second versionWhy this existsThe ideasTrapsSay it out loudRecall drillConnectionsGlossary

Validity demands more than "no back doors" — the instrument must be unrelated to the outcome except through the treatment, meaning it can't have other front doors either — and it's so hard to defend that instruments once accepted, like rainfall, have since fallen apart.

The 30-second version

  • Validity: the instrument is a source of random variation, at least once you've controlled for a few things.
  • It's stronger than no back doors: the instrument must also cause nothing else that reaches the outcome.
  • At any IV seminar, essentially every question is about whether you believe the instrument is valid.
  • Decades ago people barely worried. Now they're far more sceptical — and previously accepted instruments have collapsed.
  • Rainfall was the standard instrument for agricultural productivity. It's correlated across neighbours, and Sarsons found it predicts productivity even where dams make water access irrelevant.
  • Validity is inherently a theoretical question about your diagram.
  • Canonical designs — judge assignment, draft lotteries, twins, wind direction, Mendelian randomization — are the ones that have survived scrutiny.

Why this exists

Validity is the assumption that the instrument has no back doors between it and the outcome — or that you can close them all — so you can treat it as a source of random variation in your treatment. That's what you're after: treatment might not always be randomly assigned, but if it's randomly assigned some of the time, you can isolate that portion and use just it, exactly as in a randomized trial where you don't use all the data, only the part you actually randomized in.

The ideas

1

Validity is stronger than "no back doors"

Plain English. The instrument must reach the outcome only through treatment. Not around it, and not by any other route forward either.

Precisely. Validity requires the instrument to be unrelated to the outcome except through the treatment. That is a stronger condition than merely having no back doors.

The diagram you're claiming:

There's a treatment whose effect on an outcome you want. There's some back door for the treatment you can't control for — call it the annoying thing. And there's a source of random variation affecting whether, or how much, treatment gets assigned. You isolate the path randomization → treatment → outcome.

Two requirements:

  1. Nothing else between randomization and outcome. No back doors from the instrument to the outcome — or if there are, you must be able to control and close them so you get back to this diagram.
  2. Randomization cannot cause anything else. If it does, then when you isolate the part of treatment determined by randomization, you'll also pick up a bit of that other thing.

So it's stronger than "no back doors": there also can't be any other front doors from the instrument to the outcome except through the treatment.

Anchor image. A pipe from a reservoir to a tank. Nothing may feed the pipe from elsewhere, and the pipe must not branch off to anywhere else along the way.

2

Why this is genuinely hard

Plain English. In the social sciences, nothing is unrelated to anything.

Precisely. Go to any seminar where someone presents a paper using instrumental variables and pretty much all the questions will be about whether the instrument is actually valid. Can you really believe your chosen instrument is exogenous?

It's a tough call, especially in the social sciences or anything involving people or complex systems. If everything is related to everything else, how can one variable happen to be sitting out there minding its own business, completely unrelated to everything important? Seems unlikely.

Where standards have moved. Go back a couple of decades and you'll find people writing IV papers without worrying about this much at all — here's a variable, I'm going to pretend it's random — and not examining it further. These days people are a great deal more sceptical, and there are a number of instruments that were once considered fine and exogenous which people have come to doubt.

3

Worked example: rainfall falls apart

Plain English. Rain looks perfectly random. It isn't clean.

Worked example. People used to use rainfall as an instrument for agricultural productivity all the time. Controlling for the part of the world you're in and perhaps the season, it seems kind of random whether it rains more or less in a given year — and that makes farms more or less productive. So you could study the effect of agricultural productivity on war and conflict, on economic growth, on urbanization, on whatever, and just use rainfall as the instrument. If rainfall really is exogenous — and surely it is? — you're set.

Problem 1 — spatial correlation. Rainfall is correlated between people who live next to each other. If it's raining on your farm, it's probably raining on your neighbour's. That affects their outcomes, which affects the outcome you're looking at, in a way that isn't just through your own productivity. That's a back door through your neighbour's productivity.

Problem 2 — the dams result. A paper by Heather Sarsons looks at the effect of rainfall on agricultural productivity and finds it is pretty much the same in regions that have dams for water irrigation and in ones that don't.

That doesn't make sense given how you'd think rain affects agricultural productivity. If you have a dam, access to water isn't really the main constraint — and yet rainfall still heavily predicts agricultural productivity. Which suggests something else is going on, something you can't confidently call a source of exogenous variation.

4

Validity is a theoretical question

Plain English. No test settles it. You have to argue for the diagram.

Precisely. Thinking about whether an instrument is valid is inherently theoretical. Can you make the claim that the causal diagram really looks like the one above — no back door between instrument and outcome, or one you can measure and close? And that the additional front door through some other treatment also doesn't exist? You have to be able to assert both.

5

Canonical designs

Plain English. Certain instrument-and-treatment pairs have been used so often and scrutinised so hard that people still believe them.

Precisely. These are canonical instrumental variables designs — the same instrument/treatment pairing used across many contexts, heavily examined, and still generally trusted, or at least understood well enough that the ways they might fail are known and perhaps correctable.

DesignThe instrument
Non-compliance in an experimentRandom assignment to treatment, instrumenting for actually getting treated. Very common and well accepted.
The draftRandom assignment to being drafted, based on birth date — roughly random, or at least based on things irrelevant to the outcomes.
Judges and monitorsRandom assignment to a harsh or lenient judge at trial, which affects your sentence. Assignment is random in many criminal justice systems.
Compulsory schooling ageHow long compulsory schooling lasts in your area.
Bartik / shift-shareHow large different industries are in different areas, so a nationwide bump to an industry hits one area harder than another.
Immigrant enclavesThe original wave of immigrants settles in one location, and the next wave tends to go to the same place.
TwinsYou planned for two children and had twins, so now you have three — a random determinant of family size.
Natural disastersA hurricane that narrowly passed your town is basically random.
Wind directionWhich way the wind blows on a given day.
Mendelian randomizationThe particular genes you got from the mix of your parents, instrumenting for genetic characteristics.

The two families these fall into:

  1. Actual explicit randomization — random assignment to judges, random draft order, and so on.
  2. Things that seem like they should be completely random, or at least entirely undetermined by the system in your model. It's almost strange that they matter at all, and yet they do.

Worked example of the second kind. Wind direction for pollution. If you live in a city where the pollution tends to come from the east side, wind blowing one way brings it in and the other way carries it out. That's pretty random — and why would the wind affect anything else in particular?

6

The summary

Precisely. Validity has to hold. You must be able to say that, at least conditional on your controls, your instrument is a source of random assignment — so that you're isolating a randomly assigned portion of the treatment and can see its effect on the outcome. If that's not true, instrumental variables won't work, and there's not a lot you can do about it.

Finding a valid instrument is very tricky. Many instruments people used to think were valid aren't much believed now. And it can be genuinely difficult to convince anyone that a new and interesting instrument really is exogenous — you'll have to do a great deal of theoretical, conceptual, contextual, and data work.

If you're looking for an instrument for your own treatment, it's worth going through the chapter's list of canonical designs to see which characteristics keep recurring, so you know what to look for.

Say it out loud

Validity is the assumption that makes instrumental variables work, and it's harder than it first sounds — because it's stronger than "no back doors."

What you need is that your instrument reaches the outcome only through the treatment. So two things. Nothing can feed into your instrument that also reaches the outcome — that's the back door part. And your instrument can't cause anything else that reaches the outcome either. Because if it does, when you isolate the bit of treatment that the instrument explains, you'll drag that other thing along with it.

And this is genuinely difficult. Go to a seminar where someone presents an IV paper and I promise you, almost every question will be about whether the instrument is valid. Because think about it — in social science everything is connected to everything. What are the odds you've found one variable sitting off to the side, minding its own business, unrelated to anything that matters?

Best cautionary tale: rainfall. For years it was the instrument for agricultural productivity. Rain's random, right? More rain, more crops, and now you can study what agricultural productivity does to conflict or growth or urbanisation.

Except — rain falls on your neighbour's farm too, so their productivity moves with yours, and that reaches your outcome by a completely different route. And there's a paper by Heather Sarsons finding that rainfall predicts agricultural productivity just as strongly in regions with dams as without. Which makes no sense if the mechanism is water access. So rainfall is doing something else, and nobody's sure what.

There's no test for this. It's a theoretical argument about your diagram. Which is why people lean on canonical designs — random judge assignment, draft lotteries, twins, wind direction, Mendelian randomization. Things that have been attacked for decades and are still standing. They come in two flavours: genuine explicit randomization, or things so obviously outside your system that it's almost odd they matter at all.

Recall drill

QState the validity assumption precisely.

The instrument is unrelated to the outcome except through the treatment.

QWhy is that stronger than "no back doors"?

It also forbids the instrument from causing anything else that reaches the outcome — no other front doors, not just no back doors.

QWhat happens if the instrument causes something else?

When you isolate the part of treatment determined by the instrument, you also pick up part of that other thing.

QWhat does the diagram you're claiming look like?

Instrument → treatment → outcome, with an uncontrollable back door affecting the treatment, and no path from the instrument to the outcome except via treatment.

QWhat dominates the questions at an IV seminar?

Whether the instrument is genuinely valid — whether you can believe it's exogenous.

QWhy is validity especially hard in social science?

Because everything is related to everything else, so it's implausible that one variable sits apart, unrelated to everything important.

QHow have standards changed over time?

A couple of decades ago people would assert an instrument was random without much scrutiny. Now people are far more sceptical, and several once-accepted instruments are doubted.

QWhat was rainfall used as an instrument for?

Agricultural productivity — since it seems random whether it rains more or less in a given year, conditional on region and season.

QGive the first problem with rainfall.

It's correlated between neighbouring farms, so your neighbour's productivity moves with yours and reaches your outcome by a route other than your own productivity.

QGive the Sarsons finding and why it's damaging.

Rainfall's effect on agricultural productivity is roughly the same in regions with irrigation dams as without — which shouldn't happen if the mechanism is water access, suggesting something else is driving the relationship.

QWhat kind of question is instrument validity?

An inherently theoretical one — can you defend the claim that your causal diagram has no back door and no alternative front door from instrument to outcome?

QWhat is a canonical instrumental variables design?

An instrument/treatment pairing used across many contexts and heavily scrutinised, which people still generally believe is exogenous.

QName five canonical designs.

Random assignment under non-compliance; the draft lottery; random judge assignment; compulsory schooling age; Bartik/shift-share; immigrant enclaves; twins; natural disasters; wind direction; Mendelian randomization.

QWhat are the two families canonical designs fall into?

Those with actual explicit randomization, and those involving things that seem entirely outside the system being modelled.

QGive the wind direction example.

If a city's pollution comes from the east, the wind blowing one way brings it in and the other way carries it out — pretty random, and it's hard to see why wind would affect anything else.

QWhat happens if validity doesn't hold?

Instrumental variables won't work, and there's not a lot you can do about it.
