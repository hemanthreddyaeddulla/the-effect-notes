Ep 70Partial Identification

▶ Watch the video 15:00

Every design so far demanded a strong assumption you probably don't believe — so weaken it, accept that you'll get a range instead of a number, and answer the question that actually matters: not which assumptions must I make to get one answer, but which assumptions do I actually find reasonable, and how far do they get me?

The 30-second version

  • Every design rests on assumptions: that you closed all back doors, that your instrument is valid, that parallel trends holds.
  • Stronger assumptions get you more — and are more likely to be wrong. The sun rises; the sun rises between 6 and 7; the sun rises at 6:52.
  • Point identification gives exactly one answer. Set identification — partial identification — gives a range.
  • The alternative to a range isn't a better number. It's either an unbelievable assumption or nothing at all.
  • Nick considers it powerful and underutilised, because you can't sit around waiting for a quasi-experiment to happen.
  • Adding assumptions narrows the bounds. You choose which ones you believe rather than which ones you need.
  • New in the second edition — this chapter and these videos were added later.

Why this exists

Doing causal inference takes data and a model of the world — an understanding that lets you draw your causal diagram. The diagram tells you which back doors to close, and you close them by controlling for variables or by using a quasi-experimental design like instrumental variables or regression discontinuity.

But each of those requires assumptions:

ApproachThe assumption
Controlling for variablesYou have measured and controlled for every single variable needed to close all the back doors
Instrumental variablesThere is no front door path from the instrument to the outcome
Difference-in-differencesParallel trends holds

Here's the problem: do you actually believe all of those? It's a very strong claim to say not merely that you found and closed some of the back doors, but that you found and closed all of them — that the relationship between treatment and outcome, beyond what you've already shut down, is literally zero.

The ideas

1

Strong and weak assumptions

Plain English. The more you assume, the more you get — and the more likely you're wrong.

Precisely. A strong assumption assumes more of the world than a weak one.

Worked example — the sunrise.

AssumptionStrength
"The sun will rise tomorrow"Weak. Probably true. Nobody would question it.
"The sun will rise between 6 and 7am"Stronger. You're assuming more about the world, even with good reason.
"The sun will rise at 6:52am"Stronger still.

The trade-off. The stronger your assumptions, the more you get out of your analysis. If you're willing to assume there is literally no open back door between treatment and outcome, that lets you get away with a great deal. If you simply assume your treatment is randomly assigned — a very strong assumption — you can look at the raw relationship and treat it like an experiment. Nothing is stopping you.

It's just going to be wrong if your assumption is wrong. And the stronger the assumption, the more likely it is to be wrong. "6:52am" is more likely wrong than "between 6 and 7," which is more likely wrong than "at all."

2

Point identification and set identification

Plain English. One answer, or a range of answers.

Precisely. Partial identification takes an analysis requiring a strong assumption, weakens the assumption, and accepts that the answer will be less precise — but you still get an answer, and now you can talk far more responsibly about how likely your assumptions are to be true.

Worked example — back to the sunrise. You'd like to get up five or ten minutes before sunrise to watch it.

  • Assume it rises at exactly 6:52am → wake at 6:42am, precisely. That's point identification: enough assumptions that the question has exactly one answer.
  • Assume only that it rises between 6 and 7 → get up sometime between 5:50am and 6:50am. Still a conclusion — it still tells you not to sleep until noon — but a range.

That second case is also called set identification, because your results are a set of answers.

Anchor image. A weather forecast that says "rain between two and five" is more useful than one that confidently says "rain at 3:47" and is wrong.

3

Why Nick thinks it's underutilised

Plain English. You can't sit around waiting for a quasi-experiment to happen.

Precisely. Causal inference has a problem. The results we really believe tend to be quasi-experiments — things requiring an instrumental variable, or a discontinuity. But you can only do a quasi-experiment if the quasi-experiment happened. That severely limits the range of things you can study if you're always waiting for one to turn up.

Meanwhile, there's a great deal of work where you can confidently say you've controlled for a lot of the important stuff and closed a lot of important back doors — you just can never quite get all the way there.

Partial identification says: given that we don't quite get there, what can we say anyway? And you can often say quite a lot — something like I think the effect of this policy is between 5 and 10 percentage points, given that I know I'm still a little biased and still have a couple of omitted variables.

4

Worked example: a randomized experiment ruined by attrition

Plain English. Even randomization can't save you — but bounds can.

Precisely. Randomly assign 5,000 parents to receive $1,000: keep your kids in school, here's a thousand dollars to help you do it. The other parents get nothing. Follow up later to see whether their kids are still in school.

The problem. Getting the money might make you more likely to stay in the study, whereas people who got nothing may never report back to the researchers, and you lose track of them. That's differential attrition between the treated and control groups.

So if the money group ends up with a higher share of kids still in school — is that because the money worked, or because the money made different kinds of people more willing to stay in the study? It's a collider bias problem.

And you cannot observe the people who dropped out, so you can't adjust by controlling for anything.

The strong assumption. Assume people who dropped out are essentially random — nothing to do with whether they'd have kept their kids in school, nothing to do with treatment assignment. Then you can ignore attrition and report your results. That gives you point identification — a single estimate of exactly what the money did.

That's probably not true.

The weak assumption. The share of students who stayed in school is somewhere between 0% and 100%. That seems pretty reasonable. It has to be true.

Now do the arithmetic. Suppose 90% stayed in the study and 10% dropped out. Of the 90% who stayed, 80% kept their kids in school. Of the 10% who dropped out, the share is somewhere between 0% and 100%.

If the dropouts' share was…Overall share keeping kids in school
0%(0.9 × 80%) + (0.1 × 0%) = 72%
100%(0.9 × 80%) + (0.1 × 100%) = 82%

So you don't know exactly what share of the treatment group kept their kids in school — but it has to be between 72% and 82%.

Do the same calculation for the control group, and the two ranges give you a range of treatment effects.

What that buys you. Rather than choosing between an unrealistic assumption and reporting nothing at all, you give a range of reasonable estimates based on something you know to be true: that a share must lie between 0 and 100%.

5

Adding assumptions narrows the bounds

Plain English. Know more, assume more, get a tighter range.

Precisely. If you feel you know more about the situation, add more assumptions — provided you think they're reasonable.

Worked example. You might be willing to assume that if you stayed in the study, you're probably also more likely to have kept your kids in school — that some of the people who dropped out are people who drop out of life in general and don't send their kids to school either. Add that, and the bounds narrow.

The reframing that matters. By allowing yourself not to reach point identification, you get to choose which assumptions you think are reasonable — rather than asking which assumptions you have to make in order to get a single answer, whether or not they're true.

Say it out loud

Every design in this book rests on an assumption you probably don't fully believe. That you closed all the back doors. That your instrument is perfectly valid. That parallel trends holds exactly.

And here's the thing about assumptions: stronger ones give you more, and are more likely to be wrong. Watch. "The sun will rise tomorrow" — weak, basically certain. "The sun will rise between six and seven" — stronger, still pretty safe. "The sun will rise at 6:52" — strongest, and now you're likely to be wrong.

If I assume 6:52 and I want to watch the sunrise, I know exactly when to get up: 6:42. One answer. That's point identification. But if I only assume it rises between six and seven, I get up somewhere between 5:50 and 6:50. Less precise — but still enough to tell me not to sleep until noon. That's partial identification.

And this matters enormously, because the studies we really trust are quasi-experiments — and you can only run one if one happened to you. You can't just wait around for an instrument to fall out of the sky. So partial identification asks: given that I can't close everything, what can I still say?

Here's my favourite example. Randomized experiment: give 5,000 parents a thousand dollars to keep their kids in school. Perfectly randomized. But — the people who got money are more likely to stay in touch with the researchers. The ones who got nothing vanish. So your comparison is broken, and you can't fix it, because you can't see the people who left.

Unless. You assume one thing that has to be true: the share of dropouts who kept their kids in school is between 0% and 100%. Suppose 90% stayed and 80% of them kept their kids in school. If every dropout failed, you get 72%. If every dropout succeeded, 82%. So the truth is between 72 and 82. You didn't get a number. You got a fact.

And if you're willing to assume a bit more — say, that people who drop out of studies also tend to drop out of things generally — the range gets tighter. Which is the whole idea: stop asking what must I assume to get one answer, and start asking what do I actually believe, and how far does it get me?

Recall drill

QWhy does this chapter exist in the second edition?

It's a new chapter added for the second edition, with these videos recorded to accompany it.

QName the assumption required by each of three approaches.

Controlling for variables requires having measured and controlled for every variable needed to close all back doors; instrumental variables requires no front door from instrument to outcome; difference-in-differences requires parallel trends.

QWhat makes an assumption "strong"?

It assumes more about the world than a weak one.

QRank the three sunrise assumptions by strength.

"The sun will rise" is weakest, "it will rise between 6 and 7" is stronger, "it will rise at 6:52" is strongest.

QState the trade-off with assumption strength.

Stronger assumptions let you get more out of your analysis, but they're more likely to be wrong.

QWhat is point identification?

Having made enough assumptions that your question has exactly one answer.

QWhat is set identification?

The alternative name for partial identification — your result is a set, or range, of answers rather than a single value.

QGive the sunrise version of each.

Assuming 6:52 tells you to wake at exactly 6:42; assuming between 6 and 7 tells you to wake sometime between 5:50 and 6:50.

QWhy does Nick think partial identification is underutilised?

Because the analyses we trust most are quasi-experiments, and you can only run one if the quasi-experiment happened — which severely limits what you can study.

QDescribe the attrition problem in the school money experiment.

Getting $1,000 may make parents more likely to stay in the study, while those who got nothing may never report back — so treated and control groups have differential attrition, a collider bias problem.

QWhy can't you control your way out of it?

You can't observe the people who dropped out.

QWhat's the strong assumption available, and why is it unattractive?

That dropouts are essentially random with respect to both treatment and outcome. It gives point identification, but it's probably not true.

QWhat's the weak assumption used instead?

That the share of dropouts who kept their kids in school is between 0% and 100% — which must be true.

QWith 90% staying and 80% of those keeping kids in school, compute the bounds.

0.9 × 80% plus 0.1 × 0% gives 72%; 0.9 × 80% plus 0.1 × 100% gives 82%. So the true share is between 72% and 82%.

QHow do you turn that into a treatment effect?

Do the same calculation on the control group and compare the two ranges.

QHow can you narrow the bounds?

Add further assumptions you believe — for instance that people who stay in a study are also more likely to keep their kids in school.

QState the reframing at the heart of partial identification.

Instead of asking which assumptions you must make to get a single answer regardless of whether they're true, ask which assumptions you actually believe and how far they take you.

Glossary

Partial identification
deriving a range of possible values for an effect under assumptions weaker than those needed for a single estimate.
Set identification
another name for partial identification, since the result is a set of values.
Point identification
having made enough assumptions that exactly one answer follows.
Strong assumption
one that assumes more about the world, yielding more precision and greater risk of being wrong.
Bounds
the upper and lower limits of the plausible range of an effect.
Differential attrition
treated and control groups dropping out of a study at different rates or for different reasons.
