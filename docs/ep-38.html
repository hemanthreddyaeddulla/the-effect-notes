Ep 38Propensity Score Matching

▶ Watch the video 13:30
On this pageThe 30-second versionWhy this existsThe ideasTrapsSay it out loudRecall drillConnectionsGlossary

You don't need to close off everything a confounder does — only the part that drives treatment — so predict each person's probability of being treated, collapse all your matching variables into that single number, and weight by its inverse.

The 30-second version

  • The goal was never to control the variables. It was to close the back doors.
  • Confounders do many things; only part of what they do is affect treatment. Close off that part and you've done the job.
  • The propensity score is the probability someone gets treated, usually predicted with a probit or logit on the matching variables. Increasingly with machine learning too — it's a prediction problem.
  • Because it collapses everything into one dimension, there's no curse of dimensionality. But the regression brings the linearity assumption back.
  • Don't pick matches on it — that has known statistical problems. Use inverse probability weighting.
  • Weight treated observations by 1/p and controls by 1/(1−p): the least likely treated who were, and the most likely treated who weren't.
  • Watch for extreme scores (trim them), common support, and balance — then go back and re-estimate.

Why this exists

Distance matching works, but it collapses under many matching variables. And step back to why you're doing any of this: you want the causal effect of X on Y, back door variables are driving the correlation you observe, and you want to control for that.

But here's the insight. Closing all those back doors doesn't require closing off all the variation in those variables. That was never the goal — the goal is to close the back doors. Those confounders do all sorts of things, and only part of what they do is affect treatment. If you can close off the part of those variables that determines whether somebody is treated, and control for just that, you've done your job.

The ideas

1

The propensity score

Plain English. One number per person: how likely they were to get treated.

Precisely. The propensity score is the probability that somebody is going to be treated. Propensity score matching predicts your propensity for being treated from the matching variables, then uses that probability to find who is most like you in terms of how likely you were to be treated — because controlling for that is the real thing you were trying to do with all these back door closures.

You don't have to control for all the individual variables, so long as you can control for the propensity score.

Anchor image. You don't need to know everything about a lock. Only which key opens it.

How it's estimated. Typically with a probit or logit model: take whether or not you were treated, regress it on the set of matching variables, and use that to predict the probability you were treated. That's your estimated propensity score.

Probit and logit aren't the only options — increasingly you'll see machine learning applications predicting the propensity score, which makes sense: this is a prediction problem, and prediction is what machine learning is good at. But probit and logit remain the most common.

2

What using a regression costs and buys

Plain English. The linearity you escaped comes back — but the dimensionality problem goes away.

Precisely.

  • Cost: one of the good things about matching was that it doesn't lean on linearity assumptions the way regression does. Now you're running a regression, so that assumption comes back in.
  • Benefit: you can take a large number of matching variables and combine them in a way that handles high dimensionality far better than distance matching. You don't get the curse of dimensionality in the same way, because you're collapsing all the matching variables together — you end up back in a single dimension, and one dimension can't suffer a curse of dimensionality.
3

Don't pick matches — use inverse probability weighting

Plain English. Matching people with similar propensity scores is the obvious move, and it's the wrong one.

Precisely. The obvious approach: you have an 80% chance of being treated and you were treated, so find someone untreated with a 79% or 81% chance and call them your best match. But it's been shown that using a propensity score to pick a set of matches has statistical issues — it doesn't work all that well.

So of the two matching approaches from Ep 35, use weighting.

Inverse probability weighting:

GroupWeightConsequence
Treatedinverse of the probability of being treated — 1/pThose most likely to be treated get the lowest weight
Controlinverse of the probability of not being treated — 1/(1−p)Those least likely to be treated get the lowest weight

Why that direction. The control group didn't get treated. So if you want treated observations that resemble the control group, you want the people who were least likely to get treated — and they get the heaviest weights. Symmetrically, the treated group did get treated, so among the untreated you want people who were most likely to be treated and yet weren't — and they get the heaviest weights.

You end up comparing the people least likely to be treated who were against the people most likely to be treated who weren't — and those are likely to be pretty decent comparisons for each other.

Anchor image. Two crowds you want to make resemble each other. Turn up the volume on the unusual people in each and turn it down on the typical ones.

4

Difficulty 1: extreme propensity scores

Plain English. Dividing by a probability near zero blows up.

Precisely. Inverse probability weighting runs into problems when the probability of being treated is near one or near zero:

  • Near-zero probability of treatment but you were treated → weight is something like 1 ÷ 0, which you can't do.
  • Near-one probability but you weren't treated → weight is 1 ÷ almost zero. Same problem.

The fix: trim the propensity score — drop people with very high or very low probabilities of being treated.

5

Difficulty 2: common support

Plain English. Is there actually anybody on the other side who resembles these people?

Precisely. You're trying to find the control observations most like the treated group — the ones with the highest probability of being treated. Is there anybody actually like that? Is there anyone who wasn't treated but had a high chance of being? And symmetrically, is there anyone who was treated but had a low chance of being?

There might not be. If you have nobody to compare who is treated-but-like-the-untreated, or untreated-but-like-the-treated, you lack common support, and propensity score matching may simply not work well.

The fix: drop people whose probabilities have nobody on the other side. If nobody in the control group had an 80% or better chance of being treated, drop everyone in the treated group with an 80%+ chance and use only the people more like the control group. Which of course means dropping observations — the bias–variance trade-off, again.

6

Difficulty 3: balance

Plain English. You can match perfectly on the score and still have wildly different groups.

Precisely. Unlike distance matching, propensity score matching isn't trying to make the matching variables similar between the groups. You can easily end up with a result where the actual matching variables look very different across groups.

Worked example. Match on April credit card bill size and age, and suppose both increase the probability of not paying your April bill. Now consider:

PersonApril billAge
Avery highvery young
Bvery lowvery old

Both might be predicted to have the same probability of not paying. So matching one to the other gives you a bill that doesn't match at all and an age that matches terribly — while the propensity scores line up perfectly. Groups well matched on the score, badly matched on the individual variables. That's a failure of balance.

Where it breaks. Technically the whole idea is that if you control for the propensity score, who cares about the individual variables? But it doesn't look great, and it's a bit suspicious. So a common practice is to check whether the matching variables look at least roughly similar between the treated and control groups.

7

The check-and-return loop

Plain English. Estimate, inspect, go back, change something, repeat.

Precisely. If common support or balance is bad, your matching didn't work well — so go back to your propensity score estimation and change things. Add polynomial terms to the estimation. Add or remove some matching variables. Then re-check.

This check-and-return approach is common with matching procedures generally: do the matching, pick your matches, check common support and balance, and if they look bad, go back and try other things until the matching looks like it worked better.

Where it breaks. It means you may run your whole matching procedure many times over, which is annoying — and there are alternative methods that don't require it, which is the next episode.

Say it out loud

Here's the idea that makes propensity scores click, and it's genuinely elegant.

You've been trying to control for confounders. But your actual goal was never to neutralise those variables — it was to close the back doors. And a confounder does lots of things; only part of what it does is affect who gets treated. So if I can close off just that part — the part that determines treatment — I'm done.

That part has a name: the propensity score, your probability of being treated. Run a logit of "were you treated?" on all your matching variables, predict the probability, and that one number stands in for all of them.

Which is a big deal, because distance matching died from the curse of dimensionality — every extra variable pushed people further apart. Here everything collapses into one dimension, so that problem evaporates. The cost is that you're running a regression again, so the linearity assumption sneaks back in.

Now, the obvious next move is to match people with similar scores — 80% treated with 79% untreated. Don't. That's been shown not to work well. Use inverse probability weighting instead, and the logic is nice: weight treated people by 1 over their probability of treatment, so the people who were least likely to be treated but were, count the most. And weight controls the other way, so the people most likely to be treated who somehow weren't, count the most. You're comparing the surprising cases against each other.

Three things to watch. Extreme scores — you can't divide by zero, so trim them. Common support — is there actually anyone on the other side who looks like these people? Maybe not. And balance — you can have two people with identical propensity scores where one has a huge bill and is young, and the other has a tiny bill and is old. Same score, nothing alike.

So you check, and if it looks bad, you go back and re-estimate. Over and over.

Recall drill

QWhat's the insight behind propensity score matching?

Closing back doors doesn't require removing all variation in the confounders — only the part of them that determines whether someone is treated.

QDefine the propensity score.

The probability that somebody is going to be treated.

QHow is it usually estimated?

With a probit or logit model: regress whether you were treated on the matching variables and predict the probability.

QWhat other approach is increasingly used, and why does it fit?

Machine learning — because estimating a propensity score is a prediction problem, and prediction is what machine learning is good at.

QWhat does using a regression to estimate it cost you?

The linearity assumption you avoided by using matching instead of regression comes back in.

QWhy doesn't propensity score matching suffer the curse of dimensionality?

Because it collapses all the matching variables into a single dimension — the score — rather than measuring distance separately along each.

QShould you use the propensity score to pick a set of matches?

No. That has been shown to have statistical issues and doesn't work well. Use weighting instead.

QGive the inverse probability weights for treated and control observations.

Treated: 1 divided by the probability of being treated. Control: 1 divided by the probability of not being treated.

QWhich treated observations end up counting most, and why?

Those least likely to have been treated. The control group didn't get treated, so treated observations resembling the control group are the ones that were unlikely to be treated.

QWhich control observations count most?

Those most likely to have been treated but weren't — since they most resemble the treated group.

QWhat goes wrong with extreme propensity scores, and how do you fix it?

Weights become 1 divided by something near zero and blow up. Trim the score — drop observations with very high or very low probabilities of treatment.

QWhat is common support?

Whether there actually exist untreated people with a high probability of treatment, and treated people with a low probability. Without them you have no comparable observations.

QHow do you handle a lack of common support?

Drop observations whose probabilities have nobody on the other side — for instance, if no control had an 80%+ chance of treatment, drop treated observations above 80%.

QWhat is a failure of balance?

When groups are well matched on the propensity score but the individual matching variables still look very different between them.

QGive the balance example.

Matching on April bill and age, both of which raise the probability of not paying: someone with a very high bill who is very young and someone with a very low bill who is very old can have identical propensity scores while being nothing alike.

QIs poor balance technically a problem, and what do people do about it?

Technically the score is what you're controlling for, so it needn't be — but it looks suspicious, so people check whether the matching variables are at least roughly similar across groups.

QDescribe the check-and-return approach.

Estimate the propensity score, do the matching, check common support and balance; if they look bad, go back and change the estimation — add polynomial terms, add or remove matching variables — and repeat.

Glossary

Propensity score
the estimated probability that an observation receives treatment.
Inverse probability weighting
weighting observations by the inverse of their probability of the treatment status they actually had.
Trimming
dropping observations with extreme propensity scores to avoid exploding weights.
Common support
the existence of comparable observations on both sides across the range of propensity scores.
Balance
whether the matching variables themselves end up similar between the treated and control groups.
