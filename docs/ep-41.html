Ep 41Treatment Effect Estimation with Matching

▶ Watch the video 10:00
On this pageThe 30-second versionWhy this existsThe ideasTrapsSay it out loudRecall drillConnectionsGlossary

After all that matching, estimation is almost trivial — take the weighted average of the treated group, the weighted average of the control group, and compare — with the real subtleties sitting in standard errors (let the software do them) and in doubly robust estimators that survive getting either your matching model or your regression model wrong.

The 30-second version

  • Estimation: weighted average of the treated, weighted average of the control, compare. That's it.
  • Which average you get depends on which direction you matched: control-to-treated gives the ATT, treated-to-control gives the ATUT, and combining them by the proportion treated gives the ATE.
  • Standard errors are hard. The matching procedure itself injects uncertainty. A plain weighted regression gives standard errors that are too small.
  • Let your software handle them — or use bootstrap standard errors, redoing the whole matching each resample.
  • You can combine matching with regression: apply your matching weights inside a regression to get interactions and everything else regression is good at.
  • Doubly robust estimators are right if either the matching model or the outcome model is correct. Both wrong and you're still in trouble.

Why this exists

Odd to leave estimation to the very end of a chapter — except that once you've done the matching, it's relatively simple and straightforward. All the difficult questions were the matching questions. The actual estimation is quite a lot easier.

The ideas

1

The estimate itself

Plain English. Average the treated, average the control, subtract.

Precisely. Take your set of matched observations, or the weights you generated for your weighted matched sample, and calculate the weighted average of the outcome for the treated group and the weighted average for the control group. Compare those two. Done.

Since you've already done the matching, the resulting comparison gives you the effect of treatment on the outcome.

Anchor image. All the work was in setting the table. Serving dinner takes a minute.

2

Which treatment effect average you get

Plain English. It depends which group you made resemble which.

Precisely.

What you matchedWhat you get
Matched the control group to look like the treatment groupAverage treatment on the treated (ATT)
Matched the treated group to look like the control groupAverage treatment on the untreated (ATUT)
Both, averaged together by the proportion of people treatedAverage treatment effect (ATE)

There are also ways to choose your inverse probability weights specifically to obtain the kind of treatment effect average you're after.

3

Standard errors: hand them to the software

Plain English. Matching adds uncertainty of its own, and naive standard errors miss it.

Precisely. Standard errors with matching are tougher than in regression modelling. Nick's advice is essentially: don't listen to him on this — pick software that does matching estimation and let it calculate the standard errors for you, because a lot of difficult things go on in those calculations.

The reason: the matching procedure itself injects uncertainty that you need to account for. If you simply do the comparison — regress the outcome on treatment using your matching weights — it will not properly account for all the uncertainty that went into the matching process, and your standard errors will be too small.

Anchor image. Timing a race with a stopwatch you also had to build. The stopwatch's own error belongs in the result.

4

The exception: bootstrap standard errors

Plain English. Resample your data over and over, redoing the whole procedure each time.

Precisely. Bootstrapping means randomly drawing a new sample from your data many times over. Every single time, you redo the entire matching process and the entire estimation process, and get an estimate. Do that repeatedly and you're mimicking the sampling distribution yourself.

Why it works here: because you redo the matching every time, it incorporates the uncertainty of the matching process into your estimate. So you get the sampling distribution as a whole and can calculate your own standard errors.

Where it breaks. This works as long as you're using a weighted approach. A procedure where you're picking samples to be in or out of the control group doesn't work so well with bootstrap standard errors. Technical details are in the chapter.

5

Combining matching with regression

Plain English. Matching weights slot straight into a regression, so you get regression's conveniences too.

Precisely. You don't have to use matching by itself. Combine it with regression to do the things regression is good at.

Worked example. Suppose you want an interaction term — how the effect varies across groups. That's awkward with a plain weighted average, and very easy in a regression. So just add your matching weights into the regression model and you're set.

Broockman did exactly this in the legislator study: he didn't just take a weighted average with his matched group. He took his matching weights and applied them to a regression model that had an interaction term in it.

6

Doubly robust estimators

Plain English. Two chances to be right. Get either model correct and you're fine.

Precisely. You could misspecify two different things:

  • Your matching procedure — picking the wrong variables, or the wrong functional form. This is a model of how treatment gets assigned.
  • Your regression model — the wrong functional form, matching variables in the wrong place, a missing interaction term. This is a model of the outcome.

A doubly robust estimator gives you the right answer as long as at least one of those is correct. If one is wrong, you're okay — and you won't necessarily know which one saved you. If both are wrong, you're still in trouble.

Anchor image. A backup system. If one fails, the other catches you. Both fail and you fall.

Where it breaks. Double robustness isn't a method — it's a property that certain estimators have. Several estimators are doubly robust; some are harder to implement, some easier.

7

Inverse probability weighting with regression adjustment

Plain English. Weight, then fit an outcome model separately in each group, then predict everyone both ways and compare.

Precisely. A relatively easy doubly robust estimator:

  1. Do your propensity score estimation and get inverse probability weights, as before.
  2. Take just the treated data, apply those weights, and estimate a regression model of the outcome — a model of what would happen if everybody were treated.
  3. Take just the untreated data, apply the weights, and estimate the same regression model — a model of what would happen if everybody were untreated.
  4. Predict values for everybody under both models: here's what I predict for you if treated, here's what I predict for you if untreated. Compare the two.

That comparison — everyone's predicted treated outcome against everyone's predicted untreated outcome — is your treatment effect estimate.

Why it's doubly robust. You've got inverse probability weighting in there for the matching, and regression adjustment in there because you estimated a regression model for both groups. So if you made a specification error in your propensity score estimation, you're fine as long as your regression model is good. And if you made an error specifying your regression model, you're fine as long as the inverse probability weighting model was specified properly.

Say it out loud

After all that work on matching, here's how you actually estimate the effect: take the weighted average of your treated group, take the weighted average of your control group, and compare them. That's genuinely it. All the hard thinking was in the matching.

One thing to notice — which treatment effect you get depends on which direction you matched. If you built a control group to look like your treated group, you get the effect on the treated. Do it the other way and you get the effect on the untreated. Do both and blend them, and you get the overall average.

The part that is genuinely hard is standard errors, and the honest advice is: let your software do them. Here's why it matters. The matching process itself carries uncertainty — you made a bunch of choices, any of which could have gone differently. If you just run a weighted regression at the end, none of that gets counted, and your standard errors come out too small. You'll look more certain than you are.

The one thing you can do yourself is bootstrap. Resample your data, and each time redo the entire matching procedure from scratch, then estimate. Do that a thousand times and you've built the sampling distribution yourself — with the matching uncertainty baked in, because you redid the matching every time.

And you can combine matching with regression — just drop your matching weights into a regression. Broockman did that so he could include an interaction term.

Which leads to my favourite idea here: doubly robust estimators. You've got two models that could be wrong — your model of who gets treated, and your model of the outcome. A doubly robust estimator is right as long as at least one of them is correct. You won't know which one saved you. But unless you got both wrong, you're fine. It's a backup parachute.

Recall drill

QHow do you estimate a treatment effect once matching is done?

Take the weighted average of the outcome in the treated group and in the control group, and compare them.

QYou matched the control group to look like the treated group. Which average do you get?

The average treatment on the treated.

QAnd if you matched the treated group to look like the control group?

The average treatment on the untreated.

QHow do you get the average treatment effect?

Obtain both the ATT and the ATUT and average them together based on the proportion of people treated.

QWhy are standard errors harder with matching than with regression?

The matching procedure itself injects uncertainty that must be accounted for.

QWhat happens if you just regress the outcome on treatment using matching weights?

It fails to account for the uncertainty from the matching process, so your standard errors come out too small.

QWhat's Nick's practical advice on matching standard errors?

Let the software you're using calculate them — the computations involve a lot of difficult details.

QDescribe bootstrapping in this context.

Repeatedly draw a new random sample from your data and, each time, redo the entire matching and estimation process — mimicking the sampling distribution yourself.

QWhy does bootstrapping solve the matching uncertainty problem?

Because you redo the matching on every resample, the uncertainty of the matching process is incorporated into the resulting distribution.

QWhen does bootstrapping not work well?

With procedures that pick observations in or out of the control group, rather than weighted approaches.

QWhy combine matching with regression?

To get things regression does well — an interaction term, for example, is awkward with a weighted average but easy in a regression with matching weights applied.

QWhat did Broockman actually do?

Applied his matching weights to a regression model containing an interaction term, rather than taking a plain weighted average.

QWhat two models could be misspecified in a matching analysis?

The matching procedure — a model of how treatment gets assigned — and the regression model — a model of the outcome.

QWhat does a doubly robust estimator guarantee?

The right answer as long as at least one of those two models is correctly specified. If both are wrong, it still fails.

QIs double robustness a method?

No — it's a property that various estimators possess.

QGive the four steps of inverse probability weighting with regression adjustment.

Estimate the propensity score and get inverse probability weights; use them on just the treated data to fit an outcome regression; do the same on just the untreated data; then predict everyone's outcome under both models and compare.

QWhy is that procedure doubly robust?

If the propensity score estimation is misspecified you're saved by a good regression model, and if the regression model is misspecified you're saved by correctly specified inverse probability weights.

Glossary

Bootstrapping
repeatedly resampling the data and redoing the whole procedure to construct a sampling distribution empirically.
Doubly robust estimator
an estimator that is consistent if either the treatment assignment model or the outcome model is correctly specified.
Inverse probability weighting with regression adjustment
a doubly robust procedure that fits a weighted outcome regression separately in the treated and untreated groups and compares predictions.
Regression adjustment
modelling the outcome with a regression, as opposed to modelling treatment assignment.
