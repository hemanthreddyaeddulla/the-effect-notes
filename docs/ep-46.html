Ep 46Estimating Fixed Effects

▶ Watch the video 11:30
On this pageThe 30-second versionWhy this existsThe ideasTrapsSay it out loudRecall drillConnectionsGlossary

A fixed effect is just a categorical control variable for the individual — mathematically identical to any other categorical control, and different only in why you added it — so you can estimate it by literally adding that control, or, far more commonly, by demeaning: subtract each individual's own average from everything and regress the leftovers.

The 30-second version

  • Fixed effects and adding a categorical control are the same thing mathematically. Only the research design reasoning differs.
  • The model gets i,t subscripts (panel data) and an individual-specific intercept, which is exactly what binary category indicators do.
  • Estimation route 1: add the categorical control. Works — but you may have hundreds of thousands of individuals and therefore coefficients.
  • Estimation route 2: demeaning. Subtract each individual's own mean from y and every x, then regress the leftovers. This is what fixed-effects software actually does.
  • Cluster your standard errors at the level of the fixed effect. Very common, and it makes sense.
  • Interpretation is a normal regression interpretation — just say "within individual."
  • Two-way fixed effects adds a time fixed effect too. Easy to run, harder to interpret.

Why this exists

The last episode was about why you'd use fixed effects. This one is about actually estimating one — and the crucial thing to hold onto is that you're just controlling for a variable. You've added control variables before, and there is nothing different about adding a control for individual.

Individual is just a categorical control variable, and you already know how to add those. Fixed effects and a categorical control are mathematically the same thing. The only difference is why: you're adding this control not because you want to control for that thing specifically, but because you think it represents a bunch of other individual stuff you can't measure.

Keeping that in mind clears up a lot of the apparent difficulty.

The ideas

1

The model and its notation

Plain English. Extra subscripts to show panel data, and an intercept that varies by person.

Precisely. A fixed effects model looks like a standard regression with a couple of differences in the subscripts:

  • y with i,t subscripts — y varies over both individual i and time t. That's what tells you it's panel data: the same individuals measured in multiple periods.
  • x and the error term also carry i,t subscripts.
  • The intercept has its own i subscript — each individual gets their own intercept.

Why an intercept and not another x? Because that's what a categorical control is. Add binary indicators for countries — Austria is 1 if you're Austria and 0 otherwise. That coefficient just says your intercept changes: if you're Austria, add Austria's coefficient to the intercept; if not, don't. So a set of category indicators is exactly the same as giving each individual their own intercept.

2

Estimation route 1: add the categorical control

Plain English. Just put it in the regression.

Precisely. Literally no different from adding any categorical control. Add it to the model and you're done.

Where it breaks. Normally a categorical control has four categories, or ten. With fixed effects you're controlling for individual, and with a lot of data that might be thousands or hundreds of thousands of individuals. You don't want to estimate a regression with hundreds of thousands of coefficients — at the very least it will be extremely slow. So while you can do it this way, it generally isn't the best approach.

3

Estimation route 2: demeaning

Plain English. Subtract each person's own average out of everything, then run a normal regression on what's left.

Precisely. Take the average of your y and of all your x variables for each individual, and subtract that average out.

And you've seen this before. It's exactly one of the methods of controlling for variables from Chapter 4: take what you can predict with your control variables and subtract the prediction out. When your control variable is individual, what's the best prediction of your outcome knowing only that it's you? Your own average.

Anchor image. Measuring how much everyone grew this year rather than how tall they are. Subtract each person's baseline and the remaining numbers are comparable.

Worked example. If you average four hours of exercise a week across all the years observed, then knowing only that it's you, the best prediction is four hours. Subtract that out and you get your demeaned values — the fact that this year you exercised one hour more than average, and that year one hour less. That's your within variation.

Then simply regress your demeaned outcome on your demeaned predictors. That's it — and this is what most software specifically designed to estimate fixed effects is actually doing.

4

Clustered standard errors

Plain English. Cluster at the level of the fixed effect. Almost everyone does.

Precisely. It's very common to estimate a fixed effects model with clustered standard errors, clustered at the level of the fixed effect. You'd expect the error terms to be more related within individual than between individuals, and clustering by individual is how you allow for that.

Where it breaks. Not that you always have to — but it's very, very common, and it makes sense why.

5

Interpretation

Plain English. Same as any regression. Just add "within individual" to the sentence.

Worked example. Regress life expectancy on a country's log GDP per capita, with country fixed effects. Coefficient: 9.769.

The standard reading: a one-unit increase in log GDP per capita is associated with a 9.769-unit increase in life expectancy. Exactly as you'd interpret any regression — except that it's within country.

Spelled out: comparing a given country across time, a year in which that country has a one-unit higher log GDP per capita would be expected to have a 9.769 longer life expectancy than that same country in a different year with lower log GDP per capita.

That's all. A regular regression interpretation, keeping in mind you're controlling for individual and therefore looking at within variation.

6

Two-way fixed effects

Plain English. Add a second set of fixed effects for time, to soak up things that happened to everyone at once.

Precisely. Nothing stops you having more than one set of fixed effects. It's very common to run a two-way fixed effects model, with a fixed effect for individual and one for the time period — intended to control for shared changes over time.

Worked example. Back to trade with Germany. Different countries differ in their general characteristics — that's between variation, handled by country fixed effects. But there are also shared changes over time: maybe in one year Germany's economy is really popping, so everybody trades more with Germany that year. Add a fixed effect for time and those shared changes are controlled for.

Where it breaks. Two-way models are very common and easy to run — you just add another fixed effect. It's a bit harder with the demeaning approach, but let the software handle it.

Interpreting them is trickier, though. You have to think about looking within individual and within time, and those two within-dimensions interact with each other. These models do some weird and unexpected things sometimes. The textbook chapter has more detail.

Say it out loud

Here's the thing that makes estimating fixed effects much less scary: it's just a control variable. Individual is a categorical variable, you already know how to control for categorical variables, and mathematically that's all a fixed effect is. The only thing that's different is why you're adding it — not because you care about "which country," but because "which country" stands in for a pile of stuff you can't measure.

In the model you'll see i and t subscripts — that's telling you it's panel data, same individuals across time. And the intercept gets an i subscript, meaning every individual gets their own intercept. Which sounds exotic until you remember that's exactly what a binary indicator does — if you're Austria, add Austria's coefficient to the intercept.

Two ways to actually estimate it. You can literally add the categorical control — totally valid, and totally impractical if you have two hundred thousand individuals, because that's two hundred thousand coefficients.

So instead: demeaning. And this is the Chapter 4 trick again. Ask: knowing only that it's you, what's my best prediction of your exercise? Your own average. So subtract each person's own average out of everything — the outcome and every predictor — and regress what's left. What's left is the within variation. That's what the software is doing.

Then interpret it exactly like a normal regression, with three words added. Life expectancy on log GDP per capita, coefficient 9.769 — a one-unit rise in log GDP is associated with 9.769 more years of life expectancy, within country. Comparing a country to itself in a different year, not to other countries.

Two last things. Cluster your standard errors by individual — errors are more related within a person than across people. And you can add a second set of fixed effects for time, which mops up things that hit everyone at once — like Germany having a great economic year. Easy to run. Genuinely annoying to interpret.

Recall drill

QHow does a fixed effect differ mathematically from a categorical control variable?

It doesn't — they're the same thing. Only the reason for including it differs.

QWhy do you add it, if not to control for that variable specifically?

Because that category stands in for a bunch of individual characteristics you can't measure and want to control for.

QWhat do the i and t subscripts in a fixed effects model indicate?

That the variables vary over both individual and time — that is, you have panel data.

QWhy does the intercept get an i subscript?

Because each individual gets their own intercept, which is exactly what a set of binary category indicators does — being in a category shifts the intercept.

QWhat's the first way to estimate a fixed effects model, and its downside?

Literally add the categorical control to the regression. The downside is that with thousands or hundreds of thousands of individuals you'd be estimating that many coefficients, which is very slow.

QDescribe the demeaning approach.

Take the average of the outcome and every predictor for each individual, subtract those averages out, and regress the demeaned outcome on the demeaned predictors.

QHow is demeaning the same as the Chapter 4 method of controlling for a variable?

You take what you can predict with the control and subtract the prediction out — and knowing only which individual it is, the best prediction is that individual's own average.

QYou average four hours of exercise a week. What does demeaning leave you with?

The within variation — that this year you exercised one hour more than your average, and another year one hour less.

QWhich estimation method do most fixed-effects packages actually use?

Demeaning.

QWhat standard errors are usually used with fixed effects, and why?

Clustered standard errors, clustered at the level of the fixed effect, because error terms are likely more related within an individual than between individuals.

QLife expectancy on log GDP per capita with country fixed effects gives 9.769. Interpret it.

Within country, a year in which that country has a one-unit higher log GDP per capita would be expected to have 9.769 longer life expectancy than that same country in a year with lower log GDP per capita.

QWhat is a two-way fixed effects model?

One with a fixed effect for individual and a fixed effect for the time period.

QWhat does the time fixed effect control for?

Shared changes over time — for instance a year in which Germany's economy is booming so everybody trades more with Germany.

QWhat's the catch with two-way fixed effects?

They're easy to run but trickier to interpret, since you're looking within individual and within time and those interact — and the models sometimes behave in unexpected ways.

Individual intercept
