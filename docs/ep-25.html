Ep 25Regression and The Error Term

▶ Watch the video 14:10
On this pageThe 30-second versionWhy this existsThe ideasTrapsSay it out loudRecall drillConnectionsGlossary

Everything your model leaves out doesn't vanish — it goes into the error term, and "close all the back doors" translated into regression means exactly one thing: make your treatment unrelated to the error term, because if it isn't, OLS hands your treatment the credit for whatever is hiding in there.

The 30-second version

  • Residual = actual minus predicted, for a data point. OLS picks the line making the squared residuals smallest.
  • Error term (ε) = everything in the data generating process your model left out. Different concept.
  • The error term is not noise. It's real determinants of your outcome — parents' wealth, lottery wins — that simply aren't in your equation.
  • Adding a control variable moves it out of the error term into the model. That's what controlling is, in regression.
  • The translation: closing all back doors ≈ making X unrelated to the error term.
  • If X is related to it: omitted variable bias, X is endogenous, the effect isn't identified — all the same statement.
  • Why it biases: OLS can't see Z, but it sees X hanging around while Z does good things. So it credits X.

Why this exists

Part 2 of the book starts here — more technical, more about applying tools rather than thinking about identification in the abstract. And it starts with regression, because regression is enormously important and enormously common in causal inference.

You already met it in Chapter 4. Regression estimates a conditional relationship by picking a shape — often a straight line — and fitting the best version of that shape. You can go further than a line into a plane in 3D space by adding control variables, and that easy flexibility to add controls is a big part of why it's so popular: it lets you isolate just the part of the X–Y relationship not explained by the control.

So what's left to say? This: you've been focusing on the line you fit. Now focus on what you left out.

The ideas

1

Residual vs. error — two different things

Plain English. A residual is how wrong you were about one point. The error term is what your whole model ignores about the world.

Precisely.

  • Residual — the difference between your prediction and the actual value, written as actual minus prediction. Some points sit above the line (reality was higher than you predicted), some below. Any prediction method has residuals, and they arise for many reasons: things left out, sampling variation, whatever.
  • Error term — the difference between what your model would predict if it had all the data in the world and you had the best possible version of that estimated model, and what would still be left over. It captures only one thing: what's in your model versus what's still missing from it.

Anchor image. A weather forecast. The residual is that it said 20° and it was 22°. The error term is that the model never looks at the ocean.

Worked example. Predicting someone's wealth from their income. Higher-paying job, more wealth accumulated — probably a strong relationship.

  • Residual: you predict wealth from income, and sometimes you're a bit high, sometimes a bit low, for any individual.
  • Error term: if income is the only thing you use, then everything else that predicts wealth — your parents' wealth, winning the lottery — is not in your model. It's still real, it still determines the outcome, and it has to go somewhere. That somewhere is the error term.

Where it breaks — this is the important bit. The error term is not just random noise. Writing the data generating process as

y = β₀ + β₁x + ε

makes two claims: that x is in the data generating process for y, and that there's other stuff too. If you're predicting wealth from income, it isn't that everything besides income is noise — you can name several of those things, and they're real determinants of wealth.

2

The linguistic jump: controlling = moving things out of the error term

Plain English. A control variable doesn't create information. It relocates it.

Precisely. Start with y = β₀ + β₁x + ε. If z is part of the data generating process for y — if z was used in the creation of y — then z is already in that first model. You can't see it, but everything determining y is in there somewhere, and if it isn't x, it's in ε.

Now add z as a predictor: y = β₀ + β₁x + β₂z + ε. You haven't discovered z. You've moved z out of the error term and into the model.

Anchor image. A room where the mess is either on the shelves or under the bed. Putting something on a shelf doesn't create it — it just stops being part of the unexamined pile.

Why it matters: this changes the relationship between x and the error term. If z and x are related, then in the first model x is related to the error term. In the second, you've removed the part of the error term x was related to — and maybe now x is unrelated to it.

The jump. We've spent chapters saying close all the back doors to identify the effect of X on Y. In a regression context, that is very closely related to saying:

Make X unrelated to the error term.

3

Omitted variable bias, and its many names

Plain English. Leave out something correlated with your treatment, and your treatment absorbs its effect.

Precisely. If x is related to the error term you have omitted variable bias. You may also hear that x is endogenous. These, plus "the effect isn't causally identified" and "you haven't closed all the back doors", are all largely the same statement.

The "omitted variable" part: you've omitted from your model a variable related to x. Because it's omitted it lives in the error term, so x and the error term are related, and your estimate won't reflect the actual causal effect of x on y.

Anchor image. A colleague who is always standing next to whoever fixes things. You never see the fixer. You promote the colleague.

Worked example — why the bias happens. You estimate the relationship between y and x, and there's a z out there. Suppose z is positively related to x and positively related to y, but you fit the first model, so z sits in the error term.

Z is doing all sorts of positive things for y. OLS doesn't know z exists — you never told it. But it does see that x happens to be around whenever all those positive things are happening.

So it gives x the credit.

That's omitted variable bias: OLS assigns z's effects to x, because it doesn't know any better.

Where it breaks. The coefficient on x will mix the effect of x on y together with all the alternative explanations still sitting in the error term. It gives x the credit — or the blame — for whatever those things are doing, as long as they're related to x.

4

What this sets up

Plain English. Nearly everything in this chapter is about the error term in one way or another.

Precisely. Several assumptions about the error term are coming. Understanding what's in it and how it moves around matters for estimating standard errors and the sampling distribution of OLS coefficients, as well as for relating a regression model to the causal identification you're trying to achieve.

Say it out loud

Part two of the book, and it opens with the thing that makes regression either work or quietly lie to you: the error term.

First, don't confuse it with a residual. A residual is how wrong you were about one data point — you predicted 20, it was 22. The error term is what your model leaves out about the world entirely.

Say I predict your wealth from your income. Reasonable — higher income, more wealth. But income isn't the only thing that determines wealth. Your parents' wealth matters. Winning the lottery matters. Those things are real, they genuinely determine the outcome, and they aren't in my equation. So where do they go? They go in the error term.

And that's the thing people get wrong: the error term is not noise. It's full of real stuff you could name.

Now here's the move that makes the whole chapter click. Take y = β₀ + β₁x + ε. If Z genuinely helps determine Y, then Z is already in that equation — you just can't see it, because it's inside the ε. When I "control for Z", I haven't discovered anything. I've just moved Z out of the error term and into the model.

Which means everything we've been saying about closing back doors translates into exactly one sentence in regression: make X unrelated to the error term.

And if you don't? Watch what happens. Suppose Z pushes X up and pushes Y up, and Z is stuck in the error term. Z is out there doing all these good things for Y. OLS has no idea Z exists — but it does notice that X is always hanging around when the good things happen. So it gives X the credit.

That's omitted variable bias. Or "X is endogenous." Or "you didn't close the back doors." Same thing, three vocabularies.

Recall drill

QDefine a residual.

The difference between the actual value and your prediction for a data point — actual minus prediction.

QDefine the error term.

The difference between what your model would predict if it had all the data in the world and the best possible estimated version of itself, and what would still be left out — everything in the data generating process your model doesn't include.

QWhat does OLS minimise when choosing a line?

The squared residuals.

QYou predict wealth from income. Give examples of what's in the error term.

Everything else that determines wealth but isn't in the model — your parents' wealth, winning the lottery.

QWhy is it wrong to think of the error term as noise?

Because it contains real predictors and determinants of the outcome that you can often name. They're not random; they're just outside your equation.

QIn y = β₀ + β₁x + ε, where is a variable Z that helps determine Y but isn't a predictor?

In the error term. Everything determining Y is in the model somewhere; if it isn't an explicit predictor, it's in ε.

QWhat does adding Z as a control variable actually do?

It moves Z out of the error term and into the model itself.

QWhy does that matter for identification?

Because it changes X's relationship to the error term. If Z and X are related, X was related to the error term before; removing Z from it may leave X unrelated to what remains.

QState "close all the back doors" in regression language.

Make X unrelated to the error term.

QName three ways of saying the same problem when X is related to the error term.

Omitted variable bias; X is endogenous; the effect isn't causally identified — equivalently, you haven't closed all the back doors.

QZ is positively related to both X and Y and is left out. Explain the bias mechanism.

Z does positive things for Y. OLS can't see Z, but it sees that X is present whenever those positive things happen, so it credits X with Z's effects.

QDoes every omitted variable cause omitted variable bias?

No — it has to be related to X. Something omitted but unrelated to the treatment doesn't transfer its effect to X's coefficient.

Glossary

Residual
actual value minus predicted value for an observation.
Error term (ε)
everything in the data generating process for the outcome that the model does not include.
Omitted variable bias
bias arising when a variable related to the treatment is left in the error term, so the treatment absorbs its effect.
Endogenous
a treatment variable that is related to the error term.
Ordinary least squares
the line-fitting procedure that minimises squared residuals.
