Ep 40When Matching Goes Wrong

▶ Watch the video 18:40
On this pageThe 30-second versionWhy this existsThe ideasTrapsSay it out loudRecall drillConnectionsGlossary

Matching rests on three things — the conditional independence assumption (you matched on enough to close every back door), common support (there is actually somebody on the other side to match to), and balance (the matching did what you asked) — and the last two you can and must check.

The 30-second version

  • Conditional independence assumption: treatment is essentially random once you condition on your matching variables. This is "close all the back doors" wearing a matching-shaped hat.
  • Common support: you need substantial overlap in the matching variables' distributions between groups. If nobody untreated looks like the treated, there's nothing to match to.
  • The usual fix — dropping areas of non-common support — works, but can delete the most typical members of your treated group and leave an unrepresentative estimate.
  • Balance: after matching, did the groups actually end up similar? Check with a balance table of means.
  • Means aren't enough. Two distributions can share a mean and look nothing alike. Check distributions too.
  • If balance is bad, go back and redo the matching. That's legitimate.

Why this exists

Several episodes of how to match, and almost nothing on the assumptions that make matching appropriate — or what you can check to see whether things went well. Three things: one assumption you have to make, and two you can actually inspect.

The ideas

1

1 · The conditional independence assumption

Plain English. You matched on enough variables to close every back door.

Precisely. You pick matching variables and match the treated and control groups on them, because you want the differences between those groups to be driven by the effect of treatment rather than by other variables. The conditional independence assumption says you've achieved that: you matched on all the variables needed to close all the back doors, so treatment is basically random conditional on the matching variables you selected.

Where it breaks. It's another name for something you already know. Identify an effect → close all the back doors. In the matching context that's the conditional independence assumption; in the regression context it's getting rid of omitted variable bias. Lots of different words for the same idea.

2

2 · Common support

Plain English. There has to be somebody on the other side who looks like you.

Precisely. For matching to work you need substantial overlap in the distributions of the variables you're matching on. Look at the distribution of a matching variable in the treated group and in the control group: there must be at least some ranges where you see plenty of people in both, or you can't match on that variable.

Anchor image. A dating app where everyone in your city is already in a relationship. The app works fine. There's just nobody to match you with.

Worked example — the job training program. Suppose the program deliberately targets one neighbourhood — Capitol Hill in Seattle, where Nick's college is — and recruits everyone there. Neighbourhood is on a back door, so you'd like to match on it. But if everybody in Capitol Hill got recruited, there's nobody in Capitol Hill who wasn't treated to match to. And symmetrically there's nobody treated outside Capitol Hill to match to the untreated group. You can't match on neighbourhood at all.

Worked example — the Broockman study. Broockman sent fictionalised requests for help to legislators, using an email name that sounded like it belonged to a Black person — a fictional Tyrone Washington. The question: if you're a legislator and the email comes from outside your district, do you respond? There's no inherent benefit — they can't vote for you. And does the response rate differ between Black and non-Black legislators?

  • Treatment: whether the legislator is Black.
  • The back door: Black legislators are more likely to be elected in areas with many Black residents, so the percentage of Black people in the district sits on a back door between the legislator's race and, for other reasons, whether they respond.

Now look at the distributions of percent-Black-in-district:

GroupWhere they're concentrated
Non-Black legislatorsThe vast majority in districts without many Black residents
Black legislatorsVery likely in districts 50–75% Black

Very different distributions. There may be common support at relatively low percentages — but in the 50–75% range, where most Black legislators are, there simply aren't enough non-Black legislators to compare against. That's a failure of common support exactly where it hurts.

Common support for the propensity score. The same requirement applies to propensity scores. To compare treated to untreated while adjusting for the propensity of being treated, you need some treated people who had a decent probability of not being treated, and some untreated people who had a decent probability of being treated. In Broockman's data the propensity score distributions for Black and non-Black legislators are very disjoint — there isn't much overlap where you could say "here's a Black legislator, and here's a non-Black legislator with a similar probability."

The fix, and its cost. Drop the observations for which there's no common support — dropping areas of non-common support. You might drop all the Black legislators in 50–75% Black districts, because there's no good match for them; or drop everybody above a certain propensity score, because only the low range is comparable.

This fixes the statistical problem: better matches for whoever remains. But you're dropping a lot of observations — and here, specifically, the most common kind of Black legislator. So you've solved one problem and introduced another: how representative is your new estimate of the actual distribution?

Propensity score trimming is the same logic. Drop very low and very high scores, because you're unlikely to find matches: someone with a 90% chance of being treated probably was treated, so there won't be many controls up there; someone with a 0.05% chance won't have many treated observations to compare to.

3

3 · Balance

Plain English. Did the matching actually make the groups look alike? Go and look.

Precisely. You did your matching and got matched or weighted samples. The goal was to make the matching variables — or the propensity score — similar between the treated and control groups. So check whether that happened.

The balance table. Compare the average of each matching variable, or the propensity score, in both groups and hope they're close.

Worked example — Broockman's balance table, before and after matching:

VariableBefore matchingAfter matching
Median household income3.33 vs 4.4353.33 vs 3.316
Democrat97.8% vs 50.1%97.8% vs 97.8%

Before matching, household income was much higher in areas with white legislators than in areas with Black legislators. After matching it found areas with white legislators that had similar household incomes — 3.33 against 3.316, a far closer comparison.

The Democrat variable is even starker: 97.8% of Black legislators were Democrats against only 50.1% of non-Black legislators. After matching it's exactly 97.8% for both — it ended up selecting almost entirely Democratic non-Black legislators as comparisons.

Where it breaks — means aren't enough. A balance table compares means, and that's not the only thing you might care about. It's entirely possible for the treated and control means to be identical or very close while the distributions still don't look the same. Two distributions could both have a mean of zero and look nothing alike — perfect on a balance table, and you would not say that matching worked well.

So after matching, also look at the distribution of each matching variable, or of the propensity score.

Worked example — Broockman's distributions after matching.

  • Percentage Black in the area: looks pretty decent. It doesn't line up exactly, but there's quite a lot of similarity between Black and non-Black legislators after matching and weighting.
  • Propensity score: you'd really want this one close, since you are literally weighting on the propensity score to make the two the same. It isn't — which is concerning. There are a lot more non-Black legislators with very low propensity scores, and also some with very high ones. Even though the average is the same, that distribution would give you pause, and you might go back and redo the matching.

If balance is bad, go back and redo some of your matching process to try for a better match. That's entirely legitimate — you're just making your match work better. Add polynomials to your propensity score model, change the matching variables, and so on.

Say it out loud

Three things have to hold for matching to work. One you assume; two you can actually go and check.

The assumption is the conditional independence assumption — that you matched on enough variables that treatment is basically random once you condition on them. Which is just "you closed all the back doors" in different clothing. Same idea as no omitted variable bias in regression.

Then common support, and this is the one people forget. You need there to actually be somebody on the other side who looks like your treated people. Imagine a job training program that recruits everyone in one neighbourhood. Now try to match on neighbourhood — there's nobody in that neighbourhood who wasn't treated. There's nothing to match to.

Broockman's study is the clean example. He emailed legislators pretending to be a constituent from outside their district, and asked whether Black and non-Black legislators respond differently. The back door is the percentage of Black residents in the district — because that's where Black legislators get elected. And when you look at the distributions, non-Black legislators are almost all in districts with few Black residents, while Black legislators are mostly in districts that are 50 to 75 percent Black. There's just nobody to compare in that range.

The fix is to drop the observations with no common support — and notice what that costs. You end up deleting the most typical Black legislators in the entire study. You fixed the statistics and broke the representativeness.

Third: balance. Did the matching actually work? Broockman's balance table is lovely here — before matching, 97.8% of Black legislators were Democrats versus 50% of non-Black. After matching: 97.8% for both. Exactly.

But careful — a balance table shows means. Two distributions can have identical means and look completely different. So look at the distributions too. In Broockman's case the percent-Black distributions line up nicely after matching, but the propensity score distributions don't — which is awkward, since weighting on the propensity score is the whole thing you did. Same average, wrong shape. That's a reason to go back and try again.

Recall drill

QName the three things matching depends on.

The conditional independence assumption, common support, and balance.

QState the conditional independence assumption.

That you matched on all the variables needed to close all the back doors, so treatment is essentially random conditional on your matching variables.

QWhat are the equivalent phrasings of that assumption?

"Close all the back doors" in the causal diagram context, and "no omitted variable bias" in the regression context.

QDefine common support.

Substantial overlap in the distributions of the matching variables between the treated and control groups — ranges where there are plenty of people on both sides.

QGive the job training illustration of failed common support.

If a program recruits everyone in one neighbourhood, there's nobody in that neighbourhood who wasn't treated, and nobody treated outside it — so you can't match on neighbourhood.

QWhat was Broockman studying, and what was the treatment?

Whether legislators respond to help requests from an apparently Black constituent outside their district — a fictional Tyrone Washington. The treatment is whether the legislator is Black.

QWhat's the back door in that study?

The percentage of Black residents in the district — Black legislators are more likely to be elected there, and district composition may also affect responding for other reasons.

QDescribe the common support failure in Broockman's data.

Non-Black legislators are overwhelmingly in districts with few Black residents, while Black legislators are mostly in districts 50–75% Black — so there aren't enough non-Black legislators to compare against exactly where most Black legislators are.

QWhat does common support mean for a propensity score specifically?

You need some treated people with a decent probability of not being treated, and some untreated people with a decent probability of being treated.

QWhat is "dropping areas of non-common support", and what does it cost?

Removing observations in ranges where there's nobody comparable on the other side. It improves the remaining matches but can delete the most typical treated observations, leaving an unrepresentative estimate.

QWhy trim very high and very low propensity scores?

Someone with a 90% chance of treatment probably was treated, so there are few controls up there; someone with a 0.05% chance has few treated observations to compare to.

QWhat is a balance table?

A comparison of the average of each matching variable, or the propensity score, between the treated and control groups — ideally before and after matching.

QIn Broockman's table, what happened to the Democrat variable?

Before matching, 97.8% of Black legislators were Democrats versus 50.1% of non-Black legislators. After matching it was exactly 97.8% for both — almost entirely Democratic non-Black legislators were selected.

QWhy isn't a good balance table sufficient?

It compares means, and two distributions can have identical means while looking completely different in shape.

QWhat did Broockman's post-matching distributions show?

Percent Black in the district lined up fairly well, but the propensity score distributions did not — more non-Black legislators at very low and very high scores — which is concerning given the weighting was done on the propensity score.

QWhat should you do if balance looks bad?

Go back and redo the matching — add polynomials to the propensity score model, change matching variables — which is entirely legitimate.
