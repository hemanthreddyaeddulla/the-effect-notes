Ep 3Types of Variables

▶ Watch the video 8:45

There are five kinds of variables — continuous, count, ordinal, categorical, and qualitative — and which kind you have decides which descriptions and calculations are even legal on it.

The 30-second version

  • A variable is one measurement taken repeatedly across observations: flower height, recorded once per flower.
  • Describing a variable means telling someone what's in it without making them read every row.
  • Five types: continuous (any value in a range), count (whole, non-negative), ordinal (ordered, no meaningful spacing), categorical (unordered groups; binary is the two-option case), qualitative (too complex for maths).
  • The type isn't a label — it's a constraint. You can't average ordinal data, because there's no such thing as the average of "middle school" and "college."
  • The book works with the first four. Qualitative data has to be coded into one of them before you can use it.

Why this exists

You have thousands of flower heights in a spreadsheet. Nobody is going to read them. Your job is to say what that variable looks like — what's typical, how much it varies, whether there are lots of short ones and tall ones or whether they're all much the same.

But you can't pick a description until you know what kind of thing you're describing. The right summary for "how tall is this flower" is not the right summary for "what's this student's major", and applying one to the other produces numbers that look fine and mean nothing.

The ideas

1

Continuous

Plain English. It can be any value in a range, and there's no "next" value up.

Precisely. Between any two values there's always another, given enough measurement precision. The apparent granularity is a limit of your ruler, not of the variable.

Anchor image. A dimmer switch, not a light switch. There's no click-stop between settings.

Worked example. Flower height. It isn't that one flower is 10 inches and the next is 11 — measure precisely enough and it's 10.132765 inches and 11.12321 inches. The flowers aren't one step apart. One is just slightly taller.

2

Count

Plain English. How many of something there are.

Precisely. Whole numbers only, and normally can't go below zero — you can't count something a negative number of times. Runs from 0 up to arbitrarily large.

Anchor image. Eggs in a carton. There are eleven or twelve. There is no 11.4.

Worked example. How many flowers are in the field today.

Where it breaks. The whole-number floor at zero matters when you describe or model it — a method that happily predicts −2.3 flowers is telling you something is off.

3

Ordinal

Plain English. You can put the values in order, but you can't say how far apart they are.

Precisely. A ranking without a scale. You may write numbers on the categories, but those numbers don't carry distance, so arithmetic on them is meaningless.

Anchor image. Finishing positions in a race. Second beat third, and third beat fourth — but that tells you nothing about whether second was a hair ahead or a lap ahead.

Worked example. Education: elementary → middle school → high school → college → PhD. Everyone agrees middle school is more than elementary, and high school is more than middle school. But high school isn't "one more" than middle school and middle school isn't "0.5 more" than elementary. So what would the average of elementary, high school, and college even be? There's no answer, because there's no scale to average on.

Where it breaks. The mistake is coding it 1–5 and treating those as real numbers. The order survives that coding; the spacing you just invented does not.

4

Categorical (and binary)

Plain English. Mutually exclusive groups with no order at all.

Precisely. Every observation belongs to exactly one category, and no category is "more" than another. Binary is the special case with exactly two options.

Anchor image. Which platform your train leaves from. Platform 4 isn't more than platform 2. They're just different places.

Worked example. College majors — engineering, biology, English. Engineering isn't more major than English. Compare with education level, which does have an order: same domain, different variable type.

Binary: are you left-handed or not — two options, in or out. The unrestricted categorical version of the same idea might be left-handed / right-handed / ambidextrous / no hands.

Where it breaks. Categorical and binary variables are everywhere in social science, because so many of the comparisons we care about are between groups. That makes them the ones you'll most often be tempted to hand to a method expecting numbers.

5

Qualitative

Plain English. Too rich and complex to be represented mathematically at all.

Precisely. Not covered in the book. To use it quantitatively you must first code it — convert it into one of the other four types — and you're then analysing the coding, not the original.

Anchor image. A song. You can record its length in seconds, its key, its genre — but none of those is the song.

Worked example. The text of a newspaper article. Word count is a count variable you derived from it. Tagging it "inflation" and "military" makes categorical variables. The meaning of the article — what it actually says — is the qualitative part, and to get at that you read it.

Traps

Sounds rightOrdinal data has numbers on it, so I can average it
ActuallyActually, the numbers are labels for an order, not measurements of a distance. The average of elementary, high school, and college doesn't exist.
Sounds rightOrdinal and categorical are basically the same thing
ActuallyActually, ordinal has an order and categorical doesn't. Education is ordinal; college major is categorical. Same subject area, different type, different legal operations.
Sounds rightBinary is its own separate type
ActuallyActually, binary is just a categorical variable with two options.
Sounds rightHeight looks continuous but my data only has whole inches, so it's a count
ActuallyActually, the type is about the underlying quantity, not your measurement precision. Height is continuous, coarsely measured.

Say it out loud

Before you describe any variable you have to know what kind of thing it is, because the type decides what you're even allowed to do with it.

There are five. Continuous — any value in a range, no next value up. Flower height: one isn't one step taller than another, it's just slightly taller. Count — whole numbers, can't go negative, like how many flowers are in the field.

Then two that people constantly mix up. Ordinal is ordered but unspaced. Education: elementary, middle school, high school, college. Everyone agrees each is more than the last — but high school isn't "one more" than middle school, so you can't average them. What would the average of elementary and college be? There's no answer. And categorical has no order at all. College majors: engineering, biology, English. Engineering isn't more of a major than English, they're just different. Binary is the same thing with only two options — left-handed or not.

And qualitative — the actual text of a newspaper article. Too complex for maths. You can code it into one of the other types, count the words or tag the topics, but then you're analysing your coding, not the article.

The reason to know all this up front is simple: the way you summarise a variable depends entirely on which one it is. Use the wrong summary and you'll get a number that looks perfectly reasonable and means nothing.

Recall drill

QWhat is a variable?

One measurement taken repeatedly across many observations — the same thing recorded each time, like the height of each flower in a field.

QWhat does it mean to "describe" a variable?

Summarise what's in it — what's typical, how much it varies — so someone understands it without reading every observation.

QName the five types of variable.

Continuous, count, ordinal, categorical (including binary), and qualitative.

QWhat makes a variable continuous rather than a count?

Continuous can take any value in a range with no "next" value — measure more precisely and you get more decimals. A count is whole numbers only and can't be negative.

QEducation level and college major are both about education. Why are they different types?

Education level is ordinal — the categories have an order. College major is categorical — engineering isn't more than English, just different.

QWhy can't you take the average of an ordinal variable?

The categories have order but no meaningful spacing, so there's no scale to average on. "The average of elementary, high school, and college" doesn't refer to anything.

QHow does binary relate to categorical?

Binary is a categorical variable with exactly two options — left-handed or not.

QYou want to analyse newspaper articles quantitatively. What do you have to do first?

Code them into one of the other types — word count (count), topic tags (categorical) — and accept you're now analysing the coding, not the text itself.

QWhy does variable type matter at all?

Because the description you use depends on the type. Choosing the wrong one gives you numbers that are computable but meaningless.

Glossary

Variable
a measurement recorded repeatedly across observations.
Continuous variable
takes any value in a range; no next value up.
Count variable
a whole-number tally, non-negative.
Ordinal variable
categories with a meaningful order but no meaningful spacing.
Categorical variable
mutually exclusive, unordered groups.
Binary variable
a categorical variable with exactly two options.
Qualitative variable
too complex to represent mathematically; must be coded before quantitative use.
Coding
converting qualitative material into a countable or categorical variable.
