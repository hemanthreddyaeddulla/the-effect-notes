Ep 35Matching

▶ Watch the video 14:00
On this pageThe 30-second versionWhy this existsThe ideasTrapsSay it out loudRecall drillConnectionsGlossary

Regression closes a back door by subtracting out the variation a confounder explains; matching closes the same back door by picking a sample that has no variation in it — build a control group whose confounders look just like the treated group's, and that confounder cannot possibly be driving your comparison.

The 30-second version

  • Closing a back door means removing all variation through the confounder. Regression does it by subtracting out predictions and using residuals.
  • Matching does it by choosing a sample instead. No variation in the confounder → it can't confound.
  • More precisely: make the treated and control groups have no differences in that variable.
  • Vs. regression: matching leans less on linearity and gives more flexibility over which treatment-effect average you get. But it demands far more decisions, and results are more sensitive to them.
  • Two approaches: select a matched sample (a subset of controls that look like the treated), or weight the controls so their weighted average looks like the treated group.
  • Running underneath everything: the bias–variance trade-off. Better matches or more matches — pick one.

Why this exists

Regression is popular in causal inference largely because it's so easy to use it to control for confounders and therefore close back doors. But step back and ask why that works — what are you actually doing when you close a back door?

You're removing all variation through the variable on that back door. Take shorts and ice cream: a hot day causes both, so you'd see a positive correlation that isn't causal. Adjust for temperature and what you've done is take all the variation in ice cream eating driven by temperature, and all the variation in shorts-wearing driven by temperature, and get rid of both. In regression, that means subtracting out whatever you can predict with temperature and using just the residual.

But there are other ways to remove variation associated with a variable. What if you simply picked a sample where there isn't any?

The ideas

1

What matching is

Plain English. Don't adjust the data. Choose data that doesn't have the problem.

Precisely. Matching closes a back door — controls for something, removes a confounder — by picking a sample in which there is no variation in that variable. Or more generally, by picking a sample in which the groups you're comparing, typically a treated group against a control group, have no differences in that variable.

Anchor image. Two runners and a headwind. Regression estimates the wind and subtracts it from their times. Matching just makes them run the same race.

Worked example — the intuition. Take only hot days, and see whether ice cream eating is related to shorts-wearing within hot days. Or only cold days. In that sample temperature can't be driving anything, because it isn't varying.

Or make the groups match: your treated group has a 70-degree day and a 50-degree day, and your control group also has a 70-degree day and a 50-degree day. Temperature can't be driving any difference between those two groups — they have the exact same temperatures. They're already comparable on that basis.

2

Worked example: the job training program

Plain English. Your treated group is 80% men and your control group is 50/50. Fix that by choosing who's in the control group.

Worked example. Does a job training program affect whether you get a job?

GroupComposition
Treated (took the program)80 men, 20 women
Control (didn't)500 men, 500 women

You can't compare straight across. If the treated group turned out more likely to get a job, you couldn't tell whether that's the effect of the program or the effect of gender — men and women may have different job-getting rates, and there's a big gender imbalance in one group and not the other. That's a back door.

The fix: pick 80 men and 20 women out of the control group. Now the gender mix is identical in both groups, so whatever difference remains cannot be because of gender — there isn't a different gender mix anymore. Back door closed.

3

Matching versus regression

Plain English. Neither wins. They trade different things.

Matching is better atMatching is worse at
Not relying so heavily on linearity assumptions — OLS assumes predictors and controls enter linearly; many forms of matching don't careRequiring a lot more decision making
More flexibility in estimating different kinds of treatment effect averages (Chapter 10)Results being more sensitive to the particular decisions you make

Neither is better than the other — they do different things. And you can combine them in ways the chapter discusses.

4

Approach 1: select a matched sample

Plain English. Hand-pick control observations that resemble your treated ones.

Precisely. Choose a subset of your control observations that are like the treated observations. The job training example is exactly this — take an 80:20 ratio out of the control group.

Worked example beyond binary variables. Suppose income also differs between groups. Take a treated person who earned $100,000 before needing a job and is male — find a male control who earned $100,000. Take a treated male who earned $20,000 — find a male control earning $20,000, or at least close to it. You may not get it exactly on the nose, but you can get in the range.

Do that across every characteristic you can measure and you end up with two groups that are basically the same on all those control variables, and therefore comparable. If you've matched on enough variables to close all the back doors, any difference in their outcomes can be assigned to the effect of treatment.

5

Approach 2: weighting

Plain English. Keep everyone, but make some people count more.

Precisely. Construct a weighted average of your control observations such that the weighted average looks like your treatment group.

Worked example — what a weighted average is. Two people: one earns $100,000, the other $200,000.

  • Plain average: (100,000 + 200,000) / 2 = $150,000
  • Weighted, giving the first person double weight: count them twice. (100,000 + 100,000 + 200,000) = 400,000, and the total weight is 3 (two for the first person, one for the second). 400,000 / 3 ≈ $133,000

The average moved closer to the person with the lower income, because they counted more. That's a weighted average.

Worked example — applied to job training. Treated: 80 men, 20 women. Control: 500 men, 500 women. Assign weights so the control group's gender mix matches the treated group's:

Control groupWeightWhy
Each man80 / 500 = 0.16500 men need to account for the treated group's 80
Each woman20 / 500 = 0.04500 women need to account for the treated group's 20

Each man counts much more, because you want this sample to look like a sample with proportionally more men. Each woman counts less, because there are fewer women over there. Now the weighted average job-getting rate accounts for the gender difference — it can't be that there are simply more men in the treated group, because you're counting each control man more heavily to reflect exactly that.

6

The bias–variance trade-off

Plain English. Better comparisons or more comparisons. You can't have both.

Precisely. Most decisions in a matching analysis come down to this: do you want fewer observations to compare against — and therefore more noise from a smaller sample — or better observations, which are better comparisons and therefore less biased, doing a better job of closing the back door?

ApproachBiasVariance
WeightingHigher — you may be using worse comparisonsLower — you get to use everybody, so more precise estimates
Matched sampleLower — you're picking better observationsHigher — you throw out more observations, so more noise

Anchor image. A jury. A handful of people who genuinely understand the case, or a large panel where some are only half following. Accuracy versus reliability.

Where it breaks. This trade-off comes up over and over as you work through the matching decisions — which is exactly what the next episode is about.

Say it out loud

Here's the thing that makes matching click. Ask why controlling for a variable closes a back door. The answer: because you removed all the variation in it. If temperature doesn't vary, it can't be driving anything.

Regression does that by subtracting — it predicts what it can with temperature and works with the leftovers. But that's not the only way to kill variation in a variable. You could just pick a sample that doesn't have any. Only look at hot days. Now temperature isn't varying, so it can't confound anything. That's matching.

Real version. Say I'm evaluating a job training program. The people who took it are 80 men and 20 women. My control group is 500 men and 500 women. I can't just compare those — if the treated group does better, is that the program, or is it that they're mostly men? So instead I go into my control group and pick 80 men and 20 women. Now both groups have the same gender mix, so gender cannot possibly explain any difference I find.

There's a second way to do it: weights. Instead of picking people, keep everyone and make some count more. I want my 500 control men to stand in for 80 treated men, so each gets a weight of 80/500 — 0.16. Each woman gets 20/500 — 0.04. Now the weighted average has the right gender mix without throwing anyone away.

And that's the tension you'll hit in every single matching decision from here: bias versus variance. Pick only the best matches and your comparison is excellent but you've got barely any data, so it's noisy. Keep everyone with weights and you're precise, but you've let some genuinely bad comparisons back in.

Recall drill

QWhat are you actually doing when you close a back door?

Removing all the variation through the variable on that back door.

QHow does regression remove that variation?

By subtracting out whatever it can predict with the confounder and using just the residual.

QHow does matching remove it instead?

By picking a sample in which that variable doesn't vary — or in which the treated and control groups have no differences in it.

QGive the temperature intuition.

Look only at hot days (or only cold days). Within that sample temperature isn't varying, so it can't be driving the relationship between shorts and ice cream.

QTreated group is 80 men and 20 women; control is 500 men and 500 women. Why can't you compare directly?

A difference in job-getting rates could be the program or could be the gender imbalance — you couldn't tell which. That's an open back door.

QWhat's the matched-sample fix?

Select 80 men and 20 women from the control group, so both groups have the same gender mix.

QName matching's two advantages over regression.

It doesn't rely as heavily on linearity assumptions, and it offers more flexibility in estimating different kinds of treatment effect averages.

QName matching's two disadvantages.

It requires far more decision making, and results can be much more sensitive to those decisions.

QWhat are the two main approaches to matching?

Selecting a matched sample (a subset of controls resembling the treated), or weighting the controls so their weighted average resembles the treated group.

QTwo people earn $100,000 and $200,000. What's the weighted average if the first gets double weight?

(100,000 + 100,000 + 200,000) / 3 ≈ $133,000 — closer to the first person because they count more.

QTreated group has 80 men and 20 women; control has 500 of each. Give the weights.

Each control man gets 80/500 = 0.16; each control woman gets 20/500 = 0.04.

QState the bias–variance trade-off in matching.

Fewer, better-matched observations mean less bias but more noise. More observations, including worse matches, mean more precision but more bias.

QWhich side of that trade-off does weighting sit on, and why?

Lower variance, higher bias — you get to use everybody, which is more precise, but some of those are worse comparisons.

Glossary

Matching
closing a back door by selecting a sample in which the confounder doesn't vary between groups.
Matched sample
a subset of control observations chosen to resemble the treated observations.
Weighting
assigning weights to control observations so their weighted average resembles the treated group.
Weighted average
an average in which observations contribute unequally according to assigned weights.
Bias–variance trade-off
the tension between better comparisons (less bias) and more comparisons (less noise).
