Ep 56Staggered Treatment Difference-in-Differences

▶ Watch the video 11:40
On this pageThe 30-second versionWhy this existsThe ideasTrapsSay it out loudRecall drillConnectionsGlossary

When different groups get treated at different times, two-way fixed effects quietly uses already-treated groups as controls — and if treatment effects evolve at all, those "controls" deviate from trend and break parallel trends by themselves, which is why you must estimate separate effects per cohort per period and aggregate afterwards.

The 30-second version

  • Staggered treatment / rollout design: groups treated at different times. School A in 2012, B in 2013, C never.
  • Researchers long assumed two-way fixed effects handled this. It doesn't.
  • Why: there are two ways to show no change in treatment — never treated, or already treated. So two-way fixed effects uses already-treated units as controls.
  • If effects evolve over time, those already-treated units are deviating from trend — violating parallel trends by construction.
  • This is a two-way fixed effects problem, not a difference-in-differences problem.
  • The fix, shared by all the new estimators: allow effects to differ by cohort and by period, then aggregate.
  • Two approaches: Callaway and Sant'Anna (matching to pick eligible controls each period) and Wooldridge–Mundlak (a great many interaction terms).
  • You cannot just use two-way fixed effects. It can bias you terribly.

Why this exists

This is the secret shame of econometrics — or at least the most recent one.

A staggered treatment, or rollout design, is a difference-in-differences setup where multiple groups become treated at different times. Say three school districts: one gets the new teacher training programme in 2012, another gets it in 2013, and a third never gets it. The treatment was applied in a staggered fashion — rolled out to different places at different times.

For a long time researchers thought you could just treat this as normal and use two-way fixed effects. Which makes sense on the face of it: you still have group fixed effects for baseline differences, time fixed effects for shared changes over time, so the coefficient on treated should give you the effect.

It does not.

The ideas

1

Why two-way fixed effects fails

Plain English. "No change in treatment status" includes people who were already treated — so they get used as controls.

Precisely. Two-way fixed effects strips out within variation via the group fixed effects, then compares within variation between places where the policy went into effect and places where no change was seen.

But there are two different ways to see no change in your treatment level:

  1. You weren't treated before, and you continue to not be treated. (A real control.)
  2. You were already treated before, and you continue to be treated. (Not a control at all.)

So two-way fixed effects isn't just using the control group as a control group — it's also using anybody who has already been treated as a control group.

Anchor image. Measuring how fast a car accelerates by comparing it to a parked car and to a car already doing 60. The second isn't stationary — it's just not changing gear.

Where this might be survivable. Not necessarily fatal. Parallel trends could still hold: if the already-treated group took its jump in outcomes and, after that jump, trends stayed the same, you'd be fine.

But if treatment effects evolve over time, that's never true.

Worked example. School A gets the teacher training programme in 2012, school B in 2013. Suppose the programme doesn't do anything right away — it takes a while to start working.

You're the 2013 school. Two-way fixed effects compares you against a "control group" that includes both the genuine never-treated control and the 2012 school that's already treated.

Right away, you see nothing. But in the year afterwards, as the 2012 school's effect starts kicking in, that already-treated group starts deviating from trend. So whatever parallel trends you thought held, doesn't — the treatment effects becoming more or less effective over time is itself driving the violation. And everything falls apart.

The important framing. This is really just a problem with two-way fixed effects. It is not a conceptual problem with difference-in-differences. Difference-in-differences doesn't mind that you have several control groups, some becoming treated later and some earlier. It's that two-way fixed effects forces already-treated groups to act as though they're control groups when in fact they aren't.

2

The fix, in general

Plain English. Let every cohort have its own effect in every period, then average afterwards.

Precisely. You can still use difference-in-differences with a staggered rollout — you just need a different estimation method. As of writing, a number of estimators have been proposed and it's a fertile area of research; there may or may not be a settled standard in future. But they share the same basic idea.

The problem is that you're using the wrong control group and not allowing the effect to change over time. So fix both:

  1. Allow the effect to differ by cohort. One effect for those who started being treated in 2012, a different one for those who started in 2013 — when you started being treated gets you your own treatment effect.
  2. Allow the effect to differ by time period. The 2012 cohort gets one effect in 2013, another in 2014, another in 2015. The 2013 cohort gets its own set.
  3. Then aggregate these individual effects back into a single number if you want one — average everything for an overall effect, or average across years since treatment to get "the effect one year after being treated, whenever that happened to be," two years after, and so on.

Estimate a bunch of different effects, then aggregate them afterwards.

3

Approach 1: Callaway and Sant'Anna

Plain English. Pick a fresh, legitimate control group for each period using matching.

Precisely. First it uses matching in each period. For 2012 it asks: who's the best control group for you? And obviously it will only use people who were not actually treated at the time — already-treated people don't get to be picked as controls. Then it may use matching variables or some other process to pick a control group that's comparable to you and eligible to be a control at that time.

So you get a different control group for each time period, based on who's still available. You can choose whether to include units that will eventually be treated as controls — that's up to you.

Having done that, proceed as normal: estimate different effects for each group, comparing to its own personal control group in each future period. It often uses an inverse probability weighting approach, just as in the matching chapter.

The result is individual treatment effects — a different effect for each cohort, in each period. The 2012 cohort has effects in 2013, 2014, 2015, 2016; the 2013 cohort has its own. Aggregate as you like.

4

Approach 2: Wooldridge–Mundlak

Plain English. Interaction terms falling from the sky.

Precisely. This is Jeffrey Wooldridge's approach, only hinted at in the chapter but considerably more popular since the book was written.

It uses a Mundlak estimator — the one from the random effects video. That's an estimator where you have group fixed effects for the within variation but also control for the between variation, so both sources of variation are in the model at the same time.

On top of that, you add a great many interaction terms, allowing different difference-in-differences effects in each time period for each group. It's very similar to the dynamic difference-in-differences model, with a few slight adjustments.

The key adjustment: in dynamic difference-in-differences you took the treated-group variable and interacted it with the time period. Here you also interact with which treated group you're in — so it isn't just are you in a treated group, it's ***which treated group are you in*: did you start being treated in 2012, or in 2013?

So there's an additional set of interaction terms. Estimate them all, exactly as with dynamic difference-in-differences, then aggregate as you like.

5

The bottom line

Plain English. Use one of these. Don't use two-way fixed effects.

Precisely. These estimators all work the same way: estimate a great many treatment effects — via interaction terms, or via separate estimations as in Callaway and Sant'Anna — then aggregate them to get the kind of treatment effect you want.

The key point: you have to use one of these methods if you're looking at a situation where treatment was applied to different groups at different times. You simply cannot use the two-way fixed effects approach. It can bias you quite terribly. You don't want to do it. Use a method that allows all this variation to occur, and then tamp things back down to the single estimate you want after the fact.

Say it out loud

This one is the recent secret shame of econometrics, and it's genuinely worth understanding.

Staggered treatment is when different groups get treated at different times. One school district adopts the programme in 2012, another in 2013, a third never. And for years, everyone just ran two-way fixed effects on it. Group fixed effects, time fixed effects, coefficient on treated. Seems fine.

It is not fine. Here's why, and it's lovely once you see it.

Two-way fixed effects works by comparing units whose treatment status changed against units whose treatment status didn't change. But think about who shows "no change." Two kinds of people: those who were never treated — real controls — and those who were already treated and still are.

So your model is quietly using already-treated schools as your control group.

And that might survive, if treatment effects were constant. But remember the last episode — treatment effects evolve. Say the programme takes a couple of years to work. I'm the 2013 school, and I'm being compared against a "control" that includes the 2012 school — whose effect is just now starting to kick in. So my control group is drifting upward for reasons that have nothing to do with being a control. Parallel trends is violated by the treatment effects themselves.

Crucially: this is a two-way fixed effects problem, not a difference-in-differences problem. The idea is fine. The estimator is broken.

The fix, in every one of the new methods, is the same shape: let each cohort have its own effect — 2012 adopters get one, 2013 adopters get another — and let each of those effects differ by period. Estimate loads of little effects, then aggregate them into whatever summary you want.

Callaway and Sant'Anna does it by picking a fresh control group for each period, using matching, and never letting an already-treated unit serve as a control. Wooldridge–Mundlak does it with an enormous pile of interaction terms — same as dynamic difference-in-differences, except you interact with which cohort you're in, not just whether you're treated.

The one thing to remember: do not run two-way fixed effects on a staggered rollout. It can bias you terribly.

Recall drill

QWhat is a staggered treatment or rollout design?

A difference-in-differences setup where multiple groups become treated at different times — one school district in 2012, another in 2013, another never.

QWhat did researchers long assume about estimating it?

That you could just use two-way fixed effects, since group fixed effects handle baseline differences and time fixed effects handle shared changes.

QWhat are the two ways a unit can show no change in treatment status?

It was never treated and still isn't, or it was already treated and continues to be.

QWhy does that matter?

Because two-way fixed effects treats both as controls — so already-treated units are used as a control group when they aren't one.

QWhen would that be survivable?

If the already-treated group took its jump and then trends stayed the same, parallel trends could still hold.

QWhat makes it not survivable?

Treatment effects that evolve over time — then already-treated units are still deviating from trend.

QWalk through the 2012 and 2013 schools example.

If the programme takes time to work, the 2013 cohort is compared against a control set including the already-treated 2012 school, whose effect is only now kicking in. That school deviates from trend, violating parallel trends.

QIs this a problem with difference-in-differences itself?

No. Difference-in-differences doesn't mind multiple groups treated at different times. It's the two-way fixed effects estimator forcing already-treated groups to act as controls.

QWhat two things do all the new estimators allow?

Effects that differ by cohort — when you started being treated — and effects that differ by time period.

QWhat do you do after estimating all those effects?

Aggregate them into whatever single effect you want — an overall average, or an average by years since treatment.

QHow does Callaway and Sant'Anna choose control groups?

With matching in each period, using only units not actually treated at that time, and optionally including or excluding units that will eventually be treated.

QWhat weighting does it often use?

Inverse probability weighting, as in the matching chapter.

QWhat estimator underlies the Wooldridge approach, and what does it do?

The Mundlak estimator — group fixed effects for within variation while also controlling for the between variation, so both are in the model at once.

QWhat's the key difference between Wooldridge–Mundlak and dynamic difference-in-differences?

You interact not just with the time period but with which treated cohort you're in — whether you started being treated in 2012 or 2013.

QWhat is the bottom-line instruction?

You must use one of these methods for staggered treatment. Two-way fixed effects can bias you terribly.

QIs there a settled standard estimator?

No — it's a fertile area of research, with no consensus at the time of writing.
