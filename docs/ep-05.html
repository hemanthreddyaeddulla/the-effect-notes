Ep 5Theoretical Distributions

▶ Watch the video 14:40
On this pageThe 30-second versionWhy this existsThe ideasTrapsSay it out loudRecall drillConnectionsGlossary

You only ever get one sample, but you can reason about the theoretical distribution — how your answer would vary if you could resample the world over and over — and then use your single sample to rule out which versions of the truth are implausible.

The 30-second version

  • The goal of all of this is to learn about the world from your data. You have one small window; the world is the whole building.
  • If you resampled repeatedly you'd get a different answer every time. The distribution of those answers is the sampling distribution, or theoretical distribution.
  • You can often write it down from theory: coin flips are binomial, averages tend toward normal, counts are Poisson, and so on.
  • Its centre contains the thing you actually want — the true average, or the true effect of X on Y.
  • With one sample you can't pin down the truth. You can narrow the range by ruling out truths under which your data would be very unlikely.
  • The probability of getting your result or something more extreme, assuming a given theoretical distribution, is the p-value.

Why this exists

You want to know how the world works. Inconveniently, you don't have the world. You have a dataset — one window into it.

And here's the infuriating part: statistics doesn't hand you one answer. Go back and collect a different sample and you get a different number. Again, a different one. So the question can't be "what's the answer?" It has to be "given that my answer would jump around from sample to sample, what can this particular answer tell me about the truth underneath?"

That reframing is what theoretical distributions are for. If you can say in advance how your answer should bounce around, then the one bounce you observed becomes evidence.

The ideas

1

The theoretical distribution is the distribution of answers, not of data points

Plain English. Not "how are the values in my data spread out" but "how would my result be spread out if I redid the whole study over and over."

Precisely. The theoretical (sampling) distribution describes how a statistic varies from sample to sample, given some assumed truth about the world. Its parameters — its true mean, its true standard deviation — are properties of the world, not of your dataset.

Anchor image. One dart in the board tells you little. But if you know the shape of the scatter a given thrower produces, one dart tells you a lot about where they were aiming.

Worked example. A fair coin, flipped twice. From theory alone — the coin is fair, and coin flips follow a binomial distribution — you can state the whole distribution before collecting anything:

OutcomeHow often
One head, one tail (either order)50%
Two heads25%
Two tails25%

That's what you'd see if you flipped two coins here, then two coins over there, then two more, and plotted all the results.

Where it breaks. This is entirely conditional on your assumptions about the world. It tells you how data would be distributed if the coin were fair. Change the assumption and you get a different theoretical distribution.

2

Different situations, different distributions

Plain English. Which theoretical distribution applies depends on what kind of thing you're measuring.

Precisely. A handful you'll meet: binomial for a series of yes/no trials like coin flips; normal for averages; log-normal for a variable that's normal after taking logs; Poisson for counts of how often something happens; gamma, and many more. A statistics textbook has the full zoo.

Anchor image. Different tools leave different tool-marks. If you know the tool, you know the mark to expect.

Worked example. Why averages go normal: measure the average height in this classroom, then that classroom, then another, and plot all those averages. You get something normal shaped. Describing the distribution of averages is one of the things the normal distribution does, which is why it turns up constantly — most of what we compute is some kind of average.

3

The centre of the theoretical distribution is the thing you want

Plain English. The reason to care about this distribution is that the truth you're after is sitting at the middle of it.

Precisely. The central tendency of the theoretical distribution is the true population parameter. Learn about the distribution and you've learned about the parameter.

Worked example. For classroom heights, the centre of the theoretical distribution is the true overall average classroom height. Same structure applies to the true effect of X on Y — which is why this machinery carries straight into causal inference.

4

Narrowing the range of plausible truths

Plain English. You can't work out which theoretical distribution is the true one. You can throw out the ones that make your data look absurd.

Precisely. Consider all the candidate theoretical distributions — one for each possible truth. Your single sample can't select among them, but it can render some of them very unlikely. That's inference: not pinning down the truth, but narrowing the range you consider reasonable.

Anchor image. You can't identify a stranger from a footprint. You can rule out everyone with a different shoe size.

Worked example. Sampling average classroom heights for ten-year-olds and getting 3'7". Could the truth be 4'0"? Sure, plausibly. Could it be 6'0"? You'd have to have been extraordinarily unlucky. So you discard the theoretical distribution centred on 6'0" — not because you've proven it false, but because your data would have been wildly improbable under it.

Where it breaks. Two schools of thought on how to do this formally. Bayesians treat data far from expectation as reason to update their beliefs toward it. Frequentists run a hypothesis test: if the data would be sufficiently unlikely under a candidate truth, reject it. Nick covers the frequentist route because you'll see it more often, but rates the Bayesian approach as well worth learning — he points to Andrew Gelman, and Regression and Other Stories.

5

p-values, worked all the way through

Plain English. Assume a truth. Ask how often data as extreme as yours would show up if that truth held. If the answer is "almost never", stop believing that truth.

Precisely. The p-value is the probability of observing your result or something more extreme, assuming the theoretical distribution under consideration is correct.

Worked example. You flip 100 coins to ask whether the coin is fair.

  1. Assume the truth: the coin is fair, heads half the time.
  2. Derive the theoretical distribution: coin flips are binomial, so you can write out exactly how often each number of heads shows up across repeated 100-flip samples. It peaks at 50. But 49 is common too — getting 49 wouldn't make you doubt fairness at all.
  3. Collect one sample: you get 40 heads. Far from 50, but not impossible.
  4. Shade the tail: the probability of getting 40 heads or fewer under a fair coin is 2.8%.
  5. Two possibilities. Either this isn't the right theoretical distribution, or something unlikely happened. Unlikely things do happen — this one had a 2.8% chance.
  6. Decide: if that probability is below your cutoff, reject the candidate. Conclude the coin isn't fair.

For a two-tailed test you count extremes in both directions — 40 or fewer heads and 60 or more — giving 5.7%.

Where it breaks. Rejecting "fair" doesn't establish that the truth is 40%. You've eliminated one candidate, not identified the right one. And the whole thing can be wrong: you might simply have landed in the tails and thrown out a perfectly good distribution.

Say it out loud

The frustrating thing about statistics is that you don't get an answer. Collect a different sample and you get a different number. Again — different again.

So the trick is to stop asking "what's the answer" and instead ask: how would my answer bounce around if I could redo this study a thousand times? That spread is the theoretical distribution, and you can often work it out from theory before you have any data at all. Coin flips are binomial. Averages come out normal. Counts are Poisson.

Here's why it's worth the trouble. The centre of that distribution is the actual truth you're chasing — the true average, or the true effect of one thing on another. So if you can learn something about the distribution, you've learned something about the truth.

Let me show you. Flip a hundred coins to test whether it's fair. Assume it is fair — now the binomial tells you exactly how often you'd see each number of heads. Peaks at fifty, and forty-nine is completely normal.

You flip, and you get forty heads. Under a fair coin, the chance of getting forty or fewer is 2.8%. So one of two things happened: either the coin isn't fair, or something unlikely occurred. Unlikely things do happen — this one had a 2.8% chance. But if that's low enough for you, you reject it and say the coin isn't fair.

And notice what you did not just do. You didn't learn the coin is 40% heads. You just knocked one candidate off the list. That's all inference from a single sample ever does — it narrows the range of truths you're still willing to entertain.

Recall drill

QWhat is a theoretical (sampling) distribution the distribution of?

Of your result across repeated samples — how the statistic you computed would vary if you redid the whole study over and over. Not of the data points within one sample.

QWhy do we care about the theoretical distribution at all?

Because its central tendency is the true parameter you're after — the true average, or the true effect of X on Y.

QWhich theoretical distribution describes coin flips? Averages? Counts?

Binomial, normal, and Poisson respectively.

QWhy does the normal distribution show up so often?

Because it describes the distribution of averages, and most of what we compute is some kind of average.

QYou flip two fair coins. Give the full theoretical distribution.

50% one head and one tail (either order), 25% two heads, 25% two tails.

QYou have one sample. What can it actually do for you?

Narrow the range of plausible truths by ruling out theoretical distributions under which your data would be very unlikely. It can't identify the true one.

QDefine a p-value.

The probability of observing your result or something more extreme, assuming the theoretical distribution under consideration is correct.

Q100 flips, 40 heads, p = 2.8% one-tailed. What are the two possibilities?

Either the assumed theoretical distribution is wrong (the coin isn't fair), or the distribution is right and something unlikely happened.

QWhy is the two-tailed version of that test 5.7% rather than 2.8%?

Because it counts extremes in both directions — 40 heads or fewer and 60 heads or more — rather than only the low side.

QAfter rejecting "fair coin", what do you now know about the true rate?

Only that 50% is unlikely. You've eliminated a candidate, not identified the truth — many other values remain live.

QWhat's the difference between the Bayesian and frequentist responses to surprising data?

Bayesians update their belief about the world in the direction of the data. Frequentists ask whether the data would be sufficiently unlikely under the candidate truth and, if so, reject it.

Glossary

Theoretical distribution
the distribution a statistic would follow across repeated samples, derived from assumptions about the world.
Sampling distribution
the same idea, named for what it varies over: samples.
Data generating process
the underlying truth in the world that produces your data.
Population
the whole of what you want to learn about, as opposed to your sample.
Binomial distribution
the distribution for a series of yes/no trials, such as coin flips.
Normal distribution
the distribution averages tend to follow.
Poisson distribution
the distribution for count data.
Log-normal distribution
a variable that is normally distributed after taking logarithms.
p-value
the probability of your result or something more extreme, assuming the candidate theoretical distribution is correct.
Two-tailed test
a test counting extreme results in both directions rather than one.
Hypothesis testing
the frequentist procedure of rejecting a candidate truth when the data would be sufficiently unlikely under it.
