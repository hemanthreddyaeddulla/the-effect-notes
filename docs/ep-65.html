Ep 65Other Standard Research Designs

▶ Watch the video 17:20
On this pageThe 30-second versionWhy this existsThe ideasTrapsSay it out loudRecall drillConnectionsGlossary

Four designs the book didn't cover — synthetic control, which builds a weighted blend of control groups that matches your treated unit almost exactly before treatment; matrix completion, which fills in the counterfactuals you never observed; causal discovery, which builds part of the diagram from data; and double machine learning, which hands the prediction step to an algorithm.

The 30-second version

  • Synthetic control: mix many control groups with estimated weights into one synthetic control that tracks the treated unit before treatment. Prior trends hold by construction.
  • Needs multiple control groups and lots of pre-treatment periods. The Basque region study is the classic.
  • Matrix completion: fill in the question marks — the untreated outcomes you never got to see — using regularized regression, iterating by time and by individual.
  • Causal discovery: use data to rule out arrows and, via colliders, even orient some. Partially builds the diagram without theory.
  • Double machine learning: the predict-and-subtract of controlling for variables, done with lasso or random forests instead of regression.
  • Don't limit yourself to the stock designs just because they're the ones you've heard of.

Why this exists

There's nothing so unique about the designs covered so far that they can't be replaced. Plenty of other methods exist — some more popular, some less, some newer, some older. Some overlap with what you've seen; some are completely different. This is a rundown rather than a deep dive; the chapter points you toward the full technical treatment.

The ideas

1

Synthetic control

Plain English. Instead of picking a control group, build one out of a weighted blend of all the candidates — chosen so it matches your treated unit's history almost perfectly.

Precisely. The closest thing this book came to giving another chapter. It applies where difference-in-differences would: a treated group, a before and after period, and a set of control groups — plural, since you need several.

How it differs from difference-in-differences. Difference-in-differences literally compares the before-and-after change in the treated group against the control group. Synthetic control asks two further questions: which of all the possible control groups make most sense, and rather than a plain comparison, it chooses a mix — mashing several controls together using estimated weights to create a single synthetic control group.

It picks those weights deterministically, by a process designed to make the comparison as clean as possible, and it even uses the pre-treatment outcome variable as a matching variable. Which means you end up forcing prior trends to hold almost by design.

Anchor image. You can't find one person the same height as your subject, so you take a weighted average of several people until the average height matches exactly.

Reading the graph. In the pre-treatment period the treated line and the synthetic control line follow each other almost exactly. That isn't luck in the data — it was forced. And having pushed those two before-lines together, you might expect them to have continued sticking together afterwards absent treatment. So any divergence afterwards is your effect.

Worked example — the Basque region. One of the very first implementations, back in 2003.

There was a period of substantial additional violence in Spain due to sectarian differences, and the question was: what was the effect of that violence on economic growth?

  • Treated group: the Basque region, from the point the violence began in large amounts.
  • Control groups: all the other regions of Spain.

Some Spanish regions are more comparable to the Basque region than others. So synthetic control takes all the other regions and, based on their characteristics and their pre-treatment outcomes, constructs weights so that averaging them together produces a line looking very much like the Basque region's. In the pre-treatment period the two lines track closely — something difference-in-differences could only dream of.

A built-in test. The violence began in 1970 but didn't really kick into gear for a few extra years. Using 1970 as the treatment date, the lines continue to stick together for those extra years before the violence ramps up. Which is exactly what you'd hope: you claimed they'd stay together absent treatment, and here are a few years where treatment is effectively still absent, and they do.

Then they diverge — so the violence really did reduce economic growth in the Basque region.

Where it fits. There's a lot to like: ways to check assumption plausibility that aren't available in difference-in-differences, and randomization inference for standard errors. It's worth looking into whenever difference-in-differences makes sense — provided you have multiple control groups to synthesise from, and a lot of pre-treatment data, since you need many pre-treatment periods to make the match clean.

2

Matrix completion

Plain English. Fill in the outcomes you never observed, then compare against them.

Precisely. From machine learning. It applies to binary treatments that turn on or off for different people — a panel setting like difference-in-differences — except the treatment can turn on or off at different points in time for different groups. Very flexible.

The problem. You want to say: I see you're being treated right now, so your treatment effect is what's happening to you now compared to what it would have looked like untreated. But you can't observe that counterfactual — what some people call the fundamental problem of causal inference.

What it does. You have a lot of data, so try to calculate what the counterfactual would have been. Take two matrices of outcome data: one for people not currently treated, and one for people currently treated. Both are full of question marks where you didn't observe someone.

Worked example. Individual 2 in time period 1 has a question mark in the untreated matrix and a value in the treated matrix. That tells you individual 2 was treated in period 1, so you don't know what their outcome would have been untreated.

Matrix completion fills in the question marks in the untreated matrix, giving you something to compare your treated data against. It uses regularized regression — a machine learning tool — iteratively. First it works by time: I'm predicting a value in period 1, so I'll use the other data I have from period 1. Then it works the other way: I'm looking at individual 2, and I can see individual 2 in other periods. It goes back and forth, making the best prediction it can.

3

Causal discovery

Plain English. Use the data itself to rule out arrows — and, using colliders, even point some of them.

Precisely. This one is surprising, because it cuts against the running theme. Your causal assumptions should come from theory, and there isn't much data can do to make or even validate them — it's rare that a genuine assumption about something unobservable can be validated in data.

That isn't always true. Causal discovery says: write down a list of variables you think are relevant, use data to test all the relationships between them, and thereby rule out certain causal relationships — partially building the causal diagram from the results.

Worked example. Four variables — A, B, C, and D — and you start knowing nothing, so put a blank, headless line between all four.

Step 1 — delete an arrow. Look in the data and find that A and B are completely unrelated — or that there is a relationship, but controlling for D makes it disappear entirely. In either case there's no direct arrow between A and B. Cross it out.

Step 2 — orient arrows using colliders. Now look at the relationship between A and B while controlling for C. If that non-relationship suddenly becomes a non-zero relationship, that tells you C must be a collider on the pathway between A and B — so A → C and B → C. Because that is the only way controlling for C could create a relationship that wasn't there before.

From just those steps you've recovered part of the diagram, using nothing but the data beyond your choice of variables. You may not get further — you might never work out D's relationships — but you've done real work.

Where it breaks. It isn't a free lunch. Much of the time you won't get all the way to an answer. And you need a definition of what "unrelated" means, which is an important and unclear part of the procedure. Is it statistical insignificance? Nick doesn't think that's a great way to go about it.

4

Double machine learning

Plain English. Controlling for variables is a prediction problem. Give the prediction to a machine learning algorithm.

Precisely. Also called double de-biasing. Recall how controlling for a variable was first introduced — as a conditional conditional mean: take your control variables, predict the treatment as best you can, and subtract the prediction out; then see what you can predict of the outcome with those controls, and subtract that out too.

Do that prediction using regression and you get exactly the same results as just running a regression with the controls in it.

But you don't have to use regression to do the prediction. This is a predictive problem, and prediction is what machine learning is genuinely excellent at. Machine learning has real difficulty with causal inference — there are ways to use it, but it's hard. So if you can separate out the part of the causal inference process that is a prediction problem, you can hand that piece to machine learning, which will do it very well.

That's all double machine learning is: the same predict-and-subtract on both treatment and outcome, performed with a machine learning algorithm — lasso, a random forest, perhaps a neural network ("I don't think I've ever seen that one, but you could"). The only difference from regression is the predictive procedure.

What it buys you. Controlling for a great many control variables at once, or fitting a non-linear functional form really well.

5

The closing note

Don't limit yourself to the stock designs just because they're the ones you've heard of. If your research question doesn't lend itself to one of them, there may be a design out there that suits it better.

Say it out loud

Four designs the book skips, worth knowing exist.

Synthetic control is the big one. Difference-in-differences makes you pick a control group. Synthetic control says — why pick? Take all your candidate controls and blend them with weights, until the blend tracks your treated unit almost perfectly in the years before treatment. And it uses the pre-treatment outcomes themselves to choose those weights, so parallel trends holds by construction.

The classic study is the Basque region — what did decades of sectarian violence do to its economy? Treated: the Basque country. Controls: every other region of Spain, blended. And there's a lovely accident in it: the violence started in 1970 but didn't really ramp up for a few more years. So you see the lines stay glued together for those extra years — treatment nominally on, but not really happening — and then diverge. That's the method passing its own test.

Matrix completion attacks the fundamental problem of causal inference head on. You want to know what you'd have looked like untreated. You can't see it. So it takes your data as a grid full of question marks, and just... fills them in, using machine learning, working across time and across individuals.

Causal discovery is the surprising one, because it cuts against everything else in the book. It uses the data to build part of your diagram. If A and B are unrelated, delete that arrow. And here's the clever bit — if you control for C and a relationship appears between A and B, then C must be a collider, so A and B both point into it. You've just oriented arrows from data alone.

And double machine learning — remember that controlling for a variable is just predict-and- subtract? That's a prediction problem. So give it to a random forest.

Recall drill

QWhat does synthetic control need that difference-in-differences doesn't?

Multiple control groups to blend, and a lot of pre-treatment periods to make the match clean.

QHow does synthetic control construct its comparison?

It estimates weights over many control groups and mashes them together into a single synthetic control group.

QWhat does it use as a matching variable, and what's the consequence?

The pre-treatment outcome variable — so prior trends are forced to hold almost by design.

QWhy isn't the tight pre-treatment fit evidence that the method worked?

Because it was imposed by construction rather than found in the data.

QWhat was the Basque region study asking, and when was it done?

The effect of sectarian violence on economic growth, in one of the first synthetic control implementations, in 2003.

QWhat made the timing of the violence a useful test?

It began in 1970 but didn't ramp up for a few years — and the lines stayed together over those years, exactly as the method assumes they would absent real treatment.

QWhat can synthetic control offer for standard errors?

Randomization inference.

QWhat problem does matrix completion address?

The fundamental problem of causal inference — you can't observe what a treated unit's outcome would have been untreated.

QWhat do the question marks in the matrices represent?

Observations you didn't get to see — for instance, someone treated in period 1 has no untreated outcome recorded for that period.

QHow does matrix completion fill them in?

With regularized regression applied iteratively — predicting from other observations in the same time period, then from the same individual in other periods, going back and forth.

QWhat does causal discovery claim to do?

Use data on the relationships between a chosen list of variables to rule out causal relationships and partially construct the causal diagram.

QHow do you delete an arrow between A and B?

Find that they're unrelated, or that controlling for D makes their relationship disappear entirely.

QHow can a collider help you orient arrows?

If controlling for C creates a relationship between A and B where there was none, C must be a collider — so A → C and B → C.

QGive two limitations of causal discovery.

You often can't recover the full diagram, and you need a definition of "unrelated" — which Nick doubts should be statistical insignificance.

QWhat is double machine learning doing?

The same predict-and-subtract used when controlling for variables, but with a machine learning algorithm doing the prediction.

QWhy is that a sensible division of labour?

Machine learning struggles with causal inference but is excellent at prediction, and the controlling step is purely a prediction problem.

QName algorithms used for it, and what it buys you.

Lasso, random forests, potentially neural networks — allowing you to control for many variables at once or fit non-linear functional forms well.

Glossary

Synthetic control
constructing a weighted blend of control groups that matches the treated unit's pre-treatment outcomes.
Randomization inference
a method of obtaining standard errors used with synthetic control.
Matrix completion
filling in unobserved counterfactual outcomes using regularized regression.
Fundamental problem of causal inference
that you can never observe the same unit both treated and untreated.
Causal discovery
inferring parts of a causal diagram from patterns of conditional relationships in data.
Double machine learning
controlling for variables by predicting treatment and outcome with machine learning and subtracting the predictions out.
