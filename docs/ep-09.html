Ep 9Data Generating Processes

▶ Watch the video 9:00
On this pageThe 30-second versionWhy this existsThe ideasTrapsSay it out loudRecall drillConnectionsGlossary

Behind your data sits a data generating process — the set of laws and regularities that made the data what it is, operating whether or not you know about them — and identification is the work of getting from what you observed back to the part of that process you actually asked about.

The 30-second version

  • Identification is the link between the analysis you ran and the question you wanted answered.
  • "These two variables are correlated, therefore one causes the other" is an identification error, not a statistics error.
  • The data generating process (DGP) is the set of laws and relationships determining why you observed this data and not some other data.
  • Empirical research means using observations to reverse-engineer that process — the way planetary paths were used to work out physical laws nobody could see directly.
  • The same observations are usually consistent with many different processes. Isolating yours means dealing with the others.
  • And you must know the surrounding context, because data will lie to you if you don't. A lamp that isn't plugged in makes its own pull-string look useless.

Why this exists

You've now got both halves. Episodes 1–2 covered research questions; episodes 3–8 covered doing calculations on data. Identification is where they meet: making sure the calculation you did answers the question you asked, and not some neighbouring question.

The classic failure is correlation and causation. You can analyse two variables, follow every statistical step correctly, and be entirely justified in concluding they're correlated. Take one more step to "therefore one causes the other" and you've made an error — but not in the statistics. The statistics were fine.

The ideas

1

The data generating process

Plain English. The rules the world is running that produced your data.

Precisely. The DGP is the set of laws and relationships determining why the data looks the way it does — why you observed these particular values rather than others. It exists and operates whether or not you know about it, and every research question is, underneath, a question about some piece of it.

Anchor image. You can't see the machine, only what comes out of it. Your job is to work out the gears from the output.

Worked example. Gravity. For a long time people watched planets and could see the paths they traced across the night sky. Nobody could see the physical laws — but those laws were running the whole time, producing exactly those paths. What scientists managed to do was work backwards from the observed paths to the underlying laws.

That's the template for all empirical research. Observations in, process out.

2

Many processes produce the same observations

Plain English. The data you're looking at is consistent with several different stories, and the data alone can't tell you which one you're in.

Precisely. Any given set of observations is typically compatible with multiple data generating processes. Identification is the work of isolating just the explanation your question is about.

Anchor image. Wet pavement. Rain? A street cleaner? A burst pipe? The pavement looks the same in every case.

Worked example. A pen. Two observations, both things you can genuinely measure:

  1. Nick is holding the pen, and it's up here.
  2. He lets go, and it's down there.

Two variables: whether he's holding it, and where it is. Now — you cannot see gravity. It's part of the laws that make the world look the way it does, and it is not in your data. So suppose you don't know about gravity yet, and you're trying to infer the process from these two observations. Take that data to a room of sceptics and you'll get:

  • Maybe there's a big earth down there pulling on it — gravity.
  • Maybe pens just really love the ground.
  • Maybe he's secretly throwing it, adding momentum on release.
  • Maybe there's a wind you can't see, pushing it down.

Every one of those fits the data perfectly. And the point isn't that the sceptics are right — it's that your two observations, on their own, don't discriminate between them.

Where it breaks. The thing you most want to measure is frequently the thing you cannot observe. Gravity isn't a column in the dataset. Neither is ability, or health consciousness, or demand.

3

Data lies when you don't know the context

Plain English. If you don't understand the rest of the machine, a perfectly accurate observation can point you in exactly the wrong direction.

Precisely. Interpreting data requires knowing enough of the surrounding DGP to recognise which conditions have to hold for a relationship to show up at all. Miss one, and a real effect can look like nothing.

Anchor image. Turning the tap and getting no water, then concluding the tap is broken. The mains are off.

Worked example. Nick wants to know how a lamp works — what's the process making the light on or off? He collects data: pull the little string. The light does not go on.

Not knowing how lamps work, you'd sit there scratching your head, and you might reasonably conclude that the string does nothing.

The actual reason is that the lamp isn't plugged in.

The data was accurate. The string genuinely didn't turn on the light. And the conclusion it pushed you toward — "this string is irrelevant" — is dead wrong, because you didn't know that lamps need electricity before you can learn what the string does.

Where it breaks. No statistical method rescues you here. This is a knowledge-of-context problem, and the fix is learning your setting properly before trusting what the data appears to say.

Say it out loud

Everything from here on rests on one idea: the data generating process. That's the set of laws running behind the scenes that made your data come out the way it did — and it's running whether or not you know it exists.

Think about gravity. For centuries people watched planets trace paths across the sky. Nobody could see gravity; you can't put it in a dataset. But it was there the whole time producing those paths, and eventually people worked backwards from the paths to the law. That's what empirical research is.

Now here's the hard part. I hold up a pen. I let go. It falls. Two observations. If you didn't already know about gravity, what would you conclude? Sure — maybe the earth is pulling on it. But maybe pens just love the ground. Maybe I'm secretly throwing it downward. Maybe there's a wind you can't see. Every one of those explains my data perfectly, and no number of extra drops will separate them.

And it gets worse, because data will actively mislead you if you don't know the context. Watch: I've got a lamp, I want to know what the pull-string does. I pull it. Nothing happens. Data's accurate — the string really didn't turn on the light. So the string does nothing, right?

The lamp isn't plugged in.

You had to already know that lamps need electricity before you could learn what the string does. That's identification: figuring out which other explanations are live, and knowing enough about your setting to tell what your data is actually showing you.

Recall drill

QDefine identification.

The link between the analysis you performed and the research question you wanted answered — the process of ensuring your analysis answers that question and not a different one.

QWhy is "these variables are correlated, therefore one causes the other" not a statistics error?

The correlation finding can be entirely correct, arrived at through proper statistical steps. The failure is in the claim about what it answers — an identification error.

QWhat is a data generating process?

The set of laws and relationships determining why you observed the data you did rather than some other data. It operates whether or not you know about it.

QHow does the planets-and-gravity story map onto empirical research?

The observed paths are the data, the physical laws are the DGP, and working backwards from paths to laws is what identification aims to do.

QNick drops a pen. Give three explanations besides gravity that fit the data.

Pens love the ground; he's secretly throwing it rather than dropping it; there's an invisible wind pushing it down.

QWhat does the pen example demonstrate about observations?

The same observations are consistent with many different data generating processes, so the data alone can't tell you which one you're in.

QNick pulls the lamp's string and nothing happens. What would you conclude, and why is it wrong?

You'd conclude the string does nothing. It's wrong because the lamp isn't plugged in — you needed to know lamps require electricity before you could interpret the observation.

QWhat's the lesson from the unplugged lamp?

Data will lie to you if you don't understand the rest of the data generating process. Accurate data can support a completely wrong conclusion.

QWhy is it a problem that gravity isn't in your dataset?

Because the thing driving the process is often unobservable — like ability or health consciousness — so you have to reason about it rather than measure it.
