Ep 64Checking Regression Discontinuity Assumptions

▶ Watch the video 14:40
On this pageThe 30-second versionWhy this existsThe ideasTrapsSay it out loudRecall drillConnectionsGlossary

Two assumptions can fail — that people either side of the cutoff are genuinely comparable, and that nobody is manipulating their running variable — and you check both by running regression discontinuity again: once on your control variables, where you hope to find nothing, and once on the density of the running variable itself, where you hope you can't spot the cutoff at all.

The 30-second version

  • Regression discontinuity is not a free lunch. If the assumptions fail, so does the estimate.
  • Assumption 1: people just either side of the cutoff are comparable.
  • Check it by re-running the design with your control variables as the outcome. A jump means either something else changes at the cutoff, or you got a bad draw.
  • If something else changes, the design is broken. If it's bad luck, add a control variable.
  • Assumption 2: nobody manipulates the running variable to get treated.
  • Check it by binning the running variable and looking at the counts. You want to be unable to find the cutoff.
  • Manipulation is very hard to fix.
  • Two more running-variable problems: granularity and heaping.

Why this exists

You knew it was coming. Regression discontinuity is not a free lunch — there are assumptions required for it to work, and if they're wrong, your estimate is wrong too. It looks nice and clean, but there are assumptions hiding under the hood.

The ideas

1

Assumption 1: the two sides are comparable

Plain English. People at $11,900 and $12,100 should be alike in every way except treatment.

Precisely. Focus narrowly on the bandwidth and you believe you're picking out groups that are comparable except that some got treated. Pairing incomes of $11,900 against $12,100 isn't a huge difference, one group got the treatment and the other didn't, so you feel you can attribute the differences to the treatment.

But what if the two groups actually look quite different?

Worked example. Among people who just barely qualify for the income poverty alleviation programme — incomes from $12,000 down to $11,900 — 20% have a college degree. Among people just above the cutoff — $12,000 to $12,100 — 40% do.

That's strange. You've claimed these groups are almost exactly the same except for treatment, and yet their college completion rates are very different.

Two things it could mean:

  1. Something else changes at the cutoff. Perhaps another policy helps you get a college degree if you earn slightly more than $12,000. Then you'd be confusing the effect of the income policy you care about with the effect of a college policy you don't. Your research design doesn't make sense.
  2. Bad luck. By random sampling chance alone, you happened to draw a sample in which the two groups differ, even though on average they wouldn't.

Neither is good.

2

Checking it: run regression discontinuity again

Plain English. Use your control variables as the outcome and hope you find nothing.

Precisely. This is easy — and a lot of regression discontinuity tests work this way, which is rather neat: you just run regression discontinuity again.

Take everything you'd consider a back door control variable and use it as the dependent variable. For the income programme: do the running variable and treatment predict your college completion rate? College completion may change smoothly along the income distribution — but does it jump at the $12,000 cutoff?

If it does, then it isn't just the treatment jumping at that point. Do this with all your control variables — anything the treatment shouldn't affect. You hope to find no effect for any of them. An income poverty programme shouldn't affect whether you have a college degree.

Anchor image. Weighing the empty scale before you weigh the parcel.

3

Worked example: government transfers and political support

Precisely. From a paper called Government Transfers and Political Support, on a poverty alleviation programme: earn just below a certain amount and the government sends you a check.

The main question: does getting that check make you more supportive of the government?

The main result: support for the government jumps quite a bit right at the cutoff. Just on the untreated side, support is considerably lower and changes smoothly along the income axis — and as soon as you dip below the dashed line, government support jumps immediately. Exactly as you'd expect if the treatment is responsible.

The placebo test: produce the very same graph, but with covariates as the dependent variable instead of the outcome. Two were used, neither of which a government check should affect:

CovariateWhat the graph shows
Mean age in the householdChanges smoothly over the income distribution, with slightly higher ages at slightly higher incomes — which makes sense, since you earn more as you get older. No jump at the cutoff.
Mean education in the householdBarely related to income in this narrow range at all, and no real jump at the cutoff.

Things look pretty much the same either side of the dashed line in both cases — exactly what you want to see. A jump would indicate a difference across the cutoff you'd be at risk of confusing with the treatment.

4

What to do if you do find a difference

Plain English. Depends on whether you think it's real or random.

What you think it reflectsWhat to do
Something else going on at the same time — another policy at the same cutoff, or manipulationBe really concerned about your research design. It simply doesn't work if the people either side aren't truly comparable.
A bad random draw — you're confident the assumptions hold, but this sample happens to have older people on one sideAdd a control variable. There's nothing wrong with adding controls to a regression discontinuity specification, and it can even out sampling differences across the cutoff.

The general principle. This kind of placebo test is a common and excellent way to check plausibility: think of parts of your data where the regression discontinuity effect should not be there, and see whether it is.

5

Assumption 2: nobody manipulates the running variable

Plain English. The running variable can relate to all sorts of things — it just can't be something people are gaming.

Precisely. The running variable needs to be basically randomly assigned, give or take. It's fine that it's related to a lot of back door stuff — it isn't exogenous. What matters is that people aren't trying to change their running variable in order to land on a particular side.

Worked example — manipulation. You know that earning less than $12,000 a year gets you a check in the mail. You also know you're on track to earn $12,100 this year, which is too much to qualify. So why not work a little less on purpose so you get the check?

That makes complete sense for you, and it destroys the design. You weren't randomly assigned to a side — you chose to be below the cutoff because you knew that's where the treatment was.

6

Checking it: look at the density

Plain English. Bin the running variable, count people per bin, and hope you can't tell where the cutoff is.

Precisely. Check whether the running variable itself is smoothly distributed at the cutoff. If there's almost nobody just above the cutoff and everybody just below it, where the treatment is, that implies a lot of people who were just above nudged their way onto the treated side.

The basic method: split the running variable into bins and count how many people are in each.

What you want to see: you can't really find where the cutoff is. You certainly don't want to be missing a lot of people just on one side and finding them just on the other. You want the distribution to look exactly as it would if nobody cared about the running variable at all.

In the example graph, it looks good — nothing suggests anyone is moving themselves from one side to the other.

Where it breaks. If you do find manipulation, it's much harder to deal with. There's not a whole lot you can do in that circumstance, though there are some tricks not covered here.

7

Two more running-variable problems

Granularity. You want a nice, smoothly changing running variable. What if it's only measured in big chunks? Suppose you qualify at $12,000, but the data doesn't record exactly what you made — only that you earned somewhere between $12,000 and $13,000, perhaps because that's how it was reported, maybe for anonymity. That forces you to compare people earning $13,000 against people earning $10,000, and those are too far apart to reasonably call comparable just because they fell on opposite sides of a line.

Heaping. What if some people round their numbers? In income data some report $11,932, $11,936, $12,037 — nicely distributed. Others phone it in: I earned eleven thousand. I earned twelve thousand. So you get a few people at precise values and a lot lumped at the round values.

That's a problem if those are different kinds of people — if the ones who round differ from the ones who report precisely, that introduces back doors into your analysis.

For both granularity and heaping, the chapter has more on what to do.

Say it out loud

Regression discontinuity looks clean, but it is not a free lunch. Two assumptions, and both can break.

First: the people either side of the cutoff really are comparable. Someone on $11,900 and someone on $12,100 should be alike in every way except treatment. But suppose you look and find that 20% of the group below have college degrees and 40% of the group above do. That's very strange — and it means one of two things. Either something else changes at that cutoff, in which case your design is confusing two policies. Or you just got unlucky with your sample.

And here's the neat part — you check it by running regression discontinuity again, but with your control variables as the outcome. Does college completion jump at the cutoff? It shouldn't. Does household age jump? It shouldn't.

There's a lovely example: a study of whether government checks make people support the government. The main graph shows support jumping sharply right at the cutoff. Then they run the identical graph on household age and household education — things a check obviously can't change. Both smooth across the cutoff. Nothing. That's what a passing test looks like.

If you do find a jump: if something real is going on at that cutoff, your design is in trouble. If it's just a bad draw, add a control variable — that's completely fine.

Second assumption: nobody is gaming the running variable. Say you know you get a check if you earn under twelve thousand, and you're on track for twelve-one. So you... work a bit less. Very rational. Completely fatal, because now you chose your side.

You check that by binning your running variable and counting people. What you want is to be unable to tell where the cutoff is. A hole just above and a pile just below means people moved themselves across. And if you find that — there's honestly not much you can do.

Recall drill

QWhat's the first assumption regression discontinuity requires?

That people just either side of the cutoff are comparable except that some got the treatment.

QGive the college degree example of that failing.

20% of those just below the cutoff have college degrees against 40% of those just above — a large difference between supposedly identical groups.

QWhat two things could that difference mean?

Something else changes at the cutoff — such as another policy — or you simply drew an unlucky sample.

QHow do you check for it?

Run regression discontinuity again using your control variables as the dependent variable, and hope you find no jump.

QWhich variables should you use for that check?

Anything you'd consider a back door control — anything the treatment shouldn't affect.

QWhat was the main finding in the government transfers study?

Support for the government jumps sharply right at the cutoff, with smoothly changing and lower support just above it.

QWhat two covariates were used in the placebo check, and what did they show?

Mean household age and mean household education. Age rises gently with income with no jump at the cutoff; education is barely related to income and also shows no jump.

QWhat do you do if a covariate does jump and you think something real is happening at the cutoff?

Be seriously concerned about the research design — it doesn't work if the two sides aren't comparable.

QWhat do you do if you think it's just a bad random draw?

Add a control variable. There's nothing wrong with adding controls to a regression discontinuity specification, and it can even out sampling differences.

QState the general principle behind these placebo tests.

Find parts of your data where the regression discontinuity effect should not exist, and check whether it does.

QWhat's the second assumption?

That people aren't manipulating their running variable in order to land on a particular side of the cutoff.

QDoes the running variable have to be exogenous?

No — it can be related to plenty of back door variables. It just mustn't be something people deliberately change.

QGive the manipulation example.

Knowing you get a check below $12,000 and being on track for $12,100, you work a little less on purpose to qualify — so you chose your side rather than being assigned to it.

QHow do you check for manipulation?

Bin the running variable, count observations per bin, and see whether the distribution is smooth across the cutoff.

QWhat exactly do you hope to see?

That you can't find where the cutoff is — a distribution looking just as it would if nobody cared about the running variable.

QWhat can you do if you find manipulation?

Not a great deal — it's much harder to deal with, though some tricks exist.

QWhat is granularity, and why is it a problem?

A running variable measured only in coarse chunks — such as knowing only that income fell between $12,000 and $13,000 — which forces comparisons between people who are too far apart to be comparable.

QWhat is heaping, and when does it cause trouble?

Some people report precise values while others round to whole thousands, piling observations at round numbers. It's a problem if the people who round differ systematically from those who don't, since that introduces back doors.
