Ep 34Probit and Logit

▶ Watch the video 15:40
On this pageThe 30-second versionWhy this existsThe ideasTrapsSay it out loudRecall drillConnectionsGlossary

When your outcome is binary, a straight line predicts probabilities above 1 and below 0 and ignores that there's less room to move near the edges — so you run the same linear index function through a link function that squashes it into 0–1, and then report average marginal effects because the coefficients themselves are uninterpretable.

The 30-second version

  • OLS quietly assumes a continuous dependent variable. Plenty aren't — counts, bounded variables, and binary outcomes.
  • Run OLS on a binary outcome and you get a linear probability model. Two problems.
  • Problem 1: the line predicts probabilities like 150% or −2. Impossible.
  • Problem 2: it says a nudge moves you 5 points whether you're at 50% or at 99.9%. There's no room at 99.9%.
  • Fix: a generalized linear model. The index function β₀ + β₁x is exactly what OLS does; run it through a link function that maps any number into 0–1.
  • Logit uses the logistic function; probit uses the standard normal CDF.
  • You can't read the coefficients — they describe the index. Report average marginal effects instead.
  • Many economists use the linear probability model anyway, and Nick thinks there's some justification.

Why this exists

Everything so far assumed a linear relationship between predictors and outcome, with transformations like polynomials and logarithms available for odd shapes. But sometimes that still doesn't work — and the reason is the dependent variable.

There's been a background assumption that your dependent variable is roughly continuous: it can take any value in a range and isn't really restricted. Basic OLS assumes exactly that. Often it's false. You might be counting how many times something happened — an integer that can't be negative. You might have a continuous variable that can't be negative, or can't be positive.

And very commonly: a binary dependent variable, 0 or 1. You've used binary variables as predictors; what happens when the thing you're predicting is binary? Were you cured of the disease, yes or no. Did you get into college. Did you move house.

There's a whole world of models for different dependent variable types, and this book skips almost all of it — the focus here is causal inference. But probit and logit get a look.

The ideas

1

The linear probability model, and its two problems

Plain English. You can run OLS on a binary outcome. The line just does two silly things.

Precisely. Running ordinary least squares with a binary dependent variable gives you a linear probability model — so called because you're treating the probability of the outcome linearly, and OLS is all about linear relationships.

Picture the data: every point sits at exactly 1 or exactly 0, nothing between, because the outcome can only be binary. Now fit a straight line through it.

Problem 1 — impossible predictions. The line extends beyond 0 and 1. And that line is your prediction of the outcome — with a binary outcome, OLS is trying to give you the probability that the outcome equals 1. So for certain values of X it predicts a probability of 110%, or 150%, or 3,000% if you go far enough out, or −2. None of those are probabilities.

Problem 2 — no room at the edges. Suppose the outcome is whether you go to college, and based on your background characteristics I predict a 50% chance. I give you some tutoring, and that raises it to 55%. That five-point jump is the slope of the line.

Now take someone already at 99.999%. Give them the same tutoring. It might move the needle a little — 99.9% to 99.92%, say — but it obviously cannot add five percentage points. They were already nearly certain.

Same change on the X axis, hugely different change in probability. Near the middle, where there's lots of room to move, you should see big effects. Near the edges, where the outcome is essentially guaranteed either way, there's simply no room — so the slope should get flatter. A straight line can't do that.

Anchor image. A glass nearly full. Pouring the same amount in adds far less than it did when the glass was half empty — and at some point it just spills.

2

The fix: index function plus link function

Plain English. Do the normal linear thing, then squash the answer into the 0–1 range.

Precisely. This is a generalized linear model. Look at the equation and you'll recognise most of it:

P(y = 1)  =  f( β₀ + β₁·x )

The inside part — β₀ + β₁x — is the index function. It takes your predictors, multiplies them by coefficients, and adds them together linearly. That is exactly what OLS has been doing all along.

The only difference between OLS and a generalized linear model is that you run the index through a link function — that's the f.

Why you need one. The index can take any value: make x a very large negative number and the index is very large and negative; make it very positive and the index is very positive. So you need a function you can pass any value into and get back something on the same scale as your dependent variable — here, something between 0 and 1.

The two link functions:

ModelLink function
Logitthe logistic function
Probitthe cumulative distribution function of the standard normal distribution

The mathematical details are in the textbook chapter.

What this buys you. Both problems solved at once:

  1. You cannot predict outside 0 to 1, because the link function can't go above 1 or below 0.
  2. The curve flattens near the edges and is steep near the middle, like the OLS line is. So the model says you get big movements where there's room to move and little movement when you're already near certainty.

When it matters most. Be most sure to use probit or logit — rather than OLS — when you think the probability of the outcome is close to 100% or close to 0%, rather than in the middle.

3

Why plenty of people use the linear probability model anyway

Plain English. Proper modelling costs you elsewhere, and if you only want the slope, it may not be worth it.

Precisely. Probit, logit, and other proper modelling of the dependent variable do solve real problems — predictions are better and the slope more accurately accounts for where you are on the X axis. But they also introduce computational difficulties, including trouble with fixed effects, which come up shortly.

So: if all you really want is the slope, probit and logit do fix the slope issue somewhat — but if they make things difficult in some other way, it might not be worth it. You can argue back and forth about when it's worth doing.

Where it breaks. As Nick puts it, if you're not an economist you may be yelling at your screen right now — why would you not use a proper binary dependent variable model? But economists do like linear probability models, and he thinks there's some justification. The book has more on that argument.

4

Interpretation: you can't read the coefficients

Plain English. The coefficient tells you about the index, and nobody cares about the index.

Precisely. You can't look at a table of probit or logit coefficients and interpret them directly. What the model estimates is the β₀ and β₁ of the index function — so β₁ tells you how x relates to the index, which then has to go through a transformation to become the thing you actually want: the probability that the dependent variable equals 1. Interpreting the index is hard.

The fix: marginal effects. Take the x's, change them by a unit, see what changes about the predicted probabilities, and report that. This puts the effect of x back in terms of the probability of y — exactly what a linear probability model would have given you, and much easier to read.

So instead of "a one-unit increase in x increases the index by 2.2 units" — which means nothing to anyone — you say "a one-unit increase in x changes the probability that y equals 1 by two percentage points."

Where it breaks — there's no single marginal effect. The marginal effect is the slope of the curve, and the curve's slope is different at every point on the X axis. Everyone has their own marginal effect depending on where they sit.

The aggregation Nick recommends: the average marginal effect. Go through every data point on the graph, compute that individual's marginal effect — the slope of the logit curve at their point — then simply take the average. You get a standard error, you can test whether it differs from zero, all the usual machinery.

There are other ways to aggregate marginal effects, but this is the one he recommends, because it lets each individual person's own marginal effect be averaged together — which is typically what people want out of the whole exercise.

Say it out loud

Last piece of the regression chapter. What if the thing you're predicting is binary — did you get cured, did you get into college, yes or no?

You can just run OLS on it. That's called a linear probability model, and it has two problems.

The obvious one: a straight line keeps going. So for some values of X it'll predict a probability of 150%. Or negative 2. Those aren't probabilities.

The subtler one, and I think the better one. Say I predict you've got a 50% chance of going to college, and I give you some tutoring — that bumps you to 55%. Fine. Now take someone already at 99.999%. Same tutoring. It cannot possibly add five percentage points — there's nowhere to go. Maybe 99.9 to 99.92. So the slope should flatten out near the edges and be steep in the middle, and a straight line just can't express that.

The fix is elegant. You keep doing exactly what OLS does — take your predictors, multiply by coefficients, add them up. That's called the index function, and it's identical. The only change is that you then run it through a link function that takes any number at all and squashes it into the range zero to one. Use the logistic function and it's logit; use the normal distribution's CDF and it's probit. Same idea, different squasher.

And that fixes both problems at once — you can't predict outside 0 to 1, and the curve naturally flattens where there's no room left to move.

One catch: you cannot read the coefficients. They describe the index, and nobody cares about the index. So instead you compute marginal effects — nudge X by a unit and see how the predicted probability moves. That gets you a sentence people can use: a one-unit increase in X changes the probability by two percentage points.

Except the slope is different at every single point on the curve, so everyone has their own marginal effect. The clean answer is the average marginal effect: compute the slope at every data point, then average them.

Recall drill

QWhat does basic OLS quietly assume about the dependent variable?

That it's continuous — able to take any value in a range without real restriction.

QName some dependent variables that violate that.

Counts (non-negative integers), continuous variables that can't be negative or can't be positive, and binary outcomes.

QWhat is a linear probability model?

Ordinary least squares run with a binary dependent variable — treating the probability of the outcome linearly.

QGive the first problem with a linear probability model.

The fitted line extends beyond 0 and 1, so it predicts impossible probabilities like 150% or −2.

QGive the second problem, with the college example.

A straight line has a constant slope. Going from a 50% chance of college, tutoring might add five percentage points — but someone already at 99.999% has no room, so the same tutoring might move them only from 99.9% to 99.92%. The slope should flatten near the edges.

QWhat is the index function?

β₀ + β₁x — the predictors multiplied by coefficients and added linearly, exactly what OLS does.

QWhat is the link function for, and what's the only difference between OLS and a generalized linear model?

The link function maps the index — which can be any value — into the scale of the dependent variable, here 0 to 1. Passing the index through it is the only difference from OLS.

QWhich link functions do logit and probit use?

Logit uses the logistic function; probit uses the cumulative distribution function of the standard normal distribution.

QWhich two problems does the link function solve?

Predictions can no longer fall outside 0 to 1, and the curve flattens near the edges while staying steep in the middle.

QWhen is it most important to use probit or logit rather than a linear probability model?

When you think the probability of the outcome is close to 100% or close to 0%, rather than in the middle.

QWhy do many people use the linear probability model anyway?

Probit and logit introduce computational difficulties, including trouble with fixed effects. If all you want is the slope, the cost may not be worth it.

QWhy can't you interpret a logit coefficient directly?

It describes how x relates to the index function, which must still pass through a transformation to become the probability that y = 1. The index is hard to interpret.

QWhat's a marginal effect?

The change in the predicted probability from changing x by a unit — putting the effect back on the scale a linear probability model would have given you.

QWhy isn't there a single marginal effect?

The marginal effect is the slope of the curve, and the slope differs at every point on the X axis, so each individual has their own.

QWhat is the average marginal effect, and why does Nick recommend it?

Compute each data point's own marginal effect — the slope of the curve at that point — then average them. He recommends it because averaging each person's own marginal effect is typically what people actually want.
