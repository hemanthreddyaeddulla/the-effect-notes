Ep 22Conditional Average Treatment Effects

▶ Watch the video 13:00
On this pageThe 30-second versionWhy this existsThe ideasTrapsSay it out loudRecall drillConnectionsGlossary

A conditional average treatment effect is the average effect within a group — and the group can be defined by who you are (men, college students) or by what happened to you (the treated, the untreated), which is why randomizing within a selected sample gives you that sample's effect and comparing against a control group gives you the effect on the treated.

The 30-second version

  • A CATE is the average treatment effect among some group, conditional on being in it.
  • Rule of thumb 2: randomize within a selected population → the CATE for that population.
  • The group can also be defined by treatment status: average treatment on the treated (ATT) and average treatment on the untreated (ATUT).
  • Rule of thumb 4: using a control group to stand in for what the treated group would have looked like untreated → ATT.
  • A CATE can be exactly what you want (only randomize the cervical cancer drug among people with a cervix) or exactly what you don't (college students standing in for humanity).
  • Most "individual treatment effect" methods are elaborate CATEs — the effect for your particular interlocking set of groups.

Why this exists

Every effect is heterogeneous, and any single estimate is some average. So the practical question is: which average did my design just hand me?

This episode answers it for the most common case — averages within a group — and gives you two of the four rules of thumb for reading a design and knowing what came out.

The ideas

1

The setup: individual effects you could never actually see

Plain English. To follow the arithmetic you need a table nobody could ever have — each person's outcome both with and without treatment.

Worked example. Four types of people. Names, but think categories rather than individuals.

PersonSexUntreated outcomeTreated outcomeIndividual effect
Alfredmale121
Briannafemale154
Chizufemale253
Diegomale242

Treatment works best for Brianna (+4) and least for Alfred (+1).

The average treatment effect is the average of 1, 2, 3, and 4 = 2.5.

Where it breaks. You could never have this table in real data — you can't see the same person both treated and untreated. It's here so you can check what each estimator recovers.

2

CATE by who you are, and rule of thumb 2

Plain English. Run your experiment on only one group and you get that group's effect, not everyone's.

Precisely. A conditional average treatment effect is the average treatment effect among some group — conditional on being in that group.

Worked example. You run a randomized experiment but recruit only men. Brianna and Chizu are gone; you have Alfreds and Diegos. Random assignment treats some and not others, and you observe the outcomes.

  • Average outcome among the treated: avg(2, 4) = 3
  • Average outcome among the untreated: avg(1, 2) = 1.5
  • Difference: 1.5

What does 1.5 represent? Alfred's individual effect is 1; Diego's is 2. Their average is 1.5 — exactly the number you got. You recovered the average treatment effect among men.

Note this is not the ATE of 2.5. Randomizing within a group gives you the group's effect.

Rule of thumb 2. Randomizing within a selected population gives you the average treatment effect for that population — a CATE. This holds whether you picked the sample and randomized inside it, or found real-world randomization that only applies to a particular group and used just that group.

Where it breaks. Good or bad entirely depending on context:

  • Good: the cervical cancer drug. You'd want to randomize only among people who have a cervix. Who cares about the effect on cervical cancer among people who can't get it?
  • Bad: the psychology studies randomizing among college students while wanting to say something about the general population. You get the effect conditional on being a college student when you wanted something broader.
3

CATE by what happened to you: ATT and ATUT

Plain English. You can also condition on whether someone actually got treated.

Precisely. Two more conditional averages:

  • Average treatment on the treated (ATT) — the average effect among people who actually got the treatment.
  • Average treatment on the untreated (ATUT) — the average effect among people who did not get it: how effective would it have been for this group, had it been applied to them?

Both matter, and which you get depends on your estimation. ATT comes up more often.

4

Working out the ATT

Plain English. Compare the treated group's outcomes to a control group standing in for what the treated group would have looked like untreated.

Worked example. Same four people. This time, instead of randomizing, you simply observe that Alfred and Chizu got treated, and Brianna and Diego did not.

First, check there are no back doors. Look at what their untreated outcomes would have been:

  • Alfred and Chizu (the treated): avg(1, 2) = 1.5
  • Brianna and Diego (the untreated): avg(1, 2) = 1.5

Identical. So absent treatment, the two groups would have looked the same — no back doors here. That's what licenses using one group's untreated outcomes as a stand-in for the other's.

Now make the comparison:

  • Treated outcomes of those actually treated (Alfred, Chizu): avg(2, 5) = 3.5
  • Untreated outcomes of those actually untreated (Brianna, Diego): avg(1, 2) = 1.5
  • Difference: 3.5 − 1.5 = 2

What is that 2? Alfred's individual effect is 1; Chizu's is 3. Their average is 2 — exactly what you got. It's the average effect among the people who actually got treated: the ATT.

And notice what happened. Without ever seeing a single individual treatment effect, the one number you estimated turned out to be an average of the individual effects in the treated group. That's what "a single estimate is an average of individual effects" means in practice.

Rule of thumb 4. If your estimate uses a control group as an indication of what the treated group would have looked like without treatment, you're likely getting an average treatment on the treated. Which makes sense — you're asking how this treated group differs from what you think they'd have been without treatment.

(Rule 3 is skipped here because these follow the book's ordering rather than the videos'. It arrives next episode.)

Where it breaks. The ATUT is more involved. You have to do some adjusting to take the treated outcomes you do observe and translate them into what the treated outcomes would have been for the untreated group — the flipped version of the same idea. If that's convoluted, the book has it.

5

Individual treatment effects are mostly elaborate CATEs

Plain English. "Your personal effect" usually means "the effect for people who match you on a lot of things at once."

Precisely. Rather than one group, you estimate effects across many overlapping, intricately interlocking groupings, then use that structure to produce an effect for an individual who sits in a particular subset of categories, and apply it as their individual effect.

Anchor image. Not a bespoke suit. A very large size chart.

Worked example. Conditional on being a man, being over 35, being brown-haired, living in France — all those groups combine, and the effect for that interlocking set becomes your "individual" effect. That's how most of these approaches actually work.

Say it out loud

Every estimate is some average. So which one does your design give you?

Take four people. Alfred goes from 1 to 2 if treated — his effect is 1. Brianna goes from 1 to 5, effect of 4. Chizu, 2 to 5, effect of 3. Diego, 2 to 4, effect of 2. Average all four and the average treatment effect is 2.5. (You could never actually have this table — you can't see someone both treated and untreated — but it lets us check the arithmetic.)

Now: I run a randomized experiment and recruit only men. Treated men average 3, untreated men average 1.5, so I estimate 1.5. And look — Alfred's effect is 1, Diego's is 2, average 1.5. Exactly what I got. I recovered the effect among men, not 2.5. That's a conditional average treatment effect, and that's rule of thumb two: randomize in a selected group, get that group's effect.

Which can be great or terrible. For a cervical cancer drug, you want to only randomize among people who have a cervix. But a psychology experiment on college students claiming to describe humanity — that's the same mechanism working against you.

Now the other flavour. You can condition on what happened to you rather than who you are. Say I just observe that Alfred and Chizu got treated and Brianna and Diego didn't. Their untreated outcomes both average 1.5, so the groups are comparable — no back doors. Treated average is 3.5, untreated is 1.5, difference is 2. And Alfred's effect is 1, Chizu's is 3 — average 2. I got the effect among the people who actually got treated. That's the average treatment on the treated.

Rule of thumb four: whenever you use a control group to stand in for what the treated group would've looked like untreated, that's what you're getting.

Which — notice — I did without ever seeing a single individual effect.

Recall drill

QDefine a conditional average treatment effect.

The average treatment effect among some group — the effect conditional on being in that group.

QAlfred +1, Brianna +4, Chizu +3, Diego +2. What's the ATE?

The average of 1, 2, 3, and 4 = 2.5.

QYou randomize among men only. Treated men average 3, untreated average 1.5. What have you estimated?

1.5 — the average treatment effect among men, which is the average of Alfred's effect (1) and Diego's (2). Not the ATE of 2.5.

QState rule of thumb 2.

Randomizing within a selected population gives you the conditional average treatment effect for that population.

QDoes rule 2 only apply when you chose the sample yourself?

No — it also applies when you find real-world randomization that only reaches a particular group and you use just that group.

QGive one case where a CATE is exactly what you want and one where it isn't.

Want: randomizing a cervical cancer drug only among people who have a cervix. Don't want: psychology experiments on college students used to describe the general population.

QWhat's the difference between ATT and ATUT?

ATT is the average effect among people who actually got treated. ATUT is the average effect among those who didn't — how effective it would have been for them.

QAlfred and Chizu are treated; Brianna and Diego aren't. How do you check for back doors?

Compare what their untreated outcomes would have been. Both pairs average 1.5, so the groups would have looked identical without treatment.

QComplete the ATT calculation for that setup.

Treated outcomes of Alfred and Chizu average 3.5; untreated outcomes of Brianna and Diego average 1.5; the difference is 2 — which equals the average of Alfred's effect (1) and Chizu's (3).

QState rule of thumb 4.

If you use a control group as an indication of what the treated group would have looked like without treatment, you'll likely get the average treatment on the treated.

QWhy does rule 4 make intuitive sense?

Because you're asking how the treated group differs from what you believe they'd have been without treatment — a question about the treated group specifically.

QHow is the ATUT obtained, and why is it harder?

By adjusting the treated outcomes you observe into what treated outcomes would have been for the untreated group — the flipped version, requiring more work.

QHow do most "individual treatment effect" methods actually work?

As elaborate conditional average treatment effects — estimating effects across many overlapping groups and applying the effect for your particular interlocking set, e.g. a man over 35 with brown hair living in France.

Glossary

Conditional average treatment effect (CATE)
the average treatment effect within a specified group.
Average treatment on the treated (ATT)
the average effect among those who actually received the treatment.
Average treatment on the untreated (ATUT)
the average effect among those who did not receive it, had it been applied.
Control group
a group used to stand in for what the treated group would have looked like without treatment.
