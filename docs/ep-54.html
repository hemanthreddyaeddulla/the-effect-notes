Ep 54Supporting Parallel Trends

▶ Watch the video 12:20
On this pageThe 30-second versionWhy this existsThe ideasTrapsSay it out loudRecall drillConnectionsGlossary

You can't prove parallel trends, but you can make it credible three ways — reason about the context, test whether prior trends were equal, and run placebo tests on periods where nothing happened — while remembering that prior trends are not parallel trends, however much the names suggest otherwise.

The 30-second version

  • Parallel trends can't be checked directly. These are gut checks that make it plausible.
  • 1 · Think through the context. Is there any reason it should be violated? Anything else happening at the same time?
  • 2 · Check prior trends. Regress the outcome on group fixed effects and a time trend allowed to differ by group. The difference in slopes is β₂; you want it indistinguishable from zero.
  • Caveats: the trend needn't be linear; with few pre-periods you may lack the power to detect a real difference, so look at the graph too.
  • Prior trends ≠ parallel trends. Prior trends can hold perfectly while parallel trends fails.
  • 3 · Placebo tests. Drop the post-treatment data, pretend an earlier date was the treatment, and estimate again. You should get zero.
  • None of it is proof. Failing these makes parallel trends unlikely; passing them puts you on a good footing.

Why this exists

Parallel trends is the assumption difference-in-differences lives on: that the before-to-after change in the control group is exactly the change the treated group would have had without treatment. You can't check it directly. But there are things you can check in the data that bear on whether it's likely to hold in your particular sample.

The ideas

1

1 · Think through the context

Plain English. Ask whether you have any reason to believe it's violated.

Precisely. Do you think there's a reason parallel trends should fail here — something else happening at the same time? Do you have no reason to think the treatment and control groups would have evolved in different directions? This is reasoning rather than testing, and it comes first.

2

2 · Check prior trends

Plain English. Look at the pre-treatment periods and see whether the two groups were already moving at the same rate.

Precisely. Look at the data from before treatment went into effect for anybody, and check whether the treated and control groups appear to be trending in the same direction at the same rate.

Worked example. Looking at maths scores in a school that implemented a teacher training reform and a school that didn't, examine the years before the programme began and see whether scores were changing at the same rate in both. If they were, they'd plausibly have continued to — meaning any deviation from those trends afterwards would be the treatment itself, which is exactly how you pick out the effect.

Requirement: you need multiple periods of pre-treatment data, or there's no trend to measure.

How to test it. A linear regression of the outcome on:

  • a set of group indicators — fixed effects for group
  • a time trend, allowed to differ by whether you're treated or control

Note that time enters here as a linear variable, not as a set of fixed effects the way it did when estimating difference-in-differences.

GroupTime trend
Control (group = 0)a one-unit increase in time raises the outcome by β₁
Treated (group = 1)a one-unit increase in time raises the outcome by β₁ + β₂

So the difference between the two slopes is β₂.

  • β₂ different from zero → the groups are trending differently over time. That's evidence against parallel trends.
  • β₂ indistinguishable from zero → good news. Not proof, but suggestive evidence in your favour.

Anchor image. Two runners in the laps before the starting gun. If they were already pulling apart before the race began, you can't credit the race for the gap.

3

Three caveats on that test

Caveat 1 — linear is a choice. The test above uses a linear time trend, with time entering by itself and no squared term. But estimate the trend in whatever way makes most sense: if your trend doesn't look linear, don't use a linear one. Add a polynomial, and interact that polynomial with group as well.

Caveat 2 — statistical power. Failing to reject β₂ = 0 is suggestive evidence of similar trends, but check it properly. With only a few periods of pre-treatment data you may simply lack the power to reject the null, so even if the time trends really do differ, you might not be able to show it with a significant test.

**Any time you have a test where you want a non-significant result, you have to worry about whether you actually had the power to reject zero if it isn't zero. So also do a visual inspection** — look at the graph and ask whether these two things appear to be trending together or apart. If you see a visual difference in trend, that's a reason to worry even without significance.

Caveat 3 — prior trends are not parallel trends. These are terribly named things that sound like they're the same and are not. Parallel trends can fail even when prior trends are exactly equal.

Worked example. A school introduces a teacher training reform; the comparison school introduces a free lunch programme at the same time. Look at the period before treatment and the two schools are trending in exactly the same way — prior trends hold. But parallel trends does not, because even if the teacher training programme had never happened, the free lunch programme would have made the two deviate from each other.

4

3 · Placebo tests

Plain English. Pretend the treatment happened at a time when it didn't, and check you find nothing.

Precisely. A placebo test is any test that, if your assumptions are correct, should give you an effect of zero — so a non-zero result tells you something is missing from your model. Here you look for a difference-in-differences effect in a period where the treatment wasn't happening.

Worked example. The teacher training reform went into effect in 2012.

  1. Drop all the 2012-and-later data.
  2. Use only 2008 through 2011.
  3. Pretend the treatment occurred in 2009.
  4. Estimate difference-in-differences again.

You should find zero. If you don't, that suggests prior trends — and possibly parallel trends — aren't holding: the trends differ in a period when no treatment occurred, which suggests they wouldn't have been the same during the real treatment period either.

A second route. There's another way to run this check using a dynamic difference-in-differences model, which lets you estimate the difference-in-differences effect in many time periods at once. You then check the effects estimated in the pre-treatment periods and hope they're zero — because if they aren't, that suggests a violation. That model is the next episode.

5

What all this buys you

Plain English. Not proof. Just a defensible position.

Precisely. None of these are proof that parallel trends holds in any given context. They're suggestive gut checks. If they fail, it becomes pretty unlikely that parallel trends holds. If none of them fail, it's still not proof — but it puts you on a good footing, and makes the assumption more plausible than it would otherwise be. And you do have to argue for the assumption.

Say it out loud

You can't prove parallel trends — it's about a world you never observed. But you can do three things to make it credible.

First, just think. Is there any reason these two groups would have drifted apart anyway? Did anything else happen to either of them at the same time? That's not a test, it's knowing your setting.

Second, check prior trends. If the reform started in 2012 and you have data back to 2005, look at whether the two groups were already moving at the same rate beforehand. You can test it: regress the outcome on group and a time trend that's allowed to differ by group. The control group's slope is β₁, the treated group's is β₁ + β₂ — so β₂ is the gap between the slopes, and you want it to be indistinguishable from zero.

Two warnings there. If you've only got a few pre-treatment periods, you might fail to reject zero simply because you had no power to detect anything. Any time you're hoping for a non-significant result, ask yourself whether you could have found a real difference. So look at the graph too.

And the big one: prior trends are not parallel trends. Awful naming, genuinely different things. Watch — my treated school does a teacher reform, my control school starts free lunches the same year. Before that, they were tracking each other perfectly. Prior trends: passed. Parallel trends: completely broken, because free lunch would have pulled them apart regardless.

Third, placebo tests. Throw away all your post-treatment data. Pretend the treatment happened in 2009 instead of 2012. Run the whole difference-in-differences again. You should get zero — nothing happened in 2009. If you get something, your trends were already diverging before treatment ever arrived.

And none of this is proof. If these checks fail, parallel trends is probably dead. If they pass — you've got a decent argument. That's all you ever get.

Recall drill

QWhat are the three ways to support parallel trends?

Think through the context, check prior trends, and run placebo tests.

QWhat does checking prior trends involve?

Looking at pre-treatment data to see whether the treated and control groups were trending in the same direction at the same rate before treatment.

QWhat do you need in order to check prior trends at all?

Multiple periods of pre-treatment data — otherwise there's no trend to measure.

QDescribe the regression that tests prior trends.

Regress the outcome on group fixed effects and a time trend allowed to differ by group, with time entering as a linear variable rather than as fixed effects.

QWhat are the two groups' time trends in that regression?

The control group's is β₁; the treated group's is β₁ + β₂.

QWhat does β₂ tell you?

The difference between the two slopes. If it differs from zero, the groups are trending differently — evidence against parallel trends.

QWhat if the trend isn't linear?

Estimate it in whatever way fits — add a polynomial and interact that polynomial with group as well.

QWhy should you be cautious about a non-significant β₂?

With only a few pre-treatment periods you may lack the statistical power to reject zero even when the trends genuinely differ.

QWhat's the general lesson about tests where you want a non-significant result?

You have to ask whether you had the power to detect a real difference — and inspect the graph visually as well.

QExplain why prior trends and parallel trends aren't the same thing.

Prior trends can hold perfectly while parallel trends fails — if the control school starts a free lunch programme exactly when the treated school starts its reform, the groups tracked identically beforehand but would have diverged regardless of treatment.

QWhat is a placebo test?

A test that should yield an effect of zero if your assumptions are correct; a non-zero result indicates something is missing from your model.

QWalk through the placebo test for a 2012 reform.

Drop all data from 2012 onward, keep 2008–2011, pretend treatment occurred in 2009, and estimate difference-in-differences. You should find zero.

QWhat does a non-zero placebo result suggest?

That the trends differ in a period when no treatment occurred, so they likely wouldn't have matched during the real treatment period either.

QWhat's the second way to run this check?

A dynamic difference-in-differences model, which estimates effects across many periods at once — you check that the pre-treatment effects are zero.

QWhat do these checks actually establish?

Nothing conclusive. Failing them makes parallel trends unlikely; passing them makes the assumption more plausible and gives you an argument, but never proof.
