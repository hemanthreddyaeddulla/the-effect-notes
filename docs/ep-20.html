Ep 20Front Door Examples

▶ Watch the video 13:50
On this pageThe 30-second versionWhy this existsThe ideasTrapsSay it out loudRecall drillConnectionsGlossary

Finding exogenous variation doesn't remove the requirement to close all back doors — it moves it, from "close every back door between treatment and outcome" to "close every back door between my source of variation and the outcome" — and three real studies show that job getting steadily harder as the variation gets muddier.

The 30-second version

  • You haven't escaped the back door problem. You've relocated it — hopefully somewhere easier.
  • Lottery winnings → bankruptcy. Among people who already bought tickets, winning big vs. small is genuinely random. Very believable.
  • Wind direction → pollution → driving. Wind isn't random — it correlates with season and weather — but those are measurable and controllable. Grey area, still plausible.
  • Medicaid expansion → uncompensated care. Which states expanded was a deeply political decision. Strongest assumptions of the three.
  • The muddier the variation, the more work required to justify it as random.
  • Whether it works is contextual. You need to know the setting to know whether back doors exist.

Why this exists

Front doors sound like they let you run an experiment anywhere. That's a genuinely exciting idea, and it's also where people stop reading the fine print.

The honest statement: you're looking for something contributing to whether you're treated — it doesn't have to be the only determinant — that has no back doors of its own. If you find something that's actually random, like a policy someone assigned by lottery, you're probably fine. Outside that, you're in a grey area, and how far you're willing to go is a judgement about context.

The frustrating position this leaves you in: you can't always run an experiment. Even when you can, you may not get enough people for it to be worthwhile. You can't just control for everything, because you may not be able to measure everything, or get people to believe your diagram. And now this cool third thing — but sometimes it just isn't believable either.

The ideas

1

The requirement moved, it didn't disappear

Plain English. You still have to close all the back doors. Just for a different variable.

Precisely. Instead of closing every back door from treatment to outcome, you must close every back door from your source of random variation to the outcome. The hope is that this is easier — that you're more likely to actually know all those pathways and be able to measure them — or that if the source is truly random, there aren't any.

Anchor image. You didn't cancel the debt. You refinanced it, betting the new terms are easier to service.

2

Level 1 — lottery winnings and bankruptcy

Plain English. Everyone in the study bought a ticket. Among ticket buyers, how much you won is genuinely random.

Worked example. There's a story you hear: someone wins the lottery, spends big, and goes bankrupt because they couldn't handle the money. Does that actually happen?

The problem: people choose to play the lottery, and there could easily be a back door between being the kind of person who plays and your chances of going bankrupt.

The design: compare people who all played the lottery, some of whom won big pots and some small ones. Within the group of ticket holders, whether you win a lot or a little is basically random.

Note what would not work: comparing lottery winners to non-players. That reopens the back door from simply having bought a ticket. Once you know somebody already chose to buy tickets, winning or not is a good source of random variation.

The finding: immediately after a big win, the chance of going bankrupt drops considerably. But wait a couple of years and it rises sharply. It roughly cancels out — winning doesn't make you much more or less likely to go bankrupt overall, it pushes your bankruptcy a couple of years into the future.

Where it breaks. Barely does. There shouldn't be back doors from how much you win to bankruptcy except through the money itself, which is the treatment. Very believable.

3

Level 2 — wind direction, pollution, and driving

Plain English. You can't randomize smog. But you can use which way the wind is blowing.

Worked example. Does polluted air make people drive more? Plausibly yes — when it's smoggy you don't want to walk, don't want to bike, maybe don't even want the bus; you want your car with the air conditioning on. And that's a nasty cycle, because driving creates pollution.

Why the obvious approaches fail: you can't randomly assign more pollution to one day than another. And comparing a polluted city to a clean one doesn't work either — maybe the polluted city is polluted because people there drive more.

The design: stay within one city. In Beijing, wind direction strongly affects smog — blowing one way it pushes pollution into the city, blowing the other it carries it out. Isolate the part of pollution driven by wind direction and you have something like randomly assigned pollution.

The back doors you have to worry about:

ConcernWhy it's a back doorFix
SeasonWind might blow west more in spring, east more in autumn — and people drive differently by seasonMeasure the season, control for it
WeatherIt might rain more when the wind blows west, and rain affects drivingControl for weather the same way

The judgement: wind direction isn't entirely random — it's related to other things that relate to driving. The question is whether you can control for those. And plausibly it's easier to close the back doors from wind direction to driving than from pollution to driving, because pollution is also tied up with things like whether the factories were running that day.

The finding: when the wind blows pollution into the city, people drive a little bit more. Which makes sense. Once season and weather are controlled for, the random-assignment assumption is relatively believable — but you're leaning on an assumption in a way you weren't with the lottery.

4

Level 3 — Medicaid expansion and uncompensated care

Plain English. Compare states that expanded Medicaid to states that didn't — and assume which ones expanded was roughly random. That's a lot to assume.

Worked example. Uncompensated care is medical care a hospital provides and never gets paid for. That's a problem: losing money makes it harder to provide good care, and hospitals might stop providing it altogether.

The design: a 2014 study uses the Medicaid expansion. Medicaid funds health care in the US, particularly for the kinds of patients who might not be able to pay their hospital bills. The programme expanded at a certain point, so you compare states before and after, isolating the part of funding driven by that policy change, and see how it affects uncompensated care.

What you must assume: that it's basically random which states decided to expand Medicaid and which didn't.

Why that's hard: the decision to expand was a deeply political one, and different kinds of governments made it in different ways. Maybe you can control for which political party controlled the state at the time, and maybe that restores the random element. But the list of back doors you'd need to account for is considerably longer here than in the previous two examples.

Where it breaks. Maybe you believe it, maybe you don't. That depends on the context and on whether the assumptions can be justified and supported — which is exactly the point of running through three examples rather than one.

5

The general principle

Plain English. You can always find muddier variation. The muddier it is, the more work you owe.

Precisely. These methods work as long as the assumption that the variation is random — or that you can close all its back doors — is believable. That's contextual: you have to know the setting to know whether alternative back door paths exist. You can keep reaching for muddier and muddier sources, but the muddier they get, the more you have to do to justify calling them random.

Say it out loud

Front doors sound like a free pass — run an experiment anywhere. Here's the fine print.

You haven't got rid of the back door problem. You've moved it. Before, you had to close every back door between treatment and outcome. Now you have to close every back door between your source of random variation and the outcome. Same job, different variable — you just hope it's easier.

Three examples, getting progressively shakier.

One. Does winning the lottery make you go bankrupt? You can't compare winners to non-players — choosing to play is itself informative. So instead you compare people who all bought tickets, some of whom won big and some small. Within ticket buyers, that's genuinely random. And the finding is lovely: right after a big win, bankruptcy drops sharply. A couple of years later it spikes. It roughly cancels — winning doesn't change whether you go bankrupt, it just pushes it a few years out.

Two. Does smog make people drive more? You can't randomize pollution, and comparing a polluted city to a clean one is circular — maybe it's polluted because they drive. So: in Beijing, which way the wind blows determines how smoggy it is. Use the part of pollution that wind direction drives. But wind isn't truly random — it varies by season, and it correlates with rain, and both of those affect driving. You can measure those and control for them, and it's probably easier to handle back doors from wind than from pollution. Grey area, but I buy it. They found more smog does mean a bit more driving.

Three. A study on hospital care that never gets paid for uses the Medicaid expansion — comparing states that expanded to states that didn't. And the assumption is that which states expanded was basically random. Except... that was an intensely political decision. Different governments decided differently. Maybe you control for the governing party and recover something random-ish. Maybe you don't.

That's the whole spectrum. You can always reach for muddier variation. The muddier it gets, the more work you owe to convince anyone it's really random.

Recall drill

QWhat does finding exogenous variation actually change about the back door problem?

It relocates it. Instead of closing all back doors from treatment to outcome, you close all back doors from your source of variation to the outcome — hopefully an easier job.

QWhy can't you study lottery winnings by comparing winners to non-players?

Because people choose to play, so there's a back door from being the kind of person who buys tickets to your chances of going bankrupt.

QWhat's the design that fixes it?

Compare people who all played, some winning big pots and some small ones. Within ticket holders, how much you win is basically random.

QWhat did the lottery study find?

Bankruptcy drops considerably immediately after a big win, then rises sharply a couple of years later. It roughly cancels out — the win pushes bankruptcy into the future rather than changing whether it happens.

QWhy can't you compare a polluted city to a clean one to study pollution's effect on driving?

The polluted city may be polluted because people there drive more, which is precisely the relationship you're trying to measure.

QWhat supplies the exogenous variation in the pollution study?

Wind direction within one city — in Beijing, blowing one way brings pollution in, the other way carries it out — so you isolate the part of pollution driven by wind.

QName the two back doors from wind direction to driving, and how they're handled.

Season (wind might blow one way more in spring, another in autumn, and driving varies by season) and weather (it might rain more with certain wind directions, and rain affects driving). Both are measurable and controlled for.

QWhy might wind direction be an easier variable to work with than pollution?

Because pollution is tied up with many other things, like whether the factories were running that day, so its back doors are harder to enumerate and close.

QWhat is uncompensated care?

Medical care a hospital provides but is never paid for — a problem because losing money makes it harder to provide good care, and hospitals may stop providing it.

QWhat assumption does the Medicaid expansion study require, and why is it the shakiest of the three?

That which states chose to expand Medicaid was basically random. It's shaky because that decision was intensely political, with different governments deciding differently — so the list of back doors to close is much longer.

QWhat's the general relationship between how muddy your variation is and what you owe?

The muddier the source, the more work you must do to justify calling it random. You can always reach for muddier variation, but the burden grows.

QWhat determines whether one of these designs is believable?

Context. You have to know the setting well enough to know whether alternative back door paths exist and whether you can close them.
