## ONE SENTENCE

Five steps, in a loop: **choose a data generating process** whose truth you know, **generate a
sample**, **apply your estimator**, **store the estimate**, and **repeat many times** — then check
whether the mean of that sampling distribution comes back to the truth you put in.

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

- **Step 1 — pick the data generating process.** You choose it, so you know the truth.
- **Step 2 — generate a sample** from it.
- **Step 3 — apply your estimator** to that sample.
- **Step 4 — store the estimate.** One iteration done.
- **Step 5 — repeat many, many times**, building a whole distribution of estimates.
- Then compare the **mean of that sampling distribution** to the truth. Match → the estimator works in this context. Mismatch → it doesn't.
- For causal work, build a causal effect in by **using one variable to generate another**.
- Demonstrated entirely with a coin, pencil, and paper — no code.

## WHY THIS EXISTS

You have an estimator in mind — a regression done a particular way, matching done a particular way,
whatever — and you want to know when and how well it works. Here's how to structure a simulation
that tells you.

No code here, because the book covers several languages. Instead: a coin as the random data
generator, and the results written down. It works exactly the same way — the sample just won't be
very big, because nobody's here all day.

## IDEAS

### Step 1: choose the data generating process

**Plain English.** You decide what's true. That's the whole trick.

**Precisely.** Use a random number generator to produce data with whatever properties you want. You
decide the data generating process, **so that you know the truth**.

**Worked example.** The coin: choose one with a 50/50 chance of heads. So the true average is **50%
heads** — that's the truth. Whatever estimation method you use later should give back 50% on
average. If it doesn't, the estimation method is bad.

**How to build in a causal effect.** For causal inference you generally want a causal effect in
there that you'll then try to recover. Do it by **using one variable to generate another**:

1. Generate a random variable — call it **x** — say from a random normal distribution.
2. Generate **y** from a random normal distribution whose **mean is x**.

Now x causes y. A one-unit increase in x should be associated with a one-unit increase in y — and
when you estimate later, you hope the relationship you get back out is also **one**.

**Where it breaks.** The coin demo deliberately has no causal effect in it; it's a plain statistical
estimation. But the structure is identical.

### Step 2: generate a sample

**Worked example.** Flip the coin six times. The result: **tails, heads, heads, tails, heads, heads**
— four heads and two tails.

### Step 3: apply your estimator

**Plain English.** Run whatever method you're testing on that sample.

**Worked example.** Normally you'd estimate the proportion of heads by, well, taking the proportion
of heads. But the point is to test an estimator, so test a strange one: **take the proportion of
heads and multiply it by two thirds.** No particular reason to think it works — that's what you're
checking.

Proportion of heads = 4/6. Multiply by 2/3 → **8/18**, a bit under half. That's the estimate.

### Step 4: store it

**Precisely.** Write the estimate down. That's one complete iteration of the simulation procedure.

### Step 5: repeat, many times

**Plain English.** One run tells you nothing. Do it over and over.

**Worked example.** Three more rounds of six flips each, applying the same estimator:

| Round | Heads | Proportion | Estimate (× 2/3) |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | 4 of 6 | 4/6 | **8/18** ≈ 0.444 |
| 2 | 4 of 6 | 4/6 | **8/18** ≈ 0.444 |
| 3 | 3 of 6 | 1/2 | **1/3** ≈ 0.333 |
| 4 | 3 of 6 | 1/2 | **1/3** ≈ 0.333 |

Now look at the distribution of estimates — here, just take their average:

**Average ≈ 38.9%**

### The comparison

**Plain English.** Line the simulation's answer up against the truth you built in.

**Worked example.** The truth is **50%** — you chose a fair coin. The estimator says you get heads
about **39%** of the time. Those are different, so the estimation method **does not work**.

**Where it breaks.** Four simulations isn't nearly enough — you'd want a thousand. But you'd still
get something around 38%, so the conclusion holds.

**The general rule:** run the whole procedure many, many times to get a distribution of estimates
from all your random samples. Look at that **sampling distribution**. If its **mean matches the truth**
you built in, the estimator seems to be working in this context. If it doesn't, it doesn't.

### Why deliberately test a broken estimator

**Plain English.** He picked one he knew would fail, to show you what failure looks like.

**Precisely.** Obviously if you want to know how often a coin comes up heads, you take the proportion
of times it's heads — the ×2/3 estimator was chosen because it's wrong. The demonstration is that it
returns a wrong answer, and **that's what you're looking for**: a result that doesn't line up with
the truth tells you the method is wrong and you need a different one.

**Where it breaks — the other outcome is interesting too.** Sometimes the simulation shows the method
**does** work. It might have turned out that multiplying by two thirds doesn't mess up the answer,
and that would have been an interesting thing to find out. (It did.) And sometimes you break an
assumption, do something that seems wrong, and it's only **a little bit wrong** — and maybe that's
fine. That's also a valuable result from a simulation.

## TRAPS

- Run the simulation once and check the answer → **Actually,** a single iteration tells you nothing. You need many, to build a sampling distribution.
- The estimator is wrong if any individual estimate misses the truth → **Actually,** you compare the **mean of the sampling distribution** to the truth. Individual samples vary by construction.
- To simulate a causal effect you need special tools → **Actually,** you just use one variable to generate another — draw x, then draw y with mean x, and x causes y.
- You need real-looking data for the simulation to be informative → **Actually,** a coin and a piece of paper demonstrate the entire procedure.
- Finding that your estimator works means it always works → **Actually,** it means it works **in this context** — in the data generating process you built.
- A simulation showing a broken assumption still gives good answers is a failed simulation → **Actually,** that's a genuinely useful finding: some violations only cost you a little.

## SAY IT OUT LOUD

> Running a simulation is five steps, and you can do the whole thing with a coin.
>
> **One: choose the data generating process.** This is the magic step — because *you* choose it, you
> know the truth. I'm picking a fair coin, so the truth is 50% heads. Whatever estimator I test had
> better give me back 50%.
>
> If you want a *causal* effect in there, it's easy: draw a variable x, then draw y from a
> distribution whose mean **is** x. Now x genuinely causes y, with an effect of one — and you can go
> see whether your method recovers a one.
>
> **Two: generate a sample.** I flip six times: tails, heads, heads, tails, heads, heads. Four heads.
>
> **Three: apply your estimator.** And I'm going to test a deliberately silly one — take the
> proportion of heads and multiply by two thirds. Four sixths times two thirds is eight eighteenths.
>
> **Four: write it down.** That's one iteration.
>
> **Five: do it again. And again. And again.** I did three more rounds — got eight eighteenths again,
> then a third, then a third. Average those four: about **39%**.
>
> Now compare. Truth: **50%**. My estimator: **39%**. Doesn't match — so that estimator is broken.
>
> And I chose a broken one on purpose, so you'd see what failure looks like. Because sometimes you run
> this and the weird thing you tried turns out **fine** — and that's just as useful. Lots of times you
> break an assumption and it's only wrong by a hair. The only way to know is to try it.

## RECALL

Q: List the five steps of running a simulation.
A: Choose the data generating process; generate a sample; apply your estimator; store the estimate;
repeat many times.

Q: Why does step one matter so much?
A: Because you choose the data generating process yourself, you know the truth — which is what makes
it possible to judge the estimator.

Q: How do you build a causal effect into simulated data?
A: Use one variable to generate another — draw x from a random distribution, then draw y from a
distribution whose mean is x, so x causes y.

Q: In that setup, what effect should your estimator recover?
A: One — a one-unit increase in x should be associated with a one-unit increase in y.

Q: What was the true value in the coin demonstration?
A: 50% heads, because a fair coin was chosen.

Q: What estimator was tested, and why that one?
A: The proportion of heads multiplied by two thirds — deliberately chosen because it's wrong, to
demonstrate what failure looks like.

Q: Round one gave 4 heads out of 6. What was the estimate?
A: 4/6 × 2/3 = 8/18, about 0.444.

Q: Give the four estimates across the four rounds.
A: 8/18, 8/18, 1/3, and 1/3 — from four heads, four heads, three heads, and three heads.

Q: What was the average, and what does it tell you?
A: About 39%, against a truth of 50%. They don't match, so the estimator doesn't work.

Q: Is four iterations enough?
A: No — you'd want a thousand. But you'd still land around 38%, so the conclusion stands.

Q: What do you compare against the truth at the end?
A: The mean of the sampling distribution built from all your simulated estimates.

Q: What does it mean if the mean of the sampling distribution matches the truth?
A: The estimator seems to be working in that context — the data generating process you built.

Q: Name two useful outcomes besides "the estimator failed."
A: Discovering that something which looked wrong doesn't actually break the answer, and discovering
that a broken assumption makes results only slightly wrong.

## GLOSSARY

- **Data generating process (chosen)** — in a simulation, the truth you deliberately build into the data.
- **Iteration** — one pass of generating a sample, applying the estimator, and storing the estimate.
- **Sampling distribution (simulated)** — the distribution of estimates accumulated across many iterations.
