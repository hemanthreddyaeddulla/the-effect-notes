## ONE SENTENCE

**Two-stage least squares** does exactly what the concept says — predict treatment from the
instrument, then use **only that prediction** to predict the outcome — and whatever number comes
out is a **local average treatment effect**, weighted toward whoever responds most strongly to your
particular instrument.

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

- **Two-stage least squares:** first stage predicts treatment from instrument + controls; second stage regresses the outcome on the **predicted** treatment.
- Using only the exogenous part of treatment means **no omitted variable bias** — random variation can't relate to the error term.
- Standard errors need adjusting, because the predicted treatment was estimated rather than observed.
- **2SLS is not the same thing as instrumental variables.** It's one estimator among several.
- **Generalized method of moments** builds moment conditions from "the instrument is uncorrelated with the error term." Better under **heteroskedasticity** and with **multiple instruments**.
- The estimate is a **local average treatment effect**: never-takers and always-takers **don't count**, and stronger responders count more.
- Different instruments give different effects, because different people respond to them.

## WHY THIS EXISTS

You have the concept — a source of exogenous variation, used to narrow your view to the part of the
data that's randomly assigned. Now you need to actually estimate it.

## IDEAS

### Two-stage least squares

**Plain English.** Two regressions. Predict treatment from the instrument, then use only the
prediction.

**Precisely.** Probably the most common estimator, and conceptually the most direct. Two equations:

**First stage.** Use your instrument **z** and whatever control variables **w** you have to predict
your treatment **x**. Run that regression and get the **predicted values, x̂** — which are only the
part of x predicted by the instrument and the controls. That is your **exogenous variation**.

**Second stage.** Where you'd normally regress **y** on **x** and **w**, instead regress y on **x̂**
and w.

Because you've used only the part of x predicted by exogenous variation — the randomized part —
there can be **no omitted variable bias**: in random variation, there's nothing that can be related
to the error term. The coefficient **β₁** on x̂ is your two-stage least squares estimate of the effect
of x on y.

**Anchor image.** Filtering water and drinking only the filtrate. Whatever was dissolved in the rest
never reaches you.

**Where it breaks — a detail.** You need to **adjust your standard errors** to account for the fact
that x̂ was **estimated** rather than being an actual value in the data.

**And a naming warning.** Two-stage least squares is **one way** of estimating an instrumental
variables model. It is **not the same thing as instrumental variables** — an easy confusion, and
you'll see people talk about the two as though they're identical when they aren't.

### Generalized method of moments

**Plain English.** State what should be true in your data, then pick coefficients that make it true.

**Precisely.** Generalized method of moments takes some calculations from your data, says what they
should be equal to, and chooses coefficients such that those things are equal.

**The assumption it's built on.** In the second-stage equation — y on x and your controls, with an
error term ε — you're assuming that your **instrument is unrelated to that error term**. (With plain
ordinary least squares you'd assume your *x* is unrelated to the error term; in instrumental
variables you assume your *z* is.)

Take that assumption and build a set of **moment conditions** from it: your instrument should be
uncorrelated with your residuals. Then choose your coefficients so that holds.

**Where it fits.** You can use generalized method of moments even for ordinary linear regression with
no instruments at all. The estimates work out almost exactly the same as two-stage least squares.
Two things make it better:

1. It performs considerably better under **heteroskedasticity** — smaller *and* more accurate standard errors.
2. It handles **multiple instruments** better. Nothing stops you using several sources of exogenous variation to predict your treatment, and generalized method of moments does a little better there.

There are further options too — **limited information maximum likelihood**, among others. The point is
that several different estimators get at the same idea.

### What treatment effect are you actually getting?

**Plain English.** Only people the instrument moves are in your estimate — and the more it moves them,
the more they count.

**Precisely.** You're using only the variation in x predicted by the instrument. So you're only using
**people for whom the instrument does a good job of predicting their treatment**.

**Worked example — the drug trial.** Suppose you're deeply suspicious of medicine and won't take the
drug regardless of assignment. You're a **never taker**, and whatever effect the drug would have had
on you **doesn't count** — you drop out of the estimate. Similarly, if you're the kind of person who'd
find a way to take the drug no matter what, you don't count either.

The instrumental variables estimate reflects **only people who don't take it when not assigned, and do
take it when assigned.**

**And beyond that — the stronger your response, the more you count.**

**Worked example — exercise.** Randomize telling some people "you should exercise more" and telling
others nothing at all. How effective is that finger-wagging? Maybe one person exercises **one more
hour a week** because of it, and another is really responsive and exercises **five more hours a week**.

However effective exercise is for each of them, **you'll get far more of the five-hour person's effect
than the one-hour person's**.

**That's the local average treatment effect** — the average effect, weighted more heavily toward people
who respond more strongly to the instrument. If you're not among those who respond to the instrument,
your treatment isn't randomly assigned, so you aren't counted.

**Which means different instruments give different answers.** Suppose one instrument is finger-wagging
— "you should exercise more" — and another is showing you a picture of a really physically fit person.
Some people respond well to the first, others to the second, and they're **different people**. So you'd
pick up the effect of exercise on health differently in the two cases.

### Worked example: insurance and network effects in rural China

**Plain English.** Randomly push one farmer toward buying insurance, and see whether their neighbour
buys too.

**Precisely.** A randomized experiment by **Cai, de Janvry, and Sadoulet** in rural China, trying to
sell insurance to farmers — the kind of insurance that carries you through a really bad year without
having to sell the farm before the next one.

They randomized people into different informational settings: one where it's assumed you'll buy and
you have to **opt out**, and another where you have to **opt in**.

**But what they actually wanted were network effects.** If your neighbour was randomized into a setting
making them more likely to purchase insurance, does that make **you** more likely to buy as well? You
meet at the fence one day, they say *I just bought this insurance*, and you think — insurance, that
sounds pretty good, maybe I'll buy some too.

| Role | Variable |
|---|---|
| **Instrument** | Whether your friend was randomly assigned into the higher-pressure "default buy" setting |
| **Treatment** | Whether your friend buys insurance |
| **Outcome** | Whether **you** buy insurance |

**The two-stage least squares results:**

| Stage | Result |
|---|---|
| **First stage** — instrument → treatment | Your friend being randomly assigned into the "assume you buy" environment increased **their** probability of buying insurance by **11.8 percentage points** |
| **Second stage** — predicted treatment → outcome | Your friend buying insurance makes **you 79.1 percentage points** more likely to buy insurance |

The first stage confirms **relevance** — the random assignment really did make your friend more likely
to buy, and by a large amount, so there's genuine variation to work with.

The second stage is a **very strong network effect**. Randomly assigning one person into a setting
where they're more likely to buy insurance has a great deal of carryover into getting their friends to
buy as well — which might make their farming operations safer year to year, though it also means more
money for the insurance company. Take that as you will.

## TRAPS

- Two-stage least squares *is* instrumental variables → **Actually,** it's one estimator among several. People conflate them constantly.
- You can use the second stage's standard errors as they come out → **Actually,** they need adjusting, because the treatment values were predicted rather than observed.
- Generalized method of moments is a specialised alternative → **Actually,** it can even be used for ordinary regression, gives near-identical estimates, and is better under heteroskedasticity and with multiple instruments.
- Instrumental variables gives you the average treatment effect → **Actually,** it gives a local average treatment effect, weighted toward responders and excluding never-takers and always-takers.
- Two valid instruments for the same treatment should give the same answer → **Actually,** they weight different people, so they can legitimately differ.
- A never-taker's treatment effect is small → **Actually,** it might be enormous. It simply isn't in your estimate at all.
- A large second-stage estimate means a large effect for everyone → **Actually,** it means a large effect for the people your instrument moved.

## SAY IT OUT LOUD

> **Two-stage least squares** does exactly what the idea says, in two regressions.
>
> **Stage one:** predict your treatment using your instrument and your controls. That prediction —
> x-hat — is *only* the part of treatment that the randomness explains. **Stage two:** regress your
> outcome on that prediction, instead of on the real treatment.
>
> And that's why it works: you've thrown away every part of the treatment that could be tangled up with
> the error term, and kept only the random part. Nothing random can be correlated with anything.
>
> One naming point people get wrong constantly: **two-stage least squares is not the same thing as
> instrumental variables.** It's one way of estimating it. There's also **generalized method of
> moments**, which builds the estimate straight out of the assumption "my instrument is uncorrelated
> with the error term" — and it's better when you've got heteroskedasticity or more than one instrument.
>
> Now the part that really matters. **What effect did you just estimate?**
>
> Because you only used the part of treatment your instrument explains, **you only used the people your
> instrument moved.** If you're so suspicious of medicine that you'd never take the drug whatever I
> assign — you're a **never taker**, and however much that drug would have helped you, you're not in my
> number. Same if you'd have found the drug regardless.
>
> And it goes further: **the more you respond, the more you count.** Imagine I randomly wag my finger at
> people and tell them to exercise more. One person adds an hour a week. Another adds five. I'm going to
> get far more of the five-hour person's effect than the one-hour person's. That's a **local average
> treatment effect** — local to whoever your instrument happens to move.
>
> Which means if I'd used a *different* instrument — showing people a photo of someone very fit instead
> of nagging them — I'd be picking up **different people**, and I could get a different answer. Both
> valid. Different populations.

## RECALL

Q: Describe the two stages of two-stage least squares.
A: First, use the instrument and controls to predict the treatment. Second, regress the outcome on that
predicted treatment along with the controls.

Q: Why does that eliminate omitted variable bias?
A: Because you're using only the part of treatment predicted by exogenous variation — the random part —
and random variation can't be related to the error term.

Q: What extra step does two-stage least squares require?
A: Adjusting the standard errors to account for the fact that the predicted treatment was estimated
rather than observed.

Q: Is two-stage least squares the same as instrumental variables?
A: No — it's one estimator for an instrumental variables model, among several.

Q: What assumption does generalized method of moments build on?
A: That the instrument is unrelated to the error term in the second-stage equation.

Q: What are moment conditions here?
A: Statements that the instrument should be uncorrelated with the residuals, with coefficients chosen so
that holds.

Q: Give the two advantages of generalized method of moments over two-stage least squares.
A: It performs better under heteroskedasticity, giving smaller and more accurate standard errors, and it
handles multiple instruments better.

Q: Who is a never taker, and how do they affect the estimate?
A: Someone who wouldn't take the treatment regardless of assignment. They drop out of the estimate
entirely, however large their treatment effect would have been.

Q: Whose effect does the estimate reflect?
A: People who don't take the treatment when not assigned and do take it when assigned — weighted more
heavily the more strongly they respond.

Q: Give the exercise example of response-weighting.
A: If finger-wagging makes one person exercise one more hour a week and another five more hours, you get
far more of the five-hour person's effect in your estimate.

Q: What is this kind of estimate called?
A: A local average treatment effect.

Q: Why might two different instruments for the same treatment give different answers?
A: They move different people. Finger-wagging and a photo of a fit person appeal to different
populations, so each isolates a different part of the treatment effect.

Q: In the rural China study, what were the instrument, treatment, and outcome?
A: Instrument: whether your friend was randomly assigned to the high-pressure "default buy" setting.
Treatment: whether your friend bought insurance. Outcome: whether you bought insurance.

Q: Give the first stage result and what it establishes.
A: Random assignment to the "assume you buy" setting raised your friend's probability of buying by 11.8
percentage points — establishing relevance, with a large effect, so there's variation to work with.

Q: Give the second stage result.
A: Your friend buying insurance makes you 79.1 percentage points more likely to buy — a very strong
network effect.

## GLOSSARY

- **Two-stage least squares** — estimating instrumental variables by predicting treatment from the instrument, then regressing the outcome on that prediction.
- **First stage** — the regression predicting treatment from the instrument and controls.
- **Second stage** — the regression of the outcome on the predicted treatment.
- **Generalized method of moments** — an estimator that chooses coefficients to satisfy stated moment conditions, here that the instrument is uncorrelated with the residuals.
- **Never taker** — someone who would not take the treatment regardless of the instrument, and so contributes nothing to the estimate.
- **Local average treatment effect** — the effect among those whose treatment the instrument moves, weighted by responsiveness.
