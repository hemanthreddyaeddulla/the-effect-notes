## ONE SENTENCE

An event study only requires comparing what you observed against a predicted counterfactual — no
straight line necessary — which is why finance, where effects are **immediate and then gone**,
builds the prediction from the estimation-period **mean**, from the **market as a whole**, or from
an estimated **relationship** to the market, and calls the gap an **abnormal return**.

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

- An event study needs a counterfactual prediction. It does **not** need a straight-line regression.
- Finance is full of events, and finance responds to the **expectation** of change — so effects arrive **immediately**.
- Two consequences: **short-horizon predictions are far more reliable**, and effects **fade at once**, so fitting a post-period trend line makes no sense.
- The process: pick an **estimation period** and an **observation period**, predict, compare, analyse.
- The gap between actual and predicted is the **abnormal return**.
- Three prediction methods: **mean-adjusted**, **market-adjusted**, and **risk-adjusted** returns.
- None of it is actually specific to finance.

## WHY THIS EXISTS

The last episode estimated an event study by fitting a linear regression on the pre-treatment side
and comparing it to one from the post-treatment side. You don't have to do that.

The key component of an event study is that you're comparing what you observe in the post-treatment
period against **some predicted counterfactual** — a prediction of what you'd have seen without the
treatment. That doesn't require any sort of straight-line regression. And in finance, it's very
common not to use one. Since finance is one of the most common places you'll see event studies
applied, it's worth knowing how they do it.

## IDEAS

### Why finance suits event studies so well

**Plain English.** Lots of events, and the effects land instantly.

**Precisely.** Finance has **a lot of events**: Google merged with something — what did that do to
Google's stock? Apple unveiled the new iPhone — what did that do to Apple's? Something happens at a
particular time and you expect an effect on an easily observable outcome.

That applies in lots of fields. What's different about finance is that stock prices respond not to
literal physical changes but to the **expectation** of change — so effects go into play
**immediately**.

**Anchor image.** A thermometer versus a barometer. One waits for the weather; the other reacts to
the forecast.

**Worked contrast.** Suppose you want the effect of an education programme that gives students better
teachers, randomly assigned, on whether they graduate from college. Even if everyone's confident it
works, you will not see any change in the number of degrees awarded for about **ten years**.

Apple unveils the new iPhone and **ten seconds later** everyone can trade and bid the price up.

### The two consequences of immediacy

**Consequence 1 — short-horizon predictions are much better.** All these methods rest on predicting
what would have happened absent the event, and those predictions are far stronger over a very short
window. Forecasting how many college degrees there'll be in ten years without your policy is very
hard. Forecasting Apple's stock price **one minute from now**, knowing what it is right now, is
pretty easy.

So the immediacy of the effect makes the assumptions much more plausible. If you predict Apple's
price one minute out and then look one minute later, you can be fairly confident the change came
from the announcement and not from some long-term trend you failed to notice — **there simply isn't
time for other effects to sink in.** The time back door gets closed convincingly.

**Consequence 2 — the effects fade right away.** A corollary of immediacy: everything happens at
once and then stops. Almost all of Apple's price growth happens on the first day after the
announcement. Everyone thinks *that's going to be good for Apple, the price should be up there* —
so why wait? They bid it up **within minutes**. You might see a huge effect today and none tomorrow.

**Where it breaks — and why this chapter exists.** When the effect appears briefly and then goes
away, fitting a whole time-series **line** to the post-treatment period doesn't make sense. You
don't want a straight line. You want an effect that's there once and then gone. So you need a method
that allows for that.

### The four-step process

**Plain English.** Choose your windows, predict, compare, analyse.

**Precisely.** Throughout, assume you're looking at the effect of an event on the price of a stock —
though it applies more broadly.

**Step 1 — pick an estimation period and an observation period.** Roughly the pre-treatment and
post-treatment periods, though not necessarily all your data. You might not use everything before;
maybe you give yourself a little lag, or use only a narrow window right next to the treatment. And
you might not follow things forever afterwards — maybe just a couple of days. The estimation period
is what you use to estimate the general patterns; the observation period is where you make
predictions from those patterns.

**Step 2 — make the predictions.** Take the model estimated from the estimation period and predict
what would occur in the observation period.

**Step 3 — compare, giving the abnormal return.** You have your prediction, and you can see what
actually happened.

**Worked example.** You predict Apple's stock would go up by **3%**. It actually went up by **6%**.
That extra **3 percentage points** — beyond what you'd expect from the prior pattern — is the
**abnormal return**. Above and beyond what you'd normally expect, hence *abnormal*.

**Step 4 — analyse the abnormal returns.** You may have a different abnormal return for each day of
the observation period, or one for each treated group. Calculate standard errors for them and see
what the effect of treatment actually was.

**Where it breaks.** None of this is structurally different from the last episode: an estimation
period to fit a model, predictions in the post-treatment period, and a comparison. **The only real
difference is that you're not using a straight line to do it.**

### Prediction method 1: mean-adjusted returns

**Plain English.** Take the average from before and use it as your prediction for after.

**Precisely.** Literally that: take the average in the estimation period and make it your prediction
in the observation period. It's an assumption that whatever you saw before would have continued —
that there was basically **no trend whatsoever**.

**Where it breaks — better than it sounds.** Especially with stock prices. Stock **levels** might go
up and up and up, but the **return on a daily basis** is pretty flat: you gain about the same
percentage each day, give or take. Assuming that general percentage continues into the post period
is not the worst assumption in the world — though you'd want to think about it carefully.

### Prediction method 2: market-adjusted returns

**Plain English.** Use the whole market as your control group.

**Precisely.** You have a rather good comparison available: the market as a whole. Apple goes up and
down over time; so does the market — and you'd expect them to move in the same direction, because
some days are simply good days for the market.

So if Apple goes up 6% after the event, you don't want to confuse **Apple going up because Apple did
something cool** with **Apple going up because the entire market went up**. That's a back door
problem.

**Worked example.** Don't use pre-treatment Apple prices to build the prediction — use the returns
of the **market as a whole**.

| | Return |
|---|---|
| Apple, actual | **6%** |
| Market as a whole | **2%** |
| So you'd have expected Apple | **2%** |
| **Abnormal return** | **4%** |

That extra 4% is your treatment effect.

**Anchor image.** Everyone on the escalator rises. You want to know who was also walking.

**Where it fits.** That comparison is **like using a control group** — you're treating the market as
a whole as your control, saying Apple would have been just like everybody else absent treatment.
Control-group methods get their own chapter next.

### Prediction method 3: risk-adjusted returns

**Plain English.** Same idea, but Apple doesn't move one-for-one with the market — so estimate by
how much it does move, and use that.

**Precisely.** Market-adjusted returns assumed Apple's return would simply *be* the market's return.
Risk-adjusted returns refine that: Apple and the market go in the same direction, but **not to the
same extent**. A market that's 1% higher than normal might be associated with Apple being more than
1% higher than normal.

So estimate a model of the relationship between the market as a whole and Apple's stock price in the
estimation period, then apply that relationship in the post-treatment period.

**Worked example.** Suppose Apple tends to move about twice as much as the market.

| | Return |
|---|---|
| Market as a whole | **2%** |
| Predicted Apple, given it moves ~2× the market | **4%** |
| Apple, actual | **6%** |
| **Abnormal return** | **2%** |

**Where it goes further.** It doesn't have to be as simple as the correlation between the market
return and the stock return. There are also the **Fama-French factors**, which use not just the
overall market return but the returns of particular portfolios — ones that do things like buy small
stocks and sell big stocks. Predict Apple using those portfolios, make the same prediction in the
post-treatment period, and measure the deviation to get your abnormal returns.

### None of this is really about finance

**Plain English.** Strip the vocabulary and these are three general strategies.

| Finance name | The general idea | Where else you'd see it |
|---|---|---|
| **Mean-adjusted returns** | Assume no trend whatsoever; use the before-mean to predict the after | **Statistical process control**, common in health fields |
| **Market-adjusted returns** | Compare the individual unit against the overall group | Using a **control group** — the next chapter |
| **Risk-adjusted returns** | Predict an outcome from factors it tracks, assuming the relationship holds | Any setting where things are correlated and you expect the correlation to persist |

## TRAPS

- An event study requires fitting regression lines → **Actually,** it requires a counterfactual prediction. The line is one way to build one.
- Immediate effects make event studies harder because there's less data → **Actually,** they make them *stronger*, because short-horizon predictions are far more reliable and leave no time for other effects.
- If the effect appears and disappears within a day, the study failed → **Actually,** in finance that's expected, and it's why a post-period trend line is the wrong tool.
- The abnormal return is just the post-event return → **Actually,** it's the post-event return **minus** what you'd have predicted.
- Apple rose 6% after the announcement, so the event was worth 6% → **Actually,** if the whole market rose 2%, at most 4% is abnormal — and less if Apple normally moves more than the market.
- Market-adjusted returns are the safest choice → **Actually,** they assume Apple moves one-for-one with the market. Risk-adjusted returns relax exactly that.
- These methods only work on financial data → **Actually,** mean-adjusted returns is statistical process control, market-adjusted is a control group, and risk-adjusted is any correlated-predictor forecast.

## RECALL

Q: What does an event study actually require?
A: A prediction of the counterfactual and a comparison against it — not necessarily a straight-line
regression.

Q: Why are effects immediate in finance?
A: Because stock prices respond to the *expectation* of change rather than to literal physical change.

Q: Give the contrast used to illustrate that.
A: An education programme's effect on college graduation wouldn't show up for about ten years; Apple's
stock price moves ten seconds after an announcement.

Q: What's the first benefit of immediate effects?
A: Predictions over a very short horizon are far more reliable, so the counterfactual is more credible
and there's no time for other effects to sink in.

Q: What's the second consequence of immediacy, and what problem does it create?
A: The effect happens all at once and then fades. Fitting a time-series trend line to the post-period
makes no sense when you need an effect that appears once and disappears.

Q: List the four steps of the finance approach.
A: Pick an estimation period and an observation period; make predictions; compare to get abnormal
returns; analyse those abnormal returns.

Q: What is an abnormal return?
A: The difference between the actual return and the return you predicted from the prior pattern — a
predicted 3% against an actual 6% gives a 3-point abnormal return.

Q: What do you do in step four?
A: Analyse the abnormal returns — possibly one per day or per treated group — including calculating
standard errors.

Q: Describe the mean-adjusted returns approach.
A: Take the average from the estimation period and use it as the prediction for the observation
period, assuming no trend at all.

Q: Why is that assumption more reasonable than it sounds for stocks?
A: Stock *levels* may climb steadily, but daily *returns* are fairly flat — roughly the same percentage
each day.

Q: Describe the market-adjusted returns approach and the back door it closes.
A: Use the market's return as the prediction for the stock. It prevents confusing a rise caused by the
event with a rise caused by a good day for the entire market.

Q: Apple rose 6% and the market rose 2%. What's the market-adjusted abnormal return?
A: 4%.

Q: What is market-adjusted returns equivalent to?
A: Using a control group — the market as a whole stands in for what Apple would have done absent
treatment.

Q: How does the risk-adjusted approach differ?
A: It doesn't assume the stock moves one-for-one with the market; it estimates the relationship
between them and applies that relationship to the post-treatment period.

Q: Apple typically moves twice as much as the market, the market rose 2%, and Apple rose 6%. What's the abnormal return?
A: You'd have predicted 4%, so the abnormal return is 2%.

Q: What are the Fama-French factors used for here?
A: Predicting a stock from the returns of particular portfolios — such as ones buying small stocks and
selling big ones — not just the overall market return.

Q: Give the non-finance equivalent of each of the three methods.
A: Mean-adjusted returns is statistical process control; market-adjusted returns is using a control
group; risk-adjusted returns is predicting an outcome from factors it tracks and assuming the
relationship persists.

## GLOSSARY

- **Estimation period** — the pre-treatment window used to fit the model that generates predictions.
- **Observation period** — the post-treatment window in which predictions are compared to reality.
- **Abnormal return** — the actual return minus the predicted counterfactual return.
- **Mean-adjusted returns** — predicting the post-period using the estimation-period average.
- **Market-adjusted returns** — predicting a stock's return using the market's return, treating the market as a control group.
- **Risk-adjusted returns** — predicting a stock's return from an estimated relationship to the market or other factors.
- **Fama-French factors** — portfolio-based factors used to predict returns beyond the overall market return.
