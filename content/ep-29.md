## ONE SENTENCE

Regression gives you the best version of the shape you chose — and the best version of a bad
shape is still bad — so when a relationship curves, add powers of X to let the line bend, then
accept that the effect of X is now **different at every value of X** and read it off with a
derivative.

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

- Regression fits the **best version of the shape you picked**. Pick badly and you get the best version of something bad.
- Default OLS shape: a straight line. Plenty of relationships aren't straight.
- Fix by **transforming variables** so the transformed relationship *is* straight. The two big ones in causal inference: **polynomials** and **logarithms**.
- A **polynomial** adds powers of X. Linear = first order. **Quadratic** = X and X². **Cubic** = X, X², X³. Keep the lower terms.
- Diagnose the need by **plotting residuals against X**. A visible pattern is a warning sign.
- Stop at third order. More gets weird — don't add them willy-nilly.
- Interpretation: **there is no single effect of X**, and there shouldn't be. Take the derivative and plug in a value of X.

## WHY THIS EXISTS

Remember what regression is: a shape-fitting procedure. You supply a shape and it finds the best
version of that shape for your data. Getting the best version of a shape sounds great — but you
still had to pick the shape, and if the shape doesn't make sense for your data you get back a bad
result. **Even the best version of a bad thing is still a bad thing.**

By default, ordinary least squares fits a straight line. Look at data that clearly curves: fit a
straight line and you'll get the best possible line, which is still a poor fit, because the
relationship simply isn't well described by a straight line.

## IDEAS

### Transformations

**Plain English.** You can't bend the line, so bend the variables instead.

**Precisely.** If the relationship between your variables isn't a straight line, you can often
still get a good fit from regression by **transforming the variables** — so that while the
original data has no straight-line relationship, the transformed data does.

Several transformations are popular in statistical analysis; the book covers a few, and there are
more it doesn't. The two you'll meet most in causal inference and regression analysis are
**polynomials** and **logarithms**.

### Polynomials

**Plain English.** Add X squared to let the line curve. Add X cubed to let it curve back.

**Precisely.** A polynomial adds additional powers of the variable to your model.

| Model | Terms | What it allows |
|---|---|---|
| **Linear** — first-order polynomial | X | A straight line. Slope never changes: increase X by one and Y always moves by β₁. |
| **Quadratic** — second order | X **and** X² | One curve. Parabola-shaped, though zoomed in it may not curve back on itself. |
| **Cubic** — third order | X, X², X³ | A curve, then a curve back the other direction. |
| Quartic, quintic, … | X⁴, X⁵, … | More wiggle than you want. |

**Keep the lower-order terms.** A quadratic includes X *and* X². You don't leave the X out.

**Anchor image.** A garden hose. Straight by default. One bend, two bends — after about three
you're not shaping it, you're just tangling it.

**Where it breaks.** You're pretty much always fine with the first three orders. Beyond that,
don't get wild: too many polynomial terms makes the model behave strangely, and you shouldn't add
them willy-nilly.

### Diagnosing with residuals

**Plain English.** Plot your residuals against X. If they make a shape, your line has the wrong
shape.

**Precisely.** With a straight-line fit to curved data, the residuals sit consistently **above**
the line at low X, dip **below** in the middle, then come back **above** at high X. That's a
relationship between the residuals and X, which you don't want.

**Why it matters:** one of the assumptions you need about the error term is that it's **unrelated
to X**. What you're really worried about is the error term, not the residuals — but a visible
pattern in the residuals is still not a good sign.

**Worked example.** Plot the residuals from both fits of the same curved data:

- **Straight-line model:** a clear pattern remains — residuals shift systematically as you move along the X axis.
- **Quadratic model:** no relationship whatsoever between residuals and X. Exactly what you want.

**Where it breaks.** So the rule is: plot residuals against X, and if you see a pattern, that may
indicate you want a polynomial term to get rid of it.

### Interpreting polynomial terms

**Plain English.** You can't read one coefficient anymore — and there is no single effect of X.

**Precisely.** Once X appears in more than one place, you must consider all the places it appears
to interpret its effect. And stranger still: **there is no single effect of X, and there shouldn't
be.** The effect of a one-unit increase in X is the slope of the fitted line, and in a polynomial
model the slope is changing all the time. So the effect differs at low, middle, and high values of
X.

**How to get it:** write out the equation and take the derivative.

For a cubic model, the derivative of Y with respect to X is:

```
dY/dX  =  β₁  +  2·β₂·X  +  3·β₃·X²
```

where β₁ is the coefficient on X, β₂ on X², and β₃ on X³.

**Notice X is in that expression.** The whole expression *is* the effect of X — so to state an
effect you must pick a specific value of X to evaluate it at.

- At **X = 0**: everything else drops out, and the effect is just **β₁**.
- At **X = 1**: the effect is **β₁ + 2β₂ + 3β₃**.

### Worked example: chain restaurants

**Worked example.** Regress restaurant health inspection scores on the number of locations the
chain has, with a quadratic term:

| Predictor | Coefficient |
|---|---|
| Number of locations | **−0.08** |
| Number of locations² | **0.0001** |

You want: comparing two chain restaurants where one has one more location than the other, what's
the association with the inspection score?

Write out the model and take the derivative:

```
Score      =  −0.08 · locations  +  0.0001 · locations²  + …
dScore/dLocations  =  −0.08  +  2 · 0.0001 · locations
```

Now plug in whatever value of X you care about:

| At this many locations | The effect of one more location |
|---|---|
| **1** | −0.08 + 2(0.0001)(1) = **−0.0798** |
| **1,000** | −0.08 + 2(0.0001)(1,000) = **+0.12** |

Going from one location to two, and going from a thousand locations to a thousand and one, give
**completely different answers** — including different signs. That's exactly what a curved
relationship is telling you: the effect differs across the range of the X axis, as it should.

## TRAPS

- Regression found the best fit, so the fit is good → **Actually,** it found the best version of the shape *you* chose. The best straight line through curved data is still a bad fit.
- I'll just include X² and drop X, since X² is the term that curves → **Actually,** keep both. A quadratic is X *and* X².
- More polynomial terms means a better fit, so use plenty → **Actually,** you're nearly always fine through third order. Beyond that models get weird.
- Patterned residuals prove my error term is related to X → **Actually,** the error term is what you're worried about; residual patterns are a warning sign, not a proof.
- I can report the effect of X from a quadratic model as one number → **Actually,** there is no single effect. The slope changes with X, so you must evaluate the derivative at a chosen value.
- The coefficient on X in a quadratic model is the effect of X → **Actually,** it's the effect only at X = 0, where the other terms drop out.

## SAY IT OUT LOUD

> Remember what regression actually is: you pick a shape, and it finds the **best version of that
> shape**. Which sounds great until you realise — the best version of a bad shape is still bad.
> And by default, the shape OLS picks is a **straight line**.
>
> So what if the relationship curves? You can't bend the line — so you bend the **variables**. That's
> a transformation, and the simplest one is a **polynomial**: you just add powers of X. Add X² and
> the line can curve once. Add X³ and it can curve and then curve back. And keep the lower terms —
> a quadratic is X *and* X squared, you don't drop the X. Stop around third order; beyond that
> things get weird.
>
> How do you know you need one? **Plot your residuals against X.** If you fit a straight line
> through curved data, you'll see the residuals sit high at the start, dip below in the middle, and
> come back high at the end. That pattern is the data telling you the shape is wrong. Add the
> quadratic and the pattern vanishes.
>
> Now here's the part that trips people up. Once X is in your model twice, **there is no single
> effect of X** — and there shouldn't be! The effect is the slope, and the slope is changing the
> whole way along. So you take the **derivative**, and you plug in a value.
>
> Watch this. Restaurant inspection scores against how many locations the chain has. Coefficient on
> locations is −0.08, on locations-squared is 0.0001. Take the derivative: **−0.08 plus 2 times
> 0.0001 times locations**.
>
> At one location, one more location is worth about **−0.08**. At a thousand locations, one more is
> worth **+0.12**. Different size, different *sign*. And that's not a problem with the model —
> that's the curve telling you something real.

## RECALL

Q: What does "regression gives you the best version of the shape you picked" imply?
A: That if the shape is wrong for your data, you get the best version of a bad thing — which is
still bad.

Q: What's the default shape ordinary least squares fits?
A: A straight line.

Q: What's the general fix when a relationship isn't a straight line?
A: Transform the variables so that while the original data has no straight-line relationship, the
transformed data does.

Q: Name the two transformations most common in causal inference.
A: Polynomials and logarithms.

Q: What terms does a quadratic model include, and what's the common mistake?
A: X and X². The mistake is dropping the X and keeping only the squared term.

Q: What does a cubic allow that a quadratic doesn't?
A: A curve and then a curve back in the other direction. A quadratic allows only one curve.

Q: How far up the polynomial orders should you go?
A: You're pretty much always fine through third order. Beyond that models get strange and you
shouldn't add terms willy-nilly.

Q: How do you diagnose that you need a polynomial?
A: Plot the residuals against X. If they show a pattern — high, then low, then high — the shape is
wrong.

Q: Why do patterned residuals matter?
A: Because one assumption you need is that the error term is unrelated to X. The residuals aren't
the error term, but a pattern in them is a bad sign.

Q: In a polynomial model, why is there no single effect of X?
A: The effect is the slope, and the slope changes continuously — so the effect differs at low,
middle, and high values of X.

Q: Give the derivative of Y with respect to X for a cubic model.
A: dY/dX = β₁ + 2β₂X + 3β₃X².

Q: In that expression, what is the effect at X = 0?
A: Just β₁ — all the other terms drop out.

Q: Inspection scores on locations: coefficients −0.08 and 0.0001 on the squared term. Write the derivative.
A: dScore/dLocations = −0.08 + 2(0.0001)(locations).

Q: Evaluate that at 1 location and at 1,000 locations.
A: At 1: −0.08 + 0.0002 = −0.0798. At 1,000: −0.08 + 0.2 = +0.12 — different magnitude and
different sign.

## GLOSSARY

- **Transformation** — altering a variable so a straight-line relationship holds in the transformed data.
- **Polynomial** — adding powers of a predictor to allow the fitted line to curve.
- **Quadratic** — a second-order polynomial: X and X².
- **Cubic** — a third-order polynomial: X, X², and X³.
- **Residual plot** — residuals plotted against a predictor; a visible pattern suggests the wrong functional form.
