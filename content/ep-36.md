## ONE SENTENCE

Matching demands five decisions — what counts as **close**, whether to **pick matches or use
weights**, **how many** matches, **how fast the weights decay**, and **how bad a match is too
bad** — and every one of them is the same bias–variance trade-off wearing a different hat.

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

- **Q1 — What's the matching criterion?** What does "similar" mean? Easy with one variable (just the difference); hard with several, which needs a **distance measure**.
- **Q2 — Matches or weights?** Pick the closest control observations and discard the rest, or keep many and weight them by closeness.
- **Q3 — If matches, how many?** Each extra match is a *worse* match, but grows the comparison group.
- **Q4 — If weights, how do they decay?** How fast, and by what **kernel** — triangular, Epanechnikov, others.
- **Q5 — What's the worst acceptable match?** Beyond some distance you drop the match — and possibly drop the treated observation entirely.
- Every one is bias versus variance. Some procedures let you skip a question or two; usually you face all five.

## WHY THIS EXISTS

The goal of matching is a treated group and a control group that are comparable across all the
variables you match on. Every time you match on a variable, you close a back door. But *how*
exactly do you do that?

There turn out to be five decisions, and this episode walks each one. To keep it tractable it uses
a single matching variable — not how you'd normally run a matching study, where you'd match on
several, but much simpler to follow.

**The running example.** Data on **30,000 credit card holders in Taiwan in 2005**: their monthly
bills, and whether they repaid, for both April and September. The question is the *stickiness of
credit problems* — is being late on your April payment related to being late in September?

| Role | Variable |
|---|---|
| **Treatment** | Were you late with your bill in April? |
| **Outcome** | Were you late with your bill in September? |
| **Matching variable** | The **size** of your April bill (continuous) |

Why that back door: bigger April bills mean you're likely to have bigger September bills too, so
big bills make you more likely to be late in both. You want to isolate *how late you're willing to
be* rather than simply having difficulty paying.

## IDEAS

### Q1 · What is the matching criterion?

**Plain English.** You have to define what "similar" means before you can find similar people.

**Precisely.** You want people with similar April bill sizes. But you **can't require they be
exactly the same** — if a treated person's bill is 47,432, essentially nobody in the control group
has that exact figure, and you'd match nobody at all. So for a continuous variable you must decide
what it means to be **close**.

**Anchor image.** "Nearby" is meaningless until you say whether you're walking or flying.

**Worked example.** With one variable it's straightforward: take the difference. One person's bill
is 43,000, another's is 43,001 — a difference of 1, which is very close. The bigger the difference,
the more different they are.

On the graph, treated observation **row 10,305** has two nearby candidates: **row 27,719** (a bit
lower) and **row 27,281**. All three had April bills close to **91,600**, so they're comparable —
and you'd pick whichever is closest.

**Where it breaks.** It gets much harder once you add matching variables. If two candidates are
similar on one variable but less similar on another, how do you combine those? You need a proper
measure of **distance** — which is the next episode.

### Q2 · Select a set of matches, or construct weights?

**Plain English.** Keep only the best few, or keep lots and let closeness decide how much they
count.

**Worked example — one-to-one matching.** For treated row 10,305, pick the **single closest**
control observation — row 27,281. Those two are treated as basically the same person except one got
treated. Everything else is discarded entirely, greyed out. You could pick the two best matches, or
three, but the principle is the same: pick the best, throw away the rest.

**Worked example — weights.** Instead of in-or-out, count the closest ones more. Picture the control
observations as circles: the **biggest circles** are the ones closest to the treated observation —
the control bills most similar to it. As you move further away, the circles shrink; they're being
counted less. Past a certain distance you stop counting them entirely — too far to be a good
comparison at all.

**Where it breaks — the trade-off.** Picking only the best matches gives the **lowest bias** — the
best possible job of closing that back door — but you may have a **single** control observation,
which makes the control group's average very noisy. Weights let worse comparisons creep in, so
possibly a slightly worse job on the back door, but you have far more observations to average over
and therefore more precision.

### Q3 · If selecting matches, how many?

**Plain English.** One match, or the best five?

**Precisely.** You could take just one, but that severely limits your control group's sample size.
You can take the best two, three, four, five — whatever you want.

**Anchor image.** Going back to the well. The first bucket is the cleanest.

**Where it breaks.** As you go back for the next best match, and the next, you are **consecutively
picking worse and worse matches** — inviting more bias with each one. But you're also building a
bigger comparison group, which makes your estimate more precise. Same trade-off.

### Q4 · If using weights, how do they decay with distance?

**Plain English.** Two sub-decisions: how *fast* the weights fall off, and what *shape* the fall-off
has.

**Precisely — how fast.** A very good match gets a lot of weight and worse matches get less, but how
quickly does that drop?

- **Fast decay** → you're essentially only using the best matches. Fewer effective observations, best comparisons.
- **Slow decay** → a wide range of observations included, though with small weights. More precision, more bias.

**Precisely — what shape.** You have to give it a function so it knows what decay to apply. That
function is a **kernel**: something maximised at the centre, where you're as close to the treated
observation as possible, and getting smaller as you move away.

| Kernel | Shape |
|---|---|
| **Triangular** | straight-line decay |
| **Epanechnikov** | more curved — decays more slowly at first, then more quickly |

There are plenty of others, each with pros and cons.

### Q5 · What is the worst acceptable match?

**Plain English.** At some point the closest available match is so bad you shouldn't use it at all.

**Worked example.** A treated observation had an April bill of **1,000**. You go to the control group
to find a match — and nobody has a bill anywhere near 1,000. The single best available match has an
April bill of **6,000**. Way off.

The whole point of matching is to construct a control group that looks like the treated group on the
matching variables. Even the best match available here isn't a good comparison. So you might say
6,000-to-1,000 is simply **too far apart** to believe you're getting a comparable control group —
that match is too bad to count.

In which case you take that treated observation and **drop it from the study entirely**. No match
found, so it can't be used.

**How it plays out in each approach:**

- **Matched sample.** Is the best match within your acceptable range, or outside it? And if you wanted three matches per treated observation, are there enough good ones? Maybe one control at 1,100 is close enough but the next in line is 6,000 — so you stop at one.
- **Weights.** The weights decay as matches get worse, and at some point the kernel **goes to zero** — you don't just give it a low weight, you give it none. How fast does it get there? Quickly, or slowly, accepting some pretty bad matches along the way?

**Where it breaks.** This is a **subjective** question. Can you really justify calling 1,000 and
6,000 comparable? Probably not — but where exactly is the cutoff?

And the trade-off again: **the worse the matches you accept**, the more treated observations find a
match and stay in the sample, so more precision — but you're comparing genuinely incomparable
things, so you haven't really closed the back door, and bias comes back in. **Be strict** and that
1,000 gets dropped, shrinking your sample and costing you precision, but every comparison you keep
is a real one.

## TRAPS

- Matching means finding observations with identical values → **Actually,** with a continuous variable essentially nobody matches exactly. You must define what "close" means.
- One-to-one matching is the purest form, so it's the best → **Actually,** it minimises bias and maximises noise. A single control observation makes the control average very imprecise.
- More matches is more data, so it's better → **Actually,** each additional match is a worse match. You're buying precision with bias.
- The kernel choice is a technicality → **Actually,** it determines how quickly weights decay, which is exactly where you land on bias versus variance.
- If a treated observation has no good match, use the closest one available → **Actually,** if the closest is 6,000 against 1,000, that's not a comparison. You may need to drop the treated observation.
- Dropping unmatched treated observations wastes data → **Actually,** keeping them means comparing incomparable things, which reopens the back door.
- These five questions are specific to one matching method → **Actually,** you face them for essentially any matching procedure; some let you skip one or two.

## SAY IT OUT LOUD

> Matching sounds simple until you actually try to do it. There are five decisions, and every single
> one is the same trade-off in disguise.
>
> Here's my setup: 30,000 Taiwanese credit card holders. Treatment is being late on your April bill,
> outcome is being late in September, and I need to match on the **size** of the April bill — because
> big bills make you late in both months, and I want to isolate how late you're *willing* to be.
>
> **Question one: what does "close" even mean?** I can't demand exact matches — if your bill is
> 47,432, nobody else has exactly that. With one variable it's easy, just take the difference. 43,000
> versus 43,001? Fine. With several variables it gets hard fast, and you need a real distance measure.
>
> **Question two: do I pick matches, or use weights?** Either I find the single closest control person
> and bin everyone else, or I keep lots of people and count the close ones more heavily. Pick just the
> best and my comparison is excellent but I've got one data point — noisy. Use weights and I'm
> precise, but I've let worse comparisons in.
>
> **Question three: if I'm picking, how many?** Because the second-best match is worse than the best,
> and the third is worse still. More data, worse comparisons.
>
> **Question four: if I'm weighting, how fast do the weights fall off?** And in what shape — that's
> your **kernel**, triangular or Epanechnikov or whatever. Fast decay means basically only the best
> matches. Slow decay means lots of people counting a little.
>
> **Question five, and this one's my favourite: how bad is too bad?** Say I've got a treated person
> with a 1,000 bill and the closest control anywhere is 6,000. That's not a comparison, that's a
> guess. So do I use it anyway — or do I **drop that person from the study entirely**?
>
> Every one of those five: better matches, or more matches. Never both.

## RECALL

Q: What is the goal that all five questions serve?
A: Getting a treated group and control group that are comparable across the variables you match on
— each matched variable closes a back door.

Q: Describe the running example's treatment, outcome, and matching variable.
A: Treatment: late with your April credit card bill. Outcome: late in September. Matching variable:
the size of your April bill.

Q: Why is April bill size a back door here?
A: Bigger April bills mean bigger September bills, so large bills make you more likely to be late in
both months — which would look like stickiness when it's really difficulty paying.

Q: Why can't your matching criterion be exact equality?
A: With a continuous variable essentially nobody shares an exact value — if a bill is 47,432, no
control observation matches it — so you'd find no matches at all.

Q: With a single matching variable, what is the criterion?
A: Simply the difference between the values. 43,000 versus 43,001 is a difference of 1, so very
close; larger differences mean more different.

Q: Why does the criterion get harder with more variables?
A: Two candidates may be similar on one variable and less similar on another, and you need a
measure of distance that combines them.

Q: Describe one-to-one matching.
A: For each treated observation, pick the single closest control observation as its comparison and
discard everything else.

Q: Describe the weighting alternative.
A: Keep many control observations, giving the closest ones the largest weights and shrinking the
weights as distance grows, until beyond some distance they count for nothing.

Q: Give the bias–variance trade-off between those two.
A: Picking only the best matches gives lowest bias but a very small, noisy control group. Weighting
admits worse comparisons — more bias — but far more observations, so more precision.

Q: If you're selecting matches, what happens as you take more of them?
A: Each successive match is worse than the last, adding bias, but the comparison group grows, adding
precision.

Q: What are the two sub-decisions in how weights decay?
A: How fast they decay, and what function — what kernel — describes the decay.

Q: What is a kernel?
A: A function maximised at the centre, where you're closest to the treated observation, and getting
smaller as you move away — determining the weights.

Q: Name two kernels and how they differ.
A: The triangular kernel decays in a straight line; the Epanechnikov kernel is more curved, decaying
more slowly at first and then more quickly.

Q: A treated observation has a 1,000 bill and the closest control has 6,000. What are your options?
A: Accept it as a match despite being incomparable, or declare it too bad and drop that treated
observation from the study entirely.

Q: How does question 5 show up when using weights rather than matches?
A: The kernel eventually goes to zero — beyond some distance an observation gets no weight at all —
and you decide how quickly it reaches zero.

Q: State the bias–variance trade-off in accepting worse matches.
A: Accepting worse matches keeps more treated observations in the sample, giving more precision, but
compares incomparable things, so the back door isn't really closed and bias returns.

Q: Do these five questions apply only to certain matching methods?
A: No — you generally face all of them for any matching procedure, though some methods let you skip
one or two.

## GLOSSARY

- **Matching criterion** — the rule defining how similar two observations must be to count as matched.
- **One-to-one matching** — pairing each treated observation with its single closest control.
- **Kernel** — the function determining how matching weights decay with distance.
- **Triangular kernel** — a kernel whose weights decay in a straight line.
- **Epanechnikov kernel** — a curved kernel decaying slowly at first, then more quickly.
- **Worst acceptable match** — the distance beyond which a match is rejected, potentially dropping the treated observation.
