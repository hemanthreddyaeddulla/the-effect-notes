## ONE SENTENCE

Everyone deals with missing values by **deleting those observations**, and whether that's fine depends
entirely on **why** the data is missing — harmless if the question didn't apply or the loss was truly
random, salvageable if you can explain the missingness with other variables, and close to hopeless if
the reason is **the missing value itself**.

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

- **Listwise deletion** — dropping observations with missing values — is by far the most common approach.
- Whether it works depends on **why** the data is missing. Four reasons:
- **Not in universe:** the question doesn't apply. Deletion is fine.
- **Missing completely at random:** no rhyme or reason. Deletion is fine.
- **Missing at random:** explained by variables you **can** measure. Deletion doesn't quite work; other methods do.
- **Missing not at random:** the missingness depends on **the value itself**. Deletion can really mess you up, and there's not much you can do.
- **Multiple imputation** predicts missing values from your other data, repeatedly, with noise.
- **Limited information maximum likelihood** and the **expectation maximization algorithm** use the model itself rather than imputing first.

## WHY THIS EXISTS

Missing data is when you have a variable and a bunch of observations, but some observations don't have
all the values filled in.

**Worked example.** In a census study you know how many people live in your household, your income,
your race, and your gender — but for some reason the **married** variable isn't there. It doesn't say
you're married. It doesn't say you're not. It says *I don't know*.

That's a problem: if you want to study the effects of being married, or what predicts being married,
what do you do with this person? They're not married, they're not unmarried — and **you can't do
statistics on a value you don't have.**

## IDEAS

### Listwise deletion, and why the reason matters

**Plain English.** Everyone just drops those people. Whether that's okay depends on why they're missing.

**Precisely.** The common approach is to drop anybody with missing values on the relevant variables from
the data — *I'm studying marriage, I don't know whether you're married, so I'll ignore you entirely.*
That's **listwise deletion**, and it's the most common way of dealing with missing data.

Whether it works depends on a number of factors — importantly, **why the data is missing**. Which means
you have to think **theoretically** about why this variable might not be recorded for this particular
individual.

### Reason 1: not in universe

**Plain English.** The question doesn't apply to them.

**Worked example.** Your census data has an occupation variable — carpenter, plumber, teacher. There's
another variable asking whether you have a job at all. If you answered **no**, why would anyone ask
what your occupation is? You're **not in universe** for that question.

**Listwise deletion works perfectly fine here.** You're simply not the kind of person to whom the
question applies.

### Reason 2: missing completely at random

**Plain English.** No rhyme or reason. It just wasn't written down.

**Precisely.** A value is **missing completely at random** if it's missing for entirely random reasons,
completely unrelated to everything else. There's no explanation — it just happens to be missing.

**Worked example.** The data is stored in a filing cabinet and some of the files got wet. Or you knocked
on the door that day and didn't write it down. Or the entry got smudged.

**Listwise deletion also works fine.**

**Anchor image.** A few pages torn out of a book at random. Annoying, but the remaining pages still tell
the same story.

### Reason 3: missing at random

**Plain English.** There *is* a reason, but it's a reason you can measure.

**Precisely.** Confusingly, the name is very similar to *missing completely at random* and it's an
entirely different concept. **Missing at random** means the reason the value is missing **is related to
some variables — but variables you can measure and control for.** You can predict why somebody is
missing.

**Worked example.** Return to that marriage variable. Suppose there's a religion that forbids telling
anyone whether you're married — that's for the religion to know, not the government. You wouldn't
observe a marriage value for those individuals — **but you can say exactly why**: they're in that
religion. If you recorded religion elsewhere, you can explain the missingness. So it's random **once you
account for the variables that explain it.**

**Listwise deletion doesn't quite work here**, but other methods do.

### Reason 4: missing not at random

**Plain English.** The value is missing *because* of what the value is.

**Precisely.** The scariest version. A value is **missing not at random** if the reason it's missing is
related to **the value of the variable itself**.

**Worked example.** Suppose people are embarrassed to tell a survey taker that they're **not** married.
Then the people with missing values are far more likely to be unmarried — if you're married you'll say
so, and if you're not, some will tell you and some will decline out of embarrassment.

So the value of the variable is related to whether it's missing at all. **That's the hardest case to
deal with. Listwise deletion could really mess you up — and there's also not a whole lot you can do.**

**Anchor image.** Asking a room who's uncomfortable, and only the comfortable people answer.

### Imputation, building up

**Plain English.** Guess the missing value from what else you know about that person.

**Precisely.** The most common alternative approach is **multiple imputation**: use what else you know
about someone to predict the missing value. Build up to it:

**Mean imputation.** Your income is missing, so take the average of everybody else's income and give you
that. *That doesn't work very well*, but it's the starter idea — making a prediction and filling in your
value from other information.

**Single imputation.** Rather than the overall average, **predict your value using your other data**.
Build a model predicting income from all the other variables, estimated using everybody else's data,
then plug in your values and predict your income.

**Multiple imputation.** The same idea, **repeated many times with noise added**, which tends to improve
the predictive quality of the results. You end up with, say, 10 or 20 different predicted values for
your income — and when you estimate your model, you **run it 10 or 20 times and average the models
together** to get your results.

**Where it works and doesn't.**

- It works when data is **missing at random**, because it uses your other values to make the prediction — so to the extent those variables explain the missingness, it incorporates that.
- It works **less well when missing not at random**, because it can't incorporate the unobserved value itself. If the value is likely to be different for people who are missing, the model can't reflect that — **it has no data on the missing people.**
- And it imputes **without recognising the model it will eventually be used for.**

### Methods that use the model itself

**Plain English.** Instead of filling in the blanks first, estimate the model with whatever each person
has.

**Limited information maximum likelihood.** **Maximum likelihood** hasn't featured much in this book,
though it underlies some methods already used. The basic idea: you have a model, and that model would
generate data if you ran it. So pick the parameters that make the randomly generated data **as much
like the actual data you have as possible** — maximising the likelihood of seeing the data you do in
fact see.

Applied to missing data: not everybody can fully contribute to the estimation, **but you can still
contribute based on the variables you do have.** So take what you can from each individual, leave out
the parts that are missing, and estimate the model that way. It **doesn't impute** the missing values —
it estimates the model while accounting for the fact that you don't have full data on everyone.

**Expectation maximization algorithm.** Another approach using the model to do the imputation. It goes
**back and forth** between two estimation processes:

1. Estimate a model. That model can generate likely values — *based on this model, here's your most likely income.*
2. Now you've predicted an income and filled it into the data. **Re-estimate the model** using the filled-in values.
3. Go back and forth — using the data to estimate the model, and using the model to predict what the missing values should be — a few times, to get the best version of the model you can.

**Where it breaks.** All of these work best in the **missing at random** or **missing completely at
random** cases. For missing not at random there are methods, but they're rather complex and aren't
covered.

## TRAPS

- Dropping observations with missing values is a neutral first step → **Actually,** whether it works depends entirely on why the data is missing.
- Missing at random and missing completely at random are the same thing → **Actually,** they're different concepts with confusingly similar names. One means no reason at all; the other means a reason you can measure.
- Missing at random means the missingness is unrelated to anything → **Actually,** it's related to variables you can measure and control for.
- If you can't explain the missingness, it's missing at random → **Actually,** if the missingness depends on the value itself, it's missing not at random, and that's the dangerous case.
- Imputation fixes missing not at random data → **Actually,** it can't, because it has no data on the missing people to learn how they differ.
- Mean imputation is a reasonable quick fix → **Actually,** it doesn't work very well; it's just the entry point to the idea.
- Multiple imputation is single imputation done carefully → **Actually,** it repeats the process many times **with noise added**, and you average across the resulting models.

> race, household size — and then for *married*, it just says nothing. Not married, not unmarried.
> Nothing.
>
> And what does everyone do? **Delete that person.** That's listwise deletion, and it's overwhelmingly
> the most common approach.
>

>
> **Missing completely at random** — no reason at all. The filing cabinet leaked. Someone forgot to write
> it down. Also fine to delete.
>
> **Missing at random** — and I'm sorry about the naming, these are genuinely different things. This
> means there *is* a reason, but it's one you can **measure**. Say there's a religion that forbids
> disclosing marital status. You'll have missing values — but you know exactly who and why, because you

>

## RECALL

Q: What is missing data?
A: When some observations don't have all their values filled in — the variable is neither one value nor
another, just unknown.

Q: What is listwise deletion?
A: Dropping any observation with missing values on the relevant variables — the most common approach.

Q: What determines whether listwise deletion is acceptable?
A: Why the data is missing — which requires thinking theoretically about why the value wasn't recorded.

Q: Explain "not in universe" with an example.
A: The question doesn't apply — someone who answered that they have no job has no occupation to record.
Deletion is fine.

Q: Define missing completely at random and give examples.
A: Missing for entirely random reasons unrelated to anything — files got wet, an entry got smudged,
someone forgot to write it down. Deletion is fine.

Q: Define missing at random.
A: The missingness is related to variables you can measure and control for, so you can predict why
somebody's value is missing.

Q: Give the missing at random example.
A: A religion forbidding members from disclosing marital status — you don't observe marriage for them,
but you know why, because you recorded religion.

Q: Define missing not at random.
A: The reason a value is missing is related to the value of the variable itself.

Q: Give the missing not at random example and why it's dangerous.
A: People embarrassed to say they aren't married decline to answer, so missing values are
disproportionately unmarried people — and deleting them systematically removes them.

Q: In which cases does listwise deletion work well?
A: Not in universe, and missing completely at random.

Q: Describe mean imputation and Nick's assessment of it.
A: Replace a missing value with the average of everyone else's. It doesn't work very well — it's just the
starting idea.

Q: Describe single imputation.
A: Build a model predicting the variable from someone's other data, estimated on everybody else, then
plug in their values to predict theirs.

Q: How does multiple imputation extend that?
A: It repeats the process many times with noise added, producing perhaps 10 or 20 predicted values, then
runs the model on each and averages the results.

Q: When does multiple imputation work, and when doesn't it?
A: It works under missing at random, since it uses the other variables that explain the missingness. It
works poorly under missing not at random, because it has no data on the missing people to learn how they
differ.

Q: What's the basic idea of maximum likelihood?
A: Choose model parameters that make the data the model would generate as much like the data you actually
observe as possible.

Q: How does limited information maximum likelihood handle missing data?
A: It doesn't impute — each individual contributes based on the variables they do have, and the model is
estimated from whatever information is available.

Q: Describe the expectation maximization algorithm.
A: Estimate a model, use it to predict likely values for the missing entries, re-estimate the model using
those filled-in values, and go back and forth several times.

Q: Which missingness types do all these methods suit best?
A: Missing at random and missing completely at random.

## GLOSSARY

- **Listwise deletion** — dropping observations that have missing values on relevant variables.
- **Not in universe** — a value is missing because the question doesn't apply to that observation.
- **Missing completely at random** — missing for reasons unrelated to anything.
- **Missing at random** — missing for reasons explained by variables you can measure.
- **Missing not at random** — missing for reasons related to the missing value itself.
- **Multiple imputation** — repeatedly predicting missing values with added noise and averaging across the resulting models.
- **Limited information maximum likelihood** — estimating a model using whatever variables each observation has, without imputing.
- **Expectation maximization algorithm** — alternating between estimating a model and predicting missing values from it.
