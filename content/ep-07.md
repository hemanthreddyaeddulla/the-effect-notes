## ONE SENTENCE

A fitted line is a compressed conditional distribution: plug in a value of X, read the
height of the line, and you have the conditional mean of Y there — and the whole choice
is how much shape you let the data pick versus how much you impose in exchange for a
cleaner summary.

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

- Binning X and taking means inside bins works, but it's ad hoc — the bins are arbitrary.
- Fit a **line** instead. Its height above any X *is* the conditional mean of Y at that X.
- **Non-parametric** approaches (like a **LOESS** curve) shrink bins to almost nothing and roll along X, letting the data choose the shape. Maximum flexibility.
- **Ordinary least squares** instead fixes the shape in advance — usually a straight line — and finds the best one of that shape.
- You lose detail. You gain an **intercept and a slope**, easy statistical properties, and less noise-chasing.
- The slope is the payoff: *a one-unit increase in X is associated with a slope-sized change in Y.* **Correlation** is just that slope with the units stripped out.

## WHY THIS EXISTS

Last episode ended in an awkward place. To describe how the conditional mean of Y moves
across values of a continuous X, you binned X. But where do the bins go? How many? The
data doesn't say, and different choices tell different stories.

There's also a plain communication problem. A conditional distribution at every value of
X is an enormous amount of information. Nobody can hold eighteen thousand conditional
means in their head. You need something someone can actually take away.

## IDEAS

### A fitted line is a lookup table for conditional means

**Plain English.** Draw a shape through the data. Pick any X, look at the line's height
there — that's your predicted average Y.

**Precisely.** The fitted line maps each value of X to the conditional mean of Y at that
value. Reading the line across its whole range shows you how the conditional distribution
of Y shifts as X changes — which is exactly the relationship you set out to describe.

**Anchor image.** A conditional distribution at every X is the full sheet music. The
fitted line is the melody you can hum.

### Non-parametric: let the data pick the shape

**Plain English.** Take the binning idea and make the bins tiny, sliding smoothly along X
instead of chopping it into blocks.

**Precisely.** **Non-parametric regression** computes an average (or something slightly more
sophisticated) of Y within a very narrow window around each X. A **LOESS curve** rolls that
window along the X axis, producing a smooth curve without you specifying a functional form
in advance.

**Anchor image.** A pencil rubbing dragged along the data, taking on whatever shape is
underneath it.

**Worked example.** BMI against the proportion of people taking vitamin E. Instead of
"BMI between 10 and 15", narrow to 11–12, or 11.1–11.2, and smooth.

The LOESS curve shows: at low BMI, the share taking vitamin E rises **quickly** — going from
BMI 20 to 22 buys you a lot more vitamin E takers. Then it **flattens** — from BMI 60 to 62,
barely any change. Upward sloping overall, steep at first, shallow later.

That's a real summary. You didn't have to memorise thousands of conditional means; the
shape carries the message.

**Where it breaks.** It's still visual. Reading the curve off a graph is easy; *reporting*
it is not. Ask "how much does the expectation of Y change when X goes up by one?" and there's
no single number to give.

### Ordinary least squares: impose the shape, gain a number

**Plain English.** Decide up front what shape the relationship has — usually a straight line
— then find the best line of that shape.

**Precisely.** **Ordinary least squares** (linear regression) fits a pre-specified functional
form rather than letting the data choose. Committing to a shape costs detail and buys a
summary in two numbers: an **intercept** and a **slope**.

**Anchor image.** A tailored suit versus a bedsheet draped over someone. The sheet follows
every contour; the suit is a deliberate simplification you can describe over the phone.

**Worked example.** The same vitamin E and BMI data, fit as a straight line:

```
proportion taking vitamin E  =  0.110  +  0.002 × BMI
```

- **Intercept 0.110.** At BMI = 0 — if that were somehow possible — you'd predict 11% of people take vitamin E.
- **Slope 0.002.** This is the real payoff. For every one-unit increase in BMI, the conditional mean of vitamin E taking goes up by 0.002.

The curve from LOESS is gone. In exchange you get a sentence anyone can repeat: *a one-unit
increase in BMI is associated with a 0.002 increase in the proportion taking vitamin E.*

**Where it breaks.** You genuinely lost something — the steep-then-shallow structure is no
longer visible. Whether that trade is worth it depends on whether the detail was real signal
or noise.

### Why simplifying is often the right call

**Plain English.** More flexibility isn't free. Past a point you start fitting the wiggles in
your particular sample rather than anything real.

**Precisely.** Two arguments for OLS beyond readability. First, it has convenient statistical
properties — the **sampling distribution of an OLS coefficient** is easy to describe, which is
harder for a LOESS curve or anything more flexible. Second, there's a trade-off: enough detail
to capture what's genuinely there, but not so much that your data can't support it. Highly
detailed models get noisy.

**Anchor image.** Tracing a coastline. Zoom in far enough and you're recording individual
pebbles — technically more accurate, and useless for navigation.

**Where it breaks.** Straight isn't compulsory. Linear regression can fit curved shapes too;
that, along with using it to replace comparisons of means and ANOVAs (both special cases of
regression), waits until Chapter 13.

### Slope and correlation

**Plain English.** The sign of the slope tells you the direction. Strip the units off and you
have a correlation.

**Precisely.** A positive slope means the variables are positively related in a linear way;
negative means negatively. A **correlation is a rescaled regression slope** — nothing more. The
slope speaks in units (units of BMI, units of vitamin E); remove the units and you get a
unitless measure of strength running from −1 to 1.

**Anchor image.** Same journey described two ways: "40 miles per hour" versus "fast". One
carries units, one is pure magnitude.

## TRAPS

- LOESS is better than OLS because it's more flexible → **Actually,** flexibility costs you a reportable number, easy sampling properties, and protection from fitting noise. It's a trade, not a ranking.
- The regression intercept is a meaningful prediction → **Actually,** here it's the predicted share at BMI = 0, which can't happen. Intercepts are often mechanical anchors, not statements about the world.
- A slope of 0.002 is tiny, so the relationship is negligible → **Actually,** slopes carry units. 0.002 per one unit of BMI accumulates across a realistic BMI range.
- Correlation and regression are different methods → **Actually,** correlation is just the regression slope with units removed and rescaled to run from −1 to 1.
- Line-fitting is a causal technique → **Actually,** everything here is description. "Associated with" is doing real work in that sentence.

## SAY IT OUT LOUD

> Last time we described how two variables relate by chopping X into bins and taking the
> average of Y in each bin. It works, but where do you put the bins? Totally arbitrary.
>
> So instead, fit a **line**. And here's the way to think about a fitted line that makes it
> click: pick any value of X, look at how high the line is there — that height is the
> *average Y for that value of X*. The line is just a compressed version of every conditional
> mean at once.
>
> Two ways to draw it. The flexible way is a **LOESS** curve — you take the binning idea and
> shrink the bins to almost nothing, then roll that tiny window along the X axis. The data
> picks the shape. For BMI versus vitamin E use, you can see it rise fast at low BMI and then
> flatten out at high BMI. Genuinely informative.
>
> The other way is **ordinary least squares** — you decide the shape up front, usually a
> straight line, and find the best one. You lose that steep-then-flat detail. But look what
> you get: `vitamin E = 0.110 + 0.002 × BMI`. One number, the **slope**, and you can say a
> one-unit increase in BMI is associated with a 0.002 increase in the share taking vitamin E.
> Try saying the LOESS curve out loud like that — you can't.
>
> And simplifying isn't just about being easy to say. Overly detailed models chase noise, and
> a straight line has statistical properties that are much easier to work with.
>
> Last thing, because it surprises people: **correlation is just a regression slope with the
> units taken out**. That's the whole relationship between them.

## RECALL

Q: What does the height of a fitted line at a given X represent?
A: The conditional mean of Y at that value of X.

Q: What was wrong with using bins to describe a relationship?
A: The bin placement and count are arbitrary — different choices give different pictures, and
nothing in the data tells you which is right.

Q: What is a LOESS curve doing mechanically?
A: Rolling a very narrow window along the X axis and computing something like the average of Y
inside it at each point, producing a smooth curve without a pre-specified shape.

Q: How does OLS differ from non-parametric regression in one sentence?
A: OLS specifies the shape in advance and finds the best fit of that shape; non-parametric
methods let the data determine the shape.

Q: Interpret `vitamin E = 0.110 + 0.002 × BMI`, both terms.
A: At BMI 0 you'd predict 11% take vitamin E (mechanically — BMI 0 is impossible). Each
one-unit increase in BMI is associated with a 0.002 increase in the conditional mean of
vitamin E taking.

Q: The LOESS curve rose steeply at low BMI and flattened at high BMI. What happens to that when you fit a straight line?
A: It's lost. The straight line reports a single average slope across the range, discarding
the steep-then-shallow structure.

Q: Give two reasons to accept that loss of detail.
A: OLS coefficients have easy-to-describe sampling distributions, and simpler models are less
prone to fitting noise your data can't support.

Q: What's the relationship between correlation and a regression slope?
A: Correlation is a rescaled regression slope — the slope with units stripped out, running
from −1 to 1.

Q: Does OLS force you into a straight line?
A: No — linear regression can fit curved shapes. That comes in Chapter 13.

Q: What does a negative slope tell you?
A: The two variables are negatively related in a linear way.

## GLOSSARY

- **Line-fitting** — summarizing a relationship with a shape whose height at each X gives the conditional mean of Y.
- **Non-parametric regression** — fitting without specifying a functional form in advance.
- **LOESS curve** — a smooth non-parametric fit produced by rolling a narrow local window along X.
- **Ordinary least squares (OLS)** — linear regression; fits a pre-specified shape, usually a straight line, by best fit.
- **Intercept** — the fitted value of Y when X is zero.
- **Slope** — the change in the conditional mean of Y associated with a one-unit increase in X.
- **Correlation** — a rescaled, unitless regression slope, running from −1 to 1.
