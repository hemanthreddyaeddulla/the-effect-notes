## ONE SENTENCE

Finding exogenous variation doesn't remove the requirement to close all back doors — it
**moves** it, from "close every back door between treatment and outcome" to "close every
back door between my source of variation and the outcome" — and three real studies show
that job getting steadily harder as the variation gets muddier.

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

- You haven't escaped the back door problem. You've **relocated** it — hopefully somewhere easier.
- **Lottery winnings → bankruptcy.** Among people who already bought tickets, winning big vs. small is genuinely random. Very believable.
- **Wind direction → pollution → driving.** Wind isn't random — it correlates with season and weather — but those are measurable and controllable. Grey area, still plausible.
- **Medicaid expansion → uncompensated care.** Which states expanded was a deeply *political* decision. Strongest assumptions of the three.
- The muddier the variation, the more work required to justify it as random.
- Whether it works is **contextual**. You need to know the setting to know whether back doors exist.

## WHY THIS EXISTS

Front doors sound like they let you run an experiment anywhere. That's a genuinely exciting
idea, and it's also where people stop reading the fine print.

The honest statement: you're looking for something contributing to whether you're treated —
it doesn't have to be the only determinant — that has **no back doors** of its own. If you
find something that's *actually* random, like a policy someone assigned by lottery, you're
probably fine. Outside that, you're in a grey area, and how far you're willing to go is a
judgement about context.

The frustrating position this leaves you in: you can't always run an experiment. Even when you
can, you may not get enough people for it to be worthwhile. You can't just control for
everything, because you may not be able to measure everything, or get people to believe your
diagram. And now this cool third thing — but sometimes it just isn't believable either.

## IDEAS

### The requirement moved, it didn't disappear

**Plain English.** You still have to close all the back doors. Just for a different variable.

**Precisely.** Instead of closing every back door from treatment to outcome, you must close
every back door from your **source of random variation** to the outcome. The hope is that this
is easier — that you're more likely to actually know all those pathways and be able to measure
them — or that if the source is truly random, there aren't any.

**Anchor image.** You didn't cancel the debt. You refinanced it, betting the new terms are
easier to service.

### Level 1 — lottery winnings and bankruptcy

**Plain English.** Everyone in the study bought a ticket. Among ticket buyers, how much you won
is genuinely random.

**Worked example.** There's a story you hear: someone wins the lottery, spends big, and goes
bankrupt because they couldn't handle the money. Does that actually happen?

**The problem:** people *choose* to play the lottery, and there could easily be a back door
between being the kind of person who plays and your chances of going bankrupt.

**The design:** compare people who **all played the lottery**, some of whom won big pots and
some small ones. Within the group of ticket holders, whether you win a lot or a little is
basically random.

```dag
node P 0 0.6 "Plays lottery" unobs
node W 1.5 0.6 "Size of win" treat
node B 3 0.6 "Bankruptcy" out
edge P W
edge W B
edge P B
note 1.5 1.5 "condition on playing; within players, win size is random"
```

Note what would *not* work: comparing lottery winners to non-players. That reopens the back
door from simply having bought a ticket. Once you know somebody already chose to buy tickets,
winning or not is a good source of random variation.

**The finding:** immediately after a big win, the chance of going bankrupt drops considerably.
But wait a couple of years and it rises sharply. It roughly cancels out — winning doesn't make
you much more or less likely to go bankrupt overall, it **pushes your bankruptcy a couple of
years into the future**.

**Where it breaks.** Barely does. There shouldn't be back doors from how much you win to
bankruptcy except through the money itself, which is the treatment. Very believable.

### Level 2 — wind direction, pollution, and driving

**Plain English.** You can't randomize smog. But you can use which way the wind is blowing.

**Worked example.** Does polluted air make people drive more? Plausibly yes — when it's smoggy
you don't want to walk, don't want to bike, maybe don't even want the bus; you want your car
with the air conditioning on. And that's a nasty cycle, because driving creates pollution.

**Why the obvious approaches fail:** you can't randomly assign more pollution to one day than
another. And comparing a polluted city to a clean one doesn't work either — maybe the polluted
city is polluted *because* people there drive more.

**The design:** stay within one city. In Beijing, wind direction strongly affects smog — blowing
one way it pushes pollution into the city, blowing the other it carries it out. Isolate the part
of pollution driven by wind direction and you have something like randomly assigned pollution.

**The back doors you have to worry about:**

| Concern | Why it's a back door | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| **Season** | Wind might blow west more in spring, east more in autumn — and people drive differently by season | Measure the season, control for it |
| **Weather** | It might rain more when the wind blows west, and rain affects driving | Control for weather the same way |

**The judgement:** wind direction isn't entirely random — it's related to other things that
relate to driving. The question is whether you can control for those. And plausibly it's easier
to close the back doors from *wind direction* to driving than from *pollution* to driving,
because pollution is also tied up with things like whether the factories were running that day.

**The finding:** when the wind blows pollution into the city, people drive a little bit more.
Which makes sense. Once season and weather are controlled for, the random-assignment assumption
is relatively believable — but you're leaning on an assumption in a way you weren't with the
lottery.

### Level 3 — Medicaid expansion and uncompensated care

**Plain English.** Compare states that expanded Medicaid to states that didn't — and assume
which ones expanded was roughly random. That's a lot to assume.

**Worked example.** **Uncompensated care** is medical care a hospital provides and never gets
paid for. That's a problem: losing money makes it harder to provide good care, and hospitals
might stop providing it altogether.

**The design:** a 2014 study uses the Medicaid expansion. Medicaid funds health care in the US,
particularly for the kinds of patients who might not be able to pay their hospital bills. The
programme expanded at a certain point, so you compare states before and after, isolating the
part of funding driven by that policy change, and see how it affects uncompensated care.

**What you must assume:** that it's basically random which states decided to expand Medicaid
and which didn't.

**Why that's hard:** the decision to expand was a **deeply political** one, and different kinds
of governments made it in different ways. Maybe you can control for which political party
controlled the state at the time, and maybe that restores the random element. But the list of
back doors you'd need to account for is considerably longer here than in the previous two
examples.

**Where it breaks.** Maybe you believe it, maybe you don't. That depends on the context and on
whether the assumptions can be justified and supported — which is exactly the point of running
through three examples rather than one.

### The general principle

**Plain English.** You can always find muddier variation. The muddier it is, the more work you
owe.

**Precisely.** These methods work **as long as the assumption that the variation is random — or
that you can close all its back doors — is believable**. That's contextual: you have to know
the setting to know whether alternative back door paths exist. You can keep reaching for
muddier and muddier sources, but the muddier they get, the more you have to do to justify
calling them random.

## TRAPS

- Finding exogenous variation means I no longer need to worry about back doors → **Actually,** the requirement moved to your source of variation. Same task, different variable, hopefully easier.
- Compare lottery winners to people who didn't play → **Actually,** that reopens the back door from choosing to buy a ticket. Everyone in the comparison must already be a player.
- Winning the lottery causes bankruptcy → **Actually,** the study found it delays bankruptcy by a couple of years rather than changing whether it happens.
- Wind direction is a natural phenomenon, so it's random → **Actually,** it correlates with season and weather, both of which affect driving. It works because those are measurable.
- Compare a polluted city to a clean one → **Actually,** the polluted city may be polluted because people there drive more, which is the thing you're trying to measure.
- A policy change is a natural experiment, so it's as good as random → **Actually,** the Medicaid expansion decision was intensely political. Policy changes are made by people, for reasons.
- If I can find any exogenous variation, my study works → **Actually,** believability is contextual. Level 1 is nearly airtight, level 3 may not convince your reader at all.

## RECALL

Q: What does finding exogenous variation actually change about the back door problem?
A: It relocates it. Instead of closing all back doors from treatment to outcome, you close all
back doors from your source of variation to the outcome — hopefully an easier job.

Q: Why can't you study lottery winnings by comparing winners to non-players?
A: Because people choose to play, so there's a back door from being the kind of person who buys
tickets to your chances of going bankrupt.

Q: What's the design that fixes it?
A: Compare people who all played, some winning big pots and some small ones. Within ticket
holders, how much you win is basically random.

Q: What did the lottery study find?
A: Bankruptcy drops considerably immediately after a big win, then rises sharply a couple of
years later. It roughly cancels out — the win pushes bankruptcy into the future rather than
changing whether it happens.

Q: Why can't you compare a polluted city to a clean one to study pollution's effect on driving?
A: The polluted city may be polluted *because* people there drive more, which is precisely the
relationship you're trying to measure.

Q: What supplies the exogenous variation in the pollution study?
A: Wind direction within one city — in Beijing, blowing one way brings pollution in, the other
way carries it out — so you isolate the part of pollution driven by wind.

Q: Name the two back doors from wind direction to driving, and how they're handled.
A: Season (wind might blow one way more in spring, another in autumn, and driving varies by
season) and weather (it might rain more with certain wind directions, and rain affects driving).
Both are measurable and controlled for.

Q: Why might wind direction be an easier variable to work with than pollution?
A: Because pollution is tied up with many other things, like whether the factories were running
that day, so its back doors are harder to enumerate and close.

Q: What is uncompensated care?
A: Medical care a hospital provides but is never paid for — a problem because losing money makes
it harder to provide good care, and hospitals may stop providing it.

Q: What assumption does the Medicaid expansion study require, and why is it the shakiest of the three?
A: That which states chose to expand Medicaid was basically random. It's shaky because that
decision was intensely political, with different governments deciding differently — so the list
of back doors to close is much longer.

Q: What's the general relationship between how muddy your variation is and what you owe?
A: The muddier the source, the more work you must do to justify calling it random. You can
always reach for muddier variation, but the burden grows.

Q: What determines whether one of these designs is believable?
A: Context. You have to know the setting well enough to know whether alternative back door
paths exist and whether you can close them.

## GLOSSARY

- **Uncompensated care** — medical care provided by a hospital that it is never paid for.
- **Natural experiment** — real-world variation that mimics random assignment without a researcher having assigned it.
- **Medicaid expansion** — a US policy change adopted by some states and not others, used as a source of variation in health-care funding.
