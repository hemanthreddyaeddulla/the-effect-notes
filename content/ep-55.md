## ONE SENTENCE

Instead of one effect for "after", estimate a **separate difference-in-differences effect for every
period** — by interacting the treated indicator with time fixed effects — which lets you watch the
effect evolve and, crucially, gives you **pre-treatment estimates that should all be zero**.

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

- Don't lump all the afters together. Compare 2011 to 2013, 2011 to 2014, 2011 to 2015 — separately.
- **Why:** treatments often do nothing at first, or do a lot then fade. Forcing one effect across all periods is arbitrary.
- **How:** take the two-way fixed effects model and **interact the treated indicator with time fixed effects**.
- **You must omit one period** as the reference — the baseline everything else is compared to.
- Graph the coefficients to see the effect's trajectory.
- **The pre-treatment coefficients are placebo tests.** They should be zero. If they aren't, prior trends is violated.
- Economists call this an **event study** — nothing to do with Chapter 17's use of the term.
- Still requires everyone to be treated at the same time.

## WHY THIS EXISTS

So far, if you had multiple periods you added time fixed effects to account for different levels in
different periods. You can go further.

**A terminology warning first.** If you're an economist you may have heard this called an **event
study** — but Chapter 17 used that term for something entirely different, an entire chapter that has
nothing to do with this method. Here it's **dynamic difference-in-differences**.

## IDEAS

### The idea

**Plain English.** Why treat every post-treatment period identically? Estimate one effect per period.

**Precisely.** You have a control group, a treated group, and a before/after comparison. But rather
than comparing all the befores to all the afters, let each after period have its own effect.

**Worked example.** The teacher training reform goes into effect in **2012**.

Basic difference-in-differences compares everything from 2008–2011 against everything from
2012–2020. Instead:

- Compare **2011 to 2013** — that's one difference-in-differences effect.
- Compare **2011 to 2014** — another.
- **2011 to 2015**, **2011 to 2016**, and so on.
- And do it with pre-periods too: what's the "effect" in **2010**? In **2009**, before the policy ever went into place?

What that gives you: the effect of the policy **one year after** implementation, **two years after**,
three years after, and so on.

**Why bother.** There's no reason to force a single treatment effect. A lot of treatments — especially
those going into effect at a particular time — take a while to do anything, or do a great deal at
first and then fade out. Insisting the same effect applies in every period is a choice with nothing
behind it.

**Anchor image.** A photograph versus a film. One tells you it happened; the other tells you how.

### Estimating it

**Plain English.** Take the two-way fixed effects model and interact the treatment indicator with
every year.

**Precisely.** Start with the two-way fixed effects model: fixed effects for group, fixed effects for
time, and a **treated** indicator equal to 1 if you're in a treated group in a post-treatment period,
and 0 otherwise — either before treatment, or in the untreated group, or both.

To make it dynamic, one change: **take that treated indicator and interact it with a set of fixed
effects for time periods.**

So you build a variable that's 1 if it's **2008 and you're in the treated group**. Note the treatment
hasn't happened yet — you still want that indicator. Then one for 2009. Then 2010. **Skip 2011.**
Then 2012, 2013, 2014, 2015.

**Why you skip one.** You need a **reference group** to compare everything against — a baseline year.
That's why there's no β₀; the coefficients run from **β₋₁** to **β₁**. This model is really doing a
two-period difference-in-differences many times over, so it needs a baseline before-period — and
that's also what the *other* pre-treatment periods get compared to.

**Reading the output.** Each β is just like a coefficient from a regular two-way fixed effects
difference-in-differences. **β₋₁** is the effect of treatment in the last period before treatment
occurred; **β₁** is the effect in the first period after. Graph them and you can see how the effect
changes over time.

### Worked example: organ donation, dynamically

**Worked example.** Back to Kessler and Roth — California changing how it asks people to become organ
donors in 2011. The static model found a **negative** effect. But maybe that averaged together several
different effects and came out negative when one of them was positive. Estimate the dynamic model and
look.

**The reference period.** The point at **quarter 2 of 2011** has **no confidence interval at all**.
That's the reference group — the baseline last pre-treatment period, the rock everything else is
compared to.

**The post-treatment periods:**

| Period | Effect |
|---|---|
| **Q3 2011** | Large negative, **−0.02**, statistically significant — the confidence interval doesn't touch zero |
| **Q4 2011** | About the same, perhaps slightly smaller |
| **Q1 2012** | Back down again, and much less precise |

So an **immediate negative drop** in organ donation rates, and not much evolution afterwards — the
effect went into place and stayed there.

**Where it breaks — the case for doing this at all.** That isn't always what happens. Some treatments
do nothing at first and slowly start to matter.

**Worked example.** When gas prices jump, people start buying more fuel-efficient cars. Compare a
place where the gas price jumped a lot against one where it stayed relatively constant, and you'd
expect the fleet to get more efficient — but **it takes a while for people to buy new cars**. So the
effect right after treatment would be very small and would grow over time.

**If you forced a single post-treatment effect, you would probably lose the effect that's actually
there and miss it entirely.**

### The pre-treatment coefficients are placebo tests

**Plain English.** You estimated an "effect of treatment" in years before any treatment. It had better
be zero.

**Precisely.** Look at quarter 4 of 2010 and quarter 1 of 2011 — both **pre-treatment** periods, and
yet you're estimating an effect of treatment for them. Why? **Placebo tests.** There shouldn't be any
effect there. If there is, it implies **prior trends has been violated**, and so parallel trends may
not hold.

**What it's actually saying:** you're comparing two periods in which treatment wasn't in effect in
either place, and looking at whether the **gap between the two groups changed**. If it did, you have a
difference-in-differences effect with no treatment behind it — meaning the two groups were changing at
different rates. Uh oh.

**And in this data, you do see a little of that.** In **quarter 1 of 2011** the estimated effect is
**statistically significantly different from zero** — an effect of treatment in a period where
treatment wasn't occurring. That should make you somewhat concerned about parallel trends.

**Where it lands.** The deviation isn't very large, even though it's significant, so Nick doesn't look
at this graph and get all that concerned about this particular study — **though it's not a great
look**. If you saw a meaningfully large estimated effect in a pre-treatment period, that would make you
very concerned about parallel trends.

### What the model buys you

- Whether the effect goes into play **immediately**.
- Whether it **stays at the same level**, as with organ donation.
- Whether it **gets stronger over time**, as with fuel-efficient cars.
- Whether it does a lot right away and then **fades out** — very common in the finance effects from the event study videos.
- **Pre-treatment placebo checks** on parallel trends.

**Where it breaks.** Just like the other difference-in-differences models, this **assumes any treatment
happened at the same time for everybody**. As shown, it cannot handle different treated groups treated
at different times.

## TRAPS

- Economists' "event study" is the same thing as Chapter 17's → **Actually,** they're entirely different methods that unfortunately share a name. This is dynamic difference-in-differences.
- Estimating one effect per period is a refinement for precision → **Actually,** it can change the answer entirely — a growing effect can vanish when averaged into a single post-treatment estimate.
- You interact the treated indicator with every time period → **Actually,** you must omit one as the reference, or there's no baseline to compare against.
- The reference period's estimate is zero → **Actually,** it has no confidence interval at all, because it's the baseline rather than an estimate.
- Pre-treatment coefficients are a byproduct → **Actually,** they're the point — placebo tests on parallel trends.
- A significant pre-treatment effect invalidates the study → **Actually,** it's a concern proportional to its size. Nick reads this one as small enough not to worry much about, while noting it isn't a good look.
- Dynamic difference-in-differences handles staggered treatment since it's period-by-period → **Actually,** it still requires everyone to be treated at the same time.

## RECALL

Q: What do economists call this model, and why is that confusing?
A: An event study — which is what Chapter 17 called an entirely different method. Here it's dynamic
difference-in-differences.

Q: What does dynamic difference-in-differences do differently?
A: Rather than comparing all pre-treatment periods to all post-treatment periods, it estimates a
separate effect for each period.

Q: With a 2012 reform, describe the comparisons being made.
A: 2011 against 2013, 2011 against 2014, 2011 against 2015, and so on — plus pre-treatment comparisons
like 2011 against 2010 and 2009.

Q: Why not force a single treatment effect?
A: Many treatments take time to do anything, or do a lot at first and fade. There's no reason to assume
the same effect applies in every period.

Q: How do you estimate it?
A: Start with the two-way fixed effects model and interact the treated indicator with a set of time
period fixed effects.

Q: Why must one period be omitted?
A: You need a reference group — a baseline before-period that every other coefficient, including the
other pre-treatment ones, is compared against.

Q: What do β₋₁ and β₁ represent?
A: The effect in the last period before treatment, and the effect in the first period after treatment.

Q: Why does the reference period appear with no confidence interval?
A: It's the baseline everything is compared to, not an estimate.

Q: What did the dynamic organ donation estimates show?
A: A large significant negative effect of about −0.02 in Q3 2011, roughly the same in Q4, and a similar
but much less precise estimate in Q1 2012 — an immediate drop that then stayed put.

Q: Give an example of an effect that grows over time.
A: When gas prices jump, people buy more fuel-efficient cars — but it takes time to buy a car, so the
effect starts small and grows.

Q: What would happen to that effect under a static model?
A: You'd probably lose it entirely — averaging a growing effect with the near-zero early periods can
make it disappear.

Q: Why are pre-treatment coefficients estimated at all?
A: They're placebo tests — they should be zero, since no treatment had occurred.

Q: What does a non-zero pre-treatment coefficient mean?
A: The gap between groups was already changing when no treatment was in effect, so the groups were
trending at different rates — prior trends violated, and parallel trends in doubt.

Q: What did the organ donation data show in Q1 2011, and how concerned should you be?
A: A statistically significant effect in a pre-treatment period. The deviation isn't large, so it isn't
alarming — though it isn't a good look. A large one would be seriously concerning.

Q: List what the dynamic model lets you see.
A: Whether the effect is immediate, whether it stays constant, whether it grows, whether it fades — and
whether the pre-treatment placebo effects are zero.

Q: What limitation does it still share with the other models?
A: It assumes all treatment happened at the same time for everybody.

## GLOSSARY

- **Dynamic difference-in-differences** — estimating a separate difference-in-differences effect for each time period; called an event study by economists.
- **Reference period** — the omitted pre-treatment period against which all other period effects are measured.
- **Pre-treatment coefficients** — estimated effects in periods before treatment, which serve as placebo tests.
