## ONE SENTENCE

The scary chapter: data doesn't fall from the sky, so before any design can work you have to ask
whether your **sample** represents the population, whether your **variable** represents the concept,
whether people **behaved differently because you were watching**, and whether you simply **mishandled
the file**.

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

- These hidden assumptions apply to **every** causal inference study — standard designs, non-standard, even experiments.
- **Sampling:** what made it into your data and what didn't? **Sampling weights** can correct known response differences.
- **Construct validity:** does your measured variable actually represent the theoretical concept? Ask someone to rate their trust 1–10 and see how many ways that fails.
- A noisy proxy means you never really controlled for the thing at all.
- **Observer effect:** people answer and behave differently because they know they're being studied — including in "neutral" government data.
- **Data handling:** coding and cleaning errors are common. Genetics renamed genes because Excel kept turning them into dates.

## WHY THIS EXISTS

This is the **scary chapter**. It covers all the hidden assumptions we tend to make even when we don't
talk about them — assumptions that apply to pretty much **every** causal inference study, whether
you're using a standard design, not using one, or even running an experiment. They're frightening, so
we don't discuss them, because we'd rather not think about them. We probably should.

The question here: **when is our data not really our data?**

Whatever research design you have, whatever theoretical model of the world, in the end you're doing an
empirical study — which means relying on data. And **data does not fall from the sky.** It has to be
created or collected by humans; there's a process by which it gets out of the world and into your
computer, and that process is fraught.

## IDEAS

### Sampling

**Plain English.** Who made it into your data, and who didn't?

**Precisely.** You probably don't have data on the entire population, and you **hope** what you have is
a random sample of it. Often it isn't. In the real world certain groups are more likely to be sampled
than others — certain people, certain countries, certain companies.

**Sampling weights** can help, and you'll often see them in high-quality government data sources, which
estimate for you how likely each person in the population was to be sampled.

**Worked example — a political poll.** You want to know whether people support politician A or
politician B. Suppose politician B is strongly supported by people who are **very suspicious of the
media**. Call people on the phone and A's supporters are far more likely to pick up and answer.

Your data says politician B is losing badly. Then voting day arrives and it turns out that wasn't right
at all — because it was a sampling issue.

**The fix, if you can estimate the response rates.** If you somehow know that A's supporters pick up
50% of the time and B's pick up 10%, you can **weight B's respondents five times as much** to even
things out and be representative of the population again.

You need someone who can actually calculate how likely each group is to respond. But if that's
available, use it — you don't want your **sampling process** driving your results rather than the actual
data.

### Construct validity: when the variables aren't the variables

**Plain English.** You have a concept in your head. You have a number in your spreadsheet. Are they the
same thing?

**Precisely.** You have a theory about how the world works, written down as a causal diagram with
theoretical variables in it. But those theoretical variables often **don't line up** with your
real-world measurements. **Construct validity** asks whether the variable in your empirical model
actually reflects the theoretical variable you had in mind when you built the theory — and when you
come back at the end to interpret the results.

This is what psychometricians are all about; a psychometrics book has far more.

**Anchor image.** A photograph of a person is not the person. Usually close enough. Sometimes not
remotely.

**Worked example — trust.** You're building a model about sociality — how much you like your neighbours
— and political preferences. **Trust** seems important: how much you trust the world might influence how
much you interact with those around you, which may relate to how you form political preferences. So
trust belongs in the model.

**But how do you measure trust?** What would that even mean? How do you put a number on it, or describe
it accurately?

So you run a survey with a trust question: *rate how much you trust the world, from one to ten.* There —
you have your trust variable, and you can control for trust.

**Except.** How well does that variable reflect the actual concept?

1. **Are people good at recording their own trust levels?** Do you know yourself well enough to say accurately, on a scale of one to ten, how much you trust the world? Someone else might be better at ranking you. And even if you're the best-placed person, you might still do it badly.
2. **Will people understand the rating scale?** What does a 2 mean? A 4? A 6? You'll be guessing — so even if you genuinely knew your own trust level, you might not write it down in the right way.
3. **People might misunderstand the question entirely.** They may not know what you mean by trust. You may have a very specific academic concept in your head that nobody else shares. They might think trust means something different. It could end up **completely unrelated** to your idea of trust.
4. **The answer might measure something else.** It could reflect how **important** somebody thinks trust is: *I don't really trust people much, but I think trust is really important, so I'll put 10.* You can't force them not to think about it that way.

**Where it breaks — the consequence.** All of these mean the variable in your data is only a **noisy
representation, if that**, of the concept in your mind. So:

- If you wanted the **effect of trust**, you got the effect of something that's maybe related to trust.
- If you wanted to **control for trust**, you controlled for something sort of related to trust while leaving out important variation — so **you haven't really controlled for trust all the way.**

### The observer effect

**Plain English.** People behave differently because they know you're watching.

**Precisely.** There are several specific names for different versions of this; gathered under one
umbrella, the idea is that people react differently and give you different data **because they know
you're collecting data on them**.

**How it shows up:**

- **Being helpful.** People guess what question you're answering and act to give the answer they think you want. *I bet this trust-and-income study wants to show that more trusting people earn more. I have a high income, so I'll say I'm very trusting — that'll help them out.*
- **Being a troublemaker.** *They want to show trust and income are positively related. I make a lot of money, so I'll tell them I'm not trusting at all.*
- **Advocating.** *I'm very trusting and I want them to conclude trust is good, so I'll pretend my income is higher than it is.*
- **Behaviour, not just answers.** In a psychological experiment you see a sign on the wall saying *charity is nice*, then leave the room and find someone with a tin cup asking for money. You think — they're probably measuring whether I give this person a dollar. And you behave differently, because you know you're being watched.
- **Just looking good.** It needn't be about the study at all. You might answer in the way that makes you feel good or look good: *I'm not a very trusting person, but I want this survey taker to think I'm nice, so — 10.*

**Where it breaks — even the neutral sources.** Data that feels entirely objective, like governmental
statistics, is often collected **by survey** and subject to the same problems. The **unemployment
rate** feels like something that came from the sky. It didn't — somebody had to **ask people whether
they're looking for work**, and people may respond differently depending on what they make of the
question.

### Data handling mistakes

**Plain English.** Sometimes you just broke the file.

**Precisely.** Data cleaning errors happen all the time. Empirical analysis requires coding, and if you
do it wrong, the variable in your data won't reflect the variable you're trying to study. There are
plenty of instances of people catching errors in code or data cleaning after the fact — and sometimes
that changes the results.

**Worked example — the most embarrassing one.** It comes from genetics. A lot of geneticists like to do
their work in **Excel**, for reasons Nick does not understand.

The problem is that Excel likes to **convert anything it thinks is a date into a date**. And a lot of
genes have names that look a great deal like dates. So you load your genetic data into Excel, it turns
your gene names into dates, and suddenly your analysis doesn't work.

**This was such a huge problem that they actually changed the names of some genes** so they'd stop
looking like dates.

They did that **rather than stop using Excel.**

## TRAPS

- These issues only matter for observational studies → **Actually,** they apply to essentially every causal inference study, experiments included.
- A random sample is the default and departures are unusual → **Actually,** in the real world certain groups are systematically more likely to be sampled.
- A poll showing a big lead is measuring opinion → **Actually,** it may be measuring who picks up the phone.
- A survey question about trust measures trust → **Actually,** it may measure self-knowledge, scale interpretation, a different concept entirely, or how important the respondent thinks trust is.
- A noisy measure of a confounder still controls for it → **Actually,** it controls for something related while leaving out important variation, so you haven't really controlled for it.
- Government statistics are objective and immune to the observer effect → **Actually,** things like the unemployment rate are collected by asking people questions.
- Data cleaning errors are rare and caught early → **Actually,** they're common, sometimes change results, and in genetics prompted the renaming of genes.

## SAY IT OUT LOUD

> This is the scary chapter — all the assumptions we quietly make and would rather not examine. And they
> apply to **everything**, including experiments.
>
> The question is: **when is your data not actually your data?**
>
> Start with **sampling**. Suppose you're polling voters, and one candidate's supporters are people who
> deeply distrust the media. Who picks up the phone? Not them. So your poll shows them getting
> destroyed — and then election day comes and you were completely wrong. You weren't measuring support.
> You were measuring who answers phones.
>
> Then **construct validity**, which is the one that really haunts me. Say your model needs **trust**.
> Fine — put a question on the survey: *rate how much you trust the world, one to ten.* Done, you've
> measured trust.
>
> Have you? Do people actually **know** how trusting they are? Does anyone know what a **6** means?
> Do they even mean the same thing by "trust" as you do? And here's my favourite failure — someone might
> answer based on how **important** they think trust is: *I don't trust anyone much, but trust matters a
> lot, so — ten.*
>
> And here's why that's not a footnote: if you were **controlling** for trust to close a back door,
> you've controlled for something vaguely trust-adjacent while leaving out the part that mattered. The
> back door is still open.
>
> Then the **observer effect** — people answer and behave differently because they know they're being
> studied. Including in data that feels totally neutral. The **unemployment rate** feels like a fact from
> the sky. It isn't. Somebody knocked on doors and asked people whether they're looking for work.
>
> And finally — sometimes you just break the data. My favourite example is genetics, where people insist
> on using **Excel**, and Excel keeps converting gene names into dates. This got so bad that they
> **renamed the genes**. Rather than stop using Excel.

## RECALL

Q: Which studies do these problems apply to?
A: Essentially all of them — standard designs, non-standard designs, and even experiments.

Q: What's the central question of this episode?
A: When is your data not really your data?

Q: What should you ask about your sampling process?
A: What made it into your data and what didn't — since you hope for a random sample of the population
but often certain groups are more likely to be sampled.

Q: What are sampling weights?
A: Estimates, often supplied with high-quality government data, of how likely each person in the
population was to be sampled, used to reweight the data.

Q: Give the political poll example.
A: If one politician's supporters are suspicious of the media, they're less likely to answer the phone,
so the poll shows them losing badly when they aren't.

Q: How would you correct that, given response rates of 50% and 10%?
A: Weight the low-response group's respondents five times as much to restore representativeness.

Q: Define construct validity.
A: Whether the variable in your empirical model actually reflects the theoretical variable you had in
mind when building the theory and interpreting the results.

Q: Which field specialises in this?
A: Psychometrics.

Q: Give four ways a "rate your trust from one to ten" question can fail.
A: People may not know their own trust level; they may not understand what the numbers mean; they may
misunderstand or differently define "trust"; and they may answer based on how important they think trust
is rather than how trusting they are.

Q: Why does a noisy measure matter when you're only using it as a control?
A: Because you're controlling for something merely related to the concept while leaving out important
variation — so you haven't really closed that back door.

Q: What is the observer effect?
A: People reacting differently and giving different data because they know they're being studied.

Q: Give three motivations behind observer effects.
A: Trying to be helpful by giving the answer they think you want; deliberately being a troublemaker;
and simply answering in whatever way makes them feel or look good.

Q: How can the observer effect change behaviour rather than answers?
A: Seeing "charity is nice" on a wall in an experiment and then encountering someone collecting money,
you might give differently because you suspect you're being measured.

Q: Why aren't government statistics immune?
A: They're often collected by survey — the unemployment rate requires asking people whether they're
looking for work.

Q: What happened with genetics and Excel?
A: Excel converts anything resembling a date into a date, and many gene names look like dates — so
badly that some genes were renamed rather than abandoning Excel.

## GLOSSARY

- **Sampling weights** — weights reflecting how likely each unit was to be sampled, used to restore representativeness.
- **Construct validity** — whether a measured variable genuinely represents the theoretical concept it stands for.
- **Observer effect** — changes in responses or behaviour caused by people knowing they are being studied.
- **Data cleaning error** — a mistake in processing data such that the analysed variable doesn't reflect the intended one.
