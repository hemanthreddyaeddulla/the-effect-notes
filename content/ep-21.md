## ONE SENTENCE

There is no such thing as *the* effect — every individual has their own treatment effect,
so any single number you estimate is an **average** of individual effects, and the whole
question becomes *which* average, over *whom*.

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

- The title of the book is a lie. Effects vary by person, context, and time.
- A cervical cancer drug can't reduce Nick's risk by 1%. He doesn't have a cervix — his risk is 0% with or without.
- Even among people who *can* get cervical cancer, effects differ by age, race, background, and baseline risk. That's **heterogeneous treatment effects**, and it's true essentially everywhere.
- Two responses: estimate the **full distribution** of effects (personalized medicine — Chapter 21), or estimate **one number** and work out what it represents.
- Any single estimate is an average of individual effects. Over everybody? Over a group? Weighted?
- **Average treatment effect (ATE)** = the average over everybody. Often described as the one you want — often it isn't.
- **Rule of thumb 1:** full randomization in a **representative** sample → ATE.

## WHY THIS EXISTS

Every chapter so far has been about identifying "the causal effect of X on Y." Time to
admit that phrase doesn't refer to anything.

Effects vary. The same treatment does different things to different people, in different
contexts, at different times. Which means once you've done all the identification work and
produced a number, you have a second question waiting: *whose* effect is this? And the
answer determines what you're allowed to do with it.

## IDEAS

### There is no *the* effect

**Plain English.** A drug doesn't have one effect. It has a different effect for every
person it's given to.

**Precisely.** Individual treatment effects vary across people. A single reported effect is
some aggregation of them.

**Anchor image.** "How long does the commute take?" There's no such thing. There's your
commute, and mine, and the one at 3am.

**Worked example.** A drug reduces the rate of cervical cancer by, say, **1% on average**.

Nick doesn't have a cervix. His chance of cervical cancer is 0%, and it will still be 0%
after taking the drug. So the drug cannot possibly reduce his risk by 1%. His individual
treatment effect is exactly **zero**.

So already there are at least two effects: one for people who have a cervix to get cancer
in, and a different one for people who don't.

But why stop there? Among people who do have a cervix, why would the effect be identical?
Older versus younger, different races, different backgrounds, different baseline risk levels.
For one person it reduces risk by nothing, for another by 1%, another by half a percent,
another by 2%.

**Every individual person has a different causal effect.**

**Where it breaks.** This is one of the things making social science and causal inference
harder than the physical sciences — you're chasing a different effect for every person.

### Heterogeneous treatment effects

**Plain English.** The name for "the treatment does different things to different people."

**Precisely.** **Heterogeneous treatment effects** exist when a treatment has different
effects for different individuals. There is essentially always at least a little heterogeneity
in any effect, anywhere.

**Where it breaks.** Sometimes you can ignore it — if you think it doesn't differ much across
people. Sometimes you need to think hard about what it means.

### Two ways to deal with it

**Plain English.** Map the whole distribution of effects, or report one number and be honest
about what it is.

**Precisely.**

1. **Estimate the full heterogeneity.** Work out who the zeros are, who the 1%s are, who the 2%s are, and describe the whole distribution — enabling statements like "here is this pill's effect *for you*." That's what **personalized medicine** is after. Methods for it wait until Chapter 21.
2. **Estimate a single value**, then determine what that value represents.

**Worked example — why the second question matters.** Your estimate for the cervical cancer
drug comes back at 1%. What is that?

- If it's the average over **everybody**, it's probably understating how good the drug is, because it's dragged down by all the Nicks whose effect is zero — and you'd never give them the pill anyway, so who cares that their effect is zero.
- If it's the average among **people with a cervix**, that's a different and more useful number. And even then, is averaging over everyone with a cervix what you want?

If it's the overall figure, you should be *more* impressed by 1%, because it's being weighed
down by all those zeros. Working out who you're getting the effect for is essential to knowing
what you can do with it.

### Every single estimate is an average

**Plain English.** Whatever number comes out, it took the true individual effects and averaged
them somehow.

**Precisely.** If you produce one value, it aggregates the true individual effects — possibly
over everyone, possibly over a particular group, possibly as a weighted average. So the
question is always: *what kind of average, and for whom?*

**Anchor image.** A photograph of a crowd taken with a long exposure. It's a real image, and
it's not any one person.

**Worked example — individual effects you can't see.**

| Person | Untreated risk | Treated risk | Individual effect |
|---|---|---|---|
| Nick | 0% | 0% | **0** |
| Second person | 5% | 3% | **−2 percentage points** |
| Third person | 0.8% | 0.3% | **−0.5 percentage points** |

**Where it breaks.** You can't observe any of these directly, because you can't observe the
same person both getting and not getting the treatment. But a method can still tell you that
your estimate averages Nick's effect with the second person's and the third's — even though
you never see any of them individually.

### The average treatment effect, and rule of thumb 1

**Plain English.** The ATE is the effect if you gave the treatment to everybody.

**Precisely.** The **average treatment effect** is the simplest and most straightforward
aggregate: the average effect across everybody. It discards the heterogeneity and gives you
one value, and that value is often well worth knowing.

**Anchor image.** The average temperature of a country. Genuinely informative. Also useless
for deciding what to wear anywhere in particular.

**Rule of thumb 1.** A **representative sample** plus **full randomization** of the treatment
within it — a randomized controlled trial, say — gives you an average treatment effect. You
have randomization, and the group you randomized within is representative of the group you want
to generalise to.

**Where it breaks — two important caveats.**

First, people will tell you the ATE is the kind of average you want. **Not always true** — it
depends how you plan to use the result. For the cervical cancer drug you wouldn't want it,
because it includes a lot of people you're certain you'll never give the pill to. You'd want the
average effect among people who can actually get cervical cancer.

Second, the ATE is genuinely hard to come by. You need randomization *within a representative
group*, which is hard even when running an RCT. A randomized trial of a **selected** group won't
give you the overall ATE. Psychology has run many experiments meant to say something about human
psychology in general, and recruited only college students. It's a real randomized experiment —
but randomized only within that group, so it isn't the average treatment effect for the human
population.

Note also that **design is not destiny**. For any given research design there are usually
several estimators available that produce different kinds of treatment effect averages. These
are rules of thumb.

## TRAPS

- The study reports "the effect", so that's what the treatment does → **Actually,** there's no such thing. It's one average of many different individual effects.
- The ATE is the gold standard → **Actually,** it depends on your use. For a cervical cancer drug it's actively misleading, because it averages in people the drug can't help.
- 1% sounds like a weak drug → **Actually,** if that's the overall average it's being dragged down by everyone whose effect is zero. Among people it can help it's larger.
- It's a randomized controlled trial, so I've got the ATE → **Actually,** only if the sample is representative. Randomizing among college students gives the effect for college students.
- Heterogeneity is a complication in unusual cases → **Actually,** essentially every effect has at least some. Sometimes you can ignore it; it's always there.
- I can measure an individual's treatment effect if my data is good enough → **Actually,** you can't observe the same person both treated and untreated. Individual effects are never directly observed.
- My design determines which average I get → **Actually,** these are rules of thumb. Most designs admit several estimators giving different averages.

## RECALL

Q: Why is there no such thing as "the effect"?
A: Because individual treatment effects vary across people, contexts, and times. Any single
number is an aggregation of many different effects.

Q: What is Nick's individual treatment effect from a cervical cancer drug, and why?
A: Zero. He doesn't have a cervix, so his risk is 0% both with and without the drug.

Q: Define heterogeneous treatment effects.
A: When a treatment has different effects for different individuals. There's essentially always
at least some heterogeneity in any effect.

Q: What are the two ways to handle heterogeneity?
A: Estimate the full distribution of individual effects (personalized medicine, Chapter 21), or
estimate a single value and determine what it represents.

Q: A drug's estimated effect is 1%. Why might that understate how good it is?
A: If it averages over everybody, it includes all the people whose effect is zero — people you'd
never treat anyway — dragging the average down.

Q: What does any single treatment effect estimate actually consist of?
A: An average of the true individual treatment effects, aggregated in some way — over everyone,
over a group, or weighted.

Q: Why can't you observe an individual treatment effect directly?
A: You can't observe the same person both getting and not getting the treatment.

Q: Define the average treatment effect.
A: The average effect among everybody — what the average effect would be if the treatment were
given to everyone.

Q: State rule of thumb 1.
A: A representative sample with full randomization of the treatment — such as a randomized
controlled trial — gives you an average treatment effect.

Q: Why is the ATE hard to come by even with a randomized trial?
A: You need randomization within a *representative* group. A trial run on a selected group gives
the effect for that group, not the population.

Q: What's wrong with a randomized psychology experiment run on college students that claims to describe human psychology?
A: The randomization only happened within college students, so you get the effect for that
group, not the average treatment effect for the human population.

Q: Is the ATE always the average you want?
A: No. It depends how you'll use the result. For the cervical cancer drug you'd want the effect
among people who can actually get cervical cancer.

Q: What does "design is not destiny" mean here?
A: For any given research design there are usually several estimators available that produce
different kinds of treatment effect average. These are rules of thumb, not laws.

## GLOSSARY

- **Treatment effect** — the change in an individual's outcome caused by the treatment.
- **Individual treatment effect** — one person's own effect; never directly observable.
- **Heterogeneous treatment effects** — different individuals having different effects from the same treatment.
- **Average treatment effect (ATE)** — the average effect across everybody.
- **Personalized medicine** — the project of estimating an individual's own treatment effect.
