## ONE SENTENCE

Two assumptions can fail — that people either side of the cutoff are genuinely comparable, and that
nobody is **manipulating** their running variable — and you check both by **running regression
discontinuity again**: once on your control variables, where you hope to find nothing, and once on
the **density** of the running variable itself, where you hope you can't spot the cutoff at all.

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

- Regression discontinuity is not a free lunch. If the assumptions fail, so does the estimate.
- **Assumption 1:** people just either side of the cutoff are comparable.
- **Check it** by re-running the design with your **control variables as the outcome**. A jump means either something else changes at the cutoff, or you got a bad draw.
- If something else changes, the design is broken. If it's bad luck, **add a control variable**.
- **Assumption 2:** nobody manipulates the running variable to get treated.
- **Check it** by binning the running variable and looking at the **counts**. You want to be unable to find the cutoff.
- Manipulation is very hard to fix.
- Two more running-variable problems: **granularity** and **heaping**.

## WHY THIS EXISTS

You knew it was coming. Regression discontinuity is **not a free lunch** — there are assumptions
required for it to work, and if they're wrong, your estimate is wrong too. It looks nice and clean,
but there are assumptions hiding under the hood.

## IDEAS

### Assumption 1: the two sides are comparable

**Plain English.** People at $11,900 and $12,100 should be alike in every way except treatment.

**Precisely.** Focus narrowly on the bandwidth and you believe you're picking out groups that are
comparable except that some got treated. Pairing incomes of $11,900 against $12,100 isn't a huge
difference, one group got the treatment and the other didn't, so you feel you can attribute the
differences to the treatment.

**But what if the two groups actually look quite different?**

**Worked example.** Among people who just barely qualify for the income poverty alleviation programme
— incomes from $12,000 down to $11,900 — **20% have a college degree**. Among people just above the
cutoff — $12,000 to $12,100 — **40%** do.

That's strange. You've claimed these groups are almost exactly the same except for treatment, and yet
their college completion rates are very different.

**Two things it could mean:**

1. **Something else changes at the cutoff.** Perhaps another policy helps you get a college degree if you earn slightly more than $12,000. Then you'd be confusing the effect of the income policy you care about with the effect of a college policy you don't. **Your research design doesn't make sense.**
2. **Bad luck.** By random sampling chance alone, you happened to draw a sample in which the two groups differ, even though on average they wouldn't.

Neither is good.

### Checking it: run regression discontinuity again

**Plain English.** Use your control variables as the outcome and hope you find nothing.

**Precisely.** This is easy — and a lot of regression discontinuity tests work this way, which is
rather neat: **you just run regression discontinuity again.**

Take everything you'd consider a back door control variable and use it as the **dependent variable**.
For the income programme: do the running variable and treatment predict your **college completion
rate**? College completion may change smoothly along the income distribution — but does it **jump** at
the $12,000 cutoff?

If it does, then it isn't just the treatment jumping at that point. Do this with **all** your control
variables — anything the treatment shouldn't affect. **You hope to find no effect for any of them.**
An income poverty programme shouldn't affect whether you have a college degree.

**Anchor image.** Weighing the empty scale before you weigh the parcel.

### Worked example: government transfers and political support

**Precisely.** From a paper called *Government Transfers and Political Support*, on a poverty
alleviation programme: earn just below a certain amount and the government sends you a check.

**The main question:** does getting that check make you more supportive of the government?

**The main result:** support for the government **jumps quite a bit right at the cutoff**. Just on the
untreated side, support is considerably lower and changes smoothly along the income axis — and as soon
as you dip below the dashed line, government support jumps immediately. Exactly as you'd expect if the
treatment is responsible.

**The placebo test:** produce the very same graph, but with **covariates** as the dependent variable
instead of the outcome. Two were used, neither of which a government check should affect:

| Covariate | What the graph shows |
|---|---|
| **Mean age in the household** | Changes smoothly over the income distribution, with slightly higher ages at slightly higher incomes — which makes sense, since you earn more as you get older. **No jump at the cutoff.** |
| **Mean education in the household** | Barely related to income in this narrow range at all, and **no real jump at the cutoff**. |

Things look pretty much the same either side of the dashed line in both cases — **exactly what you
want to see.** A jump would indicate a difference across the cutoff you'd be at risk of confusing with
the treatment.

### What to do if you do find a difference

**Plain English.** Depends on whether you think it's real or random.

| What you think it reflects | What to do |
|---|---|
| **Something else going on at the same time** — another policy at the same cutoff, or manipulation | Be **really concerned about your research design**. It simply doesn't work if the people either side aren't truly comparable. |
| **A bad random draw** — you're confident the assumptions hold, but this sample happens to have older people on one side | **Add a control variable.** There's nothing wrong with adding controls to a regression discontinuity specification, and it can even out sampling differences across the cutoff. |

**The general principle.** This kind of placebo test is a common and excellent way to check
plausibility: **think of parts of your data where the regression discontinuity effect should not be
there, and see whether it is.**

### Assumption 2: nobody manipulates the running variable

**Plain English.** The running variable can relate to all sorts of things — it just can't be something
people are gaming.

**Precisely.** The running variable needs to be **basically randomly assigned, give or take**. It's
fine that it's related to a lot of back door stuff — it isn't exogenous. What matters is that **people
aren't trying to change their running variable** in order to land on a particular side.

**Worked example — manipulation.** You know that earning less than $12,000 a year gets you a check in
the mail. You also know you're on track to earn **$12,100** this year, which is too much to qualify.
So why not **work a little less on purpose** so you get the check?

That makes complete sense for you, and it destroys the design. You weren't randomly assigned to a side
— **you chose** to be below the cutoff because you knew that's where the treatment was.

### Checking it: look at the density

**Plain English.** Bin the running variable, count people per bin, and hope you can't tell where the
cutoff is.

**Precisely.** Check whether the **running variable itself is smoothly distributed** at the cutoff. If
there's almost nobody just above the cutoff and everybody just below it, where the treatment is, that
implies a lot of people who were just above **nudged their way onto the treated side**.

The basic method: split the running variable into **bins** and count how many people are in each.

**What you want to see:** you **can't really find where the cutoff is.** You certainly don't want to be
missing a lot of people just on one side and finding them just on the other. You want the distribution
to look **exactly as it would if nobody cared about the running variable at all.**

In the example graph, it looks good — nothing suggests anyone is moving themselves from one side to
the other.

**Where it breaks.** If you do find manipulation, it's much harder to deal with. There's **not a whole
lot you can do** in that circumstance, though there are some tricks not covered here.

### Two more running-variable problems

**Granularity.** You want a nice, smoothly changing running variable. What if it's only measured in big
chunks? Suppose you qualify at $12,000, but the data doesn't record exactly what you made — only that
you earned somewhere between **$12,000 and $13,000**, perhaps because that's how it was reported, maybe
for anonymity. That forces you to compare people earning $13,000 against people earning $10,000, and
those are **too far apart** to reasonably call comparable just because they fell on opposite sides of a
line.

**Heaping.** What if some people **round their numbers**? In income data some report $11,932, $11,936,
$12,037 — nicely distributed. Others phone it in: *I earned eleven thousand. I earned twelve thousand.*
So you get a few people at precise values and a **lot lumped at the round values**.

That's a problem **if those are different kinds of people** — if the ones who round differ from the
ones who report precisely, that introduces back doors into your analysis.

For both granularity and heaping, the chapter has more on what to do.

## TRAPS

- Regression discontinuity's assumptions are so mild they don't need checking → **Actually,** it's not a free lunch, and both assumptions can fail in ways that invalidate the estimate.
- A jump in a covariate at the cutoff means your treatment affected it → **Actually,** for covariates the treatment shouldn't affect, it means either something else changes there or you drew a bad sample.
- Any covariate imbalance kills the design → **Actually,** if it's genuinely a bad random draw you can simply add a control variable.
- Adding controls to a regression discontinuity is cheating → **Actually,** there's nothing wrong with it, and it can even out sampling differences across the cutoff.
- The running variable must be exogenous → **Actually,** it can relate to all sorts of back door variables. What it can't be is manipulated by the people in your data.
- Manipulation shows up in the outcome → **Actually,** you detect it in the **density** of the running variable — a hole on one side of the cutoff and a pile on the other.
- Coarsely measured running variables are just less precise → **Actually,** granularity can force comparisons between $13,000 and $10,000, which breaks comparability outright.

## RECALL

Q: What's the first assumption regression discontinuity requires?
A: That people just either side of the cutoff are comparable except that some got the treatment.

Q: Give the college degree example of that failing.
A: 20% of those just below the cutoff have college degrees against 40% of those just above — a large
difference between supposedly identical groups.

Q: What two things could that difference mean?
A: Something else changes at the cutoff — such as another policy — or you simply drew an unlucky sample.

Q: How do you check for it?
A: Run regression discontinuity again using your control variables as the dependent variable, and hope
you find no jump.

Q: Which variables should you use for that check?
A: Anything you'd consider a back door control — anything the treatment shouldn't affect.

Q: What was the main finding in the government transfers study?
A: Support for the government jumps sharply right at the cutoff, with smoothly changing and lower
support just above it.

Q: What two covariates were used in the placebo check, and what did they show?
A: Mean household age and mean household education. Age rises gently with income with no jump at the
cutoff; education is barely related to income and also shows no jump.

Q: What do you do if a covariate does jump and you think something real is happening at the cutoff?
A: Be seriously concerned about the research design — it doesn't work if the two sides aren't
comparable.

Q: What do you do if you think it's just a bad random draw?
A: Add a control variable. There's nothing wrong with adding controls to a regression discontinuity
specification, and it can even out sampling differences.

Q: State the general principle behind these placebo tests.
A: Find parts of your data where the regression discontinuity effect should not exist, and check whether
it does.

Q: What's the second assumption?
A: That people aren't manipulating their running variable in order to land on a particular side of the
cutoff.

Q: Does the running variable have to be exogenous?
A: No — it can be related to plenty of back door variables. It just mustn't be something people
deliberately change.

Q: Give the manipulation example.
A: Knowing you get a check below $12,000 and being on track for $12,100, you work a little less on
purpose to qualify — so you chose your side rather than being assigned to it.

Q: How do you check for manipulation?
A: Bin the running variable, count observations per bin, and see whether the distribution is smooth
across the cutoff.

Q: What exactly do you hope to see?
A: That you can't find where the cutoff is — a distribution looking just as it would if nobody cared
about the running variable.

Q: What can you do if you find manipulation?
A: Not a great deal — it's much harder to deal with, though some tricks exist.

Q: What is granularity, and why is it a problem?
A: A running variable measured only in coarse chunks — such as knowing only that income fell between
$12,000 and $13,000 — which forces comparisons between people who are too far apart to be comparable.

Q: What is heaping, and when does it cause trouble?
A: Some people report precise values while others round to whole thousands, piling observations at round
numbers. It's a problem if the people who round differ systematically from those who don't, since that
introduces back doors.

## GLOSSARY

- **Covariate balance test** — re-running the design with control variables as the outcome, expecting no discontinuity.
- **Manipulation** — units deliberately altering their running variable to land on the treated side of the cutoff.
- **Density test** — binning the running variable and checking that its distribution is smooth across the cutoff.
- **Granularity** — a running variable recorded only in coarse chunks, preventing close comparisons.
- **Heaping** — observations piling up at round values because some people round their reported numbers.
