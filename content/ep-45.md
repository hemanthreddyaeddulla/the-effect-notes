## ONE SENTENCE

If you can't measure a confounder but it sits inside a broader category you *can* identify — like
your birthplace sitting inside **you** — then controlling for that category controls for
everything inside it, which is why controlling for the individual strips out all the **between**
variation and leaves only the **within**.

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

- New problem: the diagram tells you what to control for, and **you don't have the data**.
- **The insight:** if an unmeasured variable is constant within a broader category you *do* observe, control for the category and you've controlled for the variable.
- Requires **panel data** — the same individuals measured repeatedly over time. People, companies, countries.
- Controlling for the individual controls for **everything unique to them that doesn't change over time**.
- This splits variation in two: **between** (differences across individuals) and **within** (changes for one individual over time).
- Fixed effects **removes all the between variation**, leaving only within.
- So back doors made of time-constant things close — even ones you could never measure. Time-varying back doors stay open.

## WHY THIS EXISTS

This starts a whole section of the book on research designs that solve one particular problem.

So far it's been ways to control for variables — regression, matching, drawing causal diagrams that
tell you what needs controlling. And there's a very, very common problem with all of it, especially
in social science: you draw the diagram, it tells you what to control for, you go to set up your
regression or your matching estimator — and you realise you **don't actually have data on it**.

Thankfully there are methods that let you estimate a causal effect even when you can't measure
everything you need to control for. They only work in particular settings, so you have to get a
little lucky and be clever about applying them. But if you can apply them, they work — and depending
on who you ask, some people think they're the **only** good way to get causal effects from
observational data when you can't run an experiment.

## IDEAS

### The key insight

**Plain English.** You can't measure the thing — but you can measure the box it lives in.

**Precisely.** If you need to control for a variable, and you can't measure it, but you **do** have
data on a broader category that the variable sits inside — **and that category explains all the
variation in that variable** — then you can control for the broader category and it controls for
everything inside it.

**Anchor image.** You can't weigh the individual ingredients after they're baked in. You can weigh
the cake.

**Worked example.** You have **panel data**: the same measurement on the same people over time — you
this year, last year, the year before. It works for individual people, companies, countries: New
Zealand this year, New Zealand last year, and so on.

Say the variable you need to control for is your **birthplace**. Your birthplace both affects whether
you were treated and affects your outcome — a back door. And you don't have data on it.

But you do have data on **you**. And your birthplace is **fixed within the category of you**. So if
you could somehow control for your identity, you'd be controlling for everything unique to you that
doesn't change over time — including your birthplace, even though you can't measure it.

**That's fixed effects:** add a control for the individual, and in doing so control for all the
things unique to that individual that are constant over time.

### Worked example: chancellor visits and trade

**Plain English.** Bundle everything unchanging about a country into one variable called "country."

**Worked example.** Does a recent visit from the Chancellor of Germany make your country trade more
with Germany? Plenty of other things determine how much you trade:

| Variable | Why it's a back door | Can you measure it? |
|---|---|---|
| **Geography** | Closer to Germany, more trade | Sort of |
| **History with Germany** | Prior trading history affects both whether the chancellor visits *and* future trade | How do you put a number on it? |
| **Culture of the population** | A German-speaking country, or shared heritage, trades more | How do you measure a population's culture? |
| **Current politics** | Is your country angry at Germany or friendly with it? | Maybe |

Several of those are genuinely hard to measure. But notice something: some of them are **constant
within country over time**.

Geography isn't changing much — Austria has had the same geography for decades. History with Germany
is an ever-evolving thing, but go back far enough and the history you had a hundred years ago isn't
changing from year to year.

So take all of those and bundle them into a single variable: **country**.

```dag
node C 0 0 "Country" unobs
node V 1.6 0.9 "Chancellor visit" treat
node T 3.2 0.9 "Trade with Germany" out
node P 1.6 -0.3 "Current politics"
edge C V
edge C T
edge C P
edge P V
edge P T
edge V T
note 1.6 1.7 "geography + history + culture, all constant within country"
```

Move between countries and these things change — different countries have different geographies,
different histories with Germany. But **stick with a single country and there's no variation left in
them**. Austria's geography doesn't vary from year to year, at least not on the time scale of your
data.

That diagram is much easier to work with. Control for **which country you are** and you close all
those back doors at once.

**Where it breaks.** It doesn't close everything. **Current politics** does change from year to
year — Austria's relationship with Germany shifts — so that back door stays open and you'd need to
control for it separately. Fixed effects gets a lot of your work done; it doesn't finish the job.

### Between and within variation

**Plain English.** Differences *across* people versus changes *within* one person over time.

**Precisely.** Controlling for the individual separates your variation into two forms:

- **Between variation** — variation between individuals. Austria has different geography than England does.
- **Within variation** — variation within an individual over time. Austria's political relationship with Germany changes from year to year.

A variable can have **both**. Politics has a between component (Austria differs from England) and a
within component (Austria changes year to year).

**The key of fixed effects:** when you control for the individual, you control away **all the between
variation**, and only the **within** variation is left.

So if your back doors are made of between variation, they close. If they're within, they don't.

### Worked example: the exercise table

**Plain English.** Split each person's numbers into "their average" and "how they varied around it."

**Worked example.** Exercise ratings on a 1–10 scale:

| | 2019 | 2020 | **Average** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **You** | 5 | 7 | **6** |
| **Me** | 4 | 3 | **3.5** |

**The between variation** is the difference in averages: your 6 against my 3.5. You got more exercise
on average than I did.

**The within variation** is each year's value minus that person's own mean:

| | 2019 | 2020 |
|---|---|---|
| **You** | 5 − 6 = **−1** | 7 − 6 = **+1** |
| **Me** | 4 − 3.5 = **+0.5** | 3 − 3.5 = **−0.5** |

Fixed effects **gets rid of the difference between 6 and 3.5** entirely. It looks only at the within
variation — only at the fact that in 2020 you were exercising more than you typically do, and I was
exercising less than I typically do.

### The graphical version, and the punchline

**Plain English.** Give every person their own origin, then look only at movement around it.

**Worked example.** Data on you and me across several years: exercise hours per week, and colds per
year. Now give each person their **own set of axes**, centred on their own average exercise hours and
average colds — a personal "home location."

Every point is now read relative to that person's axes. Some years you exercised more than you
typically do, some years less; some years you had more colds than usual, some fewer.

Take a point that's a fairly **middling** number of exercise hours overall — it might be a
particularly **low** number *for you*. It's a completely different comparison depending on whether
you're looking at the overall variation (between plus within) or relative to your own average
(within only).

- Strip out the individual points and compare only your average against mine → that isolates the **between** variation.
- Look at everything relative to the individual axes → that isolates the **within** variation.

**The punchline.** In the overall graph the relationship has an **upward** slope. Look just at Nick's
own data, relative to his own average, and there's a vague **downward** slope. Between and within can
point in opposite directions.

## TRAPS

- Fixed effects lets you control for things you can't measure → **Actually,** only things that are *constant within* the category you control for. Time-varying unmeasured confounders remain a problem.
- Fixed effects closes every back door → **Actually,** current politics changes year to year, so that one stays open and needs its own control.
- Fixed effects requires knowing what the unmeasured variables are → **Actually,** you don't need to name them. Anything constant within the individual is absorbed.
- Between and within variation are two ways of describing the same thing → **Actually,** they're different components. A variable can have both, and they can point in opposite directions.
- If the overall relationship slopes up, the within-person relationship does too → **Actually,** the exercise-and-colds graph slopes up overall and down within one person.
- Any dataset works for fixed effects → **Actually,** you need panel data — the same individuals observed repeatedly over time.

## RECALL

Q: What problem does this section of the book address?
A: That your causal diagram tells you to control for something you don't have data on.

Q: State the key insight of fixed effects.
A: If an unmeasured variable is constant within a broader category you *do* observe, controlling for
that category controls for everything inside it.

Q: What kind of data does fixed effects require?
A: Panel data — the same individuals measured repeatedly over time, whether people, companies, or
countries.

Q: How does controlling for your identity control for your birthplace?
A: Because your birthplace is fixed within the category of you, so a control for you absorbs everything
unique to you that doesn't change over time.

Q: In the chancellor-visit example, which confounders get bundled into "country"?
A: Geography, history with Germany, and the culture of the population — all effectively constant within
a country over the timescale of the data.

Q: Which confounder in that example is *not* closed by country fixed effects, and why?
A: Current politics, because a country's relationship with Germany changes from year to year, so it has
within variation.

Q: Define between variation.
A: Variation between individuals — Austria having different geography than England.

Q: Define within variation.
A: Variation within an individual over time — Austria's political relationship with Germany changing
year to year.

Q: Can a variable have both?
A: Yes. Politics differs across countries *and* changes within a country over time.

Q: What does controlling for the individual do to these two components?
A: It removes all the between variation, leaving only the within variation.

Q: You: 5 in 2019 and 7 in 2020. Me: 4 and 3. Give the averages and the between variation.
A: Your average is 6, mine is 3.5. The between variation is that difference — you exercise more on
average.

Q: Give the within variation for both people.
A: You: −1 in 2019 and +1 in 2020. Me: +0.5 in 2019 and −0.5 in 2020.

Q: What does fixed effects do with the 6-versus-3.5 difference?
A: Discards it entirely, looking only at how each person varied around their own average.

Q: In the graphical version, what does giving each person their own axes accomplish?
A: Every point is measured relative to that person's own averages, so you're seeing only within
variation.

Q: What's the punchline about slopes in the exercise-and-colds graph?
A: The overall relationship slopes upward, but within Nick's own data it slopes vaguely downward —
between and within can point in opposite directions.

Q: Does fixed effects require you to know what the unmeasured confounders are?
A: No. Anything constant within the individual is absorbed whether or not you can name or measure it.

## GLOSSARY

- **Fixed effects** — controlling for the individual, thereby absorbing everything constant within that individual over time.
- **Panel data** — repeated observations of the same individuals over time.
- **Between variation** — differences across individuals.
- **Within variation** — changes within a single individual over time.
- **Time-constant variable** — a characteristic that doesn't change within an individual, and is therefore absorbed by fixed effects.
