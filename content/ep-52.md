## ONE SENTENCE

Difference-in-differences rests entirely on **parallel trends** — that the gap between treated and
control would have stayed the same without treatment — and the maddening thing is that **you can
never check it**, because it's a claim about something you don't observe.

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

- **Parallel trends:** the change over time you see in the control group is the change you'd have seen in the treated group had it not been treated.
- Stated precisely: the **gap between the two groups would have stayed the same** from before to after. Baseline differences are fine — the *gap* must be stable.
- **You cannot check it**, because it's about a counterfactual you never observe.
- Failure mode 1: the groups were **naturally trending together or apart** — the US and China.
- Failure mode 2: **something else happened to the control group** — a free lunch programme.
- Make it plausible three ways: **know your context**, **pick similar groups**, and **check prior trends**.
- Prior trends can look **parallel** (reassuring) or **converging** (a warning).

## WHY THIS EXISTS

This is the big assumption you have to make for difference-in-differences to work — and it's really
annoying, because **you can't actually check it**. You always have to wonder whether the whole study
rests on something untrue.

## IDEAS

### What parallel trends is

**Plain English.** Whatever happened to the control group over time is exactly what would have
happened to the treated group.

**Precisely.** Look at the treated group's before-to-after difference. What does it represent? The
effect of treatment, plus whatever change would have occurred anyway from the mere passage of time —
things change naturally even when no treatment goes into effect, and you don't want to confuse that
with the effect.

So you look at the control group, see how *they* changed over time, and **assume that change is the
change you'd have seen in the treated group had they not been treated**. That's what lets you
subtract it out and be left with the treatment effect.

**That assumption is parallel trends.** It's hidden inside the subtraction, and if it's false — if
the change the treated group would have experienced differs from the control group's — the whole
thing falls apart.

**The more specific statement:** you're assuming that **the gap between the treated and control
groups would have stayed the same from before to after**, had the treatment not occurred.

**Anchor image.** Two escalators side by side. It's fine that one starts higher. What you need is
that they'd have risen at the same rate.

**Where it breaks — and this is the freeing part.** It's **okay that the treated and control groups
are different** in some way. Baseline differences are permitted. What must hold is that **the amount
by which they differ** would have stayed the same.

### Failure mode 1: naturally converging or diverging

**Plain English.** Two things already moving apart will keep moving apart, treatment or not.

**Worked example.** You want the effect of a policy the United States implemented at a particular
time — say a new tax policy — on economic growth. What can you use as a control? Let's use **China**.

But China has already been growing much faster than the United States. You'd expect China to grow
more than the US **even if the policy hadn't changed**, simply because that's what's been happening
for decades. So you'd expect parallel trends to fail: the gap between them changes regardless of any
policy.

### Failure mode 2: something else happened to the control group

**Plain English.** Your control group had its own treatment, and now you're measuring two things.

**Worked example.** A school introduces a teacher training reform, and you take a different school as
control. But what if that other school **introduced free school lunches** at the same time?

Now something changed over time for them too, and you can't tell the difference between the policy
that happened here and the policy that happened there. The treated students didn't get a free lunch,
so what you're seeing in the control group isn't just changes over time — it's **changes over time
plus the effect of a free lunch**. You can't use it as a comparison group.

### Making parallel trends plausible

**Plain English.** Three things you can do, none of which is proof.

**1 — Know your context really well.** You should be pretty certain, before running the analysis,
that there's nothing else changing for the control group at the same time the treatment goes into
effect. Did that comparison school district start a free lunch programme the same year the treated
school started its teacher training reform? You want to be able to say **no**.

**2 — Pick groups that are comparable.** Choose groups similar enough overall that it isn't
unrealistic to say they'd have evolved the same way.

**Note the subtlety:** this is **not actually a requirement** of difference-in-differences — baseline
differences are fine, as long as the gap stays constant. But it's far more **plausible and
believable** that two very similar groups would have evolved the same way absent treatment. If you
want to be advanced about it, you can use **matching procedures** to pick untreated comparison groups
that look very similar to the treated group.

**3 — Check prior trends.** You can't observe what would have happened to the treated group without
treatment — that's the whole difficulty — so you can't check parallel trends directly. But you **can
look at other pre-treatment periods**.

**Worked example.** The teacher reform goes into effect in **2012**, and you have data on both schools
from **2005 to 2011**. Look at their maths scores over that window. If both schools appear to be
heading in the same direction — scores improving at both, at roughly the same rate — that's a
plausible indication they'd have continued to improve at the same rate after treatment.

**Where it breaks.** It's not a guarantee. You could still have something like the free lunch
programme starting in 2012. But if you don't think that's what's going on, prior trends give you a
decent sense that the two trends would have continued in the same way.

### Reading prior trends

**Plain English.** Look only at the data left of the dashed line, and ask whether the gap is stable.

**Worked example — parallel prior trends.** Both groups increase over time, and before the dashed
line the **rates of increase look similar**. They finish the last pre-treatment period at roughly the
**same gap** they had in the very first period observed. That's a decent hint they'd have continued
to trend the same way and stayed equally apart afterwards.

**Worked example — converging prior trends.** The data just left of the dashed line is **very close
together**, and had been getting closer over time compared to the start of the period, where the
groups were quite far apart. The control group was **trending up more quickly** than the treated
group.

So you'd expect the control group to keep trending up more rapidly in the post-treatment period. Its
time effect is **bigger and more positive** than the treated group's time effect. You can't claim the
control group's change is the time effect the treated group would have experienced, so you can't
subtract one from the other and expect it to work.

## TRAPS

- Parallel trends requires the two groups to be at the same level → **Actually,** baseline differences are fine. What must be stable is the **gap**.
- You can test parallel trends with prior trends → **Actually,** prior trends are evidence, not a test. The assumption is about a counterfactual you never observe.
- Parallel prior trends guarantee parallel trends → **Actually,** a free lunch programme could still start exactly when treatment does.
- Similar groups are required for difference-in-differences → **Actually,** they're not required, just more believable.
- If the control group changed a lot, parallel trends failed → **Actually,** a large change is fine — that's the time effect you're subtracting. What matters is whether it matches what the treated group would have experienced.
- Converging prior trends just mean noisy data → **Actually,** they mean the control group's time effect is larger than the treated group's, so the subtraction is invalid.

## SAY IT OUT LOUD

> Difference-in-differences hangs on one assumption, and it's a genuinely uncomfortable one, because
> **you can never check it.**
>
> It's called **parallel trends**, and here's what it says. When I take the control group's change over
> time and subtract it from the treated group's, I'm assuming that the control group's change **is**
> the change the treated group would have had if nobody had treated them. Both groups would have
> evolved the same way.
>
> Said more precisely: the **gap** between the two groups would have stayed the same from before to
> after. And notice — it's completely fine that the groups are at different levels. One school can be
> better than the other. What has to hold is that the **distance between them** wouldn't have moved.
>
> Two ways this breaks. First, the groups might just be **naturally converging or diverging**. If I
> study a US tax policy and use **China** as my control, China's been growing faster than the US for
> decades. It'll keep growing faster whether or not I pass a tax law. The gap was always going to move.
>
> Second, **something else happens to the control group**. My treated school runs a teacher training
> reform. My control school starts **free school lunches** the same year. Now their change over time
> isn't "what happens over time" — it's "what happens over time, plus free lunch." Useless as a
> comparison.
>
> So what can you do? Three things. **Know your setting** well enough to be confident nothing else
> changed. **Pick similar groups** — not required, but far more believable. And **check prior trends**:
> if the reform starts in 2012 and I've got data from 2005, I can look at whether the two schools were
> already moving in parallel.
>
> And that last one has two looks. If both lines climb at the same rate and end the pre-period the same
> distance apart as they started — good sign. But if they've been **converging**, getting closer year
> after year, then the control group is already climbing faster. Subtracting its change from the
> treated group's will overcorrect, and the design doesn't work.

## RECALL

Q: State parallel trends informally.
A: The change over time observed in the control group is the change the treated group would have
experienced had it not been treated.

Q: State it more precisely.
A: The gap between the treated and control groups would have stayed the same from before to after, had
treatment not occurred.

Q: Is it a problem if the two groups differ at baseline?
A: No. Baseline differences are fine — it's the *amount* by which they differ that must stay constant.

Q: Why can't you check parallel trends?
A: It's a claim about what would have happened to the treated group without treatment, which you never
observe.

Q: Give failure mode 1 with its example.
A: The groups were naturally trending together or apart. Using China as a control for a US policy fails
because China had already been growing much faster for decades.

Q: Give failure mode 2 with its example.
A: Something else changed for the control group at the same time. If the comparison school started free
school lunches when the treated school started its teacher reform, their change reflects both.

Q: What's the first way to make parallel trends plausible?
A: Know your context well enough to be confident nothing else changed for the control group at the time
treatment began.

Q: What's the second, and why isn't it strictly required?
A: Pick comparable groups. It isn't required because baseline differences are permitted — but it makes
the assumption far more believable.

Q: How could you pick comparable groups more rigorously?
A: Use matching procedures to select untreated groups that look very similar to the treated group.

Q: What's the third way, and what does it actually look at?
A: Check prior trends — look at multiple pre-treatment periods to see whether the two groups were
already moving in parallel.

Q: Give the prior trends example.
A: A teacher reform starting in 2012 with data from 2005 to 2011; if both schools' maths scores improve
at roughly the same rate over that window, they'd plausibly have continued to.

Q: Does checking prior trends prove parallel trends?
A: No. Something like a free lunch programme could still begin exactly when treatment does.

Q: Describe parallel prior trends on a graph.
A: Both groups increase, at similar rates, ending the last pre-treatment period at roughly the same gap
they had at the first period observed.

Q: Describe converging prior trends and what they imply.
A: The groups start far apart and get steadily closer before treatment, meaning the control group is
trending up faster. Its time effect is larger than the treated group's, so subtracting it won't work.

## GLOSSARY

- **Parallel trends** — the assumption that the treated and control groups' gap would have remained constant absent treatment.
- **Prior trends** — pre-treatment periods examined to see whether the groups were already moving in parallel.
- **Converging trends** — groups growing closer together before treatment, indicating unequal time effects.
