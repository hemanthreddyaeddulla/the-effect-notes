## ONE SENTENCE

You can never see causation in data — only that two variables are related — so
identification works by **listing every other reason** they might be related and killing
those reasons one at a time, until what survives can only be the effect you're after.

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

- Causality here means: if you reached in and changed X, would Y change as a result?
- Data doesn't show you that. It shows you conditional distributions — that Y's average differs across values of X.
- So the working question becomes: **what are all the reasons these two could be related?** Then isolate the one you want.
- **Randomization** is the cleanest tool, because assignment by coin flip can't be caused by anything else in the system.
- **Conditioning** on a variable is another, and later episodes add many more.
- You've **identified** the effect when every alternative explanation is either ruled out or removed, and a relationship still remains.
- The alternatives don't disprove your effect. They just have to be cleared away before you can see it.

## WHY THIS EXISTS

The DGP is out there, and any set of observations is consistent with lots of different
ones. That's a problem, because the thing you can actually see — that X and Y move
together — is exactly the thing that every one of those processes predicts.

So identification can't work by staring harder at the relationship. It has to work by
elimination. Enumerate the other stories, dispose of them, and see what's left standing.

## IDEAS

### What data shows you versus what you want

**Plain English.** You want to know whether changing X changes Y. What you observe is
that X and Y tend to go together.

**Precisely.** The causal question is interventional: reach in, change the value of X,
does Y change as a result? What data gives you is a conditional distribution — at
different values of X you get different conditional means of Y. That, by itself, doesn't
say why the relationship exists.

**Anchor image.** Two people always leaving a building at the same time. You can see the
co-timing. You cannot see whether one is waiting for the other.

### The list of alternatives

**Plain English.** Before you claim an effect, write down every other reason the
relationship could show up.

**Precisely.** Enumerating alternative explanations converts a vague worry into a
checklist. Each entry is a data generating process that would produce your observed
relationship without the causal effect you care about — and each one tells you something
specific your analysis has to handle.

**Worked example.** The classic econometrics question: does more schooling raise your
earnings? In the data, people with more education do earn more on average. Reasons that
could be true other than education causing earnings:

- People who were going to be **better workers anyway** choose to get more education.
- People who live in **richer areas** tend to get more education *and* earn more because of where they are.
- People with a **natural aptitude** for a highly-paid field — you'd have made a good engineer regardless — think "engineering school sounds great" and go, so the schooling tags along with the aptitude.

```dag
node A 1 0 "Ability / area / aptitude" unobs
node E 0 1 "Education" treat
node W 2 1 "Earnings" out
edge A E
edge A W
edge E W "the effect you want"
note 1 1.85 "each alternative is another arrow you must deal with"
```

**Where it breaks.** Crucially, none of this means education *doesn't* raise earnings. The
effect may be entirely real. It just means it's buried among other things, and you have
to dig through them to pull out the part you want.

### Randomization removes alternatives by construction

**Plain English.** If a coin decided who got the treatment, then nothing about the people
could have decided it.

**Precisely.** Explicit randomization severs the link between treatment assignment and
everything else. Whatever else varies across people cannot have influenced who got
treated, because a random device did. That's the entire purpose of randomizing.

**Anchor image.** Dealing cards from a shuffled deck rather than letting players pick.
Nobody can claim the good hands went to the people who wanted them.

**Worked example.** Suppose you could randomly assign how much education people get.
Now revisit the alternatives: could people who'd have earned more anyway have chosen more
education? No — that never happened in your data. The *only* reason anyone got more
education is that you assigned it, and you assigned it randomly, which has nothing to do
with what their earnings would have been. The alternative is gone, not adjusted for.

**Where it breaks.** Randomization is one route, not the only one. Conditioning on a
variable — the conditional conditional distributions of Ep 8 — closes off alternatives
too, and much of the rest of the series is other methods for doing the same job when you
can't randomize.

### Worked all the way through: the lamp

**Plain English.** Take a relationship you're sure about, and see how many alternatives
still need killing.

**Worked example.** Does pulling the string cause the lamp to turn on? Collect data: pull,
light on. Pull, off. Pull, on again. Strong relationship.

Identification means not taking that at face value. So:

**Alternative 1 — the pulls are timed.** Maybe Nick isn't causing anything; maybe he's
timing his pulls to moments the light was going to change anyway, using some other
knowledge about the lamp. That would produce this exact data with no causal effect at all.

*How to kill it:* hand the decision to a coin. Heads, pull; tails, don't. Flip — heads,
pull, light changes. Flip again — heads, pull, light changes. Now the timing wasn't Nick's
choice, it was the coin's, and the coin has no knowledge of the lamp. The relationship
survives, which is evidence the string is doing the work.

**Alternative 2 — wrong mechanism.** Maybe the string controls all the electricity in the
house. The string genuinely causes the lamp to change, but by cutting power to everything,
which is a different process than the one you claimed.

*How to kill it:* pull the string and check whether other things in the house switch on and
off at the same time. They don't. Alternative pushed aside.

**Where it breaks.** This is a **lamp**. If the alternatives run to two for a lamp, imagine
the list for education and earnings — you could sit and generate them all day. The list
gets longer as the setting gets more complex, and that's the actual job.

### When you're done

**Plain English.** Alternatives all cleared, relationship still there — now you've got
something.

**Precisely.** You've **identified** the causal effect when you can show that in the data
you're analysing, every alternative explanation has either been ruled out as not happening
or had its influence removed by conditioning or design — and a relationship remains. At
that point you know your analysis answers your research question.

**Anchor image.** A police lineup. You don't prove who did it by pointing. You clear
everyone who couldn't have.

**Where it breaks.** "Every alternative explanation" is doing enormous work in that
sentence — you can only eliminate the ones you thought of. Most of the remaining videos in
the series exist because doing this properly, in real settings, is hard and situation-specific.

## TRAPS

- Finding an alternative explanation means my hypothesis is wrong → **Actually,** it means the effect is mixed in with other things. Education may well raise earnings; you just can't see it yet.
- The relationship is very strong, so it's probably causal → **Actually,** strength is no defence. The timed-pulls story produces a near-perfect relationship with zero causal effect.
- Controlling for enough variables gets you to causation → **Actually,** conditioning closes off the alternatives you thought of and conditioned on. Others survive untouched.
- Randomization is just a nice-to-have on top of good data → **Actually,** it's what makes the alternatives impossible by construction, rather than merely adjusted for after the fact.
- Once I've handled the obvious alternatives I'm done → **Actually,** the lamp needed two and it's a lamp. Complex settings generate long lists, and you only ever eliminate the ones you thought of.

## RECALL

Q: State the definition of causality being used here.
A: If you reached in and changed the value of one variable, would the other change as a
result?

Q: What can you actually observe in data, as opposed to what you want?
A: You observe a relationship — that the conditional mean of Y differs across values of X.
You want to know whether changing X changes Y, which the relationship alone doesn't tell you.

Q: Give three alternative explanations for the education–earnings relationship.
A: People who'd be better workers anyway choose more education; people in richer areas both
get more education and earn more; people with natural aptitude for a high-paying field go to
school for it, so schooling rides along with aptitude.

Q: Does an alternative explanation mean your hypothesised effect is absent?
A: No. The effect may be entirely real — it's just mixed in with other things, and you have
to isolate it.

Q: Why does randomization eliminate alternatives rather than merely adjust for them?
A: Because assignment was made by a random device, so nothing about the people could have
influenced who got treated. The alternative never occurred in your data.

Q: Pulling a string is strongly related to a lamp turning on. Give an alternative explanation and the fix.
A: Nick might be timing his pulls to when the light was going to change anyway. Fix: let a
coin decide whether to pull — the coin has no knowledge of the lamp — and see if the
relationship survives.

Q: Give the second lamp alternative and its fix.
A: The string might control all the electricity in the house, so it changes the lamp via a
different mechanism. Fix: pull the string and check whether other things in the house switch
on and off at the same time.

Q: What besides randomization can close off an alternative explanation?
A: Conditioning on a variable — the conditional conditional distributions from Ep 8 — plus
the many other designs covered later in the series.

Q: When have you identified a causal effect?
A: When every alternative explanation has been ruled out or had its influence removed, and a
relationship still remains — so you know the analysis answers your research question.

Q: Why isn't a very strong relationship evidence of causality?
A: Because alternative explanations can produce near-perfect relationships. The timed-pulls
story generates a strong relationship with no causal effect at all.

## GLOSSARY

- **Alternative explanation** — a data generating process that would produce the relationship you observed without the causal effect you're claiming.
- **Randomization** — assigning treatment by a random device, so nothing about the observations can influence who is treated.
- **Identified** — the state in which all alternative explanations have been eliminated or removed, so a remaining relationship answers your research question.
