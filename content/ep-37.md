## ONE SENTENCE

With several matching variables you need to boil many separate distances down to **one**, which
you do by treating observations as points in space and taking the straight line between them —
usually the **Mahalanobis distance**, which first divides out the correlations so shared traits
aren't counted twice — and the catch is that every dimension you add pushes everyone further
apart.

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

- One matching variable: distance is trivially the difference between values.
- Several variables: you need to combine several distances into **a single distance** to pick a closest match.
- The trick is a **spatial metaphor** — distance on each axis collapses into one straight line, and it generalises past three dimensions even though the picture doesn't.
- **Euclidean distance** is literally Pythagoras. **Mahalanobis distance** is more common, because it **divides out the correlations** between variables so a shared underlying trait isn't double-counted.
- Once you have one distance you can do anything — best match, best three, or weights.
- **Curse of dimensionality:** each variable you add is another way to be far apart, so all matches start to look bad.

## WHY THIS EXISTS

The goal hasn't changed: pick a group of control observations very similar to the treated group
across your matching variables, so that when someone asks whether a back door is driving the
difference between the groups, you can say no — you matched on it, there's no variation left in it
between the groups, so the back door is closed.

Distance matching does this by asking, literally, **how far apart are two observations** on the
matching variables. Trivial with one variable. Not trivial with several.

## IDEAS

### The problem: multiple distances

**Plain English.** Two candidates, each closer on a different variable. Which one is closer overall?

**Worked example — one variable, easy.** Match on April credit card bill size. If your bill is
6,000 and mine is 5,900, the difference is 100. To pick the best match for me, find whoever in the
control group is closest to 6,000, or build weights around it. Simple.

**Worked example — two variables, not easy.** Now match on bill size **and** age. Say I'm 35 with a
6,000 bill. Two candidates:

| Candidate | Bill | Age |
|---|---|---|
| A | 6,000 — identical | 60 — 25 years off |
| B | 1,000 — 5,000 off | 35 — identical |

Which is closer to me? You have one distance based on how far apart our bills are and a different
distance based on how far apart our ages are — and to pick "whoever is closest", you need a way to
**combine multiple distances into a single distance**, then take whoever has the smallest one.

### The spatial metaphor

**Plain English.** Treat each observation as a point in space and measure the straight line between
them.

**Precisely.** Most methods for boiling multiple distances into one are based on a metaphor with
literal distance in the real world.

**Anchor image.** Hold up two fists. There's a distance between them left-to-right. There's another
up-and-down. And one is further forward than the other. Three separate distances — and yet there is
obviously **one** distance between your fists: the straight line.

**Worked example.** With two matching variables, define the distance along one variable and the
distance along the other. Draw a straight line between the two points on that 2D plane and you have
a **single distance value** — and you pick matches by minimising it.

With three variables you have three separate distances and still one straight line between the
points. And it generalises beyond three dimensions into four, five, six — **the visuals break down
there, but the maths stays relatively easy.**

### Euclidean and Mahalanobis distance

**Plain English.** Euclidean is plain Pythagoras. Mahalanobis first removes the overlap between your
variables.

**Precisely.**

- **Euclidean distance** — literally what was just described: take this distance and that distance, apply the Pythagorean theorem, get the distance between the points.
- **Mahalanobis distance** — more common in matching. It makes a statistical adjustment that makes more sense in statistical applications: **some of those distances are double-counting the same thing**, so it divides out the **correlations** between the variables.

**Worked example.** Suppose **gender** is a back door you want to match on and close — but you can't
actually measure gender. What you *do* have is how much **arm hair** someone has and whether they
have a **beard**. Both are more common, or take higher values, among men than women.

Use both as matching variables and you're treating them as two separate distances — but you're also
**counting "being a man" twice**, because it shows up in both variables.

Mahalanobis divides out the correlation between them. Both variables stay in as matching variables,
but the shared "man" component only counts once; the double-count gets subtracted out.

**What that leaves you.** You've rescaled all the axes to take out the shared elements, so every
distance is now orthogonal to the others: here's how much arm hair you have *not counting* the
overlap with facial hair, here's how much facial hair *not counting* the overlap with arm hair. And
once you've subtracted that out, you're back to exactly the same geometric idea — points in space,
shortest line between them.

### What you do with it

**Plain English.** One number per pair, and now every matching option is open.

**Precisely.** For every treated observation you can now calculate the distance to every single
control observation. Then do whatever you like:

- Pick the **best match** — the shortest distance.
- Pick the **three best matches** — the three shortest.
- Construct **weights** where the shortest distances get the biggest weights, decaying as distance grows.

The key is that you now have a **single measure of distance**, and that's what makes any of it
possible.

### The curse of dimensionality

**Plain English.** Every variable you add is another way for two people to be far apart.

**Precisely.** The **curse of dimensionality** afflicts methods that are very sensitive to added
dimensions — and here they're literal dimensions. One matching variable, one dimension. Two
variables, two dimensions. Three, four, five. As the number of dimensions grows, **things tend to
get further and further apart**.

**Anchor image / worked example.** Two people in the same building. Look straight down from above:
they're both in the **northeast corner**, in the exact same spot. Matching in two dimensions, they
look extremely close.

Now zoom out to the side and add the third dimension. One of them is on the **fifth floor** and the
other is on the **first**. By adding a dimension you added a new way for them to be far apart — and
now they're much further apart than they looked.

**Where it breaks.** So **the more variables you match on, the harder it gets to find a decent
match.** Distance matching gets into trouble as you add matching variables, because all the matches
start to look bad. Add enough dimensions and it becomes genuinely hard to find anyone who's similar
to you across all those axes.

That's a particular problem for Q5 from the last episode — the worst allowable match — because once
you've added a lot of dimensions, *every* match looks poor. Some people recommend getting more lax
about what counts as an acceptable match the more matching variables you have. But it remains
something to think about, and it's an issue if you need to match on many variables to close many
back doors.

## TRAPS

- Distance matching with several variables is just doing the one-variable version repeatedly → **Actually,** you must combine the separate distances into a single number before you can rank matches at all.
- Euclidean distance is the standard choice → **Actually,** Mahalanobis is more common in matching, because it removes the correlation between variables.
- Mahalanobis drops correlated variables → **Actually,** both stay in as matching variables. What gets subtracted out is the double-counted shared component.
- More matching variables means better matching → **Actually,** each one is another dimension along which people can be far apart. Matches get worse, not better.
- Two people in the same corner of a building are close → **Actually,** not if one is on the fifth floor. That's the curse of dimensionality in one image.
- The dimensionality problem is solved by lowering your standards → **Actually,** being more lax about the worst acceptable match is a recommended mitigation, not a fix. The matches really are worse.

## RECALL

Q: What does distance matching ask?
A: How far apart two observations are on the matching variables — then pick the closest ones.

Q: Why is one matching variable easy?
A: The distance is simply the difference between the two values.

Q: I'm 35 with a 6,000 bill. Candidate A has a 6,000 bill and is 60; candidate B is 35 with a 1,000 bill. What's the problem?
A: You have two separate distances — one on bill, one on age — and no way to say who's closer until
you combine them into a single distance.

Q: What metaphor is used to combine multiple distances?
A: Physical distance in space — separate distances along each axis collapse into one straight line
between two points, and it generalises beyond three dimensions.

Q: What is Euclidean distance?
A: The straight-line distance computed with the Pythagorean theorem from the distances along each
axis.

Q: What does Mahalanobis distance add, and why?
A: It divides out the correlations between the matching variables, so a shared underlying component
isn't double-counted.

Q: Give the arm hair and beard example.
A: If you want to match on gender but can't measure it, arm hair and having a beard both track being
a man. Using both counts "being a man" twice, so Mahalanobis removes the correlation and lets the
shared component count once.

Q: Does Mahalanobis drop one of the correlated variables?
A: No. Both remain as matching variables; only the double-counted overlap is subtracted out.

Q: Once you have a single distance measure, what can you do with it?
A: Pick the single best match, pick the several best matches, or construct weights where the shortest
distances get the largest weights.

Q: Define the curse of dimensionality in this context.
A: As you add matching variables — and therefore dimensions — observations tend to get further and
further apart, so decent matches become harder to find.

Q: Give the building illustration.
A: Two people look identically placed when viewed from directly above — both in the northeast corner
— but adding the vertical dimension reveals one is on the fifth floor and the other on the first.

Q: What practical problem does this create for the "worst acceptable match" question?
A: With many dimensions, every match looks bad — so you may reject nearly all of them unless you
loosen your standard.

Q: What's the usual mitigation, and what's its limitation?
A: Being more lax about the worst acceptable match as you add matching variables. It's a mitigation
rather than a fix — the matches genuinely are worse.

## GLOSSARY

- **Distance matching** — matching by computing how far apart observations are on the matching variables.
- **Euclidean distance** — straight-line distance between points, computed via the Pythagorean theorem.
- **Mahalanobis distance** — a distance measure that divides out correlations between variables so shared components aren't double-counted.
- **Curse of dimensionality** — the tendency for observations to become further apart, and matches worse, as more matching variables are added.
