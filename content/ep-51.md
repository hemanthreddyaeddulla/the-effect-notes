## ONE SENTENCE

Add a group that never got treated, watch how *they* changed over the same period, and treat that
change as what would have happened to the treated group anyway — subtract it out, and what's left
is the effect.

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

- One of the **most commonly applied** designs, because the conditions are so easy to find.
- All you need: a treatment that **goes into effect at a particular time** and affects **some people and not others**.
- The problem it solves: you have a before/after comparison but don't trust any event-study way of closing the time back door — and you can't control for time, because that removes the treatment entirely.
- The fix: bring in a **control group** that was never treated.
- This makes the diagram **worse** — a second back door through group — but it lets you close the time one.
- Logic: the control group's change over time is what would have happened to the treated group anyway. Subtract it.
- **John Snow's 1850s cholera study** is a difference-in-differences before the name existed.

## WHY THIS EXISTS

Difference-in-differences is commonly applied because it's very easy to find conditions in which it
seems to apply. You don't need much: **a treatment that goes into effect at a particular time, and
a treatment that affects some people and not others.** That's it. There are further assumptions
required for it to work properly — but with that basic structure in place, you can probably get a
difference-in-differences going, which is a large part of why it's so popular.

A policy enacted in one country but not another? Difference-in-differences. A school makes a reform
and a different school doesn't? Difference-in-differences.

## IDEAS

### The problem being solved

**Plain English.** You have a before and an after, and no way to trust the before-and-after
comparison.

**Precisely.** You have a treatment that went into effect at a particular time — an event, a change,
a new policy implemented, an old one cancelled — with a before period and an after period. Event
studies handle exactly this, comparing before to after with some adjustment, perhaps for a trend.

**But what if you don't believe any of those ways of closing the time back door?**

**Worked example.** A school introduces a teaching reform and you want to know whether it improves
students' maths scores. You can look at how they were doing before and how they were doing after —
but how do you tell it's the effect of the new policy and not that things would have changed anyway?
Maybe there's no real way to use a time trend to account for that.

**And you can't just control for time.** Control for the differences in time that determine treatment
and there is literally no treatment variation left. Time and treatment go hand in hand — you can't
control for one without controlling away the entire treatment.

### The fix: make the problem more complex

**Plain English.** Bring in a second group that never got treated.

**Precisely.** So far there's only the group that gets treated, observed before and after. Now add a
group that **did not get the treatment in either period** — your **control group**, or comparison
group.

**And this makes the causal diagram look worse:**

```dag
node T 0.9 0 "Time" bad
node G 2.7 0 "Which group" bad
node X 0 1.2 "Treatment" treat
node Y 3.6 1.2 "Outcome" out
edge T X
edge T Y
edge G X
edge G Y
edge X Y
note 1.8 2 "two back doors now -- but the second one buys you the first"
```

You still have the back door through **time**, and now there's an additional back door because
**which group you're in** clearly determines whether you're treated — one group got treated, the
other didn't — and there are likely baseline differences between those groups anyway.

Things seem to have gotten worse. Except that the addition of this group is what lets you close the
time back door.

### How the control group closes the time back door

**Plain English.** Whatever happened to the untreated group is what would have happened to the
treated group anyway.

**Precisely.** The before/after comparison for the treated group contains **two things at once**:
the effect of treatment, *and* whatever would have changed over time anyway. In fixed effects
language, you're looking at that group's **within variation** — which solves some problems, but
**does not control for anything that changes over time**. And that's exactly what you're worried
about.

Now look at the control group's within variation — how *they* changed from before to after.

**The placebo-test intuition.** If there were no time variation going on — if the time back door
didn't matter, and only the treatment changed things — you'd expect the control group **not to
change at all**. So if you look at the control group and see no change, that's a decent indication
you can simply compare before and after for the treated group.

**And if the control group did change?** Say maths scores at the school with no reform went **up**.
That tells you something would have changed over time regardless. And it tells you roughly **how
much** — that's probably the amount of change you'd have expected in the treated group too.

**Worked example.**

| Group | Change from before to after |
|---|---|
| Control school (no reform) | **+5 points** |
| Treated school (reform) | **+8 points** |
| **Difference of the differences** | **+3 points** ← the treatment effect |

Five of those eight points are probably what would have happened anyway, because that's what you saw
in the untreated school. The extra three points is your effect.

**Anchor image.** Two boats rising with the tide. One also has an engine. To find out what the engine
did, measure how much *more* it rose than the other boat.

**That's the design.** Take the difference in the treated group from before to after. Take the
difference in the control group from before to after. Treat the second as what would have happened
anyway, subtract it from the first — a **difference between the differences** — and whatever remains
is the treatment effect.

### Worked example: John Snow and the London cholera epidemic

**Plain English.** A water company moved its intake upstream, and Snow compared its customers'
death rates against everyone else's — before the name "difference-in-differences" existed.

**Precisely.** In 1850s London — and quite often in European cities — cholera outbreaks were common
and extremely deadly. Nobody knew how cholera spread, which made it very hard to stop.

**The leading theory was miasma theory:** that **bad smells** transmitted disease from one person to
another. That's why old pictures show people in big robes with the large beaked nose-masks — they
packed herbs inside so it would smell nice and ward off the bad smells.

**Snow thought cholera travelled through water.** And he had a way to test it.

**The setting.** One of the main ways to get water in London was to go to a pump and pump your own.
Different companies supplied different pumps, and those companies drew their water from different
sources.

**The treatment.** The **Lambeth company** moved its water source from the middle of the Thames to a
point **before the city** — upstream, before the waste went in. Clean water flows toward London,
London dumps a lot of sewage into the river, and anyone drawing water from that downstream portion
should be getting cholera spread to them, if cholera spreads through water.

**The design.** Treated group: areas supplied by Lambeth, whose water source changed. Control group:
areas supplied by the other pumps, which didn't change.

**Death rates from cholera, per 10,000 people:**

| | Before | After (1854) | Change |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Non-Lambeth** (control) | 134.9 | 146.6 | **+11.7** |
| **Lambeth** (treated) | 130.1 | 84.9 | **−45.2** |
| | | **Difference-in-differences** | **−56.9** |

Note there were already baseline differences before treatment — 134.9 against 130.1 — though not a
huge gap.

**The reasoning.** Look at Lambeth alone: they moved their pump upstream and death rates dropped. So
maybe water does transmit the disease. **But maybe death rates would have dropped anyway** — maybe
the epidemic simply burned out on its own, which is how it had always gone before. Cholera comes,
cholera goes.

If it had gone away on its own, you'd have expected death rates to drop for the non-Lambeth
companies too. **They didn't. They got higher.** So it was the treatment that lowered death rates
for Lambeth's customers.

**Where it landed.** They ended up changing how the water system worked in response to Snow's
findings — although they didn't quite buy his theory, they were willing to go along with the results
— and it saved a great many lives. So all this observational data business does seem to have at
least some purpose.

## TRAPS

- Difference-in-differences requires similar groups → **Actually,** all you need is a treatment at a particular time affecting some people and not others. Baseline differences are allowed.
- Adding a control group simplifies the causal diagram → **Actually,** it adds a second back door through group. It's worth it because it buys you the time back door.
- The treated group's before/after change is the effect → **Actually,** it's the effect *plus* whatever would have happened anyway. That's the entire problem.
- Fixed effects on the treated group would solve this → **Actually,** within variation doesn't control for anything that changes over time, which is precisely the worry.
- Lambeth's death rate fell by 45, so the effect is 45 → **Actually,** the control group's rate *rose* by 11.7, so the estimated effect is about 57 fewer deaths per 10,000.
- If the control group changed, the design is broken → **Actually,** that's the whole point — their change is your estimate of what would have happened anyway.

## RECALL

Q: What are the minimum conditions for a difference-in-differences design?
A: A treatment that goes into effect at a particular time, and that treatment affecting some people
and not others.

Q: What problem is it solving that event studies couldn't?
A: A before/after comparison where you don't trust any of the event-study methods for closing the time
back door.

Q: Why can't you close the time back door by controlling for time?
A: Time determines treatment completely — nobody treated before, everybody after — so controlling for
time removes all treatment variation.

Q: What does adding a control group do to the causal diagram?
A: It makes it worse — adding a second back door through which group you're in, on top of the time
back door.

Q: What two things does the treated group's before/after change contain?
A: The effect of treatment, and whatever would have changed over time anyway.

Q: Why doesn't fixed effects logic solve this on its own?
A: Within variation controls for things constant over time but doesn't control for anything that
*changes* over time — which is exactly the concern.

Q: What's the placebo-test intuition for the control group?
A: If the time back door didn't matter, the untreated group shouldn't change at all. If they don't,
you can compare the treated group before and after directly.

Q: The control school gains 5 points and the treated school gains 8. What's the effect?
A: 3 points — five of the eight would have happened anyway, judging by the control school.

Q: State the design in one sentence.
A: Take the treated group's before-to-after difference, subtract the control group's before-to-after
difference, and what remains is the treatment effect.

Q: What was miasma theory?
A: The prevailing belief that bad smells transmitted disease — hence the beaked masks packed with
herbs.

Q: What was the treatment in John Snow's study?
A: The Lambeth water company moving its water source from the middle of the Thames to a point
upstream, before the city's sewage entered the river.

Q: Who were the treated and control groups?
A: Treated: areas supplied by Lambeth, whose source changed. Control: areas supplied by other pumps,
whose sources didn't.

Q: Give the four death rates per 10,000.
A: Non-Lambeth: 134.9 before, 146.6 after. Lambeth: 130.1 before, 84.9 after.

Q: Compute the difference-in-differences.
A: Lambeth fell by 45.2, non-Lambeth rose by 11.7, so the estimate is about 56.9 fewer deaths per
10,000.

Q: Why wasn't Lambeth's drop alone convincing?
A: Cholera epidemics had always burned out on their own, so the rate might have fallen regardless.

Q: What ruled that out?
A: The non-Lambeth death rate didn't fall — it rose. So the epidemic wasn't burning out on its own.

## GLOSSARY

- **Difference-in-differences** — subtracting a control group's before-to-after change from the treated group's, to remove what would have happened anyway.
- **Control group / comparison group** — a group untreated in both periods, used to estimate the time effect.
- **Miasma theory** — the 19th-century belief that disease spread through bad smells.
