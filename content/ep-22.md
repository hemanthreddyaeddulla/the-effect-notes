## ONE SENTENCE

A **conditional average treatment effect** is the average effect *within a group* — and the
group can be defined by who you are (men, college students) or by what happened to you
(the treated, the untreated), which is why randomizing within a selected sample gives you
that sample's effect and comparing against a control group gives you the effect **on the
treated**.

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

- A **CATE** is the average treatment effect among some group, conditional on being in it.
- **Rule of thumb 2:** randomize within a **selected** population → the CATE for that population.
- The group can also be defined by treatment status: **average treatment on the treated (ATT)** and **average treatment on the untreated (ATUT)**.
- **Rule of thumb 4:** using a **control group** to stand in for what the treated group would have looked like untreated → **ATT**.
- A CATE can be exactly what you want (only randomize the cervical cancer drug among people with a cervix) or exactly what you don't (college students standing in for humanity).
- Most "individual treatment effect" methods are elaborate CATEs — the effect for your particular interlocking set of groups.

## WHY THIS EXISTS

Every effect is heterogeneous, and any single estimate is some average. So the practical
question is: which average did *my* design just hand me?

This episode answers it for the most common case — averages within a group — and gives you
two of the four rules of thumb for reading a design and knowing what came out.

## IDEAS

### The setup: individual effects you could never actually see

**Plain English.** To follow the arithmetic you need a table nobody could ever have — each
person's outcome both with and without treatment.

**Worked example.** Four types of people. Names, but think categories rather than individuals.

| Person | Sex | Untreated outcome | Treated outcome | Individual effect |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Alfred | male | 1 | 2 | **1** |
| Brianna | female | 1 | 5 | **4** |
| Chizu | female | 2 | 5 | **3** |
| Diego | male | 2 | 4 | **2** |

Treatment works best for Brianna (+4) and least for Alfred (+1).

**The average treatment effect** is the average of 1, 2, 3, and 4 = **2.5**.

**Where it breaks.** You could never have this table in real data — you can't see the same
person both treated and untreated. It's here so you can check what each estimator recovers.

### CATE by who you are, and rule of thumb 2

**Plain English.** Run your experiment on only one group and you get that group's effect, not
everyone's.

**Precisely.** A **conditional average treatment effect** is the average treatment effect among
some group — conditional on being in that group.

**Worked example.** You run a randomized experiment but recruit **only men**. Brianna and Chizu
are gone; you have Alfreds and Diegos. Random assignment treats some and not others, and you
observe the outcomes.

- Average outcome among the **treated**: avg(2, 4) = **3**
- Average outcome among the **untreated**: avg(1, 2) = **1.5**
- Difference: **1.5**

What does 1.5 represent? Alfred's individual effect is 1; Diego's is 2. Their average is
**1.5** — exactly the number you got. You recovered the average treatment effect **among men**.

Note this is *not* the ATE of 2.5. Randomizing within a group gives you the group's effect.

**Rule of thumb 2.** Randomizing within a **selected** population gives you the average
treatment effect for that population — a CATE. This holds whether you picked the sample and
randomized inside it, or found real-world randomization that only applies to a particular group
and used just that group.

**Where it breaks.** Good or bad entirely depending on context:

- **Good:** the cervical cancer drug. You'd *want* to randomize only among people who have a cervix. Who cares about the effect on cervical cancer among people who can't get it?
- **Bad:** the psychology studies randomizing among college students while wanting to say something about the general population. You get the effect conditional on being a college student when you wanted something broader.

### CATE by what happened to you: ATT and ATUT

**Plain English.** You can also condition on whether someone actually got treated.

**Precisely.** Two more conditional averages:

- **Average treatment on the treated (ATT)** — the average effect among people who **actually got** the treatment.
- **Average treatment on the untreated (ATUT)** — the average effect among people who **did not** get it: how effective would it have been for this group, had it been applied to them?

Both matter, and which you get depends on your estimation. ATT comes up more often.

### Working out the ATT

**Plain English.** Compare the treated group's outcomes to a control group standing in for what
the treated group would have looked like untreated.

**Worked example.** Same four people. This time, instead of randomizing, you simply observe that
**Alfred and Chizu got treated**, and **Brianna and Diego did not**.

**First, check there are no back doors.** Look at what their *untreated* outcomes would have
been:

- Alfred and Chizu (the treated): avg(1, 2) = **1.5**
- Brianna and Diego (the untreated): avg(1, 2) = **1.5**

Identical. So absent treatment, the two groups would have looked the same — no back doors here.
That's what licenses using one group's untreated outcomes as a stand-in for the other's.

**Now make the comparison:**

- Treated outcomes of those actually treated (Alfred, Chizu): avg(2, 5) = **3.5**
- Untreated outcomes of those actually untreated (Brianna, Diego): avg(1, 2) = **1.5**
- Difference: 3.5 − 1.5 = **2**

What is that 2? Alfred's individual effect is 1; Chizu's is 3. Their average is **2** — exactly
what you got. It's the average effect **among the people who actually got treated**: the ATT.

And notice what happened. Without ever seeing a single individual treatment effect, the one
number you estimated turned out to be an average of the individual effects in the treated group.
That's what "a single estimate is an average of individual effects" means in practice.

**Rule of thumb 4.** If your estimate uses a **control group as an indication of what the treated
group would have looked like without treatment**, you're likely getting an average treatment on
the treated. Which makes sense — you're asking how this treated group differs from what you think
they'd have been without treatment.

(Rule 3 is skipped here because these follow the book's ordering rather than the videos'. It
arrives next episode.)

**Where it breaks.** The ATUT is more involved. You have to do some adjusting to take the treated
outcomes you *do* observe and translate them into what the treated outcomes would have been for
the **untreated** group — the flipped version of the same idea. If that's convoluted, the book
has it.

### Individual treatment effects are mostly elaborate CATEs

**Plain English.** "Your personal effect" usually means "the effect for people who match you on
a lot of things at once."

**Precisely.** Rather than one group, you estimate effects across many overlapping, intricately
interlocking groupings, then use that structure to produce an effect for an individual who sits
in a particular subset of categories, and apply it as their individual effect.

**Anchor image.** Not a bespoke suit. A very large size chart.

**Worked example.** Conditional on being a man, being over 35, being brown-haired, living in
France — all those groups combine, and the effect for that interlocking set becomes your
"individual" effect. That's how most of these approaches actually work.

## TRAPS

- My study was randomized, so I have the average treatment effect → **Actually,** randomizing within men gave 1.5, not the ATE of 2.5. Randomization within a group yields that group's effect.
- A conditional average treatment effect is a compromised version of the real thing → **Actually,** it's often exactly what you want. Randomizing the cervical cancer drug only among people with a cervix is correct, not a limitation.
- ATT and ATUT condition on characteristics like the other CATEs → **Actually,** they condition on treatment status — what happened to you, not who you are.
- ATT and ATUT should come out about the same → **Actually,** they're averages over different people with different effects. In the example the ATT is 2 while the ATE is 2.5.
- Getting the ATUT is just the ATT calculation reversed → **Actually,** it requires adjusting to translate observed treated outcomes into what they'd have been for the untreated group. More involved.
- Individual treatment effect methods estimate a genuinely individual effect → **Actually,** most are complex CATEs — the effect for your particular interlocking set of group memberships.

## RECALL

Q: Define a conditional average treatment effect.
A: The average treatment effect among some group — the effect conditional on being in that
group.

Q: Alfred +1, Brianna +4, Chizu +3, Diego +2. What's the ATE?
A: The average of 1, 2, 3, and 4 = 2.5.

Q: You randomize among men only. Treated men average 3, untreated average 1.5. What have you estimated?
A: 1.5 — the average treatment effect among men, which is the average of Alfred's effect (1) and
Diego's (2). Not the ATE of 2.5.

Q: State rule of thumb 2.
A: Randomizing within a selected population gives you the conditional average treatment effect
for that population.

Q: Does rule 2 only apply when you chose the sample yourself?
A: No — it also applies when you find real-world randomization that only reaches a particular
group and you use just that group.

Q: Give one case where a CATE is exactly what you want and one where it isn't.
A: Want: randomizing a cervical cancer drug only among people who have a cervix. Don't want:
psychology experiments on college students used to describe the general population.

Q: What's the difference between ATT and ATUT?
A: ATT is the average effect among people who actually got treated. ATUT is the average effect
among those who didn't — how effective it would have been for them.

Q: Alfred and Chizu are treated; Brianna and Diego aren't. How do you check for back doors?
A: Compare what their untreated outcomes would have been. Both pairs average 1.5, so the groups
would have looked identical without treatment.

Q: Complete the ATT calculation for that setup.
A: Treated outcomes of Alfred and Chizu average 3.5; untreated outcomes of Brianna and Diego
average 1.5; the difference is 2 — which equals the average of Alfred's effect (1) and Chizu's
(3).

Q: State rule of thumb 4.
A: If you use a control group as an indication of what the treated group would have looked like
without treatment, you'll likely get the average treatment on the treated.

Q: Why does rule 4 make intuitive sense?
A: Because you're asking how the treated group differs from what you believe they'd have been
without treatment — a question about the treated group specifically.

Q: How is the ATUT obtained, and why is it harder?
A: By adjusting the treated outcomes you observe into what treated outcomes would have been for
the untreated group — the flipped version, requiring more work.

Q: How do most "individual treatment effect" methods actually work?
A: As elaborate conditional average treatment effects — estimating effects across many
overlapping groups and applying the effect for your particular interlocking set, e.g. a man over
35 with brown hair living in France.

## GLOSSARY

- **Conditional average treatment effect (CATE)** — the average treatment effect within a specified group.
- **Average treatment on the treated (ATT)** — the average effect among those who actually received the treatment.
- **Average treatment on the untreated (ATUT)** — the average effect among those who did not receive it, had it been applied.
- **Control group** — a group used to stand in for what the treated group would have looked like without treatment.
