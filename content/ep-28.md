## ONE SENTENCE

There is one sentence — *a one-unit change in X is associated with a coefficient-sized unit
change in Y* — and it handles essentially every regression coefficient you will ever meet,
provided X appears in only one place in your model.

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

- **The mantra:** *a one-unit change in X is associated with a coefficient-sized unit change in Y.* Say it out loud. Fill in the blanks. Done.
- With controls: *adjusting for all the other predictors, a one-unit change in X is associated with a coefficient-sized unit change in Y.*
- **"Associated with", not "increases".** Causal language requires causal identification, which by default you don't have.
- If X is transformed — say you regressed on log(X) — the sentence still works. It's just about the log, so you have extra work to get back to X.
- **It only fails when X appears more than once** in the model: polynomials (X and X²) or interactions (X, Z, X×Z).
- When it fails, use the **derivative** instead. The effect becomes an expression, not a single number.

## WHY THIS EXISTS

This is genuinely simple, and people work far too hard to make it complicated for themselves.
If you have a regression model you're unsure how to interpret, the fix is almost always to say
the sentence again and slot the pieces in: what's X, what's Y, what does a one-unit change mean
here?

## IDEAS

### The mantra

**Plain English.** One unit up in X, coefficient-many units up in Y.

**Precisely.** If you regress Y on X and get a coefficient:

> **A one-unit change in X is associated with a coefficient-sized unit change in Y.**

Add controls and it barely changes:

> **Adjusting for all the other predictors in the model, a one-unit change in X is associated with a coefficient-sized unit change in Y.**

**Anchor image.** A currency conversion rate. One of these buys that many of those. The
coefficient is the exchange rate between X-units and Y-units.

**Where it breaks.** This always works **as long as X only shows up in one place** in your model.

### Wrinkle 1 — "associated with" is doing deliberate work

**Plain English.** The mantra is written in non-causal language on purpose.

**Precisely.** "Associated with" implies no causal relationship, and that's the correct default —
you shouldn't be assuming you've causally identified anything.

You may have heard the causal phrasing: *a one-unit change in X **increases** Y by the size of
the coefficient.* That version assumes causal identification. If you genuinely have it, you're
entitled to say "increases" or "decreases". Otherwise, "associated with".

**Anchor image.** The difference between "these two arrive together" and "this one brings the
other."

### Wrinkle 2 — transformed variables

**Plain English.** If you regressed on a transformation, the sentence describes the
transformation, not the original variable.

**Precisely.** Regress Y on log(X) and the mantra still applies exactly: *a one-unit change in
the log of the predictor is associated with a coefficient-sized unit change in Y.* The sentence
never breaks, because you're just regressing an outcome on a predictor.

**Where it breaks.** You probably don't care about a one-unit change in the log of X — you care
about X. So the sentence works, and then you have some work to do afterwards converting it into
a statement about the thing you're actually interested in. That's the next episode.

### Wrinkle 3 — when the sentence genuinely fails

**Plain English.** If X appears twice, you can't change one X without changing the other.

**Precisely.** The mantra fails in one common setting: **X shows up in more than one place among
the predictors.** Two ways that happens:

- **Polynomials** — regressing Y on X and X²
- **Interaction terms** — regressing Y on X, Z, and X×Z

Why it breaks: the full mantra says *holding all the control variables constant*, a one-unit
change in X is associated with a coefficient-sized change in Y. But you **can't hold the others
constant**. If your model contains X and X², you cannot change X by one unit without also
changing X². They move together.

### The fallback: the derivative

**Plain English.** The coefficient was always a slope. When there are several, compute the slope
directly.

**Precisely.** Another interpretation of a regression coefficient: it's the **derivative**. If
you regress Y on X, then the derivative of Y with respect to X is simply β₁. "A one-unit change
in X is associated with a certain-sized change in Y" is describing the slope of the line.

For a more complex model — Y on X and X², or anything else — do the same thing: take the
derivative of Y with respect to X. You'll get a calculation, which may itself contain
coefficients *and* variable values. Whatever that expression is, that's the relationship between
X and Y:

> **A one-unit change in X is associated with a [calculation]-sized unit change in Y.**

**Anchor image.** A single coefficient is a fixed exchange rate posted on the wall. A derivative
is a rate that depends on how much you're changing — you have to look it up for your amount.

## TRAPS

- A coefficient means X increases Y by that amount → **Actually,** "associated with" unless you've established causal identification. The causal phrasing is a separate claim.
- The mantra needs modifying for models with control variables → **Actually,** you just prepend "adjusting for all the other predictors". The structure is identical.
- If I regressed on log(X), the coefficient tells me about X → **Actually,** it tells you about a one-unit change in log(X). Getting back to X takes extra work.
- The mantra breaks whenever the model gets complicated → **Actually,** it breaks in one specific circumstance: X appearing in more than one place.
- I can hold X² constant while changing X → **Actually,** you can't. That's precisely why the mantra fails for polynomials and interactions.
- Derivatives are a separate, more advanced interpretation → **Actually,** it's the same interpretation. A single coefficient *is* the derivative; multi-place X just makes it an expression.

## SAY IT OUT LOUD

> People tie themselves in knots interpreting regression coefficients, and honestly it's one
> sentence. Here it is:
>
> **A one-unit change in X is associated with a coefficient-sized unit change in Y.**
>
> That's it. If you're staring at a model you don't understand, say that sentence again and just
> slot the pieces in — what's X, what's Y, what does one unit mean here. Got control variables?
> Add four words at the front: *adjusting for all the other predictors*, a one-unit change in X
> is associated with a coefficient-sized change in Y.
>
> Two things to notice about the wording. I said **"associated with"** — not "increases". That's
> deliberate, because by default you have **not** causally identified anything. If you genuinely
> have, then great, say "increases". Otherwise, don't.
>
> And if you regressed on some transformation — say the log of X — the sentence still works
> perfectly. It just tells you about a one-unit change in the *log*, and you probably care about
> X itself, so there's a conversion step afterwards.
>
> There's exactly one situation where the sentence genuinely fails: when **X shows up in more
> than one place**. Like if your model has X and X², or an interaction with X and Z and X times Z.
> Because the sentence secretly says *holding everything else constant* — and you cannot change X
> by one unit while holding X² constant. They move together. Impossible.
>
> When that happens, fall back to what a coefficient always was: a **derivative**. Take the
> derivative of Y with respect to X, and whatever expression falls out — that's your effect. It
> might have variable values in it, which means the effect is different at different values of X.
> And that's fine. That's the honest answer.

## RECALL

Q: State the mantra for interpreting a regression coefficient.
A: A one-unit change in X is associated with a coefficient-sized unit change in Y.

Q: How does it change when the model includes control variables?
A: You prepend "adjusting for all the other predictors in the model" — the rest is identical.

Q: Why "associated with" rather than "increases"?
A: Because "increases" is a causal claim, and by default you should not assume you've causally
identified the effect. Use "increases" only when you have.

Q: You regress Y on log(X). Does the mantra still work?
A: Yes — a one-unit change in the log of the predictor is associated with a coefficient-sized
change in Y. But you probably care about X itself, so a conversion step is needed afterwards.

Q: Under what single condition does the mantra fail?
A: When X appears in more than one place among the predictors.

Q: Name the two common ways that happens.
A: Polynomials (Y on X and X²) and interaction terms (Y on X, Z, and X×Z).

Q: Why exactly does it fail there?
A: The mantra implicitly holds the other predictors constant, and you cannot change X by one unit
while holding X² constant — they change together.

Q: What's the fallback interpretation?
A: The derivative. Take the derivative of Y with respect to X; whatever expression results is the
relationship between X and Y.

Q: In a simple regression of Y on X, what is the derivative of Y with respect to X?
A: β₁ — the coefficient itself. A single coefficient already *is* the derivative.

Q: What's different about the effect when the derivative contains variable values?
A: The effect is no longer a single number — it differs depending on the value of X you evaluate
it at.

## GLOSSARY

- **Coefficient** — the estimated slope on a predictor; the exchange rate between a unit of X and units of Y.
- **Derivative interpretation** — reading a coefficient as the derivative of the outcome with respect to the predictor; necessary when the predictor appears more than once.
- **Interaction term** — a predictor formed by multiplying two variables together, e.g. X×Z.
