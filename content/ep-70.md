## ONE SENTENCE

Every design so far demanded a **strong assumption** you probably don't believe — so weaken it,
accept that you'll get a **range** instead of a number, and answer the question that actually
matters: not *which assumptions must I make to get one answer*, but **which assumptions do I
actually find reasonable, and how far do they get me?**

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

- Every design rests on assumptions: that you closed **all** back doors, that your instrument is valid, that parallel trends holds.
- **Stronger assumptions get you more — and are more likely to be wrong.** The sun rises; the sun rises between 6 and 7; the sun rises at 6:52.
- **Point identification** gives exactly one answer. **Set identification** — partial identification — gives a range.
- The alternative to a range isn't a better number. It's either an unbelievable assumption or nothing at all.
- Nick considers it **powerful and underutilised**, because you can't sit around waiting for a quasi-experiment to happen.
- **Adding assumptions narrows the bounds.** You choose which ones you believe rather than which ones you need.
- New in the **second edition** — this chapter and these videos were added later.

## WHY THIS EXISTS

Doing causal inference takes data *and* a model of the world — an understanding that lets you draw
your causal diagram. The diagram tells you which back doors to close, and you close them by
controlling for variables or by using a quasi-experimental design like instrumental variables or
regression discontinuity.

**But each of those requires assumptions:**

| Approach | The assumption |
|---|---|
| Controlling for variables | You have measured and controlled for **every single variable** needed to close all the back doors |
| Instrumental variables | There is **no front door path** from the instrument to the outcome |
| Difference-in-differences | **Parallel trends** holds |

**Here's the problem: do you actually believe all of those?** It's a very strong claim to say not
merely that you found and closed *some* of the back doors, but that you found and closed **all** of
them — that the relationship between treatment and outcome, beyond what you've already shut down, is
**literally zero**.

## IDEAS

### Strong and weak assumptions

**Plain English.** The more you assume, the more you get — and the more likely you're wrong.

**Precisely.** A strong assumption assumes more of the world than a weak one.

**Worked example — the sunrise.**

| Assumption | Strength |
|---|---|
| "The sun will rise tomorrow" | **Weak.** Probably true. Nobody would question it. |
| "The sun will rise between 6 and 7am" | **Stronger.** You're assuming more about the world, even with good reason. |
| "The sun will rise at 6:52am" | **Stronger still.** |

**The trade-off.** The stronger your assumptions, the more you get out of your analysis. If you're
willing to assume there is literally no open back door between treatment and outcome, that lets you
get away with a great deal. If you simply assume your treatment is randomly assigned — a very strong
assumption — you can look at the raw relationship and treat it like an experiment. Nothing is
stopping you.

**It's just going to be wrong if your assumption is wrong.** And the stronger the assumption, the
more likely it is to be wrong. "6:52am" is more likely wrong than "between 6 and 7," which is more
likely wrong than "at all."

### Point identification and set identification

**Plain English.** One answer, or a range of answers.

**Precisely.** Partial identification takes an analysis requiring a strong assumption, **weakens the
assumption**, and accepts that the answer will be less precise — but you still get an answer, and now
you can talk far more responsibly about how likely your assumptions are to be true.

**Worked example — back to the sunrise.** You'd like to get up five or ten minutes before sunrise to
watch it.

- **Assume it rises at exactly 6:52am** → wake at **6:42am**, precisely. That's **point identification**: enough assumptions that the question has exactly one answer.
- **Assume only that it rises between 6 and 7** → get up sometime between **5:50am and 6:50am**. Still a conclusion — it still tells you not to sleep until noon — but a **range**.

That second case is also called **set identification**, because your results are a set of answers.

**Anchor image.** A weather forecast that says "rain between two and five" is more useful than one
that confidently says "rain at 3:47" and is wrong.

### Why Nick thinks it's underutilised

**Plain English.** You can't sit around waiting for a quasi-experiment to happen.

**Precisely.** Causal inference has a problem. The results we really believe tend to be
quasi-experiments — things requiring an instrumental variable, or a discontinuity. But **you can only
do a quasi-experiment if the quasi-experiment happened.** That severely limits the range of things
you can study if you're always waiting for one to turn up.

Meanwhile, there's a great deal of work where you can confidently say you've controlled for a lot of
the important stuff and closed a lot of important back doors — you just can never quite get all the
way there.

**Partial identification says: given that we don't quite get there, what can we say anyway?** And you
can often say quite a lot — something like *I think the effect of this policy is between 5 and 10
percentage points, given that I know I'm still a little biased and still have a couple of omitted
variables.*

### Worked example: a randomized experiment ruined by attrition

**Plain English.** Even randomization can't save you — but bounds can.

**Precisely.** Randomly assign **5,000 parents** to receive **$1,000**: *keep your kids in school,
here's a thousand dollars to help you do it.* The other parents get nothing. Follow up later to see
whether their kids are still in school.

**The problem.** Getting the money might make you **more likely to stay in the study**, whereas people
who got nothing may never report back to the researchers, and you lose track of them. That's
**differential attrition** between the treated and control groups.

So if the money group ends up with a higher share of kids still in school — is that because the money
worked, or because the money made different kinds of people more willing to stay in the study? It's a
**collider bias** problem.

And **you cannot observe the people who dropped out**, so you can't adjust by controlling for
anything.

**The strong assumption.** Assume people who dropped out are essentially random — nothing to do with
whether they'd have kept their kids in school, nothing to do with treatment assignment. Then you can
ignore attrition and report your results. That gives you point identification — a single estimate of
exactly what the money did.

**That's probably not true.**

**The weak assumption.** *The share of students who stayed in school is somewhere between 0% and
100%.* That seems pretty reasonable. It **has** to be true.

**Now do the arithmetic.** Suppose **90% stayed in the study** and **10% dropped out**. Of the 90% who
stayed, **80% kept their kids in school**. Of the 10% who dropped out, the share is somewhere between
0% and 100%.

| If the dropouts' share was… | Overall share keeping kids in school |
|---|---|
| **0%** | (0.9 × 80%) + (0.1 × 0%) = **72%** |
| **100%** | (0.9 × 80%) + (0.1 × 100%) = **82%** |

So you don't know exactly what share of the treatment group kept their kids in school — but it **has
to be between 72% and 82%.**

Do the same calculation for the control group, and the two ranges give you a **range of treatment
effects**.

**What that buys you.** Rather than choosing between an unrealistic assumption and reporting nothing
at all, you give a range of reasonable estimates based on something you know to be true: that a share
must lie between 0 and 100%.

### Adding assumptions narrows the bounds

**Plain English.** Know more, assume more, get a tighter range.

**Precisely.** If you feel you know more about the situation, add more assumptions — provided you
think they're reasonable.

**Worked example.** You might be willing to assume that **if you stayed in the study, you're probably
also more likely to have kept your kids in school** — that some of the people who dropped out are
people who drop out of life in general and don't send their kids to school either. Add that, and the
bounds narrow.

**The reframing that matters.** By allowing yourself **not** to reach point identification, you get to
**choose which assumptions you think are reasonable** — rather than asking which assumptions you
*have* to make in order to get a single answer, whether or not they're true.

## TRAPS

- A range is a weaker result than a point estimate → **Actually,** the point estimate rests on a stronger assumption, so it's more likely to be wrong. A defensible range beats an indefensible number.
- Strong assumptions are bad → **Actually,** they're more informative. They're just more likely to be false, which is the trade you're managing.
- If you can't identify the effect, you have nothing to report → **Actually,** you can report bounds, and they're often narrow enough to be useful.
- Partial identification means being vague → **Actually,** the bounds are exact consequences of assumptions you can state precisely.
- Randomization protects you from this → **Actually,** the attrition example is a randomized experiment where randomization can't help, because you can't see who left.
- Bounds are fixed by the data → **Actually,** every additional defensible assumption narrows them.
- Point identification is the goal and bounds are a fallback → **Actually,** the reframing is that you choose assumptions you believe, rather than whichever ones deliver a single number.

## GLOSSARY

- **Partial identification** — deriving a range of possible values for an effect under assumptions weaker than those needed for a single estimate.
- **Set identification** — another name for partial identification, since the result is a set of values.
- **Point identification** — having made enough assumptions that exactly one answer follows.
- **Strong assumption** — one that assumes more about the world, yielding more precision and greater risk of being wrong.
- **Bounds** — the upper and lower limits of the plausible range of an effect.
- **Differential attrition** — treated and control groups dropping out of a study at different rates or for different reasons.
