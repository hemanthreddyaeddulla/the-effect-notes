## ONE SENTENCE

A causal diagram is nothing more than a list of the variables relevant to your question
plus arrows showing which causes which — and its value is that once it's drawn you can
*see* the pathways connecting your two variables, sort them into the ones you want and
the ones you don't, and go get rid of the rest.

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

- A causal diagram is a graph: **variables** as nodes, **arrows** meaning "this causes that". Literally that's all it is.
- The **variable** is `hair color`, not `brown hair`. Brown is one of its values.
- Arrows come from **theory**, not from the data.
- One variable can cause many; one variable can be caused by many. Both are fine.
- Include variables **whether or not you can measure them** — unmeasured causes still cause things. Grey them out, or use a placeholder like `U1`.
- The payoff: a diagram shows you **pathways**. Some answer your question, some don't. Identification is keeping the first kind and killing the second.

## WHY THIS EXISTS

You know you need the data generating process, and you know what an arrow means. Now you
need to actually write one down.

Once it's on paper something useful happens that couldn't happen before: you stop seeing
"X and Y are related" as one undifferentiated fact and start seeing the *specific routes*
by which they're related. Some of those routes are your research question. Some are the
alternative explanations from Ep 10. Now they're distinguishable objects you can point at.

## IDEAS

### What a causal diagram is

**Plain English.** Dots for variables, arrows for causes. Done.

**Precisely.** A causal diagram is a set of variables relevant to what you're studying,
plus arrows linking them, each arrow meaning that one variable causes the other in the
Ep 12 sense. There's plenty of complexity hidden underneath — what counts as a variable,
how to analyse the diagram, how to know you've got the right one — but the object itself
is that simple.

### Variables, precisely

**Plain English.** A node is the *thing being measured*, not one of its answers.

**Precisely.** A variable is a measurement taken across observations, which takes
different values for different observations. The node is the measurement.

**Anchor image.** The node is the question on the form. The values are the boxes you could
tick.

**Worked example.** Take a thousand people and record hair colour: brown, blonde, red,
black. **`Hair color` is the variable.** Brown hair is not a variable — it's one value the
variable can take. Getting this backwards will wreck a diagram.

For "does hair colour affect labour-market performance?", the relevant variables might be
hair colour, earnings, intelligence, get-up-and-go, network connections, parents' social
standing. Earnings takes values like 30,000, 40,000, 100,000.

### Building one up: the cake

**Plain English.** Think about your setting, list what varies, ask what determines what.

**Worked example.** You have cake in the office kitchen. Brad comes in and offers to flip a
coin for it.

**Step 1 — two variables.** The coin flip (heads or tails) and who has the cake (Brad or
you). The flip determines who gets the cake, so:

```dag
node C 0 0 "Coin flip" treat
node K 1.4 0 "Cake" out
edge C K
```

That's a complete causal diagram.

**Step 2 — one cause, two effects.** Say the winner gets the cake but the loser keeps the
coin. Now the flip determines your money too. One variable causing two things is fine:

```dag
node C 0 0.5 "Coin flip" treat
node K 1.5 0 "Cake" out
node M 1.5 1 "Money"
edge C K
edge C M
```

**Step 3 — one effect, two causes.** Your friend Terry is sometimes in the room. If Terry
is there, they see the flip, and if you lost your coin they feel bad and give you one. So
money now has two causes — also fine:

```dag
node C 0 0.5 "Coin flip" treat
node K 1.5 0 "Cake" out
node M 1.5 1 "Money"
node T 0 1.5 "Terry in room"
node D 1.5 2 "Terry's mood" unobs
edge C K
edge C M
edge T M
edge D T
note 1.5 2.7 "dashed = can't be measured, still on the diagram"
```

**The rule that generates arrows:** anything that changes the distribution of a variable
should be drawn as a cause of it. The coin flip changes the distribution of your money,
therefore coin flip → money.

### Unmeasured variables go on the diagram anyway

**Plain English.** Draw the causes that are really there, not the ones you happen to have
data for.

**Precisely.** The diagram represents the underlying laws producing the data. Whether you
can measure a variable is irrelevant to whether it belongs. Mark unmeasurable ones — grey
them out — so a reader knows, but include them.

**Anchor image.** A map that only shows roads you've personally driven is not a map.

**Worked example.** Whether Terry is in the room might depend on Terry's mood that day. You
can't measure Terry's mood. It goes on the diagram anyway, greyed out, because it genuinely
causes Terry's presence.

Sometimes you know an unmeasured cause exists but can't name it. Use a placeholder — `U1`
— for "some unknown factor causing both of these":

```dag
node U 1 0 "U1" unobs
node S 0 1 "Shorts" treat
node I 2 1 "Ice cream" out
edge U S
edge U I
note 1 1.75 "U1 makes shorts and ice cream related with no arrow between them"
```

Whatever `U1` is — maybe temperature — when it's high or low it induces a relationship
between shorts and ice cream, even though there's no arrow directly between them.

**Where it breaks.** This matters enormously for identification. The alternative explanations
from Ep 10 show up whether or not you can measure them. Leave them off the diagram and you
won't see the problem; you'll just get the wrong answer.

### The payoff: pathways

**Plain English.** A diagram lets you trace the specific routes connecting your two
variables, and decide which ones you actually asked about.

**Worked example.** Does police presence reduce crime? A simplified diagram — you could
reasonably object that poverty is missing:

```dag
node L 0 0 "Lagged crime"
node P 0 1 "Police per capita" treat
node E 1.3 1.7 "Expected crime payout"
node R 2.6 1 "Crime" out
edge L P
edge L R
edge P R
edge P E
edge E R
```

Now read the routes from police to crime:

| Pathway | What it means | Want it? |
|---|---|---|
| Police → Crime | Police directly stop a crime in progress | **Yes** |
| Police → Expected crime payout → Crime | Deterrence: criminals think they're less likely to get away with it, so commit less | **Yes** |
| Police ← Lagged crime → Crime | High-crime areas get more police assigned, and past crime predicts current crime | **No** |

The first two are the effect of police on crime. The third is police and crime moving
together because both trace back to how much crime there already was — nothing to do with
police reducing anything.

**Where it breaks.** That third pathway is exactly an Ep 10 alternative explanation, now
drawn rather than merely worried about. And that's the real value of these diagrams: look
at one, mark which pathways you want and which you don't, then work out how to shut down
the ones you don't. That's identification, and it's the next several episodes.

## TRAPS

- `Brown hair` is a variable on my diagram → **Actually,** the variable is `hair color`; brown is a value it takes.
- I should only include variables I have data for → **Actually,** unmeasured causes still cause things and still create alternative explanations. Include them, greyed out.
- Two variables need a direct arrow between them to be related → **Actually,** a shared cause like `U1` makes shorts and ice cream related with no arrow between them at all.
- The arrows come from looking at the data → **Actually,** they come from theory. The diagram is what you bring *to* the data.
- A variable can't have two causes / can't cause two things → **Actually,** both are completely normal. The coin flip causes cake and money; money is caused by the flip and by Terry.
- Every pathway between police and crime is part of the effect of police on crime → **Actually,** the lagged-crime route is police and crime moving together for a reason that has nothing to do with policing.

## RECALL

Q: What is a causal diagram, precisely?
A: A set of variables relevant to your question, plus arrows between them, each arrow
meaning one variable causes the other.

Q: Is `red hair` a variable? Explain.
A: No. `Hair color` is the variable; red is one of the values it can take.

Q: Where do the arrows in a causal diagram come from?
A: From theory — your understanding of the setting. Not from the data.

Q: State the rule for when to draw an arrow.
A: Anything that changes the distribution of a variable should be drawn as a cause of it.

Q: Can one variable cause two things? Can one variable have two causes?
A: Both, and both are ordinary. The coin flip causes cake and money; money is caused by
both the coin flip and Terry being in the room.

Q: Should unmeasurable variables appear on the diagram?
A: Yes. Terry's mood can't be measured but genuinely causes whether Terry is in the room,
so it goes on — usually greyed out to signal it's unobserved.

Q: What is `U1` doing on a diagram?
A: Standing in for an unknown unmeasured factor causing two variables — for example
something causing both shorts-wearing and ice-cream-eating, which makes them related with
no direct arrow between them.

Q: Give the two pathways from police to crime that you *want* to count.
A: Police → Crime (directly stopping crimes in progress) and Police → Expected crime payout
→ Crime (deterrence — criminals think they're less likely to get away with it).

Q: Give the pathway from police to crime that you *don't* want, and why.
A: Police ← Lagged crime → Crime. High-crime areas get assigned more police, and past crime
predicts current crime, so the two move together for reasons unrelated to policing.

Q: What's the real value of drawing a causal diagram?
A: It makes the pathways between two variables visible, so you can sort them into the ones
that answer your question and the ones that don't, then work out how to eliminate the
latter.

Q: Two variables have no arrow between them. Can they still be related?
A: Yes — a shared cause such as `U1` will make them move together without any direct causal
link.

## GLOSSARY

- **Node** — a variable on a causal diagram.
- **Arrow** — an assertion that one variable causes another.
- **Unmeasured variable** — a real cause you have no data on; still belongs on the diagram, usually greyed out.
- **Placeholder variable (U1)** — a stand-in for an unknown unmeasured common cause.
- **Pathway** — a route through the diagram connecting two variables, traced along arrows.
