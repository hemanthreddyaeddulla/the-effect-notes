## ONE SENTENCE

If drawing a full causal diagram is hopeless — and that objection is a serious one — you
have four escapes: list only the **joint determinants** of treatment and outcome, use
**front-door methods** and skip the diagram, draw a simplified diagram and **test** it, or
accept you didn't close everything and report a **range** instead of a point.

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

- The objection: this is all too hard, and it depends entirely on having the right diagram. Plenty of people are sceptical you can model social settings this precisely.
- In a sceptic's view the real diagram is treatment → outcome, plus a back door running through **a thousand variables you'll never measure**.
- **Approach 1 — joint determinants.** Skip the full diagram; list the variables that cause both treatment and outcome. Much easier, gets you most of the way.
- **Approach 2 — front doors.** Don't model the confounding at all; find exogenous variation instead.
- **Approach 3 — draw it anyway, then test it.** Your diagram implies certain variables are unrelated. Check with placebo tests.
- **Approach 4 — partial identification.** Assume a range of severities for what you couldn't control, get a range of estimates, report the range.
- This closes Part 1 of the book.

## WHY THIS EXISTS

Everything so far demands a lot: think carefully about your model, draw the causal diagram,
find every pathway from X to Y, close them all, make sure you can actually measure everything
you need — or set up a process isolating just the front doors.

That's difficult, and it all rests on having a very good idea of what your causal diagram is.
You don't have to look hard to find people sceptical that you can model things this precisely.
Every diagram in this series came prefaced with "assume this is right for a minute," and you
can probably think of things left off even the more complex ones. People will disagree about
what a diagram should look like.

For a lot of social science, the objection is: this is simply too complex to write down in a
neat compact diagram, so why bother? In that view the honest diagram is:

```dag
node T 0 0 "Treatment" treat
node Y 2.6 0 "Outcome" out
node S 1.3 1 "...all sorts of stuff..." unobs
edge T Y
edge S T
edge S Y
note 1.3 1.85 "maybe 100 variables. maybe 1,000. you can't measure them."
```

**This is not a trivial objection.** Things really are complex, and you really do have to
simplify to use this process at all — and you could make the case that any such simplification
is too simple. So: are you out of luck? Four answers.

## IDEAS

### 1 · Focus on the joint determinants

**Plain English.** Don't draw the whole diagram. Just list what causes both the treatment and
the outcome.

**Precisely.** You may not be able to write out the full complex diagram, but you can think very
carefully about which variables are likely to sit directly on a simple back door path — the
variables that cause the treatment **and** cause the outcome. Writing down that list of likely
confounders is a far easier task than a complete diagram, and honestly it gets you most of the
way there.

**Anchor image.** You can't map the whole river system. You can find the places where two
streams feed the same pond.

**Where it breaks.** You'll miss things. You won't catch **collider variables**, and you might
get yourself into trouble with **post-treatment bias**. But at minimum you'll be thinking
carefully about what has to be controlled for, even if not in the fully proper way.

This is how a great many researchers actually operate: rather than a full diagram, they ask what
variables plausibly cause both the treatment and the outcome, deal with those, and flag anything
they can't measure as a problem to address.

### 2 · Use front-door methods and skip the diagram

**Plain English.** Don't model the confounding. Find variation that doesn't have any.

**Precisely.** Plenty of researchers reason that there's no point drawing a big causal diagram
and finding all the pathways, because at the end there'll be something they can't measure or
something they forgot. The way around it is to isolate front door paths rather than close back
doors — which is what the Chapter 9 methods are for.

**Where it breaks — Nick's own position, worth having.** There are researchers who think you can
*only* get a causal effect from a randomized controlled experiment. He thinks that's **a bit
harsh**. There are others who think you can only get one from an experiment *or* by isolating
front doors with exogenous variation. He thinks that's **a bit too strong, but closer to his own
view**.

The honest summary: it is simply a lot easier to identify a causal effect when you have a source
of exogenous variation, because it lets you ignore a great deal of stuff that's very hard to
figure out otherwise.

### 3 · Draw a simplified diagram, then test it

**Plain English.** Admit it's simplified, then check you didn't get it *egregiously* wrong.

**Precisely.** Draw the diagram knowing it's a simplification, then use the fact that a causal
diagram implies certain **conditional independences** — pairs of variables that should be
unrelated once you've closed the paths between them. Test those with **placebo tests**.

**Anchor image.** You can't prove the bridge is perfect. You can drive a truck over it.

**Worked example.** The humble version: "I know I'm leaving something out. I don't have the full
picture of all the thousands of interlocking parts that should be here. But my diagram says these
variables should be unrelated — let me check whether they are." You might get lucky and pass, and
conclude that although something's missing, it's probably not that important.

### 4 · Partial identification and sensitivity analysis

**Plain English.** You couldn't control for everything. So assume a range of severities for what
you missed, and report the range of answers that follow.

**Precisely.** **Partial identification** is the broader idea, most commonly applied as
**sensitivity analysis**. Rather than stopping when you can't close every back door, you make a
range of plausible assumptions about the leftover and compute what your estimate would be under
each — arriving at a range rather than a point. You've identified an answer; you just haven't
narrowed it to a single value.

**Anchor image.** You can't weigh the parcel, but you know it's between two and five kilos. That
is genuinely more than nothing.

**Worked example.** You're studying the effect of the average income of the zip code you grew up
in on your adult income. There's a variable you can't control for — say your **parents'
intelligence**. You don't have that data, but it's likely related both to where you grew up and
to your income as an adult. Leaving it out biases your result in some direction, because the
correlation you observe won't represent only the causal effect.

So:

1. **Assume the worst.** Suppose that relationship is *super, super strong*. Calculate how badly biased your estimate would be, and what estimate you'd get.
2. **Assume it's weak.** Do it again. What estimate would you get then?
3. **Report the range.** If it's very strong, the effect of where you grew up on adult earnings is *this* number. If it's very weak, it's *that* number. The answer is somewhere in between.

**Where it breaks.** Nick thinks this one is **a little underutilised**. More on sensitivity
analysis in the very last chapters of the book.

## TRAPS

- The scepticism about causal diagrams is just people avoiding hard work → **Actually,** it's not a trivial objection. Settings really are complex, simplification really is required, and you can argue any simplification is too much.
- Listing joint determinants is the same as drawing a diagram → **Actually,** it's a shortcut that misses colliders and can walk you into post-treatment bias. It gets you most of the way, not all.
- Only randomized controlled trials produce causal effects → **Actually,** Nick calls that a bit harsh. The version he finds closer to right is experiments *or* front-door isolation — and even that he finds a bit too strong.
- If my diagram is simplified, testing it is pointless → **Actually,** placebo tests can show you didn't make any egregious errors, and you may reasonably conclude the omission isn't important.
- If you can't close every back door, you have nothing → **Actually,** partial identification gives you a defensible range. An answer that isn't a single number is still an answer.
- Sensitivity analysis requires the missing variable → **Actually,** the whole point is that you don't have it. You assume a range of strengths for its influence and see how the estimate moves.

## RECALL

Q: What's the core objection this chapter responds to?
A: That drawing a full causal diagram is too hard, especially in social science, and everything
depends on getting it right — so why bother.

Q: What does a sceptic's version of the diagram look like?
A: Treatment → outcome, plus a back door running through "all sorts of stuff" — perhaps a hundred
or a thousand variables you could never measure and control for.

Q: Is that objection dismissed?
A: No. Settings really are complex, simplification really is required to use the method, and you
can argue any such simplification is too simple.

Q: Describe approach 1.
A: Focus only on the joint determinants — the variables likely to cause both the treatment and the
outcome, sitting on simple back door paths. Far easier than a full diagram and gets you most of
the way.

Q: What does approach 1 cost you?
A: You won't catch collider variables and you may run into post-treatment bias, since you're not
doing it in the fully proper way.

Q: Describe approach 2.
A: Skip the diagram and use front-door methods — find exogenous variation and isolate the front
door rather than closing every back door.

Q: What's Nick's view on "only RCTs give causal effects"?
A: He thinks that's a bit harsh. The stronger-but-closer view he holds is that experiments or
front-door isolation with exogenous variation are what make it work — though even that he calls a
bit too strong.

Q: Describe approach 3.
A: Draw a simplified diagram, acknowledge it's incomplete, then use the conditional independences
it implies to run placebo tests and check you haven't made egregious errors.

Q: Describe approach 4.
A: Partial identification — accept you couldn't close everything, assume a range of severities for
what you missed, and compute the corresponding range of estimates.

Q: Walk through the parents' intelligence example.
A: You're estimating the effect of your childhood zip code's average income on your adult income,
but can't control for parents' intelligence, which likely affects both. Assume that influence is
extremely strong and compute an estimate; assume it's weak and compute another; report the range
between them.

Q: Why is it called *partial* identification?
A: Because you have identified an answer — you just haven't narrowed it to a single value.

Q: What does Nick say about how often partial identification is used?
A: He thinks it's a little underutilised.

## GLOSSARY

- **Joint determinants** — variables that cause both the treatment and the outcome; the likely confounders on simple back door paths.
- **Conditional independence** — a pair of variables a causal diagram implies should be unrelated once paths between them are closed.
- **Partial identification** — identifying a range of possible values for an effect rather than a single point.
- **Sensitivity analysis** — the most common application of partial identification: varying assumptions about what you couldn't control for and reporting how the estimate moves.
