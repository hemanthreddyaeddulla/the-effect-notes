## ONE SENTENCE

Four moves turn an unusable diagram into a working one — drop the **unimportant**, bucket
the **redundant**, remove **mediators** you're not studying, and cut the **irrelevant** —
and the skill being trained is figuring out what you're allowed to ignore, because
refusing to ignore anything means never learning anything.

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

- The world is complex and your diagram should reflect it — but a diagram with everything on it is a diagram you can't use.
- **Unimportance:** drop trivial causes. Statistics models the world, it doesn't reproduce it, and every assumption you make is at least a little false.
- **Redundancy:** variables whose arrows go to and from the same places play the same role. Bucket them — race, gender, SES → `demographics`.
- **Mediators:** the variable explaining *why* X causes Y. If you're not studying the mechanism, remove it and draw X → Y directly.
- **Irrelevance:** a variable that isn't on any pathway between treatment and outcome can go, even though it's genuinely part of the DGP.
- Don't oversimplify. **Placebo tests** catch you: if the simplified diagram implies a correlation is zero and you know it isn't, you cut too much.

## WHY THIS EXISTS

You ended the last episode with a mess, and there's an obvious objection to cleaning it up:
the world *is* complex, so shouldn't the diagram be? Yes, genuinely — and you have to be
careful not to simplify into something that no longer represents reality.

But there's a counter-argument. Sometimes the diagram is a mess because you're being hard on
yourself, admitting every conceivable form of complexity. The simplifications below are the
kind that help you see the setting and design a study — they clarify rather than cheat. Some
of them don't reduce the complexity at all; they just put it in a labelled bucket so you can
see what the actual problem is.

## IDEAS

### 1 · Unimportance

**Plain English.** Trivial causes come off.

**Precisely.** There are infinitely many things related in some obscure way to your treatment
or outcome. If something is unimportant — relevant occasionally, a tiny bit — you can drop it
without much worry, even though it technically causes something on your diagram.

**Anchor image.** A map of a city that marks every drain cover is not a better map.

**Where it breaks.** The justification matters: statistics is about **modelling** the world,
not perfectly representing it. Almost every assumption you make is at least slightly false
already. There will always be some gap between your model and a reality too complex to
capture — so a little less is often a little more, if losing an unimportant distraction lets
you see the important structure.

### 2 · Redundancy

**Plain English.** If several variables do the same job on your diagram, treat them as one.

**Precisely.** If a set of variables all have their arrows going into and out of the same
places, then from the point of view of your research question they occupy the same role.
Bucket them into a single node.

**Anchor image.** Four people carrying one end of the same sofa. For the purpose of moving
the sofa, they're one force.

**Worked example.** In the online-courses diagram: race, gender, and socioeconomic status all
cause taking an online course, all cause dropping out, and don't do much else. Three
variables collapse into one:

```dag
node D 0 0 "demographics" 
node O 1.5 0 "Online course" treat
node S 3 0 "Stay in school" out
edge D O
edge D S
edge O S
note 1.5 0.85 "race + gender + SES, bucketed"
```

**Where it breaks.** Remember the bucket is a bucket. When you actually go to *use* the
diagram, `demographics` is still three separate things and you'll need to handle it as such.
This is a presentational simplification for thinking about identification.

Note this doesn't cheat — it doesn't pretend the world is simple. It puts the problem
variables in one place so you can see what the problem is.

### 3 · Remove mediators you aren't studying

**Plain English.** A mediator is the *reason* X causes Y. If you're not studying the reason,
cut it out and connect X to Y directly.

**Precisely.** A **mediator** sits between two variables and explains the mechanism. Whether
it stays depends entirely on your research question, not on the world.

**Anchor image.** A recipe that says "bake at 180°C" rather than describing protein
denaturation. Both are true; only one is what you're cooking from.

**Worked example — keep it or cut it.** Price of cigarettes → lung cancer. Raise the price
and fewer people smoke, so cancer rates fall:

```dag
node P 0 0 "Cigarette price" treat
node N 1.4 0 "Cigarettes smoked"
node C 2.8 0 "Lung cancer" out
edge P N
edge N C
```

`Cigarettes smoked` is the mediator.

- If your question is **the effect of smoking on lung cancer**, and you're only noting that smoking is also driven by price — **keep it**. It's your treatment. You don't remove a mediator just because it's a mediator.
- If your question is **the effect of price on lung cancer** — **cut it**. Draw price → cancer directly, and hold the reason in the back of your mind without studying it.

**Second worked example.** Why would online schooling affect staying in school? Maybe it
changes how affiliated you feel with the college — never set foot on campus and you don't
really think of yourself as one of the Fighting Lions, so you don't come back. Or maybe it's
a difference in education quality.

Interesting questions. But if you're not able to study them, or you're humbly leaving them
for another paper, and all they do on your diagram is sit between treatment and outcome —
pop them out. Draw online classes → stay in school.

### 4 · Irrelevance

**Plain English.** If a variable isn't on any pathway between treatment and outcome, it can
go.

**Precisely.** There's more nuance here, arriving in later videos, but the basic idea holds: a
variable genuinely part of the data generating process may still have nothing to do with your
research question.

**Worked example.** A programme at the college is structured so students tend to reach the end
of a four-year degree still needing one more class — pushing them into an extra term. That
causes plenty of dropout. It is a real determinant of your outcome and it belongs in the DGP.

But it has nothing to do with taking online courses. It's not on any pathway between treatment
and outcome, so for your purposes it comes off.

**Where it breaks.** "Not on a pathway" is doing precise work, and the precision comes in the
next chapter.

### Ignoring things is the actual skill

**Plain English.** Deciding what you can safely ignore is where the leverage is.

**Precisely.** You cannot take the full complexity of the world into account. If you're
unwilling to put your foot down and ignore some wrinkle, the only conclusion available to you
is "the world sure is complex, isn't it" — and you'll never learn anything more specific than
that. Anything you can justifiably ignore is leverage.

### Checking you didn't cut too far

**Plain English.** Ask what your simplified diagram claims, and check whether the data agrees.

**Precisely.** A simplified diagram bakes in assumptions you may not have noticed. If it implies
some relationship must be zero, and you know that relationship isn't zero, you've probably cut
too much. A **placebo test** is one way to find this.

**Worked example.** In the simplified online-schooling diagram, demographics and location end
up with no pathway between them that doesn't run through a **collider** (next chapter). That
implies the correlation between demographics and location should be **zero** in the data.

That is almost certainly false. Different locations have different racial mixes, different age
distributions. So either you simplified too far, or you left out something important — and
either way it's worth reconsidering. Occasionally you'll decide the simplification is justified
and the violation isn't important enough to matter. That's a judgement, but it should be a
conscious one.

## TRAPS

- The world is complex so my diagram should stay complex → **Actually,** a diagram with everything on it is one you can't use. The goal is a diagram that helps you identify an effect.
- Simplifying means pretending the world is simpler than it is → **Actually,** bucketing redundant variables doesn't reduce complexity at all — it packages it so you can see the problem clearly.
- Mediators should always be removed → **Actually,** it depends on the question. If cigarettes smoked is your treatment, it stays. You don't cut a mediator for being one.
- A variable that genuinely causes my outcome must stay on the diagram → **Actually,** the extra-term programme really does cause dropout and still comes off, because it's not on any pathway between treatment and outcome.
- Once bucketed, demographics is one variable → **Actually,** it's still race, gender, and SES. The bucket is for thinking about identification; you'll unpack it when you use the diagram.
- If a simplification looks reasonable it's safe → **Actually,** check what it implies. The online diagram quietly claimed demographics and location are uncorrelated, which is plainly false.

## RECALL

Q: Name the four ways to simplify a causal diagram.
A: Unimportance, redundancy, removing mediators, and irrelevance.

Q: What justifies dropping unimportant variables?
A: Statistics is about modelling the world, not perfectly representing it. Every assumption is
at least slightly false, so losing a trivial distraction to see the important structure is a
good trade.

Q: When can several variables be bucketed into one?
A: When their arrows go into and out of the same places, so from the point of view of your
research question they play the same role — like race, gender, and SES becoming `demographics`.

Q: Does bucketing variables pretend the world is simple?
A: No. It packages the complexity into one labelled node so you can see what the problem is.
You'll unpack it when you actually use the diagram.

Q: What is a mediator?
A: A variable sitting between two others that explains *why* one causes the other — like
cigarettes smoked between cigarette price and lung cancer.

Q: When do you keep a mediator and when do you cut it?
A: Keep it if the mechanism is your research question — if smoking is your treatment, it stays.
Cut it if you're studying price → cancer and aren't investigating the mechanism.

Q: Give the online-courses mediators Nick removes.
A: How affiliated you feel with the college (never being on campus, so you don't think of
yourself as one of the Fighting Lions) and differences in education quality.

Q: What is the irrelevance criterion?
A: A variable not on any pathway between your treatment and outcome can be dropped, even though
it is genuinely part of the data generating process.

Q: Give the irrelevance example.

Q: Why is deciding what to ignore described as the actual skill?

## GLOSSARY

- **Mediator** — a variable that sits between two others and explains why one causes the other.
- **Redundancy** — several variables playing the same role on a diagram, collapsible into one node.
- **Irrelevance** — a variable that is part of the data generating process but sits on no pathway between treatment and outcome.
- **Collider** — a variable caused by two others; introduced properly in Ep 17.
- **Placebo test** — checking a relationship your diagram implies should be zero; a non-zero result signals oversimplification or an omission.
