## ONE SENTENCE

Drawing a causal diagram is two steps — list the variables that explain why you'd see
your data, then use prior knowledge to decide which causes which — and the variables you
must not miss are the ones that cause **both** your treatment and your outcome, plus
anything at all that causes the treatment.

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

- Nobody hands you a diagram. You have to draw your own.
- **Step 1: list the variables.** Anything that helps explain why you'd see the data you see — measured or not.
- **Step 2: draw the arrows.** This is where prior knowledge and prior research come in. You cannot draw a diagram while refusing to assume anything.
- **Highest priority:** variables causing both treatment and outcome. Second: anything causing the **treatment**, because how well you understand *why people got treated* determines how well you can do causal inference at all.
- **Don't include everything.** In social science the list of faintly relevant things is infinite. Trivial causes can be left off.
- Variables cause each other too, not just the treatment and outcome.
- Your first attempt will be an **absolute mess**. That's normal, and simplifying it is the next episode.

## WHY THIS EXISTS

You know what a causal diagram is and what an arrow means. But being handed one is not a
thing that happens — if you want to use this to identify an effect, you have to be able to
build one from your own understanding of a setting.

The honest framing: you're trying to write down what you think the data generating process
looks like. You observed some relationship between a treatment and an outcome. What set of
underlying laws would produce that relationship, in that direction, at that strength?

## IDEAS

### Step 1: list the variables

**Plain English.** Write down everything that helps explain why the data looks the way it
does.

**Precisely.** Enumerate the measurements or concepts that plausibly bear on your data
generating process. Some will be measured variables you can get hold of. Others will be
abstract concepts you can't measure — include them anyway.

**Worked example.** Nick's own study: does taking online courses affect your chances of
staying in college? Maybe it changes how much you learn, or your affiliation with the
college.

Two variables to start with — the ones the question names:

- **Treatment:** was the class online or face-to-face?
- **Outcome:** did you stay in school or drop out?

Then ask what determines each of those:

| Variable | Why it's there |
|---|---|
| Preferences about education | Someone drawn to online courses takes more of them. Unmeasurable — include it anyway. |
| Demographics — age, race, gender | Related to preferences, and to dropout rates |
| Socioeconomic status | Same |
| Schedule freedom | Determines what kind of class you can take |
| Available time / work hours | Work all day and face-to-face classes are impossible — they run during the day |
| Internet access | No connection, no online course |

### The two categories you must not miss

**Plain English.** Anything causing both treatment and outcome. And anything causing the
treatment.

**Precisely.** Variables that cause both are the ones that will produce a relationship
between treatment and outcome that isn't your effect. Separately, and more broadly, **anything
that causes the treatment** matters enormously, because a great deal of causal inference
comes down to how well you understand *why different people got different levels of
treatment*.

**Anchor image.** Before you can say the medicine worked, you have to know who got it and
why. If you don't know how the pills were handed out, nothing you observe afterwards is
interpretable.

**Worked example.** Work hours, in the online-courses study. Someone working a lot:

- is more likely to want an online course, so it doesn't clash with the job, **and**
- is more likely to drop out — it's too much time on top of work, or they got a promotion and no longer need the degree.

Related to both. That one absolutely has to be on the diagram.

**Where it breaks.** The difficulty in causal inference is usually that you *don't* fully
understand why some people got treated and others didn't. The less you know about that, the
more back-door trouble you invite in — which is exactly what the next chapter is about.

### Don't put the whole world on it

**Plain English.** You could keep going until you'd drawn all of society. Don't.

**Precisely.** Something being *technically* a cause doesn't earn it a place. If a variable
has only a very minor effect or relationship, you can generally leave it off. In the social
sciences the list of things that might be slightly relevant is effectively infinite, and
trying to include all of it is a trap.

**Worked example.** Whether you live near a quiet café might affect whether you take an
online course — somewhere pleasant to work. For a handful of people, that's genuinely true.
It's not a major determinant for most. Leave it off.

**Where it breaks.** Judgement call, and you can get it wrong in both directions. Ep 15 gives
you a way to check.

### Step 2: draw the arrows

**Plain English.** Go pair by pair. Does this cause that? Why do you think so? Which
direction?

**Precisely.** This is where you lean on prior knowledge — things you've learned from
previous research, or things you simply know about how the world works. It's essentially
impossible to draw a causal diagram while refusing to make assumptions or stand on any
existing knowledge. You can't learn more without staking something you already believe.

**Worked example — the reasoning, one arrow at a time.**

- **Almost everything → dropout.** Demographic background characteristics all relate to dropping out; rates differ across those groups. Not necessarily *causing* dropout, but related somehow, which you might shorthand as an arrow.
- **Internet access → online classes, but not dropout.** Bad internet and you won't take an online course. Little direct effect on whether you drop out.
- **Variables cause each other, too.** Socioeconomic status → preferences: how you were raised shaped your feelings about online courses. Socioeconomic status → internet access: grow up with money and a decent connection is probably available.

That last chain is a theory Nick holds about the world. He came up with it himself — but if
you're doing the best job you can, you go looking for research that backs a given arrow up.
Basing arrows on evidence rather than invention is also how a field ends up agreeing on
diagrams.

### The result is a mess, and that's normal

**Plain English.** Your first diagram will be unusable. Everyone's is.

**Precisely.** A long list of variables, arrows ping-ponging in every direction, plus
unlabelled unobserved variables `U1` through `U5` explaining why things are related when
neither causes the other. This is the social sciences — everything is related to everything.

**Worked example.** `U5` causes both socioeconomic status and location. Why not an arrow? You
wouldn't confidently say socioeconomic status causes your location, and you wouldn't
confidently say location causes socioeconomic status — at least not strongly enough to stake
a claim. So you say they're related and give them a common cause.

```dag
node U 1 0 "U5" unobs
node S 0 1 "Socioeconomic status"
node L 2 1 "Location"
edge U S
edge U L
note 1 1.8 "related, but you won't commit to a direction"
```

Someone with a different theory might draw it differently — they might say socioeconomic
status causes location. Your theory determines your diagram, which is another reason to
ground arrows in sources.

**Where it breaks.** You can't do much with a mess. Simplification is next.

## TRAPS

- I'll stay objective and avoid assumptions while drawing → **Actually,** it's essentially impossible. Arrows come from prior knowledge and prior research; refusing to assume anything means refusing to draw.
- Only include variables you can measure → **Actually,** preferences about education can't be measured and still belong. Unmeasured causes still cause things.
- Being thorough means including every possible cause → **Actually,** the list is infinite in social science. The quiet café is a real cause for a few people and still gets cut.
- The important variables are the ones causing the outcome → **Actually,** the priority is variables causing *both*, then anything causing the **treatment** — because understanding why people got treated is what makes causal inference possible.
- Arrows only run from other variables into the treatment and outcome → **Actually,** variables cause each other. SES causes preferences and internet access.
- My diagram came out messy, so I did it wrong → **Actually,** that's the normal first result. Ep 15 is entirely about what to do next.

## RECALL

Q: What are the two steps in drawing a causal diagram?
A: List the variables relevant to the data generating process, then use prior knowledge and
research to decide which variables cause which, and draw the arrows.

Q: Why can't you draw a causal diagram without making assumptions?
A: The arrows come from prior knowledge and previous research. If you refuse to rest on
anything you already believe, there's nothing to draw arrows from — and you can't learn more
without staking something.

Q: Which variables are the highest priority to include?
A: Ones that cause both the treatment and the outcome, and — more broadly — anything at all
that causes the treatment.

Q: Why does anything causing the treatment matter so much?
A: Because much of causal inference depends on understanding why different people got
different levels of treatment. The less you understand that, the more back-door problems you
let in.

Q: In the online-courses study, why do work hours have to be on the diagram?
A: They cause both. Working a lot makes an online course more attractive (it doesn't clash
with the job) and makes dropping out more likely (too much time, or a promotion removes the
need for the degree).

Q: You realise living near a quiet café slightly affects whether people take online courses. Include it?
A: No. It's a genuine cause for a handful of people but not a major determinant, and in
social science the list of faintly relevant causes is infinite.

Q: Should unmeasurable variables like "preferences about education" be included?
A: Yes. Whether you can measure something has nothing to do with whether it causes things.

Q: Do arrows only run into the treatment and outcome?
A: No — variables cause each other. Socioeconomic status causes preferences, and it causes
internet access.

Q: Why draw `U5` causing both socioeconomic status and location instead of an arrow between them?
A: Because you wouldn't confidently claim either direction. They're related, so you give them
a common cause rather than assert a causal direction you can't defend.

Q: Your first diagram is a chaotic mess. What does that mean?
A: Nothing has gone wrong — that's the normal first result, because in social science
everything relates to everything. Simplification is the next step.

Q: Why is it better to base arrows on previous research than on your own reasoning?
A: It makes the diagram defensible and helps a field converge on the same diagram, rather
than everyone drawing from private intuition.

## GLOSSARY

- **Treatment** — the variable whose effect you want to measure.
- **Outcome** — the variable you want to see the effect on.
- **Prior knowledge** — what you already believe about a setting, from research or experience; the source of every arrow.
- **Unobserved variable (U1, U2, …)** — an unmeasured cause, often used to explain why two variables are related without committing to a direction between them.
