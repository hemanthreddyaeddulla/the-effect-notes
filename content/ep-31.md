## ONE SENTENCE

A binary predictor in a regression is just a **comparison of means**, and a categorical variable
is a set of binary indicators with **one left out as the reference group** — which means every
coefficient, and every significance test on it, is relative to whichever category you happened to
omit.

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

- A **binary predictor compares means**. Regress income on treatment, get 5,000: mean income is 5,000 higher among the treated. Same as a two-sample t-test.
- Why bother with regression? Because you can **add control variables**.
- It follows from the mantra: with a binary variable the only sensible one-unit change is 0 → 1.
- The **intercept** becomes the mean of the group coded zero.
- A **categorical** variable becomes one **binary indicator** per category — and you must **leave one out**.
- The omitted category is the **reference group**. Every other coefficient is a difference *from it*.
- Change the reference group and **every coefficient and every significance test changes**.
- To test the variable as a whole, use a **joint F test**.

## WHY THIS EXISTS

You've been working on the right-hand side of a regression — transformations, non-linear terms.
Now something you'll see constantly: predictors that are categories.

Lots of variables are like this. Did you get the treatment or not — you can't half get it. Which
continent are you on — seven mutually exclusive options, and you can't be on two at once (barring
something odd at a border). So how do you put those on the right-hand side, whether as your
treatment or as a control?

## IDEAS

### A binary predictor compares means

**Plain English.** The coefficient is the gap between the group averages.

**Precisely.** Including a binary predictor gives you a comparison of means — exactly the same
comparison as a two-sample t-test.

**Anchor image.** A step, not a slope. There's only one place to put your foot down.

**Worked example.** Regress income on whether you got the treatment, and the coefficient comes back
**5,000**. That says the mean income is 5,000 higher for the group that got the treatment than for
the group that didn't.

**Why use regression instead of a t-test?** Because you can add control variables. Then you can say:
*controlling for everything I need to identify my effect, the effect of the treatment on the outcome
is [coefficient]* — the mean of the outcome rises by that much going from 0 to 1. You also get a
standard error and a significance test on the coefficient for free, letting you check whether the
average outcome differs statistically between the yes group and the no group.

**Why it works.** Return to the mantra: a one-unit increase in X is associated with a
coefficient-sized change in Y. Now make X binary. There's only one one-unit change that makes any
sense at all — **going from 0 (not in the group) to 1 (in the group)**. So the coefficient gives the
jump in the outcome you'd see, on average, going from a no to a yes.

### The intercept becomes the reference mean

**Plain English.** Set the binary variable to zero and only the intercept is left — so the intercept
*is* the mean of that group.

**Worked example.** Regress sales on whether it's currently winter. Average sales are **10 in
winter** and **15 in not-winter**.

| Term | Coefficient | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Intercept | **15** | Set winter = 0 and only the intercept remains, so it must be the not-winter average |
| Winter | **−5** | Winter sales are 5 lower than not-winter |

Your model should give the best prediction it can — the winter average for winter, the not-winter
average for not-winter. That's exactly what these two numbers deliver.

**Where it breaks — the key idea.** The coefficient on winter is **relative to something**: winter
versus not-winter. A binary coefficient is always about being relative to something else. That's the
bridge to categorical variables with more than two categories.

### Categorical variables: one indicator per category, minus one

**Plain English.** Split the categories into yes/no variables, then throw one away.

**Precisely.** Take each category and turn it into its own binary indicator. For continents: are you
in Asia, yes or no? In Europe, yes or no? In South America, yes or no? And so on — seven categories,
seven binary indicators.

Then, when you run the regression, **you have to leave one out.** The reason is statistical (the
book has the full explanation), but you do have to. Whichever you leave out becomes your **reference
group**.

**A note on vocabulary.** You may have heard binary indicators called **dummies**. Nick avoids the
term — if you're talking to someone who doesn't know econometrics and you say "I put the female
dummy in the model," that may not go over well. **Binary indicator** is the better term.

**Where it breaks.** People in the omitted group are still in your data. They just don't get their
own coefficient — they become the intercept, and everything else is measured relative to them.

Leave out Asia, and the coefficient on Europe is **not** "the effect of being in Europe." It's **the
difference in means between Europe and Asia.** And South America's coefficient is South America
versus Asia. And Africa's is Africa versus Asia. Everything is relative to the same reference group.

Because of this, **the individual coefficients don't mean much by themselves.** They only mean
something relative to the reference group you selected.

### The reference group is arbitrary — and so are your significance tests

**Plain English.** Pick a different omitted category and every number in the table changes.

**Worked example.** Income regressed on country, with three countries in the data:

| Country | Average income |
|---|---|
| France | 30 |
| Gambia | 30.5 |
| New Zealand | 33 |

**Reference group: France.**

| Term | Coefficient | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Intercept | **30** | France's average — the prediction when both other indicators are 0 |
| Gambia | **0.5** | 30.5 − 30 |
| New Zealand | **3** | 33 − 30 |

**Now switch the reference group to Gambia.** France re-enters the model with its own term:

| Term | Coefficient | Why |
|---|---|---|
| France | **−0.5** | 30 − 30.5 |
| New Zealand | **2.5** | 33 − 30.5 |

**Where it breaks.** All the coefficients — and therefore **all the statistical significance tests**
— are completely arbitrary, determined by whichever reference group you happened to choose.

So if you want to test something, make sure it's the test you actually want. Asking whether Gambia's
coefficient is significant asks whether the *difference from France* is significant. If that isn't
your question, pick a different reference group. And you can't look at Gambia's effect on its own at
all — it's always relative to something else.

### Testing the variable as a whole: the joint F test

**Plain English.** To ask "does country matter at all?", test all the indicators at once.

**Precisely.** If you want to know whether country overall is a decent predictor of income, run a
**joint F test** on the indicators — a test of the joint significance of all the coefficients
together. The null hypothesis is that **all the country coefficients are zero**, meaning every
country is exactly the same as the reference group. An F test tells you whether you can reject that.

## TRAPS

- A binary predictor's coefficient is a slope like any other → **Actually,** it's a comparison of means — the same thing a two-sample t-test gives you.
- The intercept is a technical artefact → **Actually,** with a binary predictor it's the mean of the group coded zero. It's meaningful.
- The coefficient on Europe is the effect of being in Europe → **Actually,** it's the difference between Europe and whichever category you omitted.
- I can read one category's coefficient on its own → **Actually,** it only means something relative to the reference group. On its own it doesn't have an interpretation.
- The reference group is a technical detail that doesn't affect results → **Actually,** changing it changes every coefficient and every significance test.
- Observations in the omitted category get dropped → **Actually,** they're still in the data — they become the intercept.
- To test whether a categorical variable matters, look at whether its coefficients are significant → **Actually,** those are pairwise comparisons against an arbitrary reference. Use a joint F test.

## RECALL

Q: What does a binary predictor in a regression actually do?
A: It compares means — the coefficient is the difference in the outcome's mean between the yes group
and the no group.

Q: Which classical test is that equivalent to?
A: A two-sample t-test.

Q: So why use a regression instead?
A: Because you can add control variables and interpret the coefficient as the effect adjusting for
them — plus you get a standard error and significance test.

Q: How does the mantra explain the binary case?
A: A one-unit change in X is associated with a coefficient-sized change in Y, and for a binary
variable the only sensible one-unit change is going from 0 to 1.

Q: Sales are 10 in winter and 15 otherwise. Give the intercept and the winter coefficient.
A: Intercept 15 (the not-winter average) and −5 on winter.

Q: Why must the intercept equal the not-winter average?
A: Setting winter to zero leaves only the intercept, so it has to deliver the best prediction for
not-winter, which is that group's mean.

Q: How do you include a categorical variable with seven categories?
A: Create a binary indicator for each category, then include all but one in the regression.

Q: What is the omitted category called, and what happens to the observations in it?
A: The reference group. Those observations remain in the data — they become the intercept.

Q: Why does Nick prefer "binary indicator" to "dummy"?
A: Because saying something like "I put the female dummy in the model" to someone who doesn't know
econometrics may not go over well.

Q: With Asia omitted, what does the coefficient on Europe mean?
A: The difference in means between Europe and Asia — not the effect of being in Europe.

Q: France 30, Gambia 30.5, New Zealand 33, with France as reference. Give all three terms.
A: Intercept 30, Gambia 0.5, New Zealand 3.

Q: Now switch the reference group to Gambia. What do France and New Zealand become?
A: France −0.5 and New Zealand 2.5.

Q: What's the implication of that for significance tests?
A: They're arbitrary — they test against whichever reference group you picked. A coefficient's
significance answers "is this different from the reference?", which may not be your question.

Q: How do you test whether a categorical variable matters overall?
A: A joint F test on all its indicators, with the null that every one of those coefficients is zero
— i.e. every category is identical to the reference group.

## GLOSSARY

- **Binary predictor** — a 0/1 variable; its coefficient is a difference in means.
- **Binary indicator** — a 0/1 variable marking membership of one category; preferred term over "dummy".
- **Reference group** — the omitted category, against which all other coefficients are measured.
- **Joint F test** — a test of the null that a set of coefficients are all zero simultaneously.
