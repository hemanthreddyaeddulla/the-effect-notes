## ONE SENTENCE

After all that matching, estimation is almost trivial — take the weighted average of the treated
group, the weighted average of the control group, and compare — with the real subtleties sitting
in **standard errors** (let the software do them) and in **doubly robust** estimators that survive
getting either your matching model *or* your regression model wrong.

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

- Estimation: **weighted average of the treated, weighted average of the control, compare.** That's it.
- Which average you get depends on which direction you matched: control-to-treated gives the **ATT**, treated-to-control gives the **ATUT**, and combining them by the proportion treated gives the **ATE**.
- **Standard errors are hard.** The matching procedure itself injects uncertainty. A plain weighted regression gives standard errors that are **too small**.
- Let your software handle them — or use **bootstrap** standard errors, redoing the whole matching each resample.
- You can **combine matching with regression**: apply your matching weights inside a regression to get interactions and everything else regression is good at.
- **Doubly robust** estimators are right if **either** the matching model or the outcome model is correct. Both wrong and you're still in trouble.

## WHY THIS EXISTS

Odd to leave estimation to the very end of a chapter — except that once you've done the matching,
it's relatively simple and straightforward. All the difficult questions were the matching
questions. The actual estimation is quite a lot easier.

## IDEAS

### The estimate itself

**Plain English.** Average the treated, average the control, subtract.

**Precisely.** Take your set of matched observations, or the weights you generated for your
weighted matched sample, and calculate the **weighted average** of the outcome for the treated
group and the weighted average for the control group. Compare those two. Done.

Since you've already done the matching, the resulting comparison gives you the effect of treatment
on the outcome.

**Anchor image.** All the work was in setting the table. Serving dinner takes a minute.

### Which treatment effect average you get

**Plain English.** It depends which group you made resemble which.

**Precisely.**

| What you matched | What you get |
|---|---|
| Matched the **control group to look like the treatment group** | **Average treatment on the treated (ATT)** |
| Matched the **treated group to look like the control group** | **Average treatment on the untreated (ATUT)** |
| Both, averaged together by the proportion of people treated | **Average treatment effect (ATE)** |

There are also ways to choose your **inverse probability weights** specifically to obtain the kind
of treatment effect average you're after.

### Standard errors: hand them to the software

**Plain English.** Matching adds uncertainty of its own, and naive standard errors miss it.

**Precisely.** Standard errors with matching are tougher than in regression modelling. Nick's advice
is essentially: don't listen to him on this — pick software that does matching estimation and **let
it calculate the standard errors for you**, because a lot of difficult things go on in those
calculations.

**The reason:** the **matching procedure itself injects uncertainty** that you need to account for.
If you simply do the comparison — regress the outcome on treatment using your matching weights — it
will **not** properly account for all the uncertainty that went into the matching process, and your
standard errors will be **too small**.

**Anchor image.** Timing a race with a stopwatch you also had to build. The stopwatch's own error
belongs in the result.

### The exception: bootstrap standard errors

**Plain English.** Resample your data over and over, redoing the *whole* procedure each time.

**Precisely.** **Bootstrapping** means randomly drawing a new sample from your data many times over.
Every single time, you redo **the entire matching process and the entire estimation process**, and
get an estimate. Do that repeatedly and you're mimicking the sampling distribution yourself.

**Why it works here:** because you redo the matching every time, it **incorporates the uncertainty of
the matching process** into your estimate. So you get the sampling distribution as a whole and can
calculate your own standard errors.

**Where it breaks.** This works as long as you're using a **weighted** approach. A procedure where
you're picking samples to be in or out of the control group doesn't work so well with bootstrap
standard errors. Technical details are in the chapter.

### Combining matching with regression

**Plain English.** Matching weights slot straight into a regression, so you get regression's
conveniences too.

**Precisely.** You don't have to use matching by itself. Combine it with regression to do the things
regression is good at.

**Worked example.** Suppose you want an **interaction term** — how the effect varies across groups.
That's awkward with a plain weighted average, and very easy in a regression. So just add your
matching weights into the regression model and you're set.

Broockman did exactly this in the legislator study: he didn't just take a weighted average with his
matched group. He took his **matching weights and applied them to a regression model that had an
interaction term in it**.

### Doubly robust estimators

**Plain English.** Two chances to be right. Get either model correct and you're fine.

**Precisely.** You could misspecify two different things:

- **Your matching procedure** — picking the wrong variables, or the wrong functional form. This is a model of **how treatment gets assigned**.
- **Your regression model** — the wrong functional form, matching variables in the wrong place, a missing interaction term. This is a model of **the outcome**.

A **doubly robust estimator** gives you the right answer as long as **at least one of those is
correct**. If one is wrong, you're okay — and you won't necessarily know which one saved you. If
**both** are wrong, you're still in trouble.

**Anchor image.** A backup system. If one fails, the other catches you. Both fail and you fall.

**Where it breaks.** Double robustness isn't a method — it's a **property** that certain estimators
have. Several estimators are doubly robust; some are harder to implement, some easier.

### Inverse probability weighting with regression adjustment

**Plain English.** Weight, then fit an outcome model separately in each group, then predict everyone
both ways and compare.

**Precisely.** A relatively easy doubly robust estimator:

1. Do your **propensity score estimation** and get inverse probability weights, as before.
2. Take **just the treated data**, apply those weights, and estimate a regression model of the outcome — a model of what would happen if everybody were treated.
3. Take **just the untreated data**, apply the weights, and estimate the same regression model — a model of what would happen if everybody were untreated.
4. Predict values for everybody under both models: here's what I predict for you if treated, here's what I predict for you if untreated. **Compare the two.**

That comparison — everyone's predicted treated outcome against everyone's predicted untreated
outcome — is your treatment effect estimate.

**Why it's doubly robust.** You've got inverse probability weighting in there for the matching, and
regression adjustment in there because you estimated a regression model for both groups. So if you
made a specification error in your **propensity score** estimation, you're fine as long as your
regression model is good. And if you made an error specifying your **regression model**, you're fine
as long as the inverse probability weighting model was specified properly.

## TRAPS

- Estimation after matching must be complicated → **Actually,** it's a weighted average per group and a comparison. All the difficulty was in the matching.
- The treatment effect average you get is fixed by the method → **Actually,** it depends on the direction you matched — control-to-treated gives the ATT, treated-to-control the ATUT.
- Regress the outcome on treatment with matching weights and you're done → **Actually,** that ignores the uncertainty the matching procedure itself introduced, so your standard errors come out too small.
- Bootstrapping means resampling and recomputing the final estimate → **Actually,** you must redo the **entire** matching process on each resample; that's what captures the matching uncertainty.
- Bootstrap standard errors work for any matching approach → **Actually,** they work well with weighted approaches, less well when you're selecting observations in or out.
- Matching and regression are alternatives → **Actually,** you can put matching weights inside a regression — which is what Broockman did, to get an interaction term.
- Doubly robust means twice as accurate → **Actually,** it means correct if *either* model is right. Both wrong and it fails.
- Doubly robust is a specific estimator → **Actually,** it's a property several different estimators have.

## RECALL

Q: How do you estimate a treatment effect once matching is done?
A: Take the weighted average of the outcome in the treated group and in the control group, and
compare them.

Q: You matched the control group to look like the treated group. Which average do you get?
A: The average treatment on the treated.

Q: And if you matched the treated group to look like the control group?
A: The average treatment on the untreated.

Q: How do you get the average treatment effect?
A: Obtain both the ATT and the ATUT and average them together based on the proportion of people
treated.

Q: Why are standard errors harder with matching than with regression?
A: The matching procedure itself injects uncertainty that must be accounted for.

Q: What happens if you just regress the outcome on treatment using matching weights?
A: It fails to account for the uncertainty from the matching process, so your standard errors come out
too small.

Q: What's Nick's practical advice on matching standard errors?
A: Let the software you're using calculate them — the computations involve a lot of difficult details.

Q: Describe bootstrapping in this context.
A: Repeatedly draw a new random sample from your data and, each time, redo the entire matching and
estimation process — mimicking the sampling distribution yourself.

Q: Why does bootstrapping solve the matching uncertainty problem?
A: Because you redo the matching on every resample, the uncertainty of the matching process is
incorporated into the resulting distribution.

Q: When does bootstrapping not work well?
A: With procedures that pick observations in or out of the control group, rather than weighted
approaches.

Q: Why combine matching with regression?
A: To get things regression does well — an interaction term, for example, is awkward with a weighted
average but easy in a regression with matching weights applied.

Q: What did Broockman actually do?
A: Applied his matching weights to a regression model containing an interaction term, rather than
taking a plain weighted average.

Q: What two models could be misspecified in a matching analysis?
A: The matching procedure — a model of how treatment gets assigned — and the regression model — a model
of the outcome.

Q: What does a doubly robust estimator guarantee?
A: The right answer as long as at least one of those two models is correctly specified. If both are
wrong, it still fails.

Q: Is double robustness a method?
A: No — it's a property that various estimators possess.

Q: Give the four steps of inverse probability weighting with regression adjustment.
A: Estimate the propensity score and get inverse probability weights; use them on just the treated data
to fit an outcome regression; do the same on just the untreated data; then predict everyone's outcome
under both models and compare.

Q: Why is that procedure doubly robust?
A: If the propensity score estimation is misspecified you're saved by a good regression model, and if
the regression model is misspecified you're saved by correctly specified inverse probability weights.

## GLOSSARY

- **Bootstrapping** — repeatedly resampling the data and redoing the whole procedure to construct a sampling distribution empirically.
- **Doubly robust estimator** — an estimator that is consistent if either the treatment assignment model or the outcome model is correctly specified.
- **Inverse probability weighting with regression adjustment** — a doubly robust procedure that fits a weighted outcome regression separately in the treated and untreated groups and compares predictions.
- **Regression adjustment** — modelling the outcome with a regression, as opposed to modelling treatment assignment.
