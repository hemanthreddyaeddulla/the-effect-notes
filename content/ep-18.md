## ONE SENTENCE

All of this assumes your diagram is right — so test it: pick any two variables, close
every path between them, and the relationship in the data should be **zero**; if it isn't,
your diagram is missing something.

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

- Closing bad paths identifies your effect **only if the diagram is correct**. It's a set of assumptions, and modelling a complex world means you left something out.
- A **placebo test** takes your assumptions and derives a relationship that should be **zero**. A non-zero result means you've missed something.
- Simple version: your ad this month can't have raised sales *last* month. If your design says it did, the design is broken.
- **Every diagram is full of placebo tests.** Pick any two variables, list every path, close them all — the observed relationship should be zero.
- **Colliders do a lot of work here**, because between non-treatment variables many paths are pre-closed and need no action.
- Failing isn't fatal. The goal was never 100% correct assumptions — it's assumptions close enough not to mess you up too much.

## WHY THIS EXISTS

Everything so far has one enormous condition attached. You list the paths, sort them, close
the bad ones — and you've identified your effect *if the diagram is a reasonable
representation of the actual data generating process*.

That's hard. The world is very complex, you're modelling it, and you are definitely leaving
out detail. So the natural question is whether there's any way to check — and thankfully
there is, at least for finding out whether you got it *particularly* wrong.

## IDEAS

### What a placebo test is

**Plain English.** Use your own assumptions to predict something that must be zero. Then go
look.

**Precisely.** A placebo test takes the assumptions you've made — and writing down a causal
diagram *is* a set of assumptions — and derives from them a relationship or effect that should
be zero if those assumptions hold. Run your actual research design on it. A non-zero answer
means an assumption is wrong: something is missing, some control unaccounted for.

**Anchor image.** Calibrating a scale by weighing nothing. If it doesn't read zero, every
number it has ever given you is suspect.

**Worked example.** Does a television advertisement increase your sales? You design a study —
you'll control for a certain set of variables, and you believe that identifies the effect.

Now check your own reasoning. If the assumptions hold, this month's advertising should improve
**next** month's sales. It should have no effect whatsoever on **last** month's sales — that's
not possible.

So run the identical design, same controls, on last month's sales. If this month's advertising
appears to have raised last month's sales, that effect should have been zero and isn't. You've
missed something. Back to the drawing board.

### Every diagram is packed with placebo tests

**Plain English.** Take any two variables at all, shut every route between them, and they
should be unrelated.

**Precisely.** Pick **any** two variables on the diagram — not just treatment and outcome.
Write out every path between them. Close all of them. The relationship between those two
variables in your data should now be zero, because paths aren't only causal effects — they're
*any* reason two variables could be related. Remove all of them and nothing is left.

If they're still related, there's a path you left off the diagram, or a variable you should
have been conditioning on. Which means you don't yet know what to control for to identify your
real effect.

**Anchor image.** Seal every door and window in a room and the air should stop moving. A draught
means a gap you haven't found.

### Worked example: income and drugs

**Plain English.** In the wine diagram, pick two variables that aren't the treatment or the
outcome and test the implication.

**Worked example.** Take **income** and **drugs** from the Ep 16 wine diagram.

```dag
node U 1.5 0 "U1" unobs
node N 0.8 0.8 "Income" treat
node H 2.2 0.8 "Health"
node W 0 1.8 "Wine"
node L 3 1.8 "Lifespan" bad
node D 1.5 2.7 "Drugs" out
edge U N
edge U H
edge N W
edge N L
edge H W
edge H L
edge W D
edge D L
edge W L
note 1.5 3.5 "lifespan is a collider on every income-drugs path through it"
```

**Step 1 — list the paths.** Income → Wine → Drugs. Income ← U1 → Health → Wine → Drugs. Then
a batch running through lifespan: Income → Lifespan ← Drugs, Income → Wine → Lifespan ← Drugs,
and more.

**Step 2 — throw out the pre-closed ones.** Every path through lifespan has **both arrows
pointing at lifespan** — income → lifespan, and drugs → lifespan. Lifespan is a **collider** on
all of them, so they're pre-closed. You don't need to worry about them, and you emphatically
don't want to control for lifespan. Colliders come into play a lot once you look at pairs of
variables that aren't the treatment and outcome.

**Step 3 — close what's left.** The remaining paths both run through wine. So control for wine
— look only at wine drinkers, or only at non-drinkers, or condition on how much wine you drink
statistically.

**Step 4 — the prediction.** Having done that, the relationship between income and drugs should
be **zero**. If it isn't, there's a route from income to drugs going through neither wine nor
lifespan, and your diagram is incomplete.

**Where it breaks.** Writing the paths out already tells you it's deficient. What the diagram
claims is that the *only* reason your income relates to your drug taking is that wine affects
drug taking. Obviously insufficient — there's a lot missing from the drug-taking picture. You
can predict this test fails before running it.

### What to do when you fail

**Plain English.** Failing isn't a verdict, it's information. Sometimes you fix it, sometimes
you note it and move on.

**Precisely.** The goal in causal inference was never assumptions that are 100% correct — that's
impossible. The goal is assumptions close enough to correct that they don't mess you up too
much.

So look at the size of what you found. If income and drugs are still related but only slightly,
maybe that's acceptable: you're leaving a little out and it won't be a huge problem.

Then do the reflection regardless: *why* would income and drugs be related other than through
wine? You'll come up with a few reasons. If any look important, put them on the diagram, redo
the path analysis, work out what to close, and re-run the placebo tests.

**Anchor image.** A smoke alarm. It doesn't tell you the house is lost — it tells you to go
look, and sometimes the answer is "the toast."

**Where it breaks.** The third option is legitimate: decide it isn't much of a problem — but
now you *know* there's a problem, and you can tell the readers of your study that it's there.

## TRAPS

- I closed all the back doors, so my effect is identified → **Actually,** only if the diagram is right. That's an assumption, and placebo tests are how you probe it.
- A placebo test needs a special design or extra data → **Actually,** it's your same design and same data pointed at a relationship that should be zero.
- Placebo tests are only for the treatment and outcome → **Actually,** any pair of variables on the diagram gives you one. Every diagram is full of them.
- Failing a placebo test means the study is worthless → **Actually,** the goal was assumptions close enough not to mess you up. A small violation may be acceptable — knowingly.
- I should control for lifespan to close those income–drugs paths → **Actually,** lifespan is a collider on all of them. They're pre-closed, and controlling for it would open them.
- If I pass a placebo test, the diagram is correct → **Actually,** it tells you that you didn't get things *particularly* wrong on that implication. Other implications may still fail.

## RECALL

Q: What assumption underlies the whole close-the-bad-paths procedure?
A: That the diagram is a reasonable representation of the actual data generating process.

Q: Define a placebo test.
A: Taking your assumptions, deriving from them a relationship or effect that should be zero,
and checking. A non-zero result means an assumption is wrong.

Q: Give the advertising placebo test.
A: Run the same design on *last* month's sales. This month's advertising cannot have raised
them, so the effect should be zero; if it isn't, the design is missing something.

Q: How does any causal diagram generate placebo tests?
A: Pick any two variables, list every path between them, close them all. Since paths are any
reason two variables are related, the observed relationship should then be zero.

Q: What does a non-zero result on such a test tell you?
A: There's a path you left off the diagram, or a variable you should be conditioning on —
meaning you don't yet know what to control for to identify your real effect.

Q: In the wine diagram, why can you ignore every income–drugs path running through lifespan?
A: Income points into lifespan and drugs points into lifespan, so lifespan is a collider on
those paths and they're pre-closed.

Q: What single variable closes the remaining income–drugs paths?
A: Wine — look only at wine drinkers, only at non-drinkers, or condition on wine statistically.

Q: How can you tell this particular test will fail before running it?
A: Because the diagram claims the only reason income relates to drug taking is via wine's
effect on drugs, which is plainly insufficient.

Q: What is the actual goal for your assumptions, if not being correct?
A: Being close enough to correct that they don't mess you up too much — perfect assumptions are
impossible.

Q: Name three legitimate responses to failing a placebo test.
A: Add the missing variables to the diagram and redo the path analysis; judge the violation
small enough not to matter; or accept the problem and disclose it to readers of your study.

Q: Why are colliders especially relevant when testing pairs that aren't the treatment and outcome?
A: Because many paths between two arbitrary variables run into a common effect, making that
variable a collider and the path pre-closed — so there's much less to do than the path count
suggests.

## GLOSSARY

- **Placebo test** — deriving from your assumptions a relationship that should be zero, then checking whether it is.
- **Implied zero relationship** — a pair of variables your diagram says must be unrelated once all paths between them are closed.
