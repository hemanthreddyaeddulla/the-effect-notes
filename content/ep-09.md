## ONE SENTENCE

Behind your data sits a **data generating process** — the set of laws and regularities
that made the data what it is, operating whether or not you know about them — and
identification is the work of getting from what you observed back to the part of that
process you actually asked about.

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

- **Identification** is the link between the analysis you ran and the question you wanted answered.
- "These two variables are correlated, therefore one causes the other" is an identification error, not a statistics error.
- The **data generating process (DGP)** is the set of laws and relationships determining why you observed *this* data and not some other data.
- Empirical research means using observations to reverse-engineer that process — the way planetary paths were used to work out physical laws nobody could see directly.
- The same observations are usually consistent with **many** different processes. Isolating yours means dealing with the others.
- And you must know the surrounding context, because **data will lie to you** if you don't. A lamp that isn't plugged in makes its own pull-string look useless.

## WHY THIS EXISTS

You've now got both halves. Episodes 1–2 covered research questions; episodes 3–8
covered doing calculations on data. Identification is where they meet: making sure the
calculation you did answers the question you asked, and not some neighbouring question.

The classic failure is correlation and causation. You can analyse two variables, follow
every statistical step correctly, and be entirely justified in concluding they're
correlated. Take one more step to "therefore one causes the other" and you've made an
error — but not in the statistics. The statistics were fine.

## IDEAS

### The data generating process

**Plain English.** The rules the world is running that produced your data.

**Precisely.** The DGP is the set of laws and relationships determining why the data
looks the way it does — why you observed these particular values rather than others. It
exists and operates whether or not you know about it, and every research question is,
underneath, a question about some piece of it.

**Anchor image.** You can't see the machine, only what comes out of it. Your job is to
work out the gears from the output.

**Worked example.** Gravity. For a long time people watched planets and could see the
paths they traced across the night sky. Nobody could see the physical laws — but those
laws were running the whole time, producing exactly those paths. What scientists managed
to do was work backwards from the observed paths to the underlying laws.

That's the template for all empirical research. Observations in, process out.

### Many processes produce the same observations

**Plain English.** The data you're looking at is consistent with several different
stories, and the data alone can't tell you which one you're in.

**Precisely.** Any given set of observations is typically compatible with multiple data
generating processes. Identification is the work of isolating just the explanation your
question is about.

**Anchor image.** Wet pavement. Rain? A street cleaner? A burst pipe? The pavement looks
the same in every case.

**Worked example.** A pen. Two observations, both things you can genuinely measure:

1. Nick is holding the pen, and it's up here.
2. He lets go, and it's down there.

Two variables: whether he's holding it, and where it is. Now — you cannot *see* gravity.
It's part of the laws that make the world look the way it does, and it is not in your
data. So suppose you don't know about gravity yet, and you're trying to infer the
process from these two observations. Take that data to a room of sceptics and you'll get:

- Maybe there's a big earth down there pulling on it — gravity.
- Maybe pens just really love the ground.
- Maybe he's secretly *throwing* it, adding momentum on release.
- Maybe there's a wind you can't see, pushing it down.

Every one of those fits the data perfectly. And the point isn't that the sceptics are
right — it's that your two observations, on their own, don't discriminate between them.

**Where it breaks.** The thing you most want to measure is frequently the thing you
cannot observe. Gravity isn't a column in the dataset. Neither is ability, or health
consciousness, or demand.

### Data lies when you don't know the context

**Plain English.** If you don't understand the rest of the machine, a perfectly accurate
observation can point you in exactly the wrong direction.

**Precisely.** Interpreting data requires knowing enough of the surrounding DGP to
recognise which conditions have to hold for a relationship to show up at all. Miss one,
and a real effect can look like nothing.

**Anchor image.** Turning the tap and getting no water, then concluding the tap is
broken. The mains are off.

**Worked example.** Nick wants to know how a lamp works — what's the process making the
light on or off? He collects data: pull the little string. The light **does not** go on.

Not knowing how lamps work, you'd sit there scratching your head, and you might
reasonably conclude that the string does nothing.

The actual reason is that the lamp isn't plugged in.

The data was accurate. The string genuinely didn't turn on the light. And the conclusion
it pushed you toward — "this string is irrelevant" — is dead wrong, because you didn't
know that lamps need electricity before you can learn what the string does.

**Where it breaks.** No statistical method rescues you here. This is a knowledge-of-context
problem, and the fix is learning your setting properly before trusting what the data
appears to say.

## TRAPS

- Correlation-to-causation is a statistical mistake → **Actually,** it's an identification mistake. The finding of correlation can be entirely correct; the error is in what you claim it answers.
- If my data is accurate, my conclusion is at least pointed the right way → **Actually,** the unplugged lamp gives you completely accurate data supporting a completely wrong conclusion.
- The data generating process is something I build, like a model → **Actually,** it's out there running whether or not you know about it. Your model is an attempt to describe it.
- With enough observations the right explanation will separate itself out → **Actually,** more drops of the pen doesn't distinguish gravity from invisible wind. Both predict every one.
- Understanding the context is background reading, not real research work → **Actually,** it's what stops you concluding the string does nothing. It's load-bearing.

## RECALL

Q: Define identification.
A: The link between the analysis you performed and the research question you wanted
answered — the process of ensuring your analysis answers *that* question and not a
different one.

Q: Why is "these variables are correlated, therefore one causes the other" not a statistics error?
A: The correlation finding can be entirely correct, arrived at through proper statistical
steps. The failure is in the claim about what it answers — an identification error.

Q: What is a data generating process?
A: The set of laws and relationships determining why you observed the data you did rather
than some other data. It operates whether or not you know about it.

Q: How does the planets-and-gravity story map onto empirical research?
A: The observed paths are the data, the physical laws are the DGP, and working backwards
from paths to laws is what identification aims to do.

Q: Nick drops a pen. Give three explanations besides gravity that fit the data.
A: Pens love the ground; he's secretly throwing it rather than dropping it; there's an
invisible wind pushing it down.

Q: What does the pen example demonstrate about observations?
A: The same observations are consistent with many different data generating processes, so
the data alone can't tell you which one you're in.

Q: Nick pulls the lamp's string and nothing happens. What would you conclude, and why is it wrong?
A: You'd conclude the string does nothing. It's wrong because the lamp isn't plugged in —
you needed to know lamps require electricity before you could interpret the observation.

Q: What's the lesson from the unplugged lamp?
A: Data will lie to you if you don't understand the rest of the data generating process.
Accurate data can support a completely wrong conclusion.

Q: Why is it a problem that gravity isn't in your dataset?
A: Because the thing driving the process is often unobservable — like ability or health
consciousness — so you have to reason about it rather than measure it.

## GLOSSARY

- **Identification** — establishing that your analysis answers your research question rather than a different one.
- **Data generating process (DGP)** — the set of laws and relationships that determine why the data is what it is.
- **Alternative explanation** — a different data generating process that would produce the same observations.
