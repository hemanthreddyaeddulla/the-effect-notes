## ONE SENTENCE

A fixed effect is just a **categorical control variable for the individual** — mathematically
identical to any other categorical control, and different only in *why* you added it — so you can
estimate it by literally adding that control, or, far more commonly, by **demeaning**: subtract
each individual's own average from everything and regress the leftovers.

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

- Fixed effects and adding a categorical control are **the same thing mathematically**. Only the research design reasoning differs.
- The model gets **i,t subscripts** (panel data) and an **individual-specific intercept**, which is exactly what binary category indicators do.
- **Estimation route 1:** add the categorical control. Works — but you may have hundreds of thousands of individuals and therefore coefficients.
- **Estimation route 2: demeaning.** Subtract each individual's own mean from y and every x, then regress the leftovers. This is what fixed-effects software actually does.
- **Cluster your standard errors** at the level of the fixed effect. Very common, and it makes sense.
- Interpretation is a normal regression interpretation — just say **"within individual."**
- **Two-way fixed effects** adds a time fixed effect too. Easy to run, harder to interpret.

## WHY THIS EXISTS

The last episode was about *why* you'd use fixed effects. This one is about actually estimating one
— and the crucial thing to hold onto is that **you're just controlling for a variable**. You've added
control variables before, and there is nothing different about adding a control for individual.

Individual is just a categorical control variable, and you already know how to add those. **Fixed
effects and a categorical control are mathematically the same thing.** The only difference is *why*:
you're adding this control not because you want to control for that thing specifically, but because
you think it represents a bunch of other individual stuff you can't measure.

Keeping that in mind clears up a lot of the apparent difficulty.

## IDEAS

### The model and its notation

**Plain English.** Extra subscripts to show panel data, and an intercept that varies by person.

**Precisely.** A fixed effects model looks like a standard regression with a couple of differences
in the subscripts:

- **y with i,t subscripts** — y varies over both individual *i* and time *t*. That's what tells you it's panel data: the same individuals measured in multiple periods.
- **x and the error term** also carry i,t subscripts.
- **The intercept has its own i subscript** — each individual gets their own intercept.

**Why an intercept and not another x?** Because that's what a categorical control *is*. Add binary
indicators for countries — Austria is 1 if you're Austria and 0 otherwise. That coefficient just says
your intercept changes: if you're Austria, add Austria's coefficient to the intercept; if not, don't.
So a set of category indicators is exactly the same as **giving each individual their own intercept**.

### Estimation route 1: add the categorical control

**Plain English.** Just put it in the regression.

**Precisely.** Literally no different from adding any categorical control. Add it to the model and
you're done.

**Where it breaks.** Normally a categorical control has four categories, or ten. With fixed effects
you're controlling for **individual**, and with a lot of data that might be **thousands or hundreds of
thousands** of individuals. You don't want to estimate a regression with hundreds of thousands of
coefficients — at the very least it will be extremely slow. So while you *can* do it this way, it
generally isn't the best approach.

### Estimation route 2: demeaning

**Plain English.** Subtract each person's own average out of everything, then run a normal regression
on what's left.

**Precisely.** Take the average of your **y** and of **all your x variables** for each individual, and
subtract that average out.

**And you've seen this before.** It's exactly one of the methods of controlling for variables from
Chapter 4: take what you can predict with your control variables and subtract the prediction out. When
your control variable is *individual*, what's the best prediction of your outcome knowing only that
it's you? **Your own average.**

**Anchor image.** Measuring how much everyone grew this year rather than how tall they are. Subtract
each person's baseline and the remaining numbers are comparable.

**Worked example.** If you average four hours of exercise a week across all the years observed, then
knowing only that it's you, the best prediction is four hours. Subtract that out and you get your
demeaned values — the fact that this year you exercised one hour more than average, and that year one
hour less. **That's your within variation.**

Then simply regress your **demeaned outcome** on your **demeaned predictors**. That's it — and this is
what most software specifically designed to estimate fixed effects is actually doing.

### Clustered standard errors

**Plain English.** Cluster at the level of the fixed effect. Almost everyone does.

**Precisely.** It's very common to estimate a fixed effects model with **clustered standard errors**,
clustered at the level of the fixed effect. You'd expect the error terms to be more related **within**
individual than **between** individuals, and clustering by individual is how you allow for that.

**Where it breaks.** Not that you *always* have to — but it's very, very common, and it makes sense
why.

### Interpretation

**Plain English.** Same as any regression. Just add "within individual" to the sentence.

**Worked example.** Regress **life expectancy** on a country's **log GDP per capita**, with country
fixed effects. Coefficient: **9.769**.

The standard reading: a one-unit increase in log GDP per capita is associated with a 9.769-unit
increase in life expectancy. Exactly as you'd interpret any regression — **except that it's within
country**.

Spelled out: comparing a given country across time, a year in which that country has a one-unit
higher log GDP per capita would be expected to have a **9.769 longer life expectancy** than that
**same country** in a different year with lower log GDP per capita.

That's all. A regular regression interpretation, keeping in mind you're controlling for individual and
therefore looking at within variation.

### Two-way fixed effects

**Plain English.** Add a second set of fixed effects for time, to soak up things that happened to
everyone at once.

**Precisely.** Nothing stops you having more than one set of fixed effects. It's very common to run a
**two-way fixed effects** model, with a fixed effect for **individual** *and* one for the **time
period** — intended to control for **shared changes over time**.

**Worked example.** Back to trade with Germany. Different countries differ in their general
characteristics — that's between variation, handled by country fixed effects. But there are also
shared changes over time: maybe in one year Germany's economy is really popping, so **everybody**
trades more with Germany that year. Add a fixed effect for time and those shared changes are
controlled for.

**Where it breaks.** Two-way models are very common and easy to run — you just add another fixed
effect. It's a bit harder with the demeaning approach, but let the software handle it.

**Interpreting them is trickier**, though. You have to think about looking within individual *and*
within time, and those two within-dimensions **interact with each other**. These models do some weird
and unexpected things sometimes. The textbook chapter has more detail.

## TRAPS

- Fixed effects is a special estimator → **Actually,** it's mathematically identical to adding a categorical control variable. The difference is the research design reasoning.
- Giving each individual their own intercept is a different model from adding category indicators → **Actually,** they're the same thing — a binary indicator just shifts the intercept for that group.
- Just add the categorical control and you're done → **Actually,** with hundreds of thousands of individuals that means hundreds of thousands of coefficients and a very slow model.
- Demeaning is a new technique → **Actually,** it's the Chapter 4 method of controlling for a variable, with the individual's own average as the prediction.
- Clustered standard errors are optional in panel data → **Actually,** it's very common and well-motivated: errors are likely more related within individual than between.
- The coefficient means what it would in any regression → **Actually,** it means that *within* an individual — comparing a country to itself across years, not to other countries.
- Two-way fixed effects is just twice as good → **Actually,** it's easy to run and genuinely tricky to interpret, because the two within-dimensions interact.

## RECALL

Q: How does a fixed effect differ mathematically from a categorical control variable?
A: It doesn't — they're the same thing. Only the reason for including it differs.

Q: Why do you add it, if not to control for that variable specifically?
A: Because that category stands in for a bunch of individual characteristics you can't measure and
want to control for.

Q: What do the i and t subscripts in a fixed effects model indicate?
A: That the variables vary over both individual and time — that is, you have panel data.

Q: Why does the intercept get an i subscript?
A: Because each individual gets their own intercept, which is exactly what a set of binary category
indicators does — being in a category shifts the intercept.

Q: What's the first way to estimate a fixed effects model, and its downside?
A: Literally add the categorical control to the regression. The downside is that with thousands or
hundreds of thousands of individuals you'd be estimating that many coefficients, which is very slow.

Q: Describe the demeaning approach.
A: Take the average of the outcome and every predictor for each individual, subtract those averages
out, and regress the demeaned outcome on the demeaned predictors.

Q: How is demeaning the same as the Chapter 4 method of controlling for a variable?
A: You take what you can predict with the control and subtract the prediction out — and knowing only
which individual it is, the best prediction is that individual's own average.

Q: You average four hours of exercise a week. What does demeaning leave you with?
A: The within variation — that this year you exercised one hour more than your average, and another
year one hour less.

Q: Which estimation method do most fixed-effects packages actually use?
A: Demeaning.

Q: What standard errors are usually used with fixed effects, and why?
A: Clustered standard errors, clustered at the level of the fixed effect, because error terms are
likely more related within an individual than between individuals.

Q: Life expectancy on log GDP per capita with country fixed effects gives 9.769. Interpret it.
A: Within country, a year in which that country has a one-unit higher log GDP per capita would be
expected to have 9.769 longer life expectancy than that same country in a year with lower log GDP per
capita.

Q: What is a two-way fixed effects model?
A: One with a fixed effect for individual and a fixed effect for the time period.

Q: What does the time fixed effect control for?
A: Shared changes over time — for instance a year in which Germany's economy is booming so everybody
trades more with Germany.

Q: What's the catch with two-way fixed effects?
A: They're easy to run but trickier to interpret, since you're looking within individual *and* within
time and those interact — and the models sometimes behave in unexpected ways.

## GLOSSARY

- **Demeaning** — subtracting each individual's own mean from every variable, leaving only within variation.
- **Individual intercept** — the per-individual constant that a fixed effect estimates.
- **Two-way fixed effects** — a model including both individual and time fixed effects.
- **Clustered standard errors** — standard errors allowing errors to be correlated within groups, here within individuals.
