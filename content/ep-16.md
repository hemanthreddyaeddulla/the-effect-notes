## ONE SENTENCE

Every way you can walk across a diagram from treatment to outcome is a separate reason
those two variables are related — so list all the paths, sort them into the ones your
question wants and the ones it doesn't, and identification becomes the concrete job of
shutting down the second kind.

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

- A causal diagram's real output is **why** two variables are related — every path is one reason.
- A **path** is any walk along the diagram from one variable to another. You may **go backwards up an arrow**. You may not visit a variable twice.
- **Front door:** all arrows point away from treatment, toward outcome. **Back door:** at least one arrow somewhere points back *at* the treatment.
- **Good path:** one you want to count for your question. **Bad path:** one you don't.
- Good usually means front door — but not always. Your question decides, not the diagram.
- **Identification = close every bad path, leave every good path open.** Then the only variation left is the variation you wanted.

## WHY THIS EXISTS

You can draw a diagram and simplify it. What is it actually *for*?

This: it tells you exactly why your treatment and outcome are related. Not vaguely —
specifically, as an enumerable list. You already knew a raw correlation between treatment
and outcome wouldn't give you the causal effect, because other reasons exist. The diagram
turns "other reasons exist" into a list of routes you can point at and deal with one by
one.

## IDEAS

### What a path is

**Plain English.** Any way you can walk from one variable to another across the diagram.

**Precisely.** A path is a sequence of connected variables leading from a starting
variable to an ending one. Two rules:

1. **You can walk backwards up an arrow.** If the arrow runs B → treatment, "treatment to B" is still a legal step.
2. **No doubling back.** You can't hit the same variable twice — so `treatment → B → A → treatment → B → C → outcome` isn't a path, because it returns to treatment.

**Anchor image.** A street map where some streets are one-way for *traffic* but you're on
foot. You can walk down a one-way street the wrong way; you just can't loop back through
a junction you've already crossed.

**Worked example.** On a diagram with treatment, outcome, and variables A, B, C — where
B causes the treatment — all of these are paths from treatment to outcome:

- treatment → outcome
- treatment → A → outcome
- treatment → B → C → outcome
- treatment → A → B → C → outcome
- treatment → B → A → outcome

Note the third one starts by walking *against* the arrow from B into treatment. Perfectly
legal.

**Where it breaks.** Each path is a *separate* reason the two variables are related. The
direct arrow is only one entry on the list.

### Every path tells a story

**Plain English.** For each path you can say out loud why it would make the two variables
move together.

**Worked example.** Does drinking wine affect your lifespan? A simplified diagram:

```dag
node U 1.5 0 "U1" unobs
node N 0.8 0.8 "Income"
node H 2.2 0.8 "Health"
node W 0 1.8 "Wine" treat
node L 3 1.8 "Lifespan" out
node D 1.5 2.7 "Drugs"
edge U N
edge U H
edge N W
edge N L
edge H W
edge H L
edge W D
edge D L
edge W L
```

Income and health affect both how much wine you drink and how long you live. Wine affects
drugs — maybe wine substitutes for other drugs, maybe it's a complement and drinking makes
you want them — and drugs affect lifespan. `U1` is unobserved and relates income to health
without sitting directly between them.

Now read off the reasons wine and lifespan are related:

| Path | The story |
|---|---|
| Wine → Lifespan | Wine directly affects how long you live |
| Wine → Drugs → Lifespan | Wine changes how many other drugs you take, which affects lifespan |
| Wine ← Income → Lifespan | Richer people can afford more wine, and live longer for unrelated reasons |
| Wine ← Health → Lifespan | Healthier people feel able to drink; if you're really sick you stay off alcohol — and better health means a longer life |
| Wine ← Income ← U1 → Health → Lifespan | Richer people drink more wine; income is related to background health; health affects lifespan |

Every one of those would produce a correlation between wine and lifespan. Only some are the
effect of wine.

### Front doors and back doors

**Plain English.** Front doors run forward from treatment. Back doors have an arrow pointing
back at it.

**Precisely.** A **front door** is a path where all arrows point away from the treatment and
toward the outcome. A **back door** is a path with at least one arrow somewhere along it
pointing back *at* the treatment.

**Anchor image.** Front doors are the treatment's own influence flowing outward. Back doors
are something upstream pushing the treatment around, and that something has its own route to
the outcome.

**Worked example.** In the wine diagram:

- **Front doors:** Wine → Lifespan, and Wine → Drugs → Lifespan.
- **Back doors:** the three paths that begin by walking backwards into wine, via income or health.

**Where it breaks.** A back door means something other than random variation is driving your
treatment. That's precisely why back doors are usually the alternative explanations you have
to deal with.

### Good and bad paths — your question decides

**Plain English.** A good path is one you want to count. That's a fact about your research
question, not about the diagram.

**Precisely.** **Good paths** are the ones that should count toward your answer; **bad paths**
are everything else. Often good = all front doors. Not always.

**Anchor image.** Front door versus back door is architecture. Good versus bad is which door
you meant when you asked how people get in.

**Worked example — the same diagram, two different questions.**

*Question A: what's the effect of wine on lifespan, all in?* Then both front doors are good.
If wine extends your life because it means you take fewer drugs — sure, that counts as an
effect of wine.

*Question B: what's the effect of wine on lifespan, not counting its effect on drug taking?*
Now the only good path is Wine → Lifespan. `Wine → Drugs → Lifespan` is a **front door that
is a bad path**, and you'd want it shut.

Meanwhile `Wine ← Income → Lifespan` is bad under either question. Rich people drinking more
wine and living longer for other reasons is not an effect of wine.

**Where it breaks.** This is why you can't mechanically read identification off a diagram.
The diagram gives you the paths; you supply which ones your question wants.

### What identification is, concretely

**Plain English.** Close every bad path, keep every good path open, and what's left is your
answer.

**Precisely.** Once all the bad paths are shut and the good ones remain, the only variation
left in your data is variation you wanted — which is what it means to have identified your
effect.

**Anchor image.** A building with many doors. Lock all of them except the one you're
measuring traffic through, and every person you count came through that door.

## TRAPS

- A path has to follow the arrows → **Actually,** you can walk backwards up an arrow. That's how back doors exist.
- Front door paths are good paths → **Actually,** usually but not always. If you don't want to count wine's effect via drug taking, that front door is a bad path.
- The direct arrow from treatment to outcome is the relationship → **Actually,** it's one entry on a list. Every other path also contributes to what you'd observe.
- Bad paths mean my effect isn't real → **Actually,** they mean it's mixed in with other things. Same point as Ep 10, now made precise.
- The diagram tells me which paths to close → **Actually,** it tells you which paths *exist*. Your research question decides which are good.
- I can repeat a variable if the walk needs it → **Actually,** no doubling back. A route returning to treatment isn't a path.

## RECALL

Q: Define a path on a causal diagram.
A: Any walk from one variable to another along the diagram. You may travel backwards up an
arrow, and you may not visit a variable twice.

Q: The arrow runs B → treatment. Is "treatment to B" a legal step in a path?
A: Yes. Paths can be walked against the direction of an arrow.

Q: Why is `treatment → B → A → treatment → B → C → outcome` not a path?
A: It doubles back, visiting treatment twice.

Q: What does each path represent?
A: A separate reason the two endpoint variables would be related to each other in your data.

Q: Define front door and back door.
A: A front door is a path where all arrows point away from the treatment toward the outcome.
A back door has at least one arrow somewhere along it pointing back at the treatment.

Q: In the wine diagram, give the two front door paths.
A: Wine → Lifespan, and Wine → Drugs → Lifespan.

Q: Tell the story for the path Wine ← Health → Lifespan.
A: Healthier people feel able to drink more (if you're really sick you avoid alcohol), and
being healthier also means living longer — so wine and lifespan correlate without wine doing
anything.

Q: What's the difference between a front door path and a good path?
A: Front door is structural — it's about arrow directions. Good is about your research
question — whether you want that path counted.

Q: Give an example of a front door path that is a bad path.
A: If your question is the effect of wine on lifespan *excluding* its effect on drug taking,
then Wine → Drugs → Lifespan is a front door you want to shut.

Q: What does a back door path indicate about your treatment?
A: That something other than random variation is driving it — something upstream is
influencing who gets treated, and that something reaches the outcome by its own route.

Q: State what identification means in path terms.
A: Close all the bad paths and leave all the good paths open, so the only variation remaining
in the data is the variation you wanted to measure.

## GLOSSARY

- **Path** — a walk along a causal diagram from one variable to another, permitted to go against arrows, forbidden to repeat a variable.
- **Front door path** — a path where every arrow points away from the treatment and toward the outcome.
- **Back door path** — a path with at least one arrow pointing back at the treatment.
- **Good path** — a path you want to count toward answering your research question.
- **Bad path** — a path you don't want counted, which must be closed to identify your effect.
