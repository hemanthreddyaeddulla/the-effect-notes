## ONE SENTENCE

When different groups get treated at different times, **two-way fixed effects quietly uses
already-treated groups as controls** — and if treatment effects evolve at all, those "controls"
deviate from trend and break parallel trends by themselves, which is why you must estimate
**separate effects per cohort per period** and aggregate afterwards.

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

- **Staggered treatment / rollout design:** groups treated at different times. School A in 2012, B in 2013, C never.
- Researchers long assumed two-way fixed effects handled this. **It doesn't.**
- Why: there are **two ways to show no change in treatment** — never treated, or **already treated**. So two-way fixed effects uses **already-treated units as controls**.
- If effects **evolve over time**, those already-treated units are deviating from trend — violating parallel trends by construction.
- **This is a two-way fixed effects problem, not a difference-in-differences problem.**
- The fix, shared by all the new estimators: allow effects to differ **by cohort** and **by period**, then **aggregate**.
- Two approaches: **Callaway and Sant'Anna** (matching to pick eligible controls each period) and **Wooldridge–Mundlak** (a great many interaction terms).
- You **cannot** just use two-way fixed effects. It can bias you terribly.

## WHY THIS EXISTS

This is the secret shame of econometrics — or at least the most recent one.

A **staggered treatment**, or **rollout design**, is a difference-in-differences setup where multiple
groups become treated at different times. Say three school districts: one gets the new teacher
training programme in **2012**, another gets it in **2013**, and a third **never** gets it. The
treatment was applied in a staggered fashion — rolled out to different places at different times.

**For a long time researchers thought you could just treat this as normal** and use two-way fixed
effects. Which makes sense on the face of it: you still have group fixed effects for baseline
differences, time fixed effects for shared changes over time, so the coefficient on treated should
give you the effect.

**It does not.**

## IDEAS

### Why two-way fixed effects fails

**Plain English.** "No change in treatment status" includes people who were *already* treated — so
they get used as controls.

**Precisely.** Two-way fixed effects strips out within variation via the group fixed effects, then
compares within variation between places where the policy went into effect and places where **no
change** was seen.

But there are **two different ways to see no change in your treatment level**:

1. You weren't treated before, and you continue to not be treated. *(A real control.)*
2. **You were already treated before, and you continue to be treated.** *(Not a control at all.)*

So two-way fixed effects isn't just using the control group as a control group — **it's also using
anybody who has already been treated as a control group.**

**Anchor image.** Measuring how fast a car accelerates by comparing it to a parked car and to a car
already doing 60. The second isn't stationary — it's just not changing gear.

**Where this might be survivable.** Not necessarily fatal. Parallel trends could still hold: if the
already-treated group took its jump in outcomes and, after that jump, trends stayed the same, you'd
be fine.

**But if treatment effects evolve over time, that's never true.**

**Worked example.** School A gets the teacher training programme in **2012**, school B in **2013**.
Suppose the programme **doesn't do anything right away** — it takes a while to start working.

You're the 2013 school. Two-way fixed effects compares you against a "control group" that includes
both the genuine never-treated control **and the 2012 school that's already treated**.

Right away, you see nothing. But in the year afterwards, as the 2012 school's effect starts kicking
in, **that already-treated group starts deviating from trend.** So whatever parallel trends you
thought held, doesn't — **the treatment effects becoming more or less effective over time is itself
driving the violation.** And everything falls apart.

**The important framing.** This is really just a problem with **two-way fixed effects**. It is **not**
a conceptual problem with difference-in-differences. Difference-in-differences doesn't mind that you
have several control groups, some becoming treated later and some earlier. It's that two-way fixed
effects **forces already-treated groups to act as though they're control groups when in fact they
aren't**.

### The fix, in general

**Plain English.** Let every cohort have its own effect in every period, then average afterwards.

**Precisely.** You can still use difference-in-differences with a staggered rollout — you just need a
different estimation method. As of writing, a number of estimators have been proposed and it's a
fertile area of research; there may or may not be a settled standard in future. But they share the
same basic idea.

The problem is that you're **using the wrong control group** and **not allowing the effect to change
over time**. So fix both:

1. **Allow the effect to differ by cohort.** One effect for those who started being treated in 2012, a different one for those who started in 2013 — **when you started being treated gets you your own treatment effect.**
2. **Allow the effect to differ by time period.** The 2012 cohort gets one effect in 2013, another in 2014, another in 2015. The 2013 cohort gets its own set.
3. **Then aggregate** these individual effects back into a single number if you want one — average everything for an overall effect, or average across **years since treatment** to get "the effect one year after being treated, whenever that happened to be," two years after, and so on.

**Estimate a bunch of different effects, then aggregate them afterwards.**

### Approach 1: Callaway and Sant'Anna

**Plain English.** Pick a fresh, legitimate control group for each period using matching.

**Precisely.** First it uses **matching in each period**. For 2012 it asks: who's the best control
group for you? And obviously it will only use **people who were not actually treated at the time** —
**already-treated people don't get to be picked as controls.** Then it may use matching variables or
some other process to pick a control group that's comparable to you *and* eligible to be a control at
that time.

So you get a **different control group for each time period**, based on who's still available. You can
choose whether to include units that will **eventually** be treated as controls — that's up to you.

Having done that, proceed as normal: estimate different effects for each group, comparing to its own
personal control group in each future period. It often uses an **inverse probability weighting**
approach, just as in the matching chapter.

The result is individual treatment effects — a different effect for each cohort, in each period. The
2012 cohort has effects in 2013, 2014, 2015, 2016; the 2013 cohort has its own. Aggregate as you like.

### Approach 2: Wooldridge–Mundlak

**Plain English.** Interaction terms falling from the sky.

**Precisely.** This is Jeffrey Wooldridge's approach, only hinted at in the chapter but considerably
more popular since the book was written.

It uses a **Mundlak estimator** — the one from the random effects video. That's an estimator where you
have **group fixed effects for the within variation** but **also control for the between variation**,
so both sources of variation are in the model at the same time.

On top of that, you add **a great many interaction terms**, allowing different difference-in-differences
effects in each time period for each group. It's very similar to the dynamic difference-in-differences
model, with a few slight adjustments.

**The key adjustment:** in dynamic difference-in-differences you took the treated-group variable and
interacted it with the time period. Here you **also interact with which treated group you're in** — so
it isn't just *are you in a treated group*, it's ***which* treated group are you in**: did you start
being treated in 2012, or in 2013?

So there's an additional set of interaction terms. Estimate them all, exactly as with dynamic
difference-in-differences, then aggregate as you like.

### The bottom line

**Plain English.** Use one of these. Don't use two-way fixed effects.

**Precisely.** These estimators all work the same way: estimate a great many treatment effects — via
interaction terms, or via separate estimations as in Callaway and Sant'Anna — then aggregate them to
get the kind of treatment effect you want.

**The key point: you have to use one of these methods** if you're looking at a situation where
treatment was applied to different groups at different times. **You simply cannot use the two-way
fixed effects approach. It can bias you quite terribly. You don't want to do it.** Use a method that
allows all this variation to occur, and then tamp things back down to the single estimate you want
after the fact.

## TRAPS

- Two-way fixed effects handles staggered treatment because it has group and time fixed effects → **Actually,** it silently recruits already-treated units as controls, which is the whole problem.
- Difference-in-differences doesn't work with staggered rollout → **Actually,** the concept is fine. It's the two-way fixed effects *estimator* that breaks.
- The problem is having multiple treatment dates → **Actually,** the problem is treatment effects that **evolve over time**. With perfectly constant effects, parallel trends could survive.
- The already-treated units are still valid controls since their treatment status isn't changing → **Actually,** if their effect is still growing, they're deviating from trend — which is exactly what a control must not do.
- These new estimators replace difference-in-differences → **Actually,** they're different ways of estimating it, allowing effects to vary by cohort and period.
- Callaway and Sant'Anna must exclude eventually-treated units as controls → **Actually,** it must exclude *already*-treated units; whether to include eventually-treated ones is your choice.
- There's a settled standard method now → **Actually,** it's a fertile area of research and Nick notes no consensus at time of writing.

## RECALL

Q: What is a staggered treatment or rollout design?
A: A difference-in-differences setup where multiple groups become treated at different times — one
school district in 2012, another in 2013, another never.

Q: What did researchers long assume about estimating it?
A: That you could just use two-way fixed effects, since group fixed effects handle baseline differences
and time fixed effects handle shared changes.

Q: What are the two ways a unit can show no change in treatment status?
A: It was never treated and still isn't, or it was already treated and continues to be.

Q: Why does that matter?
A: Because two-way fixed effects treats both as controls — so already-treated units are used as a
control group when they aren't one.

Q: When would that be survivable?
A: If the already-treated group took its jump and then trends stayed the same, parallel trends could
still hold.

Q: What makes it not survivable?
A: Treatment effects that evolve over time — then already-treated units are still deviating from trend.

Q: Walk through the 2012 and 2013 schools example.
A: If the programme takes time to work, the 2013 cohort is compared against a control set including the
already-treated 2012 school, whose effect is only now kicking in. That school deviates from trend,
violating parallel trends.

Q: Is this a problem with difference-in-differences itself?
A: No. Difference-in-differences doesn't mind multiple groups treated at different times. It's the
two-way fixed effects estimator forcing already-treated groups to act as controls.

Q: What two things do all the new estimators allow?
A: Effects that differ by cohort — when you started being treated — and effects that differ by time
period.

Q: What do you do after estimating all those effects?
A: Aggregate them into whatever single effect you want — an overall average, or an average by years
since treatment.

Q: How does Callaway and Sant'Anna choose control groups?
A: With matching in each period, using only units not actually treated at that time, and optionally
including or excluding units that will eventually be treated.

Q: What weighting does it often use?
A: Inverse probability weighting, as in the matching chapter.

Q: What estimator underlies the Wooldridge approach, and what does it do?
A: The Mundlak estimator — group fixed effects for within variation while also controlling for the
between variation, so both are in the model at once.

Q: What's the key difference between Wooldridge–Mundlak and dynamic difference-in-differences?
A: You interact not just with the time period but with **which** treated cohort you're in — whether you
started being treated in 2012 or 2013.

Q: What is the bottom-line instruction?
A: You must use one of these methods for staggered treatment. Two-way fixed effects can bias you
terribly.

Q: Is there a settled standard estimator?
A: No — it's a fertile area of research, with no consensus at the time of writing.

## GLOSSARY

- **Staggered treatment / rollout design** — a setting where different groups become treated at different times.
- **Cohort** — the set of units that began being treated in the same period.
- **Callaway and Sant'Anna estimator** — a staggered-treatment estimator that selects a fresh, eligible control group per period via matching.
- **Wooldridge–Mundlak approach** — a staggered-treatment estimator using a Mundlak specification plus cohort-by-period interaction terms.
