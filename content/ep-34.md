## ONE SENTENCE

When your outcome is binary, a straight line predicts probabilities above 1 and below 0 and
ignores that there's less room to move near the edges — so you run the same linear **index
function** through a **link function** that squashes it into 0–1, and then report **average
marginal effects** because the coefficients themselves are uninterpretable.

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

- OLS quietly assumes a **continuous** dependent variable. Plenty aren't — counts, bounded variables, and **binary** outcomes.
- Run OLS on a binary outcome and you get a **linear probability model**. Two problems.
- **Problem 1:** the line predicts probabilities like 150% or −2. Impossible.
- **Problem 2:** it says a nudge moves you 5 points whether you're at 50% or at 99.9%. There's no room at 99.9%.
- **Fix:** a generalized linear model. The **index function** β₀ + β₁x is exactly what OLS does; run it through a **link function** that maps any number into 0–1.
- **Logit** uses the logistic function; **probit** uses the standard normal CDF.
- **You can't read the coefficients** — they describe the index. Report **average marginal effects** instead.
- Many economists use the linear probability model anyway, and Nick thinks there's some justification.

## WHY THIS EXISTS

Everything so far assumed a linear relationship between predictors and outcome, with
transformations like polynomials and logarithms available for odd shapes. But sometimes that
still doesn't work — and the reason is the **dependent variable**.

There's been a background assumption that your dependent variable is roughly continuous: it can
take any value in a range and isn't really restricted. Basic OLS assumes exactly that. Often it's
false. You might be counting how many times something happened — an integer that can't be
negative. You might have a continuous variable that can't be negative, or can't be positive.

And very commonly: a **binary** dependent variable, 0 or 1. You've used binary variables as
predictors; what happens when the thing you're *predicting* is binary? Were you cured of the
disease, yes or no. Did you get into college. Did you move house.

There's a whole world of models for different dependent variable types, and this book skips almost
all of it — the focus here is causal inference. But probit and logit get a look.

## IDEAS

### The linear probability model, and its two problems

**Plain English.** You *can* run OLS on a binary outcome. The line just does two silly things.

**Precisely.** Running ordinary least squares with a binary dependent variable gives you a **linear
probability model** — so called because you're treating the probability of the outcome linearly,
and OLS is all about linear relationships.

Picture the data: every point sits at exactly 1 or exactly 0, nothing between, because the outcome
can only be binary. Now fit a straight line through it.

**Problem 1 — impossible predictions.** The line extends beyond 0 and 1. And that line *is* your
prediction of the outcome — with a binary outcome, OLS is trying to give you the probability that
the outcome equals 1. So for certain values of X it predicts a probability of 110%, or 150%, or
3,000% if you go far enough out, or −2. None of those are probabilities.

**Problem 2 — no room at the edges.** Suppose the outcome is whether you go to college, and based
on your background characteristics I predict a **50%** chance. I give you some tutoring, and that
raises it to 55%. That five-point jump is the slope of the line.

Now take someone already at **99.999%**. Give them the same tutoring. It might move the needle a
little — 99.9% to 99.92%, say — but it obviously cannot add five percentage points. They were
already nearly certain.

Same change on the X axis, hugely different change in probability. Near the middle, where there's
lots of room to move, you should see big effects. Near the edges, where the outcome is essentially
guaranteed either way, there's simply no room — so the slope **should get flatter**. A straight
line can't do that.

**Anchor image.** A glass nearly full. Pouring the same amount in adds far less than it did when
the glass was half empty — and at some point it just spills.

### The fix: index function plus link function

**Plain English.** Do the normal linear thing, then squash the answer into the 0–1 range.

**Precisely.** This is a **generalized linear model**. Look at the equation and you'll recognise
most of it:

```
P(y = 1)  =  f( β₀ + β₁·x )
```

The inside part — **β₀ + β₁x** — is the **index function**. It takes your predictors, multiplies
them by coefficients, and adds them together linearly. That is exactly what OLS has been doing all
along.

**The only difference** between OLS and a generalized linear model is that you run the index
through a **link function** — that's the `f`.

**Why you need one.** The index can take any value: make x a very large negative number and the
index is very large and negative; make it very positive and the index is very positive. So you
need a function you can pass any value into and get back something on the same scale as your
dependent variable — here, something between 0 and 1.

**The two link functions:**

| Model | Link function |
|---|---|
| **Logit** | the logistic function |
| **Probit** | the cumulative distribution function of the standard normal distribution |

The mathematical details are in the textbook chapter.

**What this buys you.** Both problems solved at once:

1. **You cannot predict outside 0 to 1**, because the link function can't go above 1 or below 0.
2. **The curve flattens near the edges** and is steep near the middle, like the OLS line is. So the model says you get big movements where there's room to move and little movement when you're already near certainty.

**When it matters most.** Be most sure to use probit or logit — rather than OLS — when you think
the probability of the outcome is **close to 100% or close to 0%**, rather than in the middle.

### Why plenty of people use the linear probability model anyway

**Plain English.** Proper modelling costs you elsewhere, and if you only want the slope, it may not
be worth it.

**Precisely.** Probit, logit, and other proper modelling of the dependent variable do solve real
problems — predictions are better and the slope more accurately accounts for where you are on the
X axis. But they also introduce **computational difficulties**, including trouble with **fixed
effects**, which come up shortly.

So: if all you really want is the slope, probit and logit do fix the slope issue somewhat — but if
they make things difficult in some other way, it might not be worth it. You can argue back and
forth about when it's worth doing.

**Where it breaks.** As Nick puts it, if you're not an economist you may be yelling at your screen
right now — *why would you not use a proper binary dependent variable model?* But economists do
like linear probability models, and he thinks there's some justification. The book has more on
that argument.

### Interpretation: you can't read the coefficients

**Plain English.** The coefficient tells you about the index, and nobody cares about the index.

**Precisely.** You can't look at a table of probit or logit coefficients and interpret them
directly. What the model estimates is the β₀ and β₁ **of the index function** — so β₁ tells you
how x relates to the index, which then has to go through a transformation to become the thing you
actually want: the probability that the dependent variable equals 1. Interpreting the index is
hard.

**The fix: marginal effects.** Take the x's, change them by a unit, see what changes about the
**predicted probabilities**, and report that. This puts the effect of x back in terms of the
probability of y — exactly what a linear probability model would have given you, and much easier
to read.

So instead of *"a one-unit increase in x increases the index by 2.2 units"* — which means nothing
to anyone — you say *"a one-unit increase in x changes the probability that y equals 1 by two
percentage points."*

**Where it breaks — there's no single marginal effect.** The marginal effect is the slope of the
curve, and the curve's slope is **different at every point on the X axis**. Everyone has their own
marginal effect depending on where they sit.

**The aggregation Nick recommends: the average marginal effect.** Go through every data point on
the graph, compute that individual's marginal effect — the slope of the logit curve at their point
— then simply **take the average**. You get a standard error, you can test whether it differs from
zero, all the usual machinery.

There are other ways to aggregate marginal effects, but this is the one he recommends, because it
lets each individual person's own marginal effect be averaged together — which is typically what
people want out of the whole exercise.

## TRAPS

- OLS can't be used with a binary outcome → **Actually,** it can, and it's called a linear probability model. It just has two specific problems.
- The only problem with a linear probability model is impossible predictions → **Actually,** the second problem is arguably worse: a constant slope ignores that there's no room to move near 0 or 1.
- A generalized linear model is fundamentally different from OLS → **Actually,** the index function is exactly what OLS does. The only difference is passing it through a link function.
- Logit and probit are substantially different models → **Actually,** they're the same structure with different link functions — the logistic function versus the standard normal CDF.
- You should always prefer probit or logit for binary outcomes → **Actually,** they bring computational difficulties, including with fixed effects. Many economists use the LPM, and Nick sees justification for it.
- A logit coefficient of 2.2 means the probability rises by 2.2 → **Actually,** it describes the index function. Convert to marginal effects before interpreting anything.
- There's one marginal effect to report → **Actually,** the slope differs at every point on the X axis. The average marginal effect averages each individual's own.

## RECALL

Q: What does basic OLS quietly assume about the dependent variable?
A: That it's continuous — able to take any value in a range without real restriction.

Q: Name some dependent variables that violate that.
A: Counts (non-negative integers), continuous variables that can't be negative or can't be
positive, and binary outcomes.

Q: What is a linear probability model?
A: Ordinary least squares run with a binary dependent variable — treating the probability of the
outcome linearly.

Q: Give the first problem with a linear probability model.
A: The fitted line extends beyond 0 and 1, so it predicts impossible probabilities like 150% or −2.

Q: Give the second problem, with the college example.
A: A straight line has a constant slope. Going from a 50% chance of college, tutoring might add
five percentage points — but someone already at 99.999% has no room, so the same tutoring might
move them only from 99.9% to 99.92%. The slope should flatten near the edges.

Q: What is the index function?
A: β₀ + β₁x — the predictors multiplied by coefficients and added linearly, exactly what OLS does.

Q: What is the link function for, and what's the only difference between OLS and a generalized linear model?
A: The link function maps the index — which can be any value — into the scale of the dependent
variable, here 0 to 1. Passing the index through it is the only difference from OLS.

Q: Which link functions do logit and probit use?
A: Logit uses the logistic function; probit uses the cumulative distribution function of the
standard normal distribution.

Q: Which two problems does the link function solve?
A: Predictions can no longer fall outside 0 to 1, and the curve flattens near the edges while
staying steep in the middle.

Q: When is it most important to use probit or logit rather than a linear probability model?
A: When you think the probability of the outcome is close to 100% or close to 0%, rather than in
the middle.

Q: Why do many people use the linear probability model anyway?
A: Probit and logit introduce computational difficulties, including trouble with fixed effects. If
all you want is the slope, the cost may not be worth it.

Q: Why can't you interpret a logit coefficient directly?
A: It describes how x relates to the *index function*, which must still pass through a
transformation to become the probability that y = 1. The index is hard to interpret.

Q: What's a marginal effect?
A: The change in the predicted *probability* from changing x by a unit — putting the effect back on
the scale a linear probability model would have given you.

Q: Why isn't there a single marginal effect?
A: The marginal effect is the slope of the curve, and the slope differs at every point on the X
axis, so each individual has their own.

Q: What is the average marginal effect, and why does Nick recommend it?
A: Compute each data point's own marginal effect — the slope of the curve at that point — then
average them. He recommends it because averaging each person's own marginal effect is typically
what people actually want.

## GLOSSARY

- **Linear probability model** — OLS applied to a binary dependent variable.
- **Generalized linear model** — a model that passes a linear index through a link function.
- **Index function** — the linear combination of predictors and coefficients, β₀ + β₁x.
- **Link function** — the function mapping the index onto the dependent variable's scale.
- **Logit** — a generalized linear model using the logistic link function.
- **Probit** — a generalized linear model using the standard normal CDF as its link function.
- **Marginal effect** — the change in predicted probability from a one-unit change in a predictor.
- **Average marginal effect** — the mean of every observation's own marginal effect.
