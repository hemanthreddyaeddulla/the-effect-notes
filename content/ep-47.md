## ONE SENTENCE

Fixed effects throws away **all** the between variation, which costs you statistical power and any
hope of studying time-constant variables — so random effects instead **assumes the individual
effects follow a distribution**, buying precision back with an assumption, and its better variants
let you keep the between variation and the within variation separately.

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

- Fixed effects works, but it **discards a lot of interesting information**.
- Cost 1: you **can't study anything that's purely between** — birthplace has no within variation, so it's gone.
- Cost 2: **poor statistical power**, because you threw out so much information.
- **Random effects** doesn't remove *all* the between variation. It **constrains the individual effects to follow a distribution**.
- Any assumption buys precision and risks being wrong. Plain random effects needs the individual effect to be **unrelated to the controls and the treatment** — usually false.
- **Hierarchical linear modeling** predicts where you sit in that distribution using measurable characteristics. Common — more common than fixed effects in many fields.
- **Correlated random effects** (the **Mundlak estimator**) includes both the **mean** and the **demeaned** version of each variable, separating between from within.

## WHY THIS EXISTS

This chapter has been about the difference between **between** variation — Austria has different
geography than England — and **within** variation — Austria's politics differ from one year to the
next. Fixed effects controls away all the between variation and leaves you with the within, and it's
effective at that.

There are downsides.

## IDEAS

### The two costs of fixed effects

**Plain English.** You binned a lot of real information, and the bin had useful things in it.

**Precisely.**

**Cost 1 — you can't study anything that's purely between.** Want to know the effect of your
birthplace? You **can't** use fixed effects to study that, because birthplace has no within variation.
You threw out all the between variation, and anything interesting in there went with it.

**Cost 2 — poor statistical power.** You're discarding a lot of information you actually have, and
discarding information makes your estimates less precise.

**Anchor image.** Throwing out the packaging to get at the contents — and finding the receipt was in
the packaging.

### What random effects does

**Plain English.** Don't delete the individual differences. Assume they follow a pattern.

**Precisely.** Random effects tries to deal with the between variation without getting rid of **all**
of it — improving statistical performance and letting you study some of what lives in the between
variation, while still closing some of the back doors you care about.

The mechanism: individuals really are different — Austria differs from England differs from France;
you differ from me differs from our neighbour down the street. But instead of letting the individual
effects be **anything at all**, random effects **forces them to follow a distribution**. You're willing
to say something like: these effects follow a **normal distribution**.

**The trade.** Any time in statistics that you make an assumption, you give yourself back some
statistical power — and run the risk that if the assumption is wrong, your results are bad. By
constraining the individual effects rather than letting them run free, you may not remove all the
between variation, but you get precision back.

**Where it breaks.** Doing just that doesn't always work well. For plain random effects to really close
those between-variation back doors, you have to assume the **individual effect is unrelated to all the
control variables and the treatment** — which is usually not the case.

### Hierarchical linear modeling

**Plain English.** Don't just assume a distribution — predict where each individual sits in it.

**Precisely.** Hierarchical linear modeling is **very common — probably more common than fixed effects
in a lot of fields**, though economics tends to prefer fixed effects.

It says: we have these individual effects, we'll allow them to follow distributions — **and we'll use
measurable characteristics to predict where you are in that distribution.** So there's an individual
effect for you, and things like your **geography**, which can be measured, are used to predict where
your individual effect sits.

**Why that helps.** It gives more information about where you are on the distribution, so you
understand your individual effect better — and crucially, **it's now okay if your individual effect is
related to geography**, because you've incorporated that relationship into the estimation. That was
exactly the assumption plain random effects couldn't survive.

**Anchor image.** Not just "people vary in height" but "and taller parents predict taller children."
Explaining the variation instead of merely allowing for it.

**Bonus.** You don't have to stop at the individual effect. You can also model **how the effect of one
variable on another varies with individual characteristics** — making it an alternative to interaction
terms in a standard regression.

### Correlated random effects and the Mundlak estimator

**Plain English.** Put both the individual's average and their deviations from it into the model.

**Precisely.** Correlated random effects is the other route: again allow the individual random effect to
be **related to characteristics you can measure**. The typical implementation is the **Mundlak
estimator**.

One variant is beautifully simple. Recall demeaning — take each individual's average and subtract it
out. Here you do that, **and use the demeaned variable in the model** — but you **also include the mean
itself**.

So you have **two versions of each variable**:

| Version | What it carries |
|---|---|
| **The mean** | The **between** variation |
| **The demeaned variable** | The **within** variation |

Put both in the model and you've separated the between and within variation, so you can use whatever
mix of the two you want. Here's the between, here's the within — do what you like.

### Where this leaves you

**Plain English.** Plain random effects blends between and within. The advanced versions hand you both,
labelled.

**Precisely.** Standard random effects doesn't get rid of all the between variation — it gives you a
**mix** of the between effect and the within effect. Use a more advanced method like hierarchical
linear modeling or correlated random effects and it says: **here's the between effect, here's the
within effect, do what you will** — improving your statistical modelling in the process.

**Where it breaks.** Random effects isn't always the right answer, but it's an attractive alternative to
fixed effects for controlling away between variation, with nice statistical properties — **as long as
the assumptions you need actually hold**. Those assumptions usually concern the relationship between the
individual effect (or at least whatever part of it you haven't modelled with covariates, if you're using
correlated random effects or hierarchical linear modeling) and the **treatment variable** you care about.

## TRAPS

- Random effects is a version of fixed effects → **Actually,** it's a different approach: it constrains individual effects to a distribution rather than removing them.
- Fixed effects is strictly safer, so prefer it → **Actually,** it costs statistical power and makes time-constant variables unstudyable. There's a real trade.
- You can study the effect of birthplace with country fixed effects → **Actually,** birthplace has no within variation, so fixed effects removes exactly the variation you'd need.
- Assuming a distribution is a technicality → **Actually,** it's the trade at the heart of the method: an assumption buys precision and risks wrong results.
- Plain random effects handles confounding as well as fixed effects → **Actually,** it requires the individual effect to be unrelated to the controls and treatment, which is usually false.
- Fixed effects is the standard everywhere → **Actually,** hierarchical linear modeling is probably more common than fixed effects in many fields; economics is the outlier in preferring fixed effects.
- Correlated random effects is complicated → **Actually,** the Mundlak variant just adds the individual mean alongside the demeaned variable.

## RECALL

Q: What does fixed effects do to the between variation?
A: It controls all of it away, leaving only within variation.

Q: Give the two downsides of that.
A: You can't study anything that exists only between individuals, and you lose statistical power by
discarding information.

Q: Why can't fixed effects study the effect of birthplace?
A: Birthplace has no within variation — it never changes for a person — so the variation you'd need was
exactly what got removed.

Q: What does random effects do instead?
A: It constrains the individual effects to follow a distribution rather than removing them, so it doesn't
eliminate all the between variation.

Q: What's the general trade being made?
A: Adding an assumption buys back statistical power but risks bad results if the assumption is wrong.

Q: What assumption does plain random effects need, and why is that a problem?
A: That the individual effect is unrelated to all the control variables and the treatment — which is
usually not the case.

Q: What does hierarchical linear modeling add?
A: It uses measurable characteristics — like geography — to predict where an individual sits in the
distribution of individual effects.

Q: Why does that fix the assumption problem?
A: Because it's now acceptable for the individual effect to be related to geography — that relationship
is incorporated into the estimation rather than assumed away.

Q: How common is hierarchical linear modeling?
A: Very — probably more common than fixed effects in a lot of fields, though economics tends to prefer
fixed effects.

Q: What extra thing can hierarchical linear modeling do?
A: Model how the effect of one variable on another varies with individual characteristics, as an
alternative to interaction terms.

Q: Describe the Mundlak variant of correlated random effects.
A: Demean each variable as usual and include the demeaned version — but also include the individual's
mean itself in the model.

Q: What does each of those two versions carry?
A: The mean carries the between variation; the demeaned variable carries the within variation.

Q: What does including both accomplish?
A: It separates the between and within variation so you can use whatever mix of them you want.

Q: What does plain random effects give you, versus the advanced versions?
A: Plain random effects gives a mix of the between and within effect. The advanced versions give you the
between effect and the within effect separately.

Q: What do random effects' assumptions usually concern?
A: The relationship between the individual effect — or whatever part of it you haven't modelled with
covariates — and the treatment variable you're interested in.

## GLOSSARY

- **Random effects** — treating individual effects as draws from a distribution rather than removing them.
- **Hierarchical linear modeling** — random effects where measurable characteristics predict an individual's position in the distribution of effects.
- **Correlated random effects** — random effects allowing the individual effect to relate to measured characteristics.
- **Mundlak estimator** — a correlated random effects approach including both the individual's mean and the demeaned variable.
