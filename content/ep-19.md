## ONE SENTENCE

Instead of hunting down and closing every back door — which requires a diagram everyone
agrees on *and* the ability to measure everything on it — find some source of random or
random-ish **exogenous variation** driving your treatment, isolate the part of treatment
it drives, and you've built yourself an experiment without running one.

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

- Closing back doors has three hard requirements: build the whole diagram, get others to agree it's right, and **measure everything** you need to control for.
- The alternative: pick out the **front door** directly rather than eliminating every back door.
- A **randomized experiment** does this. Randomize the treatment and no other factor can be driving it, so every back door is gone without measuring anything.
- Crucially, you then use **only the randomized data** — not everyone in the world.
- Can't randomize? Look for **exogenous variation**: something that drives treatment and has **no back doors to the outcome**. That's exactly the structure randomization creates.
- Sometimes someone else already randomized for you — the Vietnam **draft lottery**.
- You then isolate, **statistically**, the part of treatment driven by that variation.

## WHY THIS EXISTS

The last few chapters gave you a complete procedure: list the alternative explanations, draw
them as paths, close the bad ones by controlling for things. It works. It's also very hard to
actually pull off.

Three problems, in order:

1. Constructing the entire causal diagram is difficult.
2. Even if you construct one, getting other people to agree it's the correct diagram — and therefore that you really do know the full set of back doors — is another matter.
3. Even if everyone agreed, can you *measure* everything you'd need to control for? Often not.

Picking the right controls is easy once you have the diagram. The trouble is knowing the
diagram is right and being able to measure what it demands. So it's worth having another route
entirely.

## IDEAS

### Grab the front door instead of closing every back door

**Plain English.** You've got front door paths you want and back door paths you don't. Rather
than shutting each unwanted one, pick out the wanted one directly.

**Precisely.** Instead of enumerating and closing every back door from treatment to outcome,
isolate the portion of the treatment–outcome relationship that runs through front doors.

**Anchor image.** A noisy room. One approach is to find and silence every other conversation.
The other is a directional microphone pointed at the one you want.

### Why randomized experiments work

**Plain English.** A back door exists because something else is pushing your treatment around.
Randomize the treatment and nothing else is.

**Precisely.** A back door path exists because some factor drives both treatment and outcome,
producing a relationship in the data that isn't the effect. Randomize the treatment and that
factor is no longer driving it — so its route to the outcome no longer matters. All back doors
close at once, and you didn't measure a thing.

**Worked example.** Does wearing shorts make you eat more ice cream? In observed data,
temperature drives both — on a hot day you wear shorts and you eat ice cream.

Now randomly assign people to wear shorts. Shorts-wearing is no longer determined by
temperature. It doesn't matter that temperature still causes ice cream eating, because
temperature has lost its grip on the treatment — at least in this data.

**Where it breaks — and this is easy to miss:** you must then **limit yourself to the
randomized data**. You wouldn't randomize the treatment and then analyse your entire dataset.
You wouldn't randomize shorts-wearing and then include both the people in your experiment and
everyone outside it. You use the experiment.

Experiments have their own problems, which the book doesn't dig into. But they do solve this
particular identification problem cleanly.

### Exogenous variation: the generalisation

**Plain English.** You don't need actual randomization. You need something that behaves like
it.

**Precisely.** **Exogenous variation** is variation that's essentially random with respect to
your outcome. The requirement: something drives your treatment, and there are **no back doors
between that source and your outcome**.

**Anchor image.** A coin doesn't have to be *your* coin. It just has to be a coin.

**Worked example — why randomization satisfies this.** In a randomized experiment, the only
thing driving treatment is your randomization. Since you assigned it randomly, it's unrelated
to the outcome in any other way. There's no back door from the coin flip to the outcome:

```dag
node Z 0 0 "Coin flip" treat
node X 1.5 0 "Treatment"
node Y 3 0 "Outcome" out
node U 2.25 1 "Everything else" unobs
edge Z X
edge X Y
edge U X
edge U Y
note 1.5 1.8 "no back door runs from the coin flip to the outcome"
```

Anything that mimics that structure works just as well. You don't have to have done the
randomizing yourself.

### Someone else's randomization: the draft lottery

**Plain English.** The world sometimes randomizes things for its own reasons, and you can use it.

**Worked example.** A famous economics paper asks: does being in the military improve your later
labour-market prospects? It could go either way. Maybe the military teaches you valuable skills
and makes you a more effective worker, so you earn more. Or maybe it takes you away from years
you'd have spent building a civilian career, so it hurts your prospects afterwards.

You can't randomize people into the military. Nobody would let you.

But during the Vietnam War the United States had a draft, and the way it worked was that the
**order of birth dates was randomized**. Born on June 2nd? That lands early or late in the draft
order, decided at random, and whether you got drafted depended on where you fell.

That's randomization — not done for research, but real. It pushes people into treatment, and
your birth date should be largely unrelated to your later earnings. Maybe some minor things
sneak in, and you can probably control for those. It doesn't have to be *purely* random; you
just need to be able to close whatever other back doors exist.

### Isolating the part of treatment the randomization drove

**Plain English.** Don't study everyone who served. Study the people who served *because of the
lottery*.

**Precisely.** Exactly as with a real experiment, you restrict attention to the randomized
variation. Most people who did or didn't go into the military weren't really "in the experiment"
— they'd have made the same choice regardless.

**Anchor image.** A crowd, some pushed forward by one specific shove. You want the people who
moved because of the shove, not everyone standing in the same direction.

**Worked example.** You want the people driven into service by their randomly assigned birth
date. You can't point at them — you don't know who they are. But you can do it **statistically**:
use statistics to identify who was driven into service by the random assignment, then look at
the relationship between that random portion of treatment and earnings.

Do that and you've effectively found a little group inside your data for which a random
experiment was running, even though you never controlled it. That erases the back doors and
leaves the front doors — identification, without ever measuring the confounders.

**Where it breaks.** There are many specific ways to do this and many contexts where it comes
up, which is most of the remaining series. The general shape is what matters here: find
exogenous, random-ish variation with either no back doors or back doors you can easily shut.

## TRAPS

- Once I have the diagram, controls are easy, so back doors are a solved problem → **Actually,** picking controls is the easy part. Knowing the diagram is right and being able to measure everything on it are the hard parts.
- Randomizing means I can now analyse my whole dataset → **Actually,** you use only the randomized data. Randomizing shorts-wearing and then including everyone outside the experiment throws the benefit away.
- Exogenous variation has to be genuinely random → **Actually,** it needs no back doors to the outcome — or back doors you can close. The draft lottery has minor imperfections and still works.
- I should compare everyone who served to everyone who didn't → **Actually,** most of those people weren't in the experiment. You want the ones pushed into service by the lottery.
- I can identify the lottery-driven servicemen by hand → **Actually,** you don't know who they are. It's done statistically.
- Randomized experiments have no downsides → **Actually,** they have other problems the book doesn't go into. They solve *this* identification problem, not every problem.

## RECALL

Q: Name the three difficulties with the close-all-back-doors approach.
A: Constructing the entire causal diagram is hard; getting others to agree it's correct is
hard; and you may not be able to measure everything you'd need to control for.

Q: What's the alternative strategy?
A: Isolate the front door paths directly rather than enumerating and closing every back door.

Q: Explain why randomizing the treatment closes all back doors.
A: A back door exists because some factor drives both treatment and outcome. Randomize the
treatment and that factor no longer drives it, so its route to the outcome no longer produces
a relationship.

Q: You randomize shorts-wearing to study ice cream eating. What must you do with your data?
A: Use only the randomized data — the people in your experiment — not everyone in the dataset.

Q: Define exogenous variation.
A: Variation that drives your treatment and has no back doors to your outcome — essentially
random with respect to the outcome.

Q: Why does a randomized experiment satisfy that definition?
A: The only thing driving treatment is your randomization, which you did at random, so there's
no back door from the coin flip to the outcome.

Q: What question does the Vietnam draft lottery study answer, and why can't it be done directly?
A: Whether military service improves later labour-market earnings. You can't randomly assign
people to the military — nobody would permit it.

Q: How does the draft lottery supply exogenous variation?
A: The order of birth dates was randomized, and where your birthday fell in that order
determined whether you were drafted — a real randomization, done for non-research reasons, and
largely unrelated to later earnings.

Q: Does exogenous variation have to be perfectly random?
A: No. It needs no back doors to the outcome, or back doors you can close. Minor imperfections
you can control for are acceptable.

Q: Why not compare everyone who served in the military to everyone who didn't?
A: Most of them weren't in the experiment — they'd have made the same choice regardless. You
want the people driven into service by the random assignment.

Q: How do you identify those people?
A: Not by hand — you don't know who they are. You use statistics to isolate the portion of
treatment driven by the random assignment, then relate that portion to the outcome.

Q: Give both arguments for how military service might affect earnings.
A: It might teach valuable skills making you a more effective worker, raising earnings. Or it
might take you away from years you'd have spent developing a civilian career, hurting your
prospects.

## GLOSSARY

- **Exogenous variation** — variation in the treatment that has no back doors to the outcome; essentially random with respect to it.
- **Randomized controlled experiment** — a design where the researcher assigns treatment at random, closing all back doors by construction.
- **Draft lottery** — the Vietnam-era randomization of birth-date order, used as exogenous variation in military service.
