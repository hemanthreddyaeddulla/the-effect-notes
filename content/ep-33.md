## ONE SENTENCE

The standard error formula isn't a definition — it's a **calculation resting on assumptions
about the error term**, and when those fail (heteroskedasticity, autocorrelation, clustering)
your standard errors stop measuring the sampling distribution, which is the only thing they
were for.

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

- Good news first: when these assumptions fail, your **coefficient is usually fine**. It's the **standard error** that breaks.
- **Normality of errors** is the least of your worries — and it's about the *error term*, not your variables.
- The real assumption is that errors are **i.i.d.**: identically distributed and independent.
- **Not identically distributed → heteroskedasticity.** The *variance* of the error is related to X.
- **Not independent → autocorrelation** (repeated measures over time) or **clustering** (hierarchical data).
- Why it breaks the formula: σ² is meant to capture error variance, and you measure it badly if you ignore these patterns.
- Fix with **sandwich estimators** — same formula, σ² and the variance of X computed to allow for the problem.
- Many economists just always use heteroskedasticity-robust errors. Nick: seems okay to him.

## WHY THIS EXISTS

Bad news: your standard errors are probably wrong and you need to fix them. This is a scourge of
the applied econometrician — or anyone doing causal inference — and you will have to deal with it.

Here's the issue. A standard error is the standard deviation of the sampling distribution of your
coefficient, and you need it because it tells you how much you actually know — how precise a claim
you can make. Ep 26 gave you a formula for it. But **that formula is not the definition** of the
standard deviation of an OLS coefficient. It's a calculation **based on assumptions**. If the
assumptions are wrong, the calculation no longer reflects the standard deviation of the sampling
distribution — which is the only reason you computed it.

Reassuringly: when these assumptions fail, the main casualty is your estimate of the **standard
error**. Your **OLS coefficient will usually be fine**. But you still have to get the standard
errors right.

## IDEAS

### Assumption 1: normally distributed errors — mostly ignorable

**Plain English.** It's about the errors, not your variables. And even then it barely matters.

**Precisely.** In `y = β₀ + β₁x + ε`, one assumption is that **ε** follows a normal distribution.

**The widespread misunderstanding:** many people take this to mean your **variables** — your X's
and Y's — must be normally distributed. They don't. The assumption is only about the error term.

**And even past that, it doesn't matter much.** If your errors aren't normal, what breaks is that
you can no longer *prove* the OLS coefficient is normally distributed. It might follow some other
distribution — but it's usually pretty close to normal anyway, unless your errors are really,
really weird, in which case you have bigger problems than non-normality.

**Anchor image.** A warning light for a fault that almost never grounds the plane.

### Assumption 2: i.i.d. errors — the one that matters

**Plain English.** Every error comes from the same distribution, and knowing one tells you nothing
about another.

**Precisely.** **Independent and identically distributed.**

- **Identically distributed** — your error term, my error term, everyone's error term follows the same distribution.
- **Independent** — they're uncorrelated. Knowing my error term tells you nothing about that person's.

Three common ways this fails.

### Failure 1: heteroskedasticity

**Plain English.** The spread of the errors changes as X changes.

**Precisely.** **Heteroskedasticity** is when the **variance** of the error term is related to your
predictors X. Note the subtlety: you already assumed X is unrelated to **the error term itself** —
but that doesn't mean its **variance** can't still be related to X.

**Anchor image.** A river that's narrow and calm at the source and wide and choppy at the mouth.
Same river, different behaviour along its length.

**Worked example.** The classic "fan" shape: at low values of X there's very little spread around
the line — the variance of the residuals, and so probably of the errors, is small. As you move to
high values of X, things spread out; the variance grows.

**Where it breaks.** Not all heteroskedastic data looks like a fan. That's one example; other
shapes occur too.

### Failure 2: autocorrelation

**Plain English.** Anything measured repeatedly over time correlates with itself nearby in time.

**Precisely.** With **time series** data (one observation tracked over time) or **panel** data
(many individuals each measured repeatedly), observations are likely correlated with their nearest
periods in time.

**Anchor image.** Today's weather is a decent guess for tomorrow's. Errors behave the same way.

**Worked example.** Fit an OLS line to the unemployment rate over time and it looks bad
immediately — partly because there's shape you haven't accounted for, but also because of
autocorrelation. Pick any point on the graph: the points on either side of it are likely closer to
it than other observations in the data are. If you're above the line this period, you're likely
above it next period too.

And being above the line **is** the residual — how far above you are is the residual, which is your
representation of the error term. So the error terms are correlated with each other across time.

**Where it breaks.** This shows up essentially any time you have a measure that is repeatedly
measured.

### Failure 3: clustering

**Plain English.** Data collected in groups has errors that come in groups.

**Precisely.** When data is collected **hierarchically**, observations within a group resemble each
other more than they resemble observations in other groups — and so do their residuals, and so do
their errors.

**Anchor image.** Families at a restaurant. Knowing what one person ordered tells you a lot about
their table and nothing about the next one.

**Worked example.** Students taking a test. Students at the same school probably have more similar
scores than students at different schools. And within a school, students in the same classroom —
sharing a teacher — are likely more similar still. Once you fit a model and look at the residuals,
those cluster too: this classroom's errors are closer to each other than to another classroom's.

So if I learn about your error term, that tells me something about the error terms of everyone in
your class. The errors are correlated.

### Why any of this breaks the formula

**Plain English.** The formula's top term is supposed to measure error variance, and you'll measure
it badly if you ignore the structure.

**Precisely.** Look back at the standard error equation. **σ²** on top is a measure of prediction
error — the variance of the error terms. You won't do a good job measuring that if you don't
account for heteroskedasticity, clustering, or autocorrelation. Just taking the overall variance of
the errors while ignoring those relationships leaves you ignorant about how sampling variation is
actually going to behave. You may also get weirdness in the **variance of X** term.

So the calculation needs adjusting to be aware that these assumptions have failed.

### The fix: sandwich estimators

**Plain English.** Same formula, different arithmetic for the pieces that broke.

**Precisely.** A **sandwich estimator** performs the same calculation you already have, except it
computes **σ²** in a slightly different way that accounts for the problem, and adjusts the
**variance of X** term similarly.

There's one for each failure, named unsurprisingly:

| Problem | Fix |
|---|---|
| Heteroskedasticity | **Heteroskedasticity-robust** standard errors |
| Autocorrelation (with heteroskedasticity) | **Heteroskedasticity- and autocorrelation-consistent (HAC)** standard errors |
| Clustering | **Cluster-robust** standard errors |

They don't do anything wild — they take the calculation you already have and compute σ² and the
variance of X in ways that allow for the problem. The mechanics are in the book, or on Wikipedia.

**Worked example — what the heteroskedasticity-robust version is doing.** Go back to the fan graph.
At low X there's little variance in the error; at high X there's a lot. When computing the standard
error, the estimator **inflates the influence** of those high-X observations, because it knows
there's a lot of variance over there, and **shrinks the influence** of the low-X ones. The result is
an estimate of error variance that better matches the sampling variation you'd actually get from a
graph shaped like that.

**Where it breaks.** A lot of economists pretty much always use heteroskedasticity-robust standard
errors, just assuming it's always going to be a problem. Nick's verdict: he doesn't know that
that's necessarily wrong — it seems okay to him.

## TRAPS

- The standard error formula is the definition of the sampling standard deviation → **Actually,** it's a calculation built on assumptions. Break them and it stops measuring what you wanted.
- My variables need to be normally distributed → **Actually,** the assumption is about the **error term**, not your X's and Y's. And it matters little either way.
- Non-normal errors invalidate my regression → **Actually,** you just lose the *proof* that the coefficient is normally distributed. It's usually close to normal anyway.
- Failed error assumptions mean my coefficient is biased → **Actually,** the coefficient is usually fine. It's the standard error that's wrong.
- I assumed X is unrelated to the error term, so heteroskedasticity can't happen → **Actually,** X can be unrelated to the error term while still being related to its **variance**.
- Heteroskedasticity looks like a fan → **Actually,** that's one shape among several.
- Robust standard errors are a different, more complex estimator → **Actually,** a sandwich estimator is the same formula with σ² and the variance of X computed differently.

## RECALL

Q: Why can the standard error formula be wrong?
A: It isn't the definition of the sampling distribution's standard deviation — it's a calculation
resting on assumptions about the error term. If those fail, it stops reflecting the sampling
distribution.

Q: When these assumptions fail, what breaks and what survives?
A: The estimate of the standard error breaks. The OLS coefficient is usually fine.

Q: What exactly does the normality assumption apply to?
A: The error term ε — not your X and Y variables, which is the common misunderstanding.

Q: What actually goes wrong if errors aren't normally distributed?
A: You can no longer prove the OLS coefficient is normally distributed. It may follow another
distribution, but usually one close to normal unless the errors are very strange.

Q: What does i.i.d. stand for, and what does each part mean?
A: Independent and identically distributed. Identically distributed: every error follows the same
distribution. Independent: they're uncorrelated, so knowing one tells you nothing about another.

Q: Define heteroskedasticity.
A: When the variance of the error term is related to your predictors X.

Q: You assumed X is unrelated to the error term. Why doesn't that rule out heteroskedasticity?
A: Because X being unrelated to the error term itself doesn't prevent it from being related to the
error term's *variance*.

Q: Describe the fan shape.
A: At low values of X the residuals have little spread; as X increases the spread grows. It's one
example of heteroskedasticity, not the only shape.

Q: What is autocorrelation and where does it arise?
A: Errors correlated with each other across time — arising in time series data or panel data, and
essentially any time you have a repeatedly measured value.

Q: Use the unemployment example to explain why errors are autocorrelated.
A: If you're above the fitted line this period, you're likely above it next period too. Being above
the line is the residual, which represents the error term — so errors correlate across time.

Q: What is clustering, and give the example.
A: Hierarchically collected data producing grouped errors. Students at the same school have more
similar scores than students at different schools, and within a school, students sharing a
classroom and teacher are more similar still — so their residuals and errors cluster.

Q: Why do these failures break the standard error calculation?
A: σ² is supposed to measure the variance of the error terms, and computing it while ignoring
heteroskedasticity, autocorrelation, or clustering measures it badly — leaving you ignorant of how
sampling variation actually behaves. The variance of X term can also misbehave.

Q: What is a sandwich estimator doing?
A: The same standard error calculation, but computing σ² and the variance of X in ways that account
for the specific problem.

Q: Name the three fixes.
A: Heteroskedasticity-robust standard errors; heteroskedasticity- and autocorrelation-consistent
(HAC) standard errors; and cluster-robust standard errors.

Q: How does the heteroskedasticity-robust version handle the fan graph?
A: It inflates the influence of the high-X observations, where variance is large, and shrinks the
influence of the low-X ones — giving an error-variance estimate closer to the sampling variation
you'd really get.

Q: What do many economists do by default?
A: Always use heteroskedasticity-robust standard errors, assuming it's always a problem — which
Nick considers reasonable.

## GLOSSARY

- **i.i.d.** — independent and identically distributed; the standard assumption about error terms.
- **Heteroskedasticity** — the variance of the error term being related to the predictors.
- **Autocorrelation** — error terms correlated across time in time series or panel data.
- **Clustering** — error terms correlated within groups in hierarchically collected data.
- **Sandwich estimator** — a standard error calculation that recomputes σ² and the variance of X to allow for one of these failures.
- **Robust standard errors** — standard errors computed by a sandwich estimator.
