## ONE SENTENCE

Compute **four means** and subtract twice, or run a regression with a **treated × after
interaction** where **β₃ is the answer**, or — for many groups and many periods — use a **two-way
fixed effects** model; and all of these break the moment different groups get treated at
**different times**.

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

- **Four means:** treated after minus treated before, minus (control after minus control before). Done.
- Limitations: no standard errors, and it doesn't extend past two groups and two periods.
- **Regression version:** regress the outcome on **treated group**, **after treatment**, and their **interaction**. The four coefficients recover the four means, and **β₃ is the effect**.
- **Two-way fixed effects:** a fixed effect for **group**, a fixed effect for **time**, and a variable for "currently being treated." Handles many groups and periods.
- **Cluster standard errors by group.**
- **Critical:** all of these require every treatment to begin at **the same time**. Staggered treatment breaks them.

## WHY THIS EXISTS

The concept is settled. Now you have to actually estimate it — which means comparing the treated
group's before-to-after change against the control group's, and subtracting.

## IDEAS

### The four means approach

**Plain English.** Just literally do it. Four averages, two subtractions.

**Precisely.** Calculate the average outcome after treatment for the treated group and the average
before, and subtract. Do the same for the control group. Now you have a before-to-after difference
for each, and you subtract one from the other.

### Worked example: organ donation and forced choice

**Plain English.** California made you answer the organ donor question instead of letting you skip
it, and donation rates fell.

**Precisely.** In the United States, organ donation is an **opt-in** system: when you get your
driver's licence they ask whether you want to be an organ donor, and you check a box for yes or
leave it. That produces much lower donation rates than countries with **opt-out** systems, where
you're assumed to be a donor unless you say otherwise.

**Forced choice** is a variant in between. You still have to opt in, but you're required to
**respond** — yes for yes, **no for no**. You can't simply ignore it.

**The question:** does forced choice increase organ donation rates? Kessler and Roth looked at
**California**, which adopted forced choice in **July 2011**, and compared it against a set of other,
comparable states that didn't.

**The graph.** Four lines: a dashed line before and after the July 2011 cutoff for the control
states, and a solid line before and after for California. There are three quarters of California
donation data before treatment and three quarters after; average each to get the two solid segments.
The estimation is: see how much the **dashed line jumped** at the cutoff, declare that's how much
California would have jumped anyway, and push the lines down so the dashed segments meet — moving
California's line down by the same amount.

**The four means**, in organ donation rates:

| Group | Before | After | Change |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Control** (other states) | 44.5 | 45.9 | **+1.4** |
| **California** (treated) | 27.1 | 26.3 | **−0.8** |
| | | **Difference-in-differences** | **−2.2** |

**The reasoning.** Control-group donation rates were **going up** over time — so absent any policy,
California's probably would have gone up too, by around 1.4 percentage points. Instead they went
**down** by 0.8. So the policy appears to have made things **worse by 2.2 percentage points**.

The arithmetic is just the four means in the right order: (26.3 − 27.1) − (45.9 − 44.5) = (−0.8) −
(1.4) = **−2.2**.

**Where it breaks.** Two obvious limitations. It's hard to get a **standard error** this way. And
it's very difficult to go beyond **two groups and two time periods** — what if you have data from
more periods, or more groups?

### The regression version

**Plain English.** Outcome on treated, on after, and on their interaction. The interaction *is* the
answer.

**Precisely.** Set up a regression that estimates those four means for you. Regress the outcome on:

- an indicator for being in the **treated group**
- an indicator for being **after treatment**
- the **interaction** between the two — which is also just asking, *are you currently being treated?*

The four coefficients map onto the four means:

| treated group | after treatment | Combination | Which mean |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0 | 0 | **β₀** | Untreated group, before |
| 1 | 0 | **β₀ + β₁** | Treated group, before — so β₁ is the baseline difference |
| 0 | 1 | **β₀ + β₂** | Untreated group, after |
| 1 | 1 | **β₀ + β₁ + β₂ + β₃** | Treated group, after |

**β₃ is your difference-in-differences effect** — the regression does the calculation for you.

**Why.** Ask what happens when `after` goes from 0 to 1:

- **Untreated group:** from β₀ to β₀ + β₂ — an increase of **β₂**. That's the time effect.
- **Treated group:** from β₀ + β₁ to all four terms — an increase of **β₂ + β₃**. That's the time effect *and* the treatment effect.

Subtract the time effect β₂ and you're left with **β₃**.

**Anchor image.** Two receipts with a shared surcharge. Subtract the common line item and only the
thing you actually bought remains.

**Where it breaks.** That's a lot to hold at once — it's a fairly standard application of an
interaction term, so reviewing **Ep 32** helps as much as re-reading this.

### Beyond two groups and two periods: two-way fixed effects

**Plain English.** Fixed effects for group and time, plus one variable saying "treated right now."

**Precisely.** The organ donation study actually had **six time periods** — three quarters before and
three after — and a number of different states. You might not want to lump them all into a single
control and treatment, before and after; you might want their individual variation to show.

And you already know the tool. You're looking at **within variation** — the before-to-after change
for the treated group, and the before-to-after change for the control group. Within variation means
**fixed effects**.

So regress the outcome on:

| Term | What it does |
|---|---|
| **α_g** — fixed effect for group | Ensures you're working within within-variation |
| **α_t** — fixed effect for time | Subtracts out the shared time effects, estimated using the control group |
| **treated** — 1 if you're in a treated group in a post-treatment period | Its coefficient **β₁** is the effect of treatment |

That leaves **β₁** as the treatment effect from before to after. This is the **two-way fixed effects
model**, and it's probably the easiest way to estimate difference-in-differences. It gives you nice
standard errors, and you can **cluster them by group** — a common choice, since you'd expect
observations within a group to be more similar than across groups.

### The warning that matters most

**Plain English.** Everything above assumes everyone got treated at the same moment.

**Precisely.** All of these methods — the four means approach and **all** the regression methods —
**only work if all of the treatments went into effect at the same time.**

**Worked example.** California is fine: there's only one California, treated once, in July 2011. But
suppose Wyoming also switched to forced choice — California in **July 2011**, Wyoming in **July
2012**.

That's **staggered treatment**. You can still work with it, but **you cannot use these regression
methods to do it. It doesn't work properly.**

## TRAPS

- The four means approach is a simplified teaching version → **Actually,** it gives the correct point estimate. What it can't give you is standard errors or more than two groups and two periods.
- β₁ in the interaction regression is the treatment effect → **Actually,** β₁ is the *baseline difference* between groups. β₃ is the effect.
- β₂ is the treatment effect since it's the after-treatment coefficient → **Actually,** β₂ is the time effect — what happened to the untreated group.
- Two-way fixed effects is a different design → **Actually,** it's the same difference-in-differences, generalised to many groups and periods.
- California's rate is far below the control states, so they're not comparable → **Actually,** 27% against 45% is a large baseline difference, and parallel trends permits that.
- Donation rates fell by 0.8, so the effect is −0.8 → **Actually,** the control group rose 1.4, so the estimate is −2.2.
- If two-way fixed effects handles many groups and periods, it handles different treatment timings → **Actually,** it doesn't. Staggered treatment breaks every method in this episode.

## SAY IT OUT LOUD

> Three ways to actually estimate a difference-in-differences, and they're the same thing getting
> progressively more general.
>
> **Four means.** Average the treated group before and after, subtract. Average the control before and
> after, subtract. Subtract those two differences. Done.
>
> Great example: organ donation. In the US it's **opt-in** — you tick a box for your driver's licence,
> or don't. California switched to **forced choice** in July 2011, meaning you now *have* to answer:
> yes for yes, **no for no**. Question is whether that raises donation rates.
>
> Control states went from **44.5 to 45.9** — up 1.4. California went from **27.1 to 26.3** — *down*
> 0.8. So everybody else was drifting up, California drifted down, and the estimate is **minus 2.2
> percentage points**. The policy appears to have made things worse.
>
> Four means gets you the number but no standard errors, and it can't handle more than two groups. So
> **regression**: outcome on treated, on after, and on the **interaction**. Four coefficients for four
> means — and **β₃, the interaction, is your answer.** Because when you go from before to after, the
> untreated group moves by β₂ — that's just time — and the treated group moves by β₂ *plus* β₃.
> Subtract time and you're left with β₃.
>
> Then for many groups and many periods: **two-way fixed effects.** Fixed effect for group, fixed
> effect for time, and one variable that says "are you being treated right now." That last coefficient
> is your effect. Cluster your standard errors by group.
>
> And now the warning, and it's a big one. **Every single method here assumes everyone got treated at
> the same time.** California in July 2011 — fine. But if Wyoming switches in July **2012**, you have
> **staggered treatment**, and none of this works. That's a whole separate problem.

## RECALL

Q: Describe the four means approach.
A: Compute the treated group's average before and after and subtract; do the same for the control
group; then subtract the control group's difference from the treated group's.

Q: What is forced choice, and how does it differ from opt-in and opt-out?
A: You still have to opt in, but you're required to answer the question — yes for yes, no for no —
rather than being able to ignore it. It sits between opt-in and opt-out.

Q: What did Kessler and Roth study, and what was the treatment?
A: Whether forced choice raises organ donation rates. California adopted forced choice in July 2011,
compared against other comparable states.

Q: Give the four means.
A: Control states 44.5 before and 45.9 after; California 27.1 before and 26.3 after.

Q: Compute the difference-in-differences and interpret it.
A: (26.3 − 27.1) − (45.9 − 44.5) = −0.8 − 1.4 = −2.2 percentage points — the policy appears to have
reduced donation rates by 2.2 points.

Q: Why isn't California's 0.8-point drop the answer on its own?
A: Because the control states rose by 1.4, so California would likely have risen too. The shortfall is
2.2, not 0.8.

Q: What are the two limitations of the four means approach?
A: It's hard to obtain standard errors, and it doesn't extend beyond two groups and two time periods.

Q: What three terms go in the regression version?
A: An indicator for being in the treated group, an indicator for being after treatment, and their
interaction.

Q: What does each coefficient represent?
A: β₀ is the untreated group before; β₀ + β₁ the treated group before, so β₁ is the baseline
difference; β₀ + β₂ the untreated group after, so β₂ is the time effect; and all four terms give the
treated group after.

Q: Which coefficient is the difference-in-differences effect, and why?
A: β₃ — because going from before to after, the untreated group rises by β₂ while the treated group
rises by β₂ + β₃, so subtracting the time effect leaves β₃.

Q: What three terms make up the two-way fixed effects model?
A: A fixed effect for group, a fixed effect for time, and a variable equal to one if you're in a
treated group during a post-treatment period.

Q: What does each of the two fixed effects accomplish?
A: The group fixed effect keeps you working within within-variation; the time fixed effect subtracts
out the shared time effects estimated from the control group.

Q: How should standard errors usually be handled here?
A: Clustered by group, since observations within a group are likely more similar than across groups.

Q: What is the crucial limitation shared by every method in this episode?
A: They only work if all treatments went into effect at the same time.

Q: What is staggered treatment, and what can you do about it?
A: When different groups get treated at different times — California in July 2011 and Wyoming in July
2012. You can still analyse it, but not with any of these regression methods.

## GLOSSARY

- **Four means approach** — computing difference-in-differences directly from four group-by-period averages.
- **Opt-in / opt-out / forced choice** — organ donation default regimes; forced choice requires an explicit answer either way.
- **Two-way fixed effects model** — difference-in-differences estimated with group and time fixed effects plus a currently-treated indicator.
- **Staggered treatment** — different units receiving treatment at different times, which breaks the standard estimators.
