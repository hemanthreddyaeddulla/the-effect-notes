## ONE SENTENCE

Where a **smooth change** is followed by a **sudden one at a cutoff**, the people just on either
side of that cutoff are essentially identical except that one got treated — so comparing within a
narrow **bandwidth** closes every back door, including ones you could never measure.

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

- The setup: a **running variable** changes smoothly, a **cutoff** produces a sudden jump, and a **bandwidth** narrows you to the comparable cases.
- **San Diego to Tijuana:** a few miles takes income from $85,000 to $50,000. A few **feet** takes it from $50,000 to $25,000.
- Why it works: $12,001 and $11,999 of income are **basically the same people**, but only one qualifies.
- This is a **front door** design, like instrumental variables — not a close-the-back-doors design.
- **You can't control for the running variable** — it determines treatment entirely. You control for it *everywhere except* around the cutoff.
- Applies to geographic borders, **close elections**, and any policy with a threshold.
- Probably the most trusted design after randomized experiments — though Nick suspects future **statistical power** problems.

## WHY THIS EXISTS

Regression discontinuity is all about **smooth changes followed by big changes at cutoffs**. And
the cutoff is where the attention goes.

**Worked example — the border.** San Diego sits at the very southern end of California, and it's
fairly wealthy: median income **$85,000**, quite a bit above the national average. Downtown is
roughly at the northern end, and as you move south toward Mexico, average incomes fall. By the time
you reach **San Ysidro**, the neighbourhood just above the Mexican border, average incomes are more
like **$50,000**.

Then you get out of your car, and there's the border, with all the stands of people guarding it. You
walk a few feet — from San Ysidro into **Tijuana** — and incomes drop precipitously, to something like
**$25,000**.

**It took a few miles to fall from 85 to 50. It takes a few feet to fall from 50 to 25.**

A continuous change as you go further south, and then a big change **all at once** when you cross. That
is a **discontinuity** in the relationship between how far south you are and how much income there is.

## IDEAS

### The three pieces

**Plain English.** Something that changes gradually, a line it crosses, and how close you stay to that
line.

| Term | What it is | In the border example |
|---|---|---|
| **Running variable** | The continuous variable that changes slowly and determines when you pass the cutoff | How far south you are |
| **Cutoff** | The point at which something changes abruptly | The US–Mexico border |
| **Bandwidth** | A narrow range around the cutoff, giving a cleaner comparison | Only people living very near the border |

**Why the bandwidth matters.** Going further south predicts lower income all by itself. So if you
simply compare everyone in Mexico against everyone in the United States, one of those groups is
**further south than the other**, and that influence contaminates the comparison.

But focus on people living **just** on one side of the border against people **just** on the other, and
they're **about as south as each other** — while being on different sides. Comparing within a narrow
range around the border strips out the influence of "going south" and isolates the influence of
"crossing the border."

**Anchor image.** Two houses on opposite sides of a street that happens to be a county line. Same
weather, same soil, same shops — different laws.

### Where it applies

**Plain English.** Anywhere a threshold flips something on.

- **Geographic borders** — a literal national border, or one determining police jurisdiction, and therefore which kind of policing you're exposed to.
- **Close elections** — vote share moves smoothly. Party A is winning at 50-something percent, then 50.1%, then 50.01% — and a tiny change from 50.01% to 49.9% **dramatically changes who won the election**. So you can see the effect of having one politician rather than another.
- **Threshold policies** — say a poverty alleviation policy you qualify for by earning less than a set amount. To find its effect on your future chances of a job: as your income falls, that's related to your future prospects anyway. But right at the cutoff — at $12,001 you don't get it, at $11,999 you do.

### Why it identifies anything

**Plain English.** At the cutoff, which side you land on is essentially random.

**Precisely.** The causal diagram:

```dag
node B 1.6 0 "Back door stuff" unobs
node R 0 1.2 "Running variable"
node C 1.6 1.2 "Above cutoff?" treat
node X 3.2 1.2 "Treatment"
node Y 4.8 1.2 "Outcome" out
edge B R
edge B Y
edge R C
edge C X
edge X Y
note 2.4 2 "narrow bandwidth: no variation in the running variable left"
```

Whether you're treated is entirely determined by whether you're above or below the cutoff, which is
determined by the running variable. And there are plenty of back door variables related to how far
south you are — or, in the policy case, how much income you have. Almost certainly you couldn't
control for them all.

**But this isn't a design where you control for things.** Like instrumental variables, it's a design
where you **isolate a front door**. By looking only at a bandwidth around the cutoff, you focus on the
part of the data where there **isn't really any variation in the running variable**, and it becomes
rather like being randomly assigned to one side or the other.

**Worked example.** Yes, lots of back door stuff relates to income. But compare people earning
**$12,001** against people earning **$11,999**. Those are basically the same people — the same on
pretty much every characteristic you can imagine — except one got treated and the other didn't. It's
almost random which of those you happen to earn in a given year: maybe one of them showed up to work
fifteen minutes early one day, got an extra fifteen minutes on their paycheck, and that pushed them
over the edge.

So they're comparable, one was treated and one wasn't, and **you close all the back doors — even for
things you cannot measure.**

### Why you can't just control for the running variable

**Plain English.** It determines treatment perfectly, so controlling for it leaves nothing.

**Precisely.** If you tried to control for the running variable directly, that wouldn't work — the
running variable is the only thing determining treatment, so there'd be **no variation left**.

What you're actually doing is **controlling for all parts of the running variable except the part just
around the cutoff.** You're controlling for how "south" affects income as you move through San Diego,
and how it affects income as you move through Mexico — but **not** for how south affects income in that
tiny area around the border. Isolating just that part isolates a relationship between going south and
the outcome that has a **causal interpretation**.

### How much people trust it

**Plain English.** Highly — arguably more than anything except a randomized experiment.

**Precisely.** Right now regression discontinuity is probably the causal inference research design that
people trust most, aside from randomized experiments. There are a number of literal **government
policies** stating that evidence will only be accepted as high quality if it comes from a randomized
experiment **or a regression discontinuity design**.

**Is that justified?** Nick isn't sure — he thinks there may be **statistical power** issues with
regression discontinuity in future, which comes up later.

**Why it's compelling.** It's very intuitive: you can genuinely see how it picks out a part of the
variation amounting to random assignment. The assumptions are fairly plausible even if statistical
power is a bit weak, and you get nice, intuitive, easy-to-understand results — graphs where a variable
changes as it leads up to the cutoff and then, at the cutoff, **changes right away**.

That's convincing, because when you look at variation in the running variable **before** the treatment
comes into play, there's no real effect; when you look **after**, no real effect; but **right where the
treatment changes** is exactly where the effect appears. Which tells you the treatment is probably what
did it.

**Where it breaks.** It only applies where treatment is assigned **based on a cutoff**, which isn't
always the case. So the places you can use it are somewhat limited — but when you can, it's very good.

## TRAPS

- Compare everyone on one side of the border to everyone on the other → **Actually,** one group is further south, and southness predicts income by itself. You need a narrow bandwidth.
- Control for the running variable to close its back doors → **Actually,** it determines treatment completely, so controlling for it removes all treatment variation.
- Regression discontinuity closes back doors by measuring confounders → **Actually,** it's a front door design. It isolates variation that is as good as random.
- You need to know what the back door variables are → **Actually,** the whole point is that near the cutoff they're balanced, including ones you could never measure.
- Anywhere a variable changes fast is a discontinuity design → **Actually,** you need a genuine cutoff at which treatment assignment jumps, not merely a steep slope.
- It's the most trusted design so it has no drawbacks → **Actually,** Nick flags likely statistical power problems, and it only applies where a cutoff assigns treatment.

## RECALL

Q: What structure does regression discontinuity require?
A: A smooth change in some variable followed by a sudden change at a cutoff.

Q: Give the San Diego to Tijuana figures.
A: Median income around $85,000 in San Diego, about $50,000 in San Ysidro just north of the border, and
about $25,000 in Tijuana just across it.

Q: What makes that a discontinuity rather than just a decline?
A: It takes a few miles to fall from 85 to 50, but only a few feet to fall from 50 to 25 — a sudden
change at a specific point.

Q: Define the running variable.
A: The continuous variable that changes smoothly and determines whether you fall above or below the
cutoff.

Q: Define the bandwidth and explain why it matters.
A: A narrow range around the cutoff. It matters because comparing everyone on each side confounds the
treatment with the running variable itself — but people just either side of the cutoff have nearly the
same value of the running variable.

Q: Name three settings where regression discontinuity applies.
A: Geographic borders (including policing jurisdictions), close elections, and policies with a
qualifying threshold.

Q: How do close elections form a discontinuity?
A: Vote share moves smoothly, but a tiny change from 50.01% to 49.9% dramatically changes who won — so
you can compare the effect of having one politician rather than another.

Q: Why are people earning $12,001 and $11,999 comparable?
A: They're essentially the same on every characteristic you can imagine, and which side you land on is
almost random — someone arriving fifteen minutes early once could tip it.

Q: Is this a back door or front door design?
A: A front door design, like instrumental variables — it isolates variation that's as good as random
rather than measuring and controlling confounders.

Q: Why can't you control for the running variable directly?
A: It's the only thing determining treatment, so controlling for it leaves no treatment variation.

Q: What are you actually controlling for instead?
A: All parts of the running variable except the portion just around the cutoff.

Q: How highly is regression discontinuity regarded?
A: Probably the most trusted design after randomized experiments — some government policies accept
evidence as high quality only from randomized experiments or regression discontinuity designs.

Q: What reservation does Nick raise?
A: He suspects there may be statistical power problems with regression discontinuity.

Q: Why are regression discontinuity graphs so convincing?
A: Because there's no real effect from the running variable before the cutoff, none after, but a clear
change exactly at the point treatment changes.

Q: What limits where you can use it?
A: It only applies where treatment is assigned based on a cutoff.

## GLOSSARY

- **Regression discontinuity** — a design exploiting a treatment assigned by whether a continuous variable crosses a cutoff.
- **Running variable** — the continuous variable determining which side of the cutoff you fall on.
- **Cutoff** — the threshold at which treatment assignment changes abruptly.
- **Bandwidth** — the narrow range around the cutoff within which comparisons are made.
- **Discontinuity** — a sudden change in a relationship at a specific point.
