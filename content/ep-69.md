## ONE SENTENCE

Four last assumptions nobody wants to discuss — that treatment doesn't **spill over** to the
untreated, that you picked the **right model** out of the hundreds you could have, that your variable
actually **has a mean**, and the one that should haunt you: if your treated and control groups are
genuinely so comparable, **why was one of them treated?**

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

- **SUTVA:** no spillovers. If a tutored child passes tips to friends, the untreated group is treated a little.
- Fix by **redefining treatment** broadly enough to contain the spillover — the share of a classroom tutored, rather than one student.
- **Model uncertainty:** you had to make decisions on imperfect information. This isn't sampling variation — it's not knowing which of many models is right.
- Try every combination of controls and build a **specification diagram**. Colliders and omitted variables you never thought of can't be fixed that way.
- **Nonexistent moments:** some **fat-tailed** distributions have no mean to estimate. Ed Sheeran versus a 1500m runner.
- **The treatment mystery:** if they're so similar, why did one get treated? Regression discontinuity can answer this. Twin studies can't.

## IDEAS

### SUTVA: the stable unit treatment value assumption

**Plain English.** Do you actually know what "treated" means? Because if treatment leaks, the untreated
are treated too.

**Precisely.** SUTVA says the treatment you're estimating is the treatment **on the individual you're
estimating it on**. Which sounds circular — so think of it as **no spillover effects**.

**Worked example.** You're studying the effect of a tutoring programme on children's grades. You
randomize tutoring: completely random, no back doors, everything's fine.

But what if your kid, being tutored, **passes tips and tricks along to the friends they study with**?

Then tutoring has spillovers. Being treated is being treated — and **not being treated might also mean
being treated, just a little bit.** Look at the effect in your data and you're mixing the two up,
because the untreated population is partly treated.

**Anchor image.** Testing a perfume by having one person wear it in a small room. Everybody's wearing
it now.

**A way around it — redefine the treatment.** Choose a treatment area broad enough that spillovers stay
inside it. If tutoring might spill between friends in the same classroom, but probably not between
classrooms, then don't study the effect of tutoring **on an individual student**. Study the effect of
the **share of students tutored within a classroom**.

That way you know what treatment actually is. A single student being treated is ill-defined, because
the treatment may reach other people. The share of a classroom being treated really only affects
students in that classroom.

**Where it breaks.** This is a simplified rendering — a more technical treatment is worth reading. And
SUTVA is, in Nick's phrase, **the terror of many a causal inference researcher.**

### Model uncertainty

**Plain English.** You made a lot of judgement calls. You could have made them differently.

**Precisely.** The whole book has said: know your model, write it down, use it to build your research
design and your estimate. And that's right — you're theorising, thinking carefully about where your
data came from.

But once you actually do it, you find **a lot of decisions you can't be 100% certain about**. So you
shouldn't be perfectly certain that the model you ended up with is the correct one.

**And this is not sampling variation.** It's that there were many different models you could have run.
Maybe one is right — you just don't know which. Some decisions have to be made on imperfect
information. You had to go with something.

**What you can do — when it's about controls.** The simplest form of model uncertainty is not being
sure which **control variables** to include, and that's easy to address: run all the combinations. With
this control, without it. With that one, without that one. Ten control variables gives you 2¹⁰ models —
prune it a bit if you like, but you can run a few hundred and average things together.

That gives you a **specification diagram** showing the **distribution of your estimated effect across
all those control choices** — a much better sense of what your estimate might have looked like had you
known which set was right.

**Where it breaks.** Other forms of model uncertainty aren't so tractable. **Is this variable a collider
or not?** **Am I leaving something out entirely** — is there a control that ought to be in here that I
never thought of and that isn't even in my data? You can't simulate your way out of those.

**We are never going to be completely certain about our model. And that is going to plague our dreams.**

### Nonexistent moments and power laws

**Plain English.** Some variables don't have a mean. You can still compute one — it just isn't measuring
anything.

**Precisely.** Almost every method in this book estimates **means** — or more generally **moments**, even
when it doesn't look like it. A **moment** is a statistic based on the distribution of your variable,
involving a central tendency with deviations raised to some power. The mean is a moment. So are the
variance and the skewness.

**The problem: some distributions literally do not have those moments.** It is possible to have a
variable with **no expectation**.

You can always calculate a mean from a sample — nothing stops you. **But that mean isn't estimating any
real part of the distribution.** And when a distribution has no first moment, the sample mean **jumps
around sharply from sample to sample**. Normally you'd expect the mean to be normally distributed across
samples and well-behaved, landing close to the truth, a bit above or below. Here, any given sample tells
you very little, and you can't infer much from it.

**How it arises: fat tails.** A **fat-tailed** distribution has a lot of data concentrated in one area
plus **a great many very large outliers**.

**The distinction from a normal distribution.** A normal distribution technically has infinite range —
centred at zero with a standard deviation of one, a value of a billion is theoretically possible, just
vanishingly rare. With fat tails, those extreme values aren't merely possible: **they happen a lot.**
Regularly.

**Worked example — income.** Many people cluster around $40,000–$50,000. But there are plenty of
millionaires and billionaires making millions every year — nowhere near as rare as a normal distribution
would suggest.

**Worked example — an even fatter tail: music streaming.** Spotify follower counts when Nick checked:

| Rank | Artist | Followers |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Ed Sheeran | **78.7 million** |
| 2 | Ariana Grande | **61 million** |
| 20 | Alan Walker | **27.9 million** |

First place is **29% bigger than second** — a big drop between #1 and #2. And first place is **281% as
large as twentieth**: you lose **more than half** your followers going from 1st to 20th.

That's a bigger gap than in income or wealth. **The wealthiest person in the world is not three times as
wealthy as the second wealthiest.** So music popularity is a genuinely big fat tail.

**The contrast — a distribution without a fat tail: running 1500 metres.**

| | Time |
|---|---|
| Fastest ever recorded | **3:26.00** |
| Second place | **3:26.34** |

A difference of **0.16%** — against the 29% drop from first to second on Spotify.

**And in perspective.** Time Nick running a mile. He can run a mile, but he is not a particularly fast
man — say **6 minutes**. That's **not even twice as long** as the first-place runner. So going from first
place to Nick, who'd be "in like billionth place at best," is only about a **100% difference**.

Compare that to going from **Ed Sheeran to Alan Walker** — merely 1st to 20th — which is a **bigger
percentage difference than that.**

Or: if Nick started a band and got **a thousand followers** on Spotify, which would be pretty darn
successful, he'd be **billions of times** less successful than Ed Sheeran. Whereas running his mile, he's
only **half as good** as the fastest human in history.

**Where it breaks.** With fat tails, all your methods work badly. **If you're lucky**, taking the
**logarithm** of the skewed variable fixes it and you proceed as normal — which is what most people try.
But some variables are **too skewed even for that**: financial returns over long periods may have tails
so fat that even a logarithm isn't enough to tame them.

### The treatment mystery

**Plain English.** If your two groups really are so alike, why did one of them get treated?

**Precisely.** A great many causal inference methods are about **finding comparable groups** — comparing
people as similar as possible except that one got treated. You do it explicitly in **matching**. You do
it inherently in **regression** when you add controls. You do it in **difference-in-differences**,
comparing a treated group to a similar untreated control group.

**But then the question arrives: if they're so similar, why was one treated and the other not?** That's
a mystery.

**Sometimes it's obvious.** When you genuinely understand why people got treated, you can answer it.
**Regression discontinuity**, for instance: you know exactly why one of two very similar people got
treated — one was just barely high enough on the running variable and the other just barely too low.
Mystery solved.

**But** if you're using control variables, or controlling away unobserved variation with fixed effects or
difference-in-differences, you have to ask yourself: **if I really believe these two groups are that
similar, why did one get treated?**

**Worked example — twin studies.** A common back door across social science is **genetics**: your genes
influence a lot of things we'd call treatments and a lot of things we'd call outcomes. Studying the
effect of **education on earnings**, your genetics might make you do better in school **and** make you
earn more later in life.

The standard fix: use **twins**. Find a pair of twins where one got more education than the other. Their
genetics are identical, so whatever difference you see is probably the education.

**Then the mystery.** If you think they're so similar and comparable — **why did one of them get more
education than the other?**

Maybe they aren't so comparable. Maybe they have the same genetics, but **something really different
happened to one of them as a kid** — and *that* is what's driving their income, not the education.

**The lesson.** If you don't really understand where your treatment assignment comes from, and you're
just using data to ferret out a good comparison, you always have to wonder: **if I think they're so
comparable, why aren't they the same?**

That's the treatment mystery. And that's the end of the series — "hopefully I have scared you enough to
put a nice little bow on all these videos. I am done. I'm going to sleep. Good night."

## TRAPS

- Randomizing the treatment guarantees the untreated group is untreated → **Actually,** spillovers mean the control group can be partly treated, which is what SUTVA rules out.
- Spillovers are unfixable → **Actually,** you can often redefine the treatment broadly enough to contain them — share of a classroom rather than an individual student.
- Model uncertainty is another word for sampling variation → **Actually,** it's about which of many possible models is correct, not about which sample you drew.
- Running every combination of controls solves model uncertainty → **Actually,** it addresses only the control-selection version. Colliders and unthought-of omitted variables remain.
- Every variable has a mean you can estimate → **Actually,** you can always compute one, but for some fat-tailed distributions it isn't estimating any real feature of the distribution.
- Fat tails just mean big outliers → **Actually,** a normal distribution permits huge outliers too. With fat tails, they happen **regularly**.
- Taking logs handles any skew → **Actually,** some variables — long-run financial returns, for example — have tails too fat even for that.
- Similar treated and control groups is the goal, so achieving it means you're done → **Actually,** the more similar they are, the more urgent the question of why one was treated.

## RECALL

Q: What does SUTVA assume, informally?
A: That there are no spillover effects — the treatment you're estimating is the treatment on the
individual you're estimating it on.

Q: Give the tutoring spillover example.
A: A tutored child passes tips to the friends they study with, so the untreated group is partly treated
and the comparison mixes the two.

Q: How can you get around a spillover problem?
A: Redefine treatment broadly enough to contain the spillover — study the share of students tutored
within a classroom rather than whether an individual student was tutored.

Q: Why does that redefinition help?
A: Because a single student's treatment is ill-defined when it reaches others, while a classroom-level
share only affects students in that classroom.

Q: What is model uncertainty, and what is it *not*?
A: Uncertainty about whether the model you chose is the correct one, given the many decisions made on
imperfect information. It is not sampling variation.

Q: How do you address the control-selection version?
A: Run every combination of control variables — 2¹⁰ models for ten controls — and average or examine the
results.

Q: What's a specification diagram?
A: A display of the distribution of your estimated effect across all those different control variable
choices.

Q: Which forms of model uncertainty can't be handled that way?
A: Whether a variable is a collider, and whether you've omitted something entirely that isn't even in
your data.

Q: What is a moment, and why does it matter here?
A: A statistic based on the distribution of a variable involving a central tendency with deviations to
some power — the mean, variance, and skewness are moments, and nearly all the book's methods estimate
them.

Q: What does it mean for a distribution to lack a first moment?
A: It has no expectation to estimate. You can compute a sample mean, but it isn't measuring any real
feature of the distribution, and it jumps sharply from sample to sample.

Q: What is a fat-tailed distribution, and how does it differ from a normal one?
A: One with data concentrated in one area plus many very large outliers. A normal distribution permits
huge values but they're vanishingly rare; with fat tails they occur regularly.

Q: Give the Spotify figures.
A: Ed Sheeran 78.7 million followers, Ariana Grande 61 million, Alan Walker 27.9 million at twentieth —
so first is 29% above second and 281% of twentieth.

Q: Give the 1500m comparison.
A: The fastest time ever is 3:26.00 and second place is 3:26.34 — a 0.16% difference, against a 29% drop
between first and second on Spotify.

Q: What's the mile-running perspective?
A: Nick running a six-minute mile would be roughly billionth place and only about twice as slow as the
record — whereas a band with a thousand followers would be billions of times smaller than Ed Sheeran.

Q: What's the usual fix for fat tails, and when does it fail?
A: Taking the logarithm. It fails for variables too skewed even for that, such as financial returns over
long periods.

Q: State the treatment mystery.
A: If your treated and control groups really are so comparable, why was one treated and the other not?

Q: Which design answers it cleanly, and how?
A: Regression discontinuity — one unit was just barely above the running variable cutoff and the other
just barely below.

Q: Which designs leave it unanswered?
A: Ones using control variables, or controlling away unobserved variation via fixed effects or
difference-in-differences.

Q: Explain the twin study version.
A: Twins share genetics, so a difference in earnings between a more- and less-educated twin looks like
the effect of education — but you must ask why one twin got more education, since something different
happened to one of them, and that may be what's driving earnings.

## GLOSSARY

- **SUTVA (stable unit treatment value assumption)** — the assumption that treatment has no spillover effects onto other units.
- **Spillover** — treatment applied to one unit affecting the outcomes of untreated units.
- **Model uncertainty** — not knowing which of many defensible model specifications is correct.
- **Specification diagram** — a display of estimated effects across many combinations of control variables.
- **Moment** — a distributional statistic such as the mean, variance, or skewness.
- **Fat-tailed distribution** — one in which very large outliers occur regularly rather than rarely.
- **The treatment mystery** — the question of why one of two supposedly comparable units was treated and the other wasn't.
