## ONE SENTENCE

Every cell in a regression table is the same three things — **estimate**, **standard error in
parentheses**, **stars for the p-value** — and the fit statistics underneath (R², adjusted R²,
F) describe the model as a whole, which for causal work you should mostly ignore.

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

- A regression table is compact and lets you compare models side by side. **Each column is one regression.**
- Rows are the predictors, plus an **intercept** (a.k.a. constant, β₀, α).
- Each cell: **estimate**, then in parentheses a **standard error** — or sometimes a **t statistic** — then **significance stars**.
- Stars encode the p-value against a default null of **coefficient = 0**, and they **stack**.
- What the stars mean varies by field. **Look for the table note.**
- Below: **number of observations**, then model fit statistics — **R²**, **adjusted R²**, **F statistic**.
- For causal inference, R² is mostly irrelevant. A clean randomized experiment often has a **small** R² and a very good model.

## WHY THIS EXISTS

After that rant, something straightforward. A regression table is just a table with regression
results on it — a very common presentation because it's compact, easy to read, and lets you show
several models at once, all read the same way.

## IDEAS

### The layout

**Plain English.** Columns are models. Rows are variables. The stuff at the bottom is about the
model, not any one variable.

**Worked example.** Two regressions predicting restaurant health **inspection scores**:

| | (1) | (2) |
|---|---|---|
| Intercept | … | … |
| Number of locations | **−0.019**<br>(0.000)*** | … |
| Year of inspection | | **−0.065**<br>(0.006)*** |
| | | |
| Observations | 27,178 | … |
| R² | 0.065 | … |

The top names the **dependent variable** — what you're predicting, your y. Here it's inspection
scores in both columns.

The first row is the **intercept**, which some tables call the *constant*; in an equation it's β₀
or α.

Then the predictors — the independent variables, your x and your controls. Here: number of
locations (for chain restaurants) and year of inspection. Column 1 uses only number of locations;
column 2 includes both.

### Reading a single cell

**Plain English.** Three numbers stacked: what you estimated, how precise it is, how surprising it
is under a null of zero.

**Worked example.** Take **−0.019** in column 1.

**1 · The estimate.** The first number is the estimated coefficient. If you wrote out
`y = β₀ + β₁ × number of locations`, this is your **β₁-hat** — the estimated slope on number of
locations is −0.019.

**2 · The parentheses.** Below sits **0.000**, a **standard error**. Some tables instead show a
**t statistic** — the coefficient divided by the standard error. A table note usually says which;
if not, here's the heuristic:

- Significant coefficients with **small** values in parentheses → **standard errors**
- Significant coefficients with **large** values in parentheses → **t statistics**

And 0.000 doesn't mean the standard error is actually zero — it can't be. It means it's smaller
than the smallest value the table is willing to display.

**3 · The stars.** Those asterisks are **significance stars**, representing the p-value. By default
in essentially all regression tables, the null being tested is that **the coefficient is zero**.

So: assume the slope on number of locations is zero, build a sampling distribution centred on zero
with a standard error of (here) roughly zero, and ask how unlikely it would be to see an estimate
of −0.019 or further away in either direction. That probability is the p-value, and the stars
encode it.

In this table — the standard convention in economics:

| Stars | Meaning |
|---|---|
| `*` | p < 0.1 |
| `**` | p < 0.05 |
| `***` | p < 0.01 |

Other fields commonly use `*` for 0.05, `**` for 0.01, `***` for 0.001. **Look for the table note.**

Three stars therefore means p < 0.01 — significant if you'd chosen a 0.01 cutoff. And you can read
it in reverse: had you picked 0.01 as your threshold, this coefficient clears it. Stars **stack**,
because if p < 0.01 then it's also below 0.05 and 0.1.

Column 2's year-of-inspection cell reads the same way: estimate **−0.065**, standard error
**0.006**, p < 0.01.

**Where it breaks.** Depending on how the table is built, standard errors sometimes sit **beside**
the coefficient rather than below. That's fine — just a formatting choice.

### Model fit statistics

**Plain English.** Numbers at the bottom describing the whole model rather than any coefficient.

**Number of observations.** Here, 27,178.

**R².** The correlation between your model's predicted values and the actual outcome, squared. It
represents the **proportion of the outcome's variance explained by the model.**

With R² = 0.065: doing 1 − 0.065 = 0.935 says that if you split the outcome into the part your
model predicted and the part left in the residual, **6.5% of the variation is explained** and
**93.5% is not**. It partitions the outcome into in-the-model and out-of-the-model.

**Where R² breaks — and it gets misused a lot.**

- R² measures **only** how much variance you explained. It does **not** tell you how good the model is.
- For causal work you want to estimate **one parameter** very well. The concern is whether −0.019 reflects the causal effect of number of locations on inspection score. That plenty of other things drive inspection scores simply doesn't matter to you.
- **Never change your model to raise R².**
- The clinching example: a **randomized controlled experiment** has very clean, very easy causal identification, and often a **small** R², because a lot is going on in the outcome that the randomly assigned treatment doesn't explain. R² looks bad; the model is excellent.
- If your only goal is **prediction**, R² makes more sense — that's what it's about. Even then, there are probably better measures.

Overall, in this book, you can more or less ignore R². It's a nice quick diagnostic with a sharp
interpretation — the share of outcome variation inside versus outside your model — but that's all
it does, and it isn't answering a question you care about.

**Adjusted R².** R² will **always** go up as you add variables, because more predictors means more
flexibility to fit the data — and that's true even if the variables you add are complete nonsense.
Adjusted R² instead asks whether the variables you included explain **more than you'd expect from
adding random noise**.

Nick's view: he uses adjusted R² *less* than R², because R² is much easier to interpret as a share
while adjusted R² muddles that — and since he isn't especially concerned with R² anyway, and has
already done the work of removing nonsense predictors while constructing the model, he doesn't need
adjusted R² to do it for him. He flags this as a more contentious opinion.

**F statistic.** A form of hypothesis test. A model with no predictors at all would just predict
the mean inspection score for everybody. Adding predictors will always improve on that, because
you have more flexibility. The F statistic asks: **is the improvement more than you'd expect by
random chance**, given how many predictors you added?

The null is that **all your predictors have coefficients of zero**. The F statistic asks how
unlikely the coefficients you observed would be under that null — and if the probability is very
small, you reject that they're all zero.

## TRAPS

- The standard error is 0.000, so there's no sampling variation → **Actually,** it can't be zero. It's below the smallest value the table displays.
- The number in parentheses is always a standard error → **Actually,** it may be a t statistic. Check the table note; failing that, small values on significant coefficients suggest standard errors, large values suggest t statistics.
- Three stars means the same thing in every paper → **Actually,** conventions differ by field. In economics `***` is p < 0.01; elsewhere it can be p < 0.001.
- A low R² means a bad model → **Actually,** a randomized controlled experiment — the cleanest identification there is — often has a small R².
- I should add controls until R² looks respectable → **Actually,** never modify a model to raise R². For causal work it isn't the question you're asking.
- Adjusted R² is strictly better than R² → **Actually,** Nick uses it less: R² is easier to read as a share, and nonsense predictors should have been dealt with when building the model.
- The F statistic tests whether the model is good → **Actually,** it tests the null that all coefficients are zero — whether the improvement over predicting the mean exceeds what you'd expect by chance.

## RECALL

Q: What does each column in a regression table represent?
A: A separate regression model.

Q: What three things appear in a typical coefficient cell?
A: The estimated coefficient, a standard error (or sometimes a t statistic) in parentheses, and
significance stars.

Q: The parentheses show 0.000. What does that mean?
A: The standard error is smaller than the smallest value the table displays — it isn't literally
zero.

Q: How do you tell a standard error from a t statistic when there's no table note?
A: On significant coefficients, small values in parentheses suggest standard errors and large
values suggest t statistics.

Q: What is a t statistic?
A: The coefficient divided by its standard error.

Q: What null hypothesis do significance stars test by default?
A: That the coefficient is zero.

Q: In the economics convention used here, what do one, two, and three stars mean?
A: p < 0.1, p < 0.05, and p < 0.01 respectively — though conventions differ by field, so check the
table note.

Q: Why do stars stack?
A: Because if p < 0.01 it is necessarily also below 0.05 and 0.1, so significance at a tight
threshold implies significance at looser ones.

Q: Define R².
A: The squared correlation between the model's predicted values and the actual outcome —
equivalently, the proportion of the outcome's variance explained by the model.

Q: R² is 0.065. State what that partitions.
A: 6.5% of the variation in the outcome is explained by the model; 93.5% remains in the residual.

Q: Why is R² largely irrelevant for causal inference?
A: Because you want to estimate one parameter well. Whether other things also drive the outcome
doesn't bear on whether your coefficient is the causal effect.

Q: What's the example showing a low R² can accompany an excellent model?
A: A randomized controlled experiment — identification is very clean, but the randomly assigned
treatment often explains little of the outcome's variation.

Q: What does adjusted R² fix, and why?
A: R² always rises when you add predictors, even nonsense ones, because more flexibility fits the
data better. Adjusted R² asks whether the added variables explain more than you'd expect from
random noise.

Q: What is Nick's view on adjusted R²?
A: He uses it less than R², because R² is easier to interpret as a share, he isn't concerned with
R² anyway, and he removes nonsense predictors while building the model rather than relying on a
statistic to do it. He flags this as contentious.

Q: What does the F statistic test?
A: The null that all your predictors have coefficients of zero — asking whether your model's
improvement over simply predicting the mean is more than you'd expect by random chance given how
many predictors you added.

## GLOSSARY

- **Regression table** — a compact presentation of regression results, one column per model.
- **Intercept / constant** — the β₀ term; the fitted outcome when all predictors are zero.
- **Dependent variable** — the outcome being predicted, named at the top of the table.
- **t statistic** — a coefficient divided by its standard error.
- **Significance stars** — asterisks encoding the p-value against a null of zero.
- **R²** — the share of the outcome's variance explained by the model.
- **Adjusted R²** — R² adjusted to ask whether added predictors explain more than random noise would.
- **F statistic** — a test of the null that all predictors' coefficients are zero.
