## ONE SENTENCE

Matching rests on three things — the **conditional independence assumption** (you matched on
enough to close every back door), **common support** (there is actually somebody on the other
side to match to), and **balance** (the matching did what you asked) — and the last two you can
and must check.

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

- **Conditional independence assumption:** treatment is essentially random once you condition on your matching variables. This is "close all the back doors" wearing a matching-shaped hat.
- **Common support:** you need **substantial overlap** in the matching variables' distributions between groups. If nobody untreated looks like the treated, there's nothing to match to.
- The usual fix — **dropping areas of non-common support** — works, but can delete the most typical members of your treated group and leave an unrepresentative estimate.
- **Balance:** after matching, did the groups actually end up similar? Check with a **balance table** of means.
- **Means aren't enough.** Two distributions can share a mean and look nothing alike. Check distributions too.
- If balance is bad, go back and redo the matching. That's legitimate.

## WHY THIS EXISTS

Several episodes of *how* to match, and almost nothing on the assumptions that make matching
appropriate — or what you can check to see whether things went well. Three things: one assumption
you have to make, and two you can actually inspect.

## IDEAS

### 1 · The conditional independence assumption

**Plain English.** You matched on enough variables to close every back door.

**Precisely.** You pick matching variables and match the treated and control groups on them,
because you want the differences between those groups to be driven by the effect of treatment
rather than by other variables. The **conditional independence assumption** says you've achieved
that: you matched on all the variables needed to close all the back doors, so **treatment is
basically random conditional on the matching variables you selected**.

**Where it breaks.** It's another name for something you already know. Identify an effect →
close all the back doors. In the matching context that's the conditional independence assumption;
in the regression context it's getting rid of omitted variable bias. Lots of different words for
the same idea.

### 2 · Common support

**Plain English.** There has to be somebody on the other side who looks like you.

**Precisely.** For matching to work you need **substantial overlap** in the distributions of the
variables you're matching on. Look at the distribution of a matching variable in the treated group
and in the control group: there must be at least some ranges where you see plenty of people in
**both**, or you can't match on that variable.

**Anchor image.** A dating app where everyone in your city is already in a relationship. The app
works fine. There's just nobody to match you with.

**Worked example — the job training program.** Suppose the program deliberately targets one
neighbourhood — Capitol Hill in Seattle, where Nick's college is — and recruits everyone there.
Neighbourhood is on a back door, so you'd like to match on it. But if **everybody in Capitol Hill
got recruited**, there's nobody in Capitol Hill who wasn't treated to match to. And symmetrically
there's nobody treated outside Capitol Hill to match to the untreated group. You can't match on
neighbourhood at all.

**Worked example — the Broockman study.** Broockman sent fictionalised requests for help to
legislators, using an email name that sounded like it belonged to a Black person — a fictional
*Tyrone Washington*. The question: if you're a legislator and the email comes from **outside your
district**, do you respond? There's no inherent benefit — they can't vote for you. And does the
response rate differ between Black and non-Black legislators?

- **Treatment:** whether the legislator is Black.
- **The back door:** Black legislators are more likely to be elected in areas with many Black residents, so the **percentage of Black people in the district** sits on a back door between the legislator's race and, for other reasons, whether they respond.

Now look at the distributions of percent-Black-in-district:

| Group | Where they're concentrated |
|---|---|
| Non-Black legislators | The vast majority in districts **without many** Black residents |
| Black legislators | Very likely in districts **50–75% Black** |

Very different distributions. There may be common support at relatively low percentages — but in
the 50–75% range, where **most Black legislators are**, there simply aren't enough non-Black
legislators to compare against. That's a **failure of common support** exactly where it hurts.

**Common support for the propensity score.** The same requirement applies to propensity scores. To
compare treated to untreated while adjusting for the propensity of being treated, you need some
treated people who had a decent probability of *not* being treated, and some untreated people who
had a decent probability of being treated. In Broockman's data the propensity score distributions
for Black and non-Black legislators are **very disjoint** — there isn't much overlap where you
could say "here's a Black legislator, and here's a non-Black legislator with a similar probability."

**The fix, and its cost.** Drop the observations for which there's no common support — **dropping
areas of non-common support**. You might drop all the Black legislators in 50–75% Black districts,
because there's no good match for them; or drop everybody above a certain propensity score, because
only the low range is comparable.

This fixes the statistical problem: better matches for whoever remains. But you're **dropping a lot
of observations** — and here, specifically, **the most common kind of Black legislator**. So you've
solved one problem and introduced another: how representative is your new estimate of the actual
distribution?

**Propensity score trimming** is the same logic. Drop very low and very high scores, because
you're unlikely to find matches: someone with a 90% chance of being treated probably was treated,
so there won't be many controls up there; someone with a 0.05% chance won't have many treated
observations to compare to.

### 3 · Balance

**Plain English.** Did the matching actually make the groups look alike? Go and look.

**Precisely.** You did your matching and got matched or weighted samples. The goal was to make the
matching variables — or the propensity score — similar between the treated and control groups. So
check whether that happened.

**The balance table.** Compare the average of each matching variable, or the propensity score, in
both groups and hope they're close.

**Worked example — Broockman's balance table**, before and after matching:

| Variable | Before matching | After matching |
|---|---|---|
| Median household income | 3.33 vs **4.435** | 3.33 vs **3.316** |
| Democrat | 97.8% vs **50.1%** | 97.8% vs **97.8%** |

Before matching, household income was much higher in areas with white legislators than in areas
with Black legislators. After matching it found areas with white legislators that had similar
household incomes — 3.33 against 3.316, a far closer comparison.

The Democrat variable is even starker: 97.8% of Black legislators were Democrats against only 50.1%
of non-Black legislators. After matching it's **exactly 97.8% for both** — it ended up selecting
almost entirely Democratic non-Black legislators as comparisons.

**Where it breaks — means aren't enough.** A balance table compares **means**, and that's not the
only thing you might care about. It's entirely possible for the treated and control means to be
identical or very close while the **distributions still don't look the same**. Two distributions
could both have a mean of zero and look nothing alike — perfect on a balance table, and you would
not say that matching worked well.

So after matching, also look at the **distribution** of each matching variable, or of the propensity
score.

**Worked example — Broockman's distributions after matching.**

- **Percentage Black in the area:** looks pretty decent. It doesn't line up exactly, but there's quite a lot of similarity between Black and non-Black legislators after matching and weighting.
- **Propensity score:** you'd really want this one close, since you are **literally weighting on the propensity score** to make the two the same. It isn't — which is concerning. There are a lot more non-Black legislators with very low propensity scores, and also some with very high ones. Even though the average is the same, that distribution would give you pause, and you might go back and redo the matching.

**If balance is bad**, go back and redo some of your matching process to try for a better match.
That's entirely legitimate — you're just making your match work better. Add polynomials to your
propensity score model, change the matching variables, and so on.

## TRAPS

- The conditional independence assumption is a new requirement specific to matching → **Actually,** it's "close all the back doors" in matching vocabulary — the same idea as no omitted variable bias in regression.
- If my matching procedure ran successfully, I have common support → **Actually,** it can run and still be matching people to nobody comparable. You have to inspect the overlap.
- Dropping observations without common support solves the problem → **Actually,** it fixes the statistical problem and creates a representativeness problem, especially when it deletes the most typical treated observations.
- A clean balance table means matching worked → **Actually,** it compares means. Two distributions can share a mean and be completely different shapes.
- Good balance on the matching variables means good balance on the propensity score → **Actually,** in Broockman's study the percent-Black distributions look decent while the propensity score distributions don't.
- Going back and redoing your matching is p-hacking → **Actually,** re-doing the match to achieve better balance is legitimate — you're improving the match, not selecting on the result.
- Trimming extreme propensity scores is arbitrary → **Actually,** it reflects a real fact: someone with a 90% chance of treatment probably was treated, so there are few controls to compare against.

## RECALL

Q: Name the three things matching depends on.
A: The conditional independence assumption, common support, and balance.

Q: State the conditional independence assumption.
A: That you matched on all the variables needed to close all the back doors, so treatment is
essentially random conditional on your matching variables.

Q: What are the equivalent phrasings of that assumption?
A: "Close all the back doors" in the causal diagram context, and "no omitted variable bias" in the
regression context.

Q: Define common support.
A: Substantial overlap in the distributions of the matching variables between the treated and control
groups — ranges where there are plenty of people on both sides.

Q: Give the job training illustration of failed common support.
A: If a program recruits everyone in one neighbourhood, there's nobody in that neighbourhood who wasn't
treated, and nobody treated outside it — so you can't match on neighbourhood.

Q: What was Broockman studying, and what was the treatment?
A: Whether legislators respond to help requests from an apparently Black constituent outside their
district — a fictional Tyrone Washington. The treatment is whether the legislator is Black.

Q: What's the back door in that study?
A: The percentage of Black residents in the district — Black legislators are more likely to be elected
there, and district composition may also affect responding for other reasons.

Q: Describe the common support failure in Broockman's data.
A: Non-Black legislators are overwhelmingly in districts with few Black residents, while Black
legislators are mostly in districts 50–75% Black — so there aren't enough non-Black legislators to
compare against exactly where most Black legislators are.

Q: What does common support mean for a propensity score specifically?
A: You need some treated people with a decent probability of *not* being treated, and some untreated
people with a decent probability of being treated.

Q: What is "dropping areas of non-common support", and what does it cost?
A: Removing observations in ranges where there's nobody comparable on the other side. It improves the
remaining matches but can delete the most typical treated observations, leaving an unrepresentative
estimate.

Q: Why trim very high and very low propensity scores?
A: Someone with a 90% chance of treatment probably was treated, so there are few controls up there;
someone with a 0.05% chance has few treated observations to compare to.

Q: What is a balance table?
A: A comparison of the average of each matching variable, or the propensity score, between the treated
and control groups — ideally before and after matching.

Q: In Broockman's table, what happened to the Democrat variable?
A: Before matching, 97.8% of Black legislators were Democrats versus 50.1% of non-Black legislators.
After matching it was exactly 97.8% for both — almost entirely Democratic non-Black legislators were
selected.

Q: Why isn't a good balance table sufficient?
A: It compares means, and two distributions can have identical means while looking completely
different in shape.

Q: What did Broockman's post-matching distributions show?
A: Percent Black in the district lined up fairly well, but the propensity score distributions did not —
more non-Black legislators at very low and very high scores — which is concerning given the weighting
was done on the propensity score.

Q: What should you do if balance looks bad?
A: Go back and redo the matching — add polynomials to the propensity score model, change matching
variables — which is entirely legitimate.

## GLOSSARY

- **Conditional independence assumption** — that treatment is as good as random once you condition on the matching variables.
- **Common support** — substantial overlap between groups in the distribution of a matching variable or propensity score.
- **Dropping areas of non-common support** — removing observations in ranges with no comparable observations on the other side.
- **Balance** — whether the matching variables actually ended up similar between treated and control groups.
- **Balance table** — a comparison of matching variable means across groups, typically before and after matching.
