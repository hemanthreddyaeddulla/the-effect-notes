## ONE SENTENCE

Every variable has a **distribution** — how often each value occurs — and summarizing a
variable means compressing that distribution down to a central tendency and a spread,
choosing either the mean family or the percentile family depending on how much you
want outliers to count.

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

- A **distribution** tells you how often each possible value occurs. A fair coin: 50% heads, 50% tails. That's the whole distribution.
- For a **categorical** variable, listing the proportion in each category *is* the complete distribution. You're done.
- For a **continuous** variable you can't list values, so you bin them into a **histogram**, then shrink the bins to nothing and get a **density**.
- Even a density is a lot of information, so you compress further: a **central tendency** plus a **spread**.
- Two families. **Mean-based**: mean + standard deviation. **Percentile-based**: median + interquartile range. Percentiles resist outliers; means are easier to work with mathematically.
- **Skew** is spread that's lopsided. Taking a **logarithm** pulls the long tail in.

## WHY THIS EXISTS

You have thousands of observations and you want to tell someone what's in them. You
can't hand them the dataset — nobody can hold thousands of numbers in their head. So
you compress, and the whole question is how to compress without throwing away the part
that mattered.

Each step down this ladder loses information on purpose: full distribution → density →
central tendency + spread. Knowing what you dropped at each rung is what stops the
summary from lying.

## IDEAS

### Distribution

**Plain English.** How often each value comes up.

**Precisely.** A distribution maps every possible outcome to how frequently it occurs.
For a fair coin that's heads 50%, tails 50% — and that is the complete description of
the random variable. Nothing is left over.

**Anchor image.** Not the list of every coin flip you made, but the *rule* the flips
obey.

**Worked example.** Rather than "I flipped a thousand coins; the first was heads, the
second tails, the third tails…", you say: I flipped a thousand coins and 502 came up
heads. One number replaces a thousand and loses nothing you cared about.

### Categorical variables are easy — just list the proportions

**Plain English.** Say how often each category occurred. That's it, you've fully
described it.

**Precisely.** For a categorical variable the sample distribution is exactly the set of
category proportions. There's no further compression to do because there was no detail
lost.

**Worked example.** A dataset of colleges, by the kind of degree they award:

| Degree awarded | Share of sample |
|---|---|
| Less than two-year | 47.1% |
| Two-year | 22.2% |
| Four-year or more | 30.7% |

Three numbers, and you now know everything about that variable in that sample.

### Continuous variables: histogram, then density

**Plain English.** You can't list every value, so group them into bins and count. Then
imagine the bins getting infinitely narrow.

**Precisely.** A **histogram** turns a continuous variable into an ordered categorical
one by binning — incomes 0–5,000, 5,000–10,000, and so on — and counts observations per
bin. Shrink the bins toward zero width and you get a **density**, which describes the
probability of landing *near* a point rather than the count inside a bin.

**Anchor image.** Sorting coins into jars by size. Wide jars, coarse picture. Narrow
jars, finer picture. Infinitely many infinitely narrow jars, and the tops of the jars
trace a smooth curve.

**Worked example.** Earnings of students after graduating from those colleges. The
histogram shows most students attended a college where typical post-graduation earnings
land between $20,000 and $40,000. The density sharpens it: the peak sits around
$30,000–$35,000. A few colleges have cohorts averaging over $100,000 — visible, but the
curve is low there, so there's little area beneath it. Same at the bottom around
$12,000: it happens, rarely.

**Where it breaks.** Why the value can't be read straight off a density: what you're
reading is a density, not a probability. The numbers differ from probabilities even
though the idea — "how likely is roughly this?" — is the same.

### Central tendency: mean vs median

**Plain English.** One number standing in for the middle of the distribution. Two ways
to pick it, and they answer different questions.

**Precisely.** The **mean** — add all values, divide by how many — is the value you'd
break even betting on. A **percentile** is the value with that share of the sample below
it: line 100 people up shortest to tallest, and if the fifth is 4'10", then 4'10" is the
5th percentile. The **median** is the 50th percentile — whoever stands in the middle.

**Anchor image.** The mean is where the seesaw balances. The median is the person
standing in the middle of the queue. Move one person to the far end of the field and
the seesaw tips hard; the queue barely notices.

**Worked example.** A hundred people lined up by income, the middle one earning $30,000.
Jeff Bezos walks in. The **median** shifts from that person to whoever was standing next
to them — maybe $31,000. The **mean** jumps to something absurd once his income is
averaged in.

**Where it breaks.** Neither is more correct — they serve different purposes. The mean
gives you the typical *value* you'd get back from the distribution. The median gives you
the typical *observation* you'd run into. Percentiles resist outliers but are harder to
work with mathematically, which is why means dominate in practice.

### Spread: standard deviation vs interquartile range

**Plain English.** How much things scatter around the centre. Two distributions can
share a centre and be nothing alike.

**Precisely.** The mean-family measure is the **standard deviation**: subtract the mean
from each value, square the differences, average the squares (that's the **variance**),
then take the square root to get back to the original units. The percentile-family
measure is the **interquartile range**: the distance from the 25th to the 75th
percentile.

**Anchor image.** Two archers both average dead centre. One's arrows are in a fist-sized
cluster; the other's are scattered across the whole target. The average says they're
identical. The spread is the entire difference between them.

**Worked example.** Both of these have a central tendency of about two:

| Variable | Central tendency | Spread |
|---|---|---|
| Number of children an adult has | ~2 | Large — plenty of people at 0, 1, 10 |
| Number of eyes a person has | 2 | Tiny — almost everyone is exactly 2 |

By percentiles: for eyes, the 10th percentile is 2 and the 50th is 2 — no spread. For
children, the 10th is 0 and the 50th is 2 — real spread.

**Where it breaks.** The IQR is the more flexible of the two, because the percentiles
don't have to be 25 and 75. Use 10 and 90, or any pair that suits the question.

### Skew, and the log fix

**Plain English.** Sometimes the spread is all on one side. Then "centre plus spread"
quietly misleads, because it implies symmetry.

**Precisely.** **Skew** is spread concentrated in one direction. Reporting only a
central tendency and a spread hides the fact that one tail is long and the other isn't.
Taking a **logarithm** compresses the very large values inward, pulling a skewed
distribution toward symmetry.

**Anchor image.** A city where nearly everyone lives in low-rise housing and three
towers spike out of the skyline. "Average building height plus spread" describes a city
that doesn't exist.

**Worked example.** Income. Most people clustered near the bottom, and a handful way out
to the right stretching the distribution far past everyone else. Take the logarithm and
it comes back to something much closer to symmetric — not perfect, but workable.

**Where it breaks.** You don't *need* symmetry to describe a variable properly. But
mean-based calculations describe data more accurately when it's roughly symmetric, which
is why the log trick shows up so often — real-world data is skewed constantly. The
transform also rests on assumptions, covered later.

## TRAPS

- Two variables with the same average are broadly similar → **Actually,** children and eyes both centre on 2 and are completely different variables. The spread is where the difference lives.
- The mean is the real centre and the median is an approximation → **Actually,** they answer different questions. Mean = the typical value; median = the typical observation.
- Median is always the safer choice because outliers can't move it → **Actually,** that robustness costs you: percentiles are harder to work with mathematically, which is why means dominate.
- A density curve tells me the probability of a given value → **Actually,** it's a density, not a probability. The idea is the same, the numbers are not.
- My data is skewed, so my summary is broken → **Actually,** skewed data can be described perfectly well. The log transform is there to help mean-based calculations behave, not to fix an error.

## SAY IT OUT LOUD

> Summarising a variable is a ladder, and each rung deliberately throws away
> information.
>
> Top rung: the full **distribution** — how often each value comes up. For a coin
> that's "50% heads, 50% tails" and you're done, nothing lost. For anything
> **categorical** it's just as easy: 47% of these colleges award less than two-year
> degrees, 22% two-year, 31% four-year. Three numbers, complete description.
>
> **Continuous** variables break that, because basically nobody shares an exact value —
> your income is $17,432.16 and your coworker's is $17,487.22. So you bin them into a
> **histogram**, and then shrink the bins until they're infinitely narrow and you get a
> smooth **density** curve.
>
> But a whole curve is still a lot. So you compress again, to two numbers: a **centre**
> and a **spread**. And there are two families. Mean and standard deviation, or median
> and interquartile range.
>
> The difference is outliers. Line a hundred people up by income, the middle one earns
> thirty thousand. Jeff Bezos walks in. The **median** moves to whoever was standing next
> to them — thirty-one thousand. The **mean** explodes. Neither is wrong; the mean tells
> you the typical *value*, the median tells you the typical *observation*.
>
> And you need the spread, not just the centre. Number of children someone has averages
> about two. Number of eyes someone has averages about two. Completely different
> variables — and only the spread tells you that.

## RECALL

Q: What is a distribution?
A: A description of how often each possible value occurs. For a fair coin: 50% heads,
50% tails — that's the complete distribution.

Q: Why is a categorical variable so easy to summarize?
A: Listing the proportion in each category *is* the full distribution. There's nothing
further to compress and nothing lost.

Q: Why can't you use the same approach for a continuous variable?
A: Almost no two observations share an exact value, so the table would have one row per
observation — you'd just be looking at the dataset again.

Q: What's the relationship between a histogram and a density?
A: A histogram bins a continuous variable and counts each bin. Shrink the bins toward
zero width and you get a density — the same idea, describing the likelihood of landing
near a point rather than a count within a bin.

Q: State the difference between what a mean tells you and what a median tells you.
A: The mean is representative of the typical *value* you'd get back from the
distribution. The median is representative of the typical *observation* you'd
encounter.

Q: A hundred people, median income $30,000. Jeff Bezos walks in. What happens to the median, and to the mean?
A: The median barely moves — to whoever was standing next to the middle person, maybe
$31,000. The mean rockets upward, because his income gets averaged in with everyone
else's.

Q: How is a standard deviation constructed?
A: Subtract the mean from each value, square the differences, take the mean of those
squares (the variance), then square-root back into the original units.

Q: Children per adult and eyes per person both centre on about 2. What distinguishes them, and how would percentiles show it?
A: The spread. For eyes the 10th and 50th percentiles are both 2 — no spread. For
children the 10th is 0 and the 50th is 2 — real spread.

Q: Why is the interquartile range described as more flexible than the standard deviation?
A: Because the percentiles don't have to be 25 and 75 — you can compare the 10th and
90th, or any pair suited to the question.

Q: What is skew, and why does centre-plus-spread handle it badly?
A: Skew is spread concentrated on one side. A centre and a spread implies symmetry, so
reporting only those hides the fact that one tail is long and the other isn't.

Q: What does taking a logarithm do to a skewed variable, and why bother?
A: It shrinks the very large values inward, making the distribution more symmetric — so
mean-based calculations describe the data more accurately instead of being dominated by
the extreme values.

## GLOSSARY

- **Distribution** — how often each possible value of a variable occurs.
- **Sample distribution** — the distribution actually observed in your data, as opposed to in theory.
- **Histogram** — a continuous variable cut into bins, with observations counted per bin.
- **Density** — the limit of a histogram as bin width goes to zero; describes likelihood near a point.
- **Central tendency** — a single value representing the centre of a distribution.
- **Mean** — the sum of values divided by the count; the mean-family central tendency.
- **Percentile** — the value below which a given share of the sample falls.
- **Median** — the 50th percentile; the middle observation.
- **Standard deviation** — the square root of the variance; the mean-family measure of spread.
- **Variance** — the mean of squared deviations from the mean.
- **Interquartile range** — the distance from the 25th to the 75th percentile; the percentile-family measure of spread.
- **Skew** — spread concentrated in one direction, producing one long tail.
