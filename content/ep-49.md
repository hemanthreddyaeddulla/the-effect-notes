## ONE SENTENCE

Fit a time trend before the event, fit another after, and look at where they meet — and if you
**centre time at the event date**, the whole thing becomes a regression with an interaction term
where the jump you care about is just **β₂**.

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

- Fit a trend in the pre-treatment period, fit a trend in the post-treatment period, see where they line up at the cutoff.
- Mathematically **quite similar to regression discontinuity**, a few chapters ahead.
- Implement it with a regression on **time**, an **after-treatment indicator**, and their **interaction**.
- **Centre time so it equals zero at the event.** Then the intercepts *are* the predictions at the cutoff, and the jump is **β₂**.
- Pre-treatment line: intercept **β₀**, slope **β₁**. Post-treatment: intercept **β₀ + β₂**, slope **β₁ + β₃**.
- You can add **polynomials** — just interact them with the after indicator too.
- Two serious cautions: **autocorrelation** makes you over-reject, and the result is **very sensitive to functional form**.

## WHY THIS EXISTS

Event studies compare what you saw to what you predicted you'd see. The big question is **how do you
build that prediction?**

Interrupted time series is probably the simplest way, and it's a good method when you think there's
likely to be a consistent trend over time, then another consistent trend afterwards, and you're
interested in the **difference between the two trends**.

## IDEAS

### The method

**Plain English.** Draw a line through the before, draw a line through the after, look at the gap
where they meet.

**Precisely.** First fit a time trend — perhaps by regression — in the **pre-treatment** period.
Then fit a time trend in the **post-treatment** period. Then look at where those two trends meet up
at the cutoff.

**Worked example of the shape.** Suppose you have a **declining** trend beforehand, heading downward.
Then you fit the post-treatment trend and it starts up higher. You predicted the trend would have
continued down to *here*, and instead you ended up at *there* — that's a **jump**.

That's the name: a time trend going along, then the event **interrupts** it, and it moves on to
something else.

**Anchor image.** A pencil line drawn along a ruler, the ruler nudged, and the line continuing from
a different place. The offset at the join is what you're measuring.

**Where it fits.** This is, at least mathematically, quite similar to **regression discontinuity**,
which arrives a few chapters later.

### Implementing it with a regression

**Plain English.** Outcome on time, an after-indicator, and their interaction — with one crucial
recentring.

**Precisely.** You could use advanced time series methodology to make a forecast, and honestly it's
probably better if you do. But a simpler route is linear regression with an interaction term.

Regress the outcome on:

- **time** — a continuous measure, maybe the week or year the observation was made
- **after** — a binary indicator for being in the post-treatment period
- **time × after** — the interaction

**The crucial adjustment:** take the time variable and **subtract the cutoff date from it**, so that
time equals **zero at the moment the event goes into effect**. Now the interpretation is very
straightforward:

| Period | `after` | Intercept | Slope |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Pre-treatment** | 0 | **β₀** | **β₁** |
| **Post-treatment** | 1 | **β₀ + β₂** | **β₁ + β₃** |

In the untreated period, `after` is zero so the β₂ and β₃ terms fall away, leaving β₀ + β₁·t.

**Why centring matters so much.** What you usually want is **how things jumped at the cutoff** — the
immediate change at the moment treatment went into effect. And the **intercept is the prediction at
time zero**. Since you just centred time so that zero *is* the event date, the prediction at the
event is simply the intercept.

So compare the intercept in the untreated period, **β₀**, against the intercept in the treated
period, **β₀ + β₂**. The difference — the jump at the intercept, which is the jump at the event — is
**β₂**.

**β₂ is your treatment effect.**

### Worked example: ambulance quality improvement

**Worked example.** A study of a quality improvement programme for **ambulances** — a way of making
ambulances coordinate information better. Does it improve their performance? The treatment went into
place at a particular time, and the question is what changed about the effectiveness rating.

Graphically, fit a straight line either side of the cutoff:

- **Left of the cutoff** (untreated): a straight line sloping **upwards slightly** — but only a little.
- **Right of the cutoff** (treated): an entirely separate line.

What you see at the cutoff: **no real jump** in the prediction. Which suggests there wasn't an
immediate effect of the policy going into place.

But the **slope gets steeper** afterwards. So the reading is: no immediate effect, but the quality
improvement programme seems to have made things better **slowly, over time**.

### Two cautions

**Caution 1 — you don't need a straight line, but functional form matters enormously.**

You can add **polynomials** quite easily — just add an interaction between your `after` indicator and
all the polynomial terms, which lets you fit non-straight shapes that your data may require.

But this is an obvious downside of the approach: it's **very sensitive to the choice of functional
form.** It isn't a willy-nilly decision whether to use a linear form or a polynomial — it has big
implications for what effect you find.

**Why:** a curve makes its weirdest predictions **near the edges**. A straight line might predict one
thing at the end; add a curve and the middle barely changes while the edge changes a lot. And the
edge is **exactly where you're looking** — right at the point the event goes into effect.

**Anchor image.** Extrapolating a curve is like pointing a laser. A tiny change in angle barely moves
the beam nearby and moves it enormously at the far end.

**Caution 2 — autocorrelation, which you almost certainly have.**

Standard errors here are difficult, because you're likely to have **autocorrelation** in the error
term — see the regression chapter. An observation in one period is likely correlated with the
observation in the next.

If you run an interrupted time series with a plain regression, no bells and whistles, no adjustment
for autocorrelation, you tend to **over-reject the null**. That is: you're likely to say you've found
effects when you haven't found anything at all. Not good.

## TRAPS

- Interrupted time series is a fundamentally different technique from regression → **Actually,** you can implement it as a regression on time, an after-indicator, and their interaction.
- Centring the time variable is cosmetic → **Actually,** it's what makes the intercept the prediction at the event, so the jump becomes a single coefficient.
- β₁ is the treatment effect → **Actually,** β₁ is the pre-treatment slope. The jump at the cutoff is **β₂**.
- No jump at the cutoff means no effect → **Actually,** the ambulance study had no jump but a steeper post-treatment slope — an effect that accumulates rather than arrives.
- Adding polynomials is a harmless improvement in flexibility → **Actually,** functional form choice has large consequences precisely because curves behave strangely at the edges, and the edge is where you're measuring.
- Standard regression standard errors are fine here → **Actually,** autocorrelation is near-certain in time series, and ignoring it makes you over-reject the null.

## SAY IT OUT LOUD

> Interrupted time series is the simplest way to build that counterfactual. Fit a line through the
> data **before** the event. Fit a separate line through the data **after**. Then look at where they
> meet at the cutoff — is there a **jump**?
>
> And you can do this with a plain regression: outcome on time, on a **binary indicator for being
> after**, and on their **interaction**.
>
> But here's the trick that makes it clean. **Subtract the event date from your time variable**, so
> that time equals **zero exactly at the event**. Why does that help? Because the **intercept is your
> prediction at time zero** — and time zero is now the event. So the intercept literally *is* the
> prediction at the cutoff.
>
> Which means: before the event, the intercept is **β₀**. After the event, it's **β₀ + β₂**. So the
> jump — the whole thing you came here to measure — is just **β₂**. One coefficient.
>
> Nice example: a programme to make ambulances coordinate information better. Fit a line before, a
> line after, and at the cutoff — **no jump at all**. So no immediate effect. But the **slope gets
> steeper** afterwards. So the programme didn't do anything on day one; it made things better
> gradually.
>
> Two things to be careful about. First, **autocorrelation**. In time series data, this period is
> correlated with next period, and if you ignore that your standard errors are wrong in a specific
> nasty direction — you **over-reject**, meaning you'll announce effects that aren't there.
>
> Second, **functional form matters more than you'd think.** Straight line or polynomial isn't a
> casual choice, because a curve does its strangest stuff at the **edges** — and the edge is exactly
> where you're taking your measurement.

## GLOSSARY

- **Interrupted time series** — fitting separate time trends before and after an event and measuring the discontinuity between them.
- **Centring the time variable** — subtracting the event date so that time equals zero at the cutoff, making intercepts the predictions there.
- **Over-rejecting the null** — declaring effects that aren't there, the consequence of ignoring autocorrelation.
- **Functional form** — the shape assumed for the fitted trend, whose choice strongly affects the estimated jump.
