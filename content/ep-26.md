## ONE SENTENCE

An OLS coefficient is **normally distributed across samples**, centred on the population
parameter, with a spread — the **standard error** — driven by how well your model predicts,
how much your X varies, and how many observations you have; and hypothesis testing uses that
distribution to ask whether your starting assumption survives, which is a much narrower
question than people treat it as.

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

- Set identification aside. You don't have all the universe's data, so your estimate carries **sampling variation**.
- Across samples, an OLS coefficient follows a **normal distribution**, centred on the **population parameter**.
- Its standard deviation is the **standard error**, and three things drive it: model prediction error **σ** (up top), the **variance of X**, and the **sample size n** (both below).
- All three are about **information**. More information, more precision.
- **Hypothesis testing:** assume a null, build the sampling distribution it implies, see how unlikely your result was. Either something unlikely happened, or the assumption was wrong.
- It does **not** tell you the probability your estimate is correct.
- Nick's rant: the null is arbitrary, significance depends on sample size, insignificance can be the right answer, and **never** change your model to chase stars.

## WHY THIS EXISTS

You're doing statistics, which means you don't have all of the universe's data. The estimate
you got isn't a perfect representation of the truth — ideally it's a representation of the
truth *plus* some sampling variation and noise built in.

So you've run a regression and got a coefficient. What does that estimate actually mean? To
answer that you need to know how it would have bounced around if you'd drawn different data.

## IDEAS

### The sampling distribution of an OLS coefficient

**Plain English.** Collect data, estimate, repeat forever, and plot all the estimates. You get
a bell curve centred on the truth.

**Precisely.** An OLS coefficient follows a **normal distribution** across samples — provable,
though not proved here. Its mean is the **population parameter**: what you'd get if you did have
all the data in the universe and estimated your model. So on average, OLS gives you the
population parameter.

**Anchor image.** A well-calibrated archer. Individual arrows scatter; the centre of the scatter
is where they aimed.

**Worked example.** You estimate 2.5. You don't know the population parameter — maybe it's 2.5,
maybe 3, maybe 2. But you can say the truth is probably **not 10**. That's the whole game: using
the distribution to make claims about the population parameter from your single estimate.

**Where it breaks.** "On average OLS gives you the population parameter" is not the same as
giving you what you want. **That population parameter can itself be biased** if you haven't got
causal identification. This episode is only about sampling variation.

### The standard error and its three ingredients

**Plain English.** The standard error is how wide the bell curve is. Three things make it
narrow, and all three are versions of "you have more information."

**Precisely.** A **standard error is the standard deviation of a sampling distribution.** For an
OLS coefficient it has three components:

| Component | Where | What it means |
|---|---|---|
| **σ** — prediction error | numerator | The variance of the error term, estimated by the variance of the residuals. How closely your model can predict the outcome. |
| **Variance of X** | denominator | How much your predictor moves around. |
| **Sample size n** | denominator | How many observations you have. |

**σ — model precision.** If your model predicts the outcome very accurately, you know a lot about
what's going on, so you can state your parameter with real confidence. Small residuals → small σ →
small standard error. Knowing a lot about the outcome means your coefficient won't vary much from
sample to sample.

**Variance of X.** You can say more about a variable's effect if it moves around a lot — and the
intuition is straightforward. If X moves a lot and Y stays still, you know nothing is happening.
But if X barely moves and Y doesn't change much either, maybe you just can't see it. It's simply
hard to study things with statistics that don't vary. More movement along X makes it far easier to
see whether there's movement along Y.

**Sample size.** More observations, more information, more precision. A large sample gives a small
standard error because you have a lot to build inferences from.

**Anchor image.** Reading a sign in fog. It gets clearer if the fog thins (σ), if you can walk
back and forth to see it from angles (variance of X), or if a hundred people read it and compare
(n).

### Why statisticians care about distributions at all

**Plain English.** Knowing how a thing varies lets you rule out claims about the truth.

**Worked example.** You estimate an OLS coefficient of **2**. Could that have come from a
distribution with population parameter 100 and standard deviation 1? Essentially never — you'd
basically never see a value that low. And yet you did. So that's the wrong sampling distribution.

### Hypothesis testing, carefully

**Plain English.** Assume something, work out what data you'd expect, and see whether what you got
is absurd under that assumption.

**Precisely.** The procedure:

1. Make an assumption about the world — the **null hypothesis**. Say the true population parameter is 100.
2. Build the sampling distribution that assumption implies. You know it's normal, you know it's centred on the assumed parameter, and you know the standard deviation — so you know **exactly** what the sampling distribution would be, and how likely any particular estimate is under it.
3. Compare your actual estimate to it.
4. If your outcome was very unlikely under that distribution, exactly two things could have happened: **something very unlikely just occurred**, or **your starting assumption was wrong**.

**Worked example.** Null hypothesis: the average person is 5 feet 7 inches tall. That gives you a
sampling distribution of sample averages centred on 5'7", varying a bit above and below from
sample to sample.

Now you draw a sample and it averages **3 feet**. Under the null, that is wildly unlikely from
random chance alone. So: either something extraordinary happened, or 5'7" was wrong.

**Where it breaks — the part people get wrong.** Hypothesis testing does **not** tell you how
likely it is that the outcome you got is correct. All it tells you is whether the assumption you
started with is unlikely to still be true.

**The p-value.** The probability, under the null's sampling distribution, of getting your result or
something even weirder — in either direction for a two-sided test. Null of 5'7", result of 3 feet:
the probability of getting 3 feet or lower, plus something similarly strange in the other direction.

**The cutoff.** Choose a threshold; if the p-value falls below it, reject the null. The logic: at
under 5%, the trade-off between "something weird happened" and "the assumption was wrong" tilts
toward the assumption being wrong. At p = 0.25, that distribution produces such results a quarter
of the time — still not likely, but not unlikely enough to abandon your starting assumption.

### The rant: why not to take significance too seriously

**Plain English.** Nick keeps using significance testing because it's ubiquitous and it's a good
way to think about sampling variation. He'd rather you didn't take it too seriously.

**1 · The null you chose is arbitrary.** The most common null is **zero**. Often that isn't an
interesting question — in social science pretty much all relationships are non-zero at least a
little, so rejecting a null of zero rejects an assumption nobody expected to be true. Big whoop.

More usefully: ask which nulls you *can't* reject. Sure, you rejected zero — but can you reject
0.01? 0.02? 0.03? If the truth were 0.03, would you still say "well, at least it's not zero"? For
that, use a **confidence interval**, which shows the range of nulls you can't reject. Different
question entirely.

**2 · Significance isn't only about whether an effect is real.** It also depends on sample size
and everything else determining precision. Plenty of effects are statistically significant with a
large sample and insignificant with a small one — **the true effect didn't change, the sample size
did.** Significance is about whether you have evidence the null is wrong, not about whether it is
wrong.

**3 · An insignificant result can be the correct result.** We talk about a significant finding as a
"positive result", as though it were a success. But if there is no effect, then finding an
insignificant result **is** the success — you found the truth.

**4 · Never change your model to get significance.** Two reasons:

- **It makes the test wrong.** If you got p = 0.15, changed the model, and got p = 0.04 — you have not found statistical significance, because every one of those calculations assumes you didn't do the thing you just did. Your p-value is wrong, and you may not be significant at your chosen level at all.
- **With enough choices, you'll always find one.** Many decisions go into an analysis, and if you try enough you'll find a way to make results significant. Those choices don't change the truth — but they change your results. You want to reflect the truth, and to let the data prove you wrong.

Statistics professors always say this, and everyone remembers the opposite.

**5 · Cutoffs are strange in general.** Isn't it odd that p = 0.049 is significant and p = 0.051 is
not? That isn't a real difference between two results, and yet you'd make substantially different
choices on the basis of it. It's artificially induced by having a cutoff at all — and it doesn't
matter what the cutoff is, only that there is one. Statistics works on sliding scales, mores and
lesses, not yeses and noes.

**The recommendation:** think about the **precision** of your estimate. What's your standard error?
What does your confidence interval look like? That gives a far more nuanced view of what you can
learn about the world than worrying about whether you're above or below some arbitrarily chosen
cutoff.

## TRAPS

- A p-value tells me the probability my result is correct → **Actually,** it's the probability of data this extreme or weirder *given the null*. It only speaks to the starting assumption.
- On average OLS gives the population parameter, so it's unbiased → **Actually,** it's unbiased for the population parameter, and that parameter can itself be biased if you haven't achieved identification.
- A statistically insignificant result is a failed study → **Actually,** if there's no effect, insignificance is the correct finding. That's a success.
- p = 0.04 is meaningfully different from p = 0.06 → **Actually,** 0.049 versus 0.051 reflects no real difference. The sharp line is an artefact of choosing a cutoff.
- My effect became significant after I adjusted the model, so it's significant → **Actually,** the p-value calculation assumes you didn't do that, so your reported p-value is simply wrong.
- Rejecting a null of zero is an important finding → **Actually,** in social science almost nothing is exactly zero. Ask which non-zero nulls you can also reject, via a confidence interval.
- A big sample proves the effect is real → **Actually,** sample size changes significance without changing the truth. It changes your evidence, not the world.

## RECALL

Q: What distribution does an OLS coefficient follow across samples, and where is it centred?
A: A normal distribution, centred on the population parameter — what you'd get with all the data
in the universe.

Q: What is a standard error?
A: The standard deviation of a sampling distribution.

Q: Name the three components of an OLS standard error and where each sits.
A: σ, the model's prediction error (numerator); the variance of X (denominator); the sample size n
(denominator).

Q: Why does more variance in X reduce the standard error?
A: Because if X moves a lot it's easy to see whether Y moves with it. If X barely varies, you may
simply be unable to detect an effect — statistics can't study things that don't vary.

Q: "On average, OLS gives you the population parameter." Why isn't that enough?
A: Because the population parameter itself can be biased if you haven't achieved causal
identification. Unbiasedness for the population parameter is not the same as getting the causal
effect.

Q: Describe the logic of hypothesis testing in four steps.
A: Assume a null; build the sampling distribution it implies; see how likely your result is under
it; conclude that either something very unlikely happened or the assumption was wrong.

Q: What does hypothesis testing *not* tell you?
A: How likely it is that the estimate you got is correct. It only speaks to whether your starting
assumption is still tenable.

Q: Define a p-value.
A: The probability, under the null's sampling distribution, of getting your result or something
even more extreme — in either direction, for a two-sided test.

Q: Why is a null of zero often uninteresting?
A: In social science almost all relationships are non-zero at least a little, so rejecting zero
rejects an assumption nobody expected to be true.

Q: What should you look at instead of a single sharp test?
A: A confidence interval — the range of nulls you can't reject — and more generally the precision
of your estimate.

Q: Why can the same true effect be significant in one study and not another?
A: Because significance depends on sample size and other determinants of precision, not only on
whether an effect exists.

Q: Give the two reasons not to modify your model to obtain significance.
A: It invalidates the test — the p-value calculation assumes you didn't do that — and with enough
analytical choices you can always find one that works, changing your results without changing the
truth.

Q: Why is a statistically insignificant result sometimes a success?
A: If there is genuinely no effect, finding insignificance means you found the truth.

Q: What's odd about a 0.05 cutoff?
A: p = 0.049 is significant and p = 0.051 is not, though there's no real difference between them.
The sharp boundary is an artefact of having a cutoff at all.

## GLOSSARY

- **Sampling variation** — the variation in an estimate arising from having drawn one particular sample.
- **Population parameter** — the value you'd estimate if you had all the data in the universe.
- **Standard error** — the standard deviation of a sampling distribution.
- **Null hypothesis** — the assumption about the world used to build the sampling distribution you test against.
- **p-value** — the probability of your result or something more extreme, given the null's sampling distribution.
- **Confidence interval** — the range of null values you would not reject.
