## ONE SENTENCE

Fit one line on each side of the cutoff, **centre the running variable so it equals zero there**, and
the jump between the two intercepts — **β₂** — is your effect; then wrestle with bandwidth, functional
form, and the fact that a **fuzzy** cutoff turns the whole thing into instrumental variables.

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

- Regress the outcome on **(running variable − cutoff)**, a **treated** indicator, and their **interaction**. Two lines, one per side.
- **Subtracting the cutoff** makes the running variable zero there, so the intercepts become the predictions **at** the cutoff.
- Untreated side: intercept **β₀**, slope **β₁**. Treated side: intercept **β₀ + β₂**, slope **β₁ + β₃**. The effect is **β₂**.
- **Mathematically identical to interrupted time series** — but a very different design, because "randomly on one side" is far more plausible than "randomly before or after."
- **Bandwidth** is a bias–variance trade-off: narrow means credible but noisy, wide means precise but less comparable.
- **Functional form matters enormously**, because you're extrapolating to where each line hits the cutoff. A quadratic is usually as far as you go.
- **Fuzzy regression discontinuity** — where the cutoff only partly determines treatment — is solved with **instrumental variables**.

## WHY THIS EXISTS

You have the design. Now estimate it. And as ever, **the research design and the estimation method are
not the same thing** — there are often many ways to estimate a given design, and the same estimation
method can apply to different designs depending on context.

## IDEAS

### The basic regression

**Plain English.** One line each side, and the gap where they meet is the answer.

**Precisely.** Regress your outcome **y** on:

- **(running variable − cutoff)**
- an indicator for being **treated** — being on the correct side of the cutoff
- the **interaction** between the two

You're effectively estimating **two lines**: one linear regression to the left of the cutoff, a
different one to the right.

| Side | `treated` | Intercept | Slope |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Untreated** | 0 | **β₀** | **β₁** |
| **Treated** | 1 | **β₀ + β₂** | **β₁ + β₃** |

### Why you subtract the cutoff

**Plain English.** So that "zero" means "at the cutoff" — which makes the intercepts do the work for
you.

**Precisely.** Take your running variable — say the income that qualifies you for some relief programme
— and subtract the cutoff. If the cutoff is **$12,000** and you earn **$12,005**, your centred running
variable is **5**, because you're five above the cutoff.

Now think back to the interpretation of an intercept: it's your **prediction of the outcome when all
other predictors are zero**. So set the running variable to zero and the intercept is your prediction at
zero — and by centring, **zero is the cutoff**. The intercept becomes **your prediction at the cutoff**,
which is exactly the point where the two lines meet.

**Reading the graph.** Fit one regression line on the left and a different one on the right. Where the
left-hand line hits the dashed cutoff line, that's the left intercept — **β₀**. Where the right-hand
line hits it, that's the right intercept — **β₀ + β₂**.

So the **jump** at the cutoff, where the treatment goes into effect, is the change from one intercept to
the other: **β₂ is your regression discontinuity effect.**

**Anchor image.** Two roads approaching a river from opposite banks. What you're measuring is the height
difference where each one reaches the water.

### It's mathematically the same as interrupted time series

**Plain English.** Same maths, completely different assumption.

**Precisely.** If you watched the event study videos, this is **mathematically the exact same thing** as
the interrupted time series approach estimated with linear regression. And there's a reason: event
studies and regression discontinuity are basically the same thing mathematically. In event studies the
**running variable is time**; in regression discontinuity it's something else.

**So why treat them as entirely separate designs?** Because the assumptions you need are very different.

It is a **much** different assumption to say that people just before and just after an event, within a
narrow window, are basically comparable — versus people on one side of a cutoff or the other. The idea
that you're **randomly falling on one side or the other** makes a lot more sense for running variables
that aren't time. **You can't really randomly be before or after.**

In general people consider **regression discontinuity a lot more plausible than event studies** for
exactly that reason.

### Bandwidth and the power trade-off

**Plain English.** The whole design says only the cases near the cutoff are comparable — and then you
went and used all the data.

**Precisely.** The basic regression above used the **entire range** of the data to estimate its lines,
which may not be what you want. So a common step is to **narrow your range**, dropping data far from the
cutoff.

**Worked example.** With the income cutoff at $12,000, you might decide people earning between
**$11,900 and $12,100** have incomes close enough to be comparable, limit the data to that, and fit your
regression lines within it.

**The trade-off — and this is the statistical power issue.**

| Bandwidth | Bias | Precision |
|---|---|---|
| **Narrow** | Low — you genuinely believe in the identification | Poor — you drop a lot of data, the sample gets very small, the estimate gets very noisy |
| **Wide** | Higher — you're comparing someone on $13,000 to someone on $10,000, and back door variables may return | Better — more observations |

Widening asks whether $13,000 and $10,000 of income are really comparable. Maybe, maybe not — and at
some point you start to lose belief in the identification.

**Choosing one.** There are procedures called **optimal bandwidth selection**. Alternatively, just try a
bunch of different bandwidths and see whether your results are **stable** as they widen and your
estimates get more precise. If the estimate itself doesn't change much as you bring in more data, you
might as well bring it in — it doesn't seem to be biasing you much.

### Functional form matters more than usual

**Plain English.** You're extrapolating each line to the cutoff, so getting the shape wrong moves your
answer directly.

**Precisely.** You don't have to use a linear regression on either side. In the example graph the fit is
clearly not good. You can use a **curve or polynomial** on either side to better match the shape of the
data — in particular a squared term, a **quadratic**. You probably don't want to go beyond that, for
reasons given in the chapter.

**Why this matters so much here.** You want a functional form that fits your data well, because
otherwise you won't do a good job of telling **where the data is going to hit that dashed line** — and
that is the entire goal. In the original graph, the data hits the dashed line at a **very different
point** than the fitted line does. Adding a polynomial term would substantially improve the estimate.

**How to add it.** Before, you had (running − cutoff), interacted with treated. For a polynomial: add
(running − cutoff) **squared**, and interact **that** with treated as well. That's it.

### Fuzzy regression discontinuity

**Plain English.** If crossing the cutoff only *raises the chance* of treatment, use the cutoff as an
instrument.

**Precisely.** What if the cutoff doesn't perfectly determine your treatment? **Fuzzy regression
discontinuity** is when the running variable only **partially** determines treatment.

**Worked example.** Back to the income relief. If you earn below $12,000 a year you **qualify** — but you
also have to **apply**, and not everybody does. So instead of going from 0% treated above $12,000 to 100%
treated below it, you might get **0% treated above and 50% treated below**.

**The fix — and it's the thread from the last chapter.** You still have a source of random-ish
assignment: being just above or just below the cutoff. And that still **predicts** your treatment. So use
**being on one side of the cutoff as an instrument for actually being treated**.

Your income being just below $12,000 increases the probability that you're treated; use that predicted
increase in treatment probability to see the effect of treatment on your outcomes. That's taking all the
instrumental variables machinery from the previous chapter and applying it here, with **side of the
cutoff as the instrument**. Technical details are in the chapter.

## TRAPS

- The running variable goes into the regression as-is → **Actually,** you subtract the cutoff so it's zero there, which is what makes the intercepts the predictions at the cutoff.
- β₁ or β₃ is the effect → **Actually,** β₁ is the untreated slope and β₃ shifts the slope. The effect is **β₂**, the jump between intercepts.
- Regression discontinuity and interrupted time series are different methods → **Actually,** they're mathematically identical. They're different *designs*, because the assumptions differ.
- Event studies are as credible as regression discontinuity since the maths matches → **Actually,** "randomly landing either side of a cutoff" is far more plausible than "randomly landing before or after a date."
- Use all your data — more is better → **Actually,** the design's logic only holds near the cutoff, so you face a genuine bandwidth trade-off.
- A narrow bandwidth is always the safer choice → **Actually,** it can leave you with so little data that the estimate is worthless. That's the power problem.
- Higher-order polynomials fit better, so use them → **Actually,** a quadratic is usually as far as you should go.
- Functional form is a minor modelling choice → **Actually,** you're extrapolating each line to the cutoff, so the shape directly determines the estimated jump.

> intercept becomes your **prediction at the cutoff** — the very point where the two lines meet. So the

> variable was **time**.

## RECALL

Q: What three terms go into the basic regression discontinuity regression?
A: The running variable minus the cutoff, an indicator for being treated, and their interaction.

Q: Why subtract the cutoff from the running variable?
A: So the running variable equals zero at the cutoff, which makes the intercepts the predicted outcomes
at the cutoff — the point where the two lines meet.

Q: Cutoff is $12,000 and you earn $12,005. What's your centred running variable?
A: 5.

Q: Give the intercept and slope on each side.
A: Untreated side: intercept β₀, slope β₁. Treated side: intercept β₀ + β₂, slope β₁ + β₃.

Q: Which coefficient is the regression discontinuity effect?
A: β₂ — the jump between the two intercepts at the cutoff.

Q: What other method is this mathematically identical to?
A: The interrupted time series approach from the event studies chapter, estimated with linear regression.

Q: What distinguishes the two designs, given identical mathematics?
A: The running variable. Event studies use time, and the assumption that people just before and just
after an event are comparable is much weaker than the assumption for a non-time cutoff.

Q: Why is "randomly on one side" more plausible than "randomly before or after"?
A: You can't really randomly be before or after a date, whereas landing just above or below a threshold
genuinely can be near-random.

Q: What's the problem with the basic regression as first specified?
A: It uses the entire range of the data, when the design's logic only holds near the cutoff.

Q: State the bandwidth trade-off.
A: Narrow bandwidths give more credible identification but drop so much data that estimates get very
noisy; wide bandwidths give precision but bring back less comparable observations and possible bias.

Q: Give two ways to choose a bandwidth.
A: Optimal bandwidth selection procedures, or trying several bandwidths and checking whether the estimate
is stable as they widen.

Q: What does stability across bandwidths tell you?
A: That widening isn't biasing you much, so you might as well use the extra data for precision.

Q: Why does functional form matter so much in regression discontinuity?
A: Because you're extrapolating each line to the cutoff, and a poorly fitting shape puts the line in the
wrong place exactly where you're measuring the jump.

Q: How do you add a quadratic to the model?
A: Include the squared centred running variable and interact that with the treated indicator as well.

Q: How far up the polynomial orders should you go?
A: Usually no further than a quadratic.

Q: What is fuzzy regression discontinuity?
A: When the running variable only partially determines treatment — for instance, qualifying below
$12,000 but also having to apply, so 0% are treated above and only 50% below.

Q: How do you estimate a fuzzy design?
A: Use being on one side of the cutoff as an instrument for actually being treated, and apply
instrumental variables.

## GLOSSARY

- **Centred running variable** — the running variable minus the cutoff, so it equals zero at the threshold.
- **Fuzzy regression discontinuity** — a design where crossing the cutoff changes the probability of treatment rather than determining it.
- **Sharp regression discontinuity** — the standard case, where the cutoff fully determines treatment.
- **Optimal bandwidth selection** — procedures for choosing how wide a window around the cutoff to use.
