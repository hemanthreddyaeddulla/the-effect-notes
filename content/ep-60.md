## ONE SENTENCE

**Relevance** you can genuinely check — via the first stage F statistic, though the "F above 10"
rule is wrong and the honest reading is Stock and Yogo's tables — while **validity** is inherently
theoretical, and the two tests that claim to check it both amount to treating a **failure to reject
a null** as proof, which Nick doesn't buy.

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

- Conditions: **relevance**, **validity**, and also **monotonicity** — the instrument must push everyone's treatment probability in the same direction.
- **Weak instruments** are disastrous: little variation in predicted treatment means wildly noisy estimates.
- Worse, instrumental variables is **biased in finite samples**, and weak instruments let that bias dominate.
- **First stage F statistic** tests joint significance of the instruments. The bigger, the smaller the IV bias **relative to OLS bias** — but it never vanishes until F is enormous.
- **F > 10 is not how it works.** Use the **Stock and Yogo tables**.
- Abandoning studies on a failed F test creates **publication bias**. Better: proceed and use **Anderson–Rubin confidence intervals**.
- Validity is **theoretical**. The **Durbin–Wu–Hausman** and **over-identification** tests both rest on failing to reject a null.

## WHY THIS EXISTS

For instrumental variables to work you need **relevance** — the instrument genuinely predicts the
treatment, because if it doesn't, isolating the random part of treatment leaves nothing to study —
and **validity** — no open back door between instrument and outcome, and no front door from
instrument to outcome except through the treatment you care about. Your instrument can't be causing
two different treatments; that gets confusing.

There are other assumptions too, such as **monotonicity**: the instrument must affect the probability
of being treated in the **same direction for everybody**. That one isn't covered much here; the
chapter has more.

So: what can you actually check in the data?

## IDEAS

### Relevance, and why weak instruments are so bad

**Plain English.** If your instrument barely moves treatment, your estimate is noise.

**Precisely.** Relevance is the easy one — just look at how closely related the instrument and
treatment are. Strongly related, good. Weakly related, and you have a **weak instrument problem**,
which can be very bad because it's very noisy.

**Why.** Recall the components of an OLS standard error: the more variation there is in the predictor,
the more precisely you can estimate. If x moves around a lot, it's easy to see whether y moves with
it; if x barely moves, it's hard to tell. And in instrumental variables you're using **only the part
of x predicted by z**. So if z predicts only a little variation in x, there's very little variation in
your predicted values — very little movement down in the first stage — which makes it very hard to see
what's happening up in the second.

The result is **extremely noisy estimates** — estimates that can change by **thousands of percentage
points** from sample to sample, which makes the whole approach worthless.

**Anchor image.** Steering by a compass needle that barely moves. Every tiny wobble sends you somewhere
completely different.

### Small sample bias

**Plain English.** In any finite sample, "unrelated to the error term" isn't exactly true — and that
introduces bias.

**Precisely.** Instrumental variables has **small sample bias**: as an estimator it is genuinely
**biased in a finite sample**. The reason is the assumption that your instrument is unrelated to the
second-stage error term.

In the **population** that might literally be true — you may be able to say theoretically, with real
confidence, that the true model has no back door from your instrument to the outcome. But in a **small
sample**, by random chance, that relationship won't be **exactly** zero. And because it isn't exactly
zero, a little bias creeps into the estimation.

**Where it breaks.** Not a huge problem unless you have a really small sample or a really weak
instrument. But **the weaker the instrument, the more that bias takes up of what's going on in your
estimate**, and the less of it is the actual effect.

### The first stage F statistic

**Plain English.** Test whether your instruments jointly predict treatment. Bigger is better.

**Precisely.** The most common check. Look at the first stage and perform an **F test of the joint
significance of your instruments** — whether the coefficient on your instrument is zero, or, with
multiple instruments, whether all their coefficients are zero simultaneously. **The bigger the F
statistic, the better a job your instruments do of predicting treatment.**

**Where the rule of thumb goes wrong.** You may have heard that an F statistic of **10 or above** means
you're fine. **That's not really how it works.** What's actually true is that the bigger your F
statistic, the **smaller the instrumental variables bias is relative to the bias you'd get from plain
ordinary least squares** if you didn't correct for endogeneity at all.

So a bigger F means instrumental variables looks better relative to that comparison — **but the bias is
still there**. It doesn't simply go away, at least not until your F statistic is positively enormous,
something like **105**.

**What to use instead.** The **Stock and Yogo tables**, which show how big your F statistic needs to be
to keep the small sample bias small enough relative to the OLS bias. At least a slightly more detailed
version of the number you're looking for.

### The problem with the F statistic approach itself

**Plain English.** Abandoning studies when the F test fails quietly selects for flukes.

**Precisely.** What happens when you fail? You might conclude your instrument is too weak, drop the
study, and go away. **That introduces publication bias.**

**Worked example.** Suppose you have an instrument that's genuinely somewhat weak. Not the strongest —
but you're fairly confident it's **valid**. Because it's weak, its measured strength varies a lot from
sample to sample.

Now two different researchers both use this instrument, and both apply an F statistic cutoff. One
happens to draw a sample where the instrument looks **stronger**; the other draws one where it looks
**weaker**. The first proceeds with their study; the second doesn't.

**But the first one just happened to get a strong first stage by random fluke.** They have the worse
data, and they're the ones going ahead. That's bad.

**The better approach.** Go ahead with the study anyway, but **account for the fact that you might have
a weak instrument**. Use **Anderson–Rubin confidence intervals** — a different way of calculating the
confidence interval for your instrumental variables estimate that allows for the instrument being weak.

So rather than *our instrument's too weak, call it all off*, you say *our instrument's somewhat weak,
so we'll change the confidence intervals to account for it* — making them wider, and in fact **not even
symmetric**: you might get a larger interval on one side of your estimate than the other. The chapter's
"how the pros do it" section has the detail and the code.

### Validity: inherently theoretical

**Plain English.** There's no real empirical test, and the two that exist rest on accepting a null.

**Precisely.** Validity is trickier because it's **inherently a theoretical assumption** — about the
relationship between your instrument and something you can't necessarily observe: whether your model is
correct, whether you're omitting unobservables related to your instrument. **In general, any empirical
test for validity kind of misses the point.** You want to be able to say theoretically, with a lot of
confidence, that your instrument is valid.

There are two things you can check, both of which Nick is grumpy about.

### Test 1: Durbin–Wu–Hausman

**Plain English.** Run it with and without the instrument and see whether the answers differ.

**Precisely.** Estimate your results twice — once with instrumental variables as normal, once with
**ordinary least squares**, forgetting the instrumental variables part entirely — and check how
different the two estimates are.

The idea: instrumental variables should be **correcting for endogeneity** in your OLS model. Your OLS
model is biased because of some relationship between treatment and error term, and that's what
instrumental variables fixes. So if instrumental variables is doing anything, the coefficients should
**differ**.

- **Different** → instrumental variables is correcting for something, so use it, despite its own problems with weak instruments and validity.
- **Indistinguishable** → you might as well use ordinary least squares, which has better statistical properties and is more precise.

**Nick's objection.** Think about what this actually tells you. If you don't get a big difference,
you're concluding you **don't have an endogeneity problem**. **Do you really believe that failing to
reject a null hypothesis is proof that you don't have an endogeneity problem?** He rarely would, and
would be very sceptical of anyone saying *I wasn't sure about my instrument, but I ran this test and it
said I was fine.*

**An important clarification.** This is **not testing the validity of your instrument**. It's testing
for the presence of endogeneity in your OLS that would require instrumental variables to correct — so
it's about the exogeneity of your **treatment**, which isn't quite the same thing. Even so, he wouldn't
believe your treatment is exogenous just because you failed to find a difference.

### Test 2: the over-identification test

**Plain English.** With two instruments, trust one and use it to audit the other.

**Precisely.** This one **does** test the validity of an instrument — but it needs more than one.
Assume at least one of your instruments is genuinely valid, and use it as ground truth to check the
other.

Compare what happens using **only** the instrument you're confident about, against what happens using
**both**. If the results differ, the second instrument may be introducing invalidity. If they're the
same, you can use multiple instruments and improve your precision.

**Nick's first objection.** Same problem. If you fail to reject the statistical null, does that really
mean the new instrument is valid? He isn't going to use a statistical test to determine whether a
validity **condition** holds. That just doesn't feel right — though he notes it's a personal view.

**Nick's second objection — the local average treatment effect.** Remember instrumental variables tells
you about the effect among people whose treatment is driven by the instrument, which differs depending
on which instrument you use, since some people respond more to one than another.

So if two instruments give different results, that **could** mean one of them is invalid — **or it
could just mean they're isolating different parts of the variation and different kinds of responses**.
You might reject for that reason, and end up discarding an instrument that is in fact perfectly valid
but simply captures a different part of the treatment effect.

## TRAPS

- An F statistic above 10 means the instrument is strong enough → **Actually,** that's a rule of thumb that misrepresents the situation. The bias shrinks relative to OLS bias as F grows, and doesn't really vanish until F is enormous.
- A weak instrument just makes your estimate imprecise → **Actually,** it also lets finite-sample bias dominate, since instrumental variables is biased in small samples.
- If your F statistic fails, don't run the study → **Actually,** that creates publication bias, since whoever happened to draw a fluke-strong sample proceeds while others don't.
- Confidence intervals for weak instruments are just wider → **Actually,** Anderson–Rubin intervals need not even be symmetric around the estimate.
- Durbin–Wu–Hausman tests instrument validity → **Actually,** it tests for endogeneity in your OLS — the exogeneity of your treatment, not of your instrument.
- Passing Durbin–Wu–Hausman means you don't have an endogeneity problem → **Actually,** that's treating failure to reject a null as proof of the null.
- Over-identification test failure means one instrument is invalid → **Actually,** it may just mean the two instruments capture different local average treatment effects.

## RECALL

Q: Name the three assumptions mentioned, including the one not covered in detail.
A: Relevance, validity, and monotonicity — the instrument must affect everyone's probability of
treatment in the same direction.

Q: How do you check relevance?
A: Look at how closely related the instrument and treatment are.

Q: Why are weak instruments so damaging?
A: You use only the part of treatment predicted by the instrument, so weak prediction leaves very little
variation to work with, giving estimates that can vary by thousands of percentage points across samples.

Q: What is small sample bias in instrumental variables?
A: Instrumental variables is a biased estimator in finite samples, because the instrument's relationship
with the error term won't be exactly zero in a given sample even if it is in the population.

Q: When does small sample bias become a serious problem?
A: With a really small sample or a really weak instrument — the weaker the instrument, the more of your
estimate is bias rather than effect.

Q: What does the first stage F statistic test?
A: The joint significance of your instruments in the first stage — whether all their coefficients are
zero.

Q: Why is "F above 10" the wrong way to think about it?
A: Because a bigger F means smaller instrumental variables bias *relative to* the ordinary least squares
bias — the bias doesn't disappear until F is enormous, on the order of 105.

Q: What should you consult instead?
A: The Stock and Yogo tables, which show how large F must be to keep small sample bias small relative to
the OLS bias.

Q: How does using an F cutoff create publication bias?
A: With a genuinely weak instrument, whichever researcher happens to draw a sample where it looks strong
proceeds, while others don't — so the study that gets published is the fluke.

Q: What's the recommended alternative?
A: Proceed with the study but use Anderson–Rubin confidence intervals, which account for a possibly weak
instrument.

Q: What's unusual about those intervals?
A: They're wider, and not necessarily symmetric — the interval may be larger on one side of the estimate
than the other.

Q: Why is validity hard to test empirically?
A: It's inherently theoretical — an assumption about the relationship between your instrument and things
you can't necessarily observe. Empirical tests for it rather miss the point.

Q: Describe the Durbin–Wu–Hausman test.
A: Estimate twice — once with instrumental variables and once with plain ordinary least squares — and
compare. Differing coefficients suggest instrumental variables is correcting something; similar ones
suggest you may as well use OLS.

Q: What is Nick's objection to it?
A: It amounts to treating a failure to reject a null hypothesis as proof that you don't have an
endogeneity problem.

Q: What is that test actually testing?
A: The presence of endogeneity in your OLS — the exogeneity of your treatment — not the validity of your
instrument.

Q: Describe the over-identification test.
A: With more than one instrument, assume one is valid and use it as ground truth: compare results using
only that instrument against results using both. Differences suggest the second may be invalid.

Q: Give Nick's two objections to it.
A: It again treats failure to reject a null as evidence of validity; and differing results may simply
reflect that the two instruments isolate different local average treatment effects, so you might discard
a perfectly valid instrument.

## GLOSSARY

- **Monotonicity** — the requirement that the instrument moves everyone's treatment probability in the same direction.
- **Weak instrument problem** — an instrument that predicts treatment poorly, yielding extremely noisy and bias-dominated estimates.
- **Small sample bias** — the finite-sample bias of instrumental variables, arising because the instrument's correlation with the error term isn't exactly zero in any given sample.
- **First stage F statistic** — a test of the joint significance of the instruments in predicting treatment.
- **Stock and Yogo tables** — reference tables giving the F statistic needed to keep small sample bias acceptably small.
- **Anderson–Rubin confidence intervals** — confidence intervals valid under weak instruments, wider and possibly asymmetric.
- **Durbin–Wu–Hausman test** — a comparison of instrumental variables and OLS estimates, testing for endogeneity in the treatment.
- **Over-identification test** — with multiple instruments, using one assumed-valid instrument to assess another.
