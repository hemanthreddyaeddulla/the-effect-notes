## ONE SENTENCE

You don't need to close off everything a confounder does — only the part that **drives
treatment** — so predict each person's **probability of being treated**, collapse all your
matching variables into that single number, and weight by its inverse.

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

- The goal was never to control the variables. It was to **close the back doors**.
- Confounders do many things; only part of what they do is affect treatment. Close off **that** part and you've done the job.
- The **propensity score** is the probability someone gets treated, usually predicted with a **probit or logit** on the matching variables. Increasingly with machine learning too — it's a prediction problem.
- Because it collapses everything into **one dimension**, there's no curse of dimensionality. But the regression brings the **linearity assumption** back.
- **Don't pick matches on it** — that has known statistical problems. Use **inverse probability weighting**.
- Weight treated observations by 1/p and controls by 1/(1−p): the **least likely treated who were**, and the **most likely treated who weren't**.
- Watch for **extreme scores** (trim them), **common support**, and **balance** — then go back and re-estimate.

## WHY THIS EXISTS

Distance matching works, but it collapses under many matching variables. And step back to why
you're doing any of this: you want the causal effect of X on Y, back door variables are driving
the correlation you observe, and you want to control for that.

But here's the insight. **Closing all those back doors doesn't require closing off all the
variation in those variables.** That was never the goal — the goal is to close the back doors.
Those confounders do all sorts of things, and only *part* of what they do is affect treatment. If
you can close off the part of those variables that determines whether somebody is treated, and
control for just that, you've done your job.

## IDEAS

### The propensity score

**Plain English.** One number per person: how likely they were to get treated.

**Precisely.** The **propensity score** is the probability that somebody is going to be treated.
Propensity score matching predicts your propensity for being treated from the matching variables,
then uses that probability to find who is most like you **in terms of how likely you were to be
treated** — because controlling for that is the real thing you were trying to do with all these back
door closures.

You don't have to control for all the individual variables, so long as you can control for the
propensity score.

**Anchor image.** You don't need to know everything about a lock. Only which key opens it.

**How it's estimated.** Typically with a **probit or logit** model: take whether or not you were
treated, regress it on the set of matching variables, and use that to predict the probability you
were treated. That's your estimated propensity score.

Probit and logit aren't the only options — increasingly you'll see **machine learning** applications
predicting the propensity score, which makes sense: this is a prediction problem, and prediction is
what machine learning is good at. But probit and logit remain the most common.

### What using a regression costs and buys

**Plain English.** The linearity you escaped comes back — but the dimensionality problem goes away.

**Precisely.**

- **Cost:** one of the good things about matching was that it doesn't lean on linearity assumptions the way regression does. Now you're running a regression, so that assumption comes back in.
- **Benefit:** you can take a large number of matching variables and combine them in a way that handles high dimensionality far better than distance matching. **You don't get the curse of dimensionality in the same way**, because you're collapsing all the matching variables together — you end up back in a **single dimension**, and one dimension can't suffer a curse of dimensionality.

### Don't pick matches — use inverse probability weighting

**Plain English.** Matching people with similar propensity scores is the obvious move, and it's the
wrong one.

**Precisely.** The obvious approach: you have an 80% chance of being treated and you were treated,
so find someone untreated with a 79% or 81% chance and call them your best match. But it's been
shown that **using a propensity score to pick a set of matches has statistical issues — it doesn't
work all that well.**

So of the two matching approaches from Ep 35, use **weighting**.

**Inverse probability weighting:**

| Group | Weight | Consequence |
|---|---|---|
| **Treated** | inverse of the probability of being treated — **1/p** | Those *most* likely to be treated get the **lowest** weight |
| **Control** | inverse of the probability of *not* being treated — **1/(1−p)** | Those *least* likely to be treated get the **lowest** weight |

**Why that direction.** The control group didn't get treated. So if you want treated observations
that resemble the control group, you want the people who were **least likely to get treated** — and
they get the heaviest weights. Symmetrically, the treated group did get treated, so among the
untreated you want people who were **most likely to be treated and yet weren't** — and they get the
heaviest weights.

You end up comparing the people **least likely to be treated who were** against the people **most
likely to be treated who weren't** — and those are likely to be pretty decent comparisons for each
other.

**Anchor image.** Two crowds you want to make resemble each other. Turn up the volume on the
unusual people in each and turn it down on the typical ones.

### Difficulty 1: extreme propensity scores

**Plain English.** Dividing by a probability near zero blows up.

**Precisely.** Inverse probability weighting runs into problems when the probability of being treated
is near one or near zero:

- Near-zero probability of treatment but you *were* treated → weight is something like 1 ÷ 0, which you can't do.
- Near-one probability but you *weren't* treated → weight is 1 ÷ almost zero. Same problem.

**The fix:** **trim** the propensity score — drop people with very high or very low probabilities of
being treated.

### Difficulty 2: common support

**Plain English.** Is there actually anybody on the other side who resembles these people?

**Precisely.** You're trying to find the control observations most like the treated group — the ones
with the highest probability of being treated. **Is there anybody actually like that?** Is there
anyone who wasn't treated but had a high chance of being? And symmetrically, is there anyone who was
treated but had a low chance of being?

There might not be. If you have nobody to compare who is treated-but-like-the-untreated, or
untreated-but-like-the-treated, you **lack common support**, and propensity score matching may
simply not work well.

**The fix:** drop people whose probabilities have nobody on the other side. If nobody in the control
group had an 80% or better chance of being treated, drop everyone in the treated group with an 80%+
chance and use only the people more like the control group. Which of course means dropping
observations — the bias–variance trade-off, again.

### Difficulty 3: balance

**Plain English.** You can match perfectly on the score and still have wildly different groups.

**Precisely.** Unlike distance matching, propensity score matching **isn't trying to make the
matching variables similar between the groups**. You can easily end up with a result where the
actual matching variables look very different across groups.

**Worked example.** Match on April credit card bill size and age, and suppose both increase the
probability of not paying your April bill. Now consider:

| Person | April bill | Age |
|---|---|---|
| A | very high | very young |
| B | very low | very old |

Both might be predicted to have **the same probability** of not paying. So matching one to the other
gives you a bill that doesn't match at all and an age that matches terribly — while the propensity
scores line up perfectly. Groups well matched on the score, badly matched on the individual
variables. That's a **failure of balance**.

**Where it breaks.** Technically the whole idea is that if you control for the propensity score, who
cares about the individual variables? But it doesn't look great, and it's a bit suspicious. So a
common practice is to **check whether the matching variables look at least roughly similar** between
the treated and control groups.

### The check-and-return loop

**Plain English.** Estimate, inspect, go back, change something, repeat.

**Precisely.** If common support or balance is bad, your matching didn't work well — so go back to
your propensity score estimation and change things. Add **polynomial terms** to the estimation. Add
or remove some matching variables. Then re-check.

This check-and-return approach is common with matching procedures generally: do the matching, pick
your matches, check common support and balance, and if they look bad, go back and try other things
until the matching looks like it worked better.

**Where it breaks.** It means you may run your whole matching procedure many times over, which is
annoying — and there are alternative methods that don't require it, which is the next episode.

## TRAPS

- You have to control for every confounding variable → **Actually,** you only need to close off the part of them that determines treatment — which is what the propensity score captures.
- Propensity score matching means matching people with similar scores → **Actually,** that's been shown to have statistical problems. Use inverse probability weighting instead.
- Treated people most likely to be treated should count most → **Actually,** the opposite. They get the *lowest* weight, because you want treated observations resembling the untreated group.
- Propensity scores avoid regression's assumptions → **Actually,** estimating one with probit or logit brings the linearity assumption back in.
- Propensity scores suffer the curse of dimensionality like distance matching → **Actually,** they collapse everything to one dimension, which is precisely why they handle many variables well.
- Good balance follows automatically from a good propensity score match → **Actually,** two people with identical scores can differ wildly on the underlying variables — a high bill and young age versus a low bill and old age.
- Poor balance is fine because the score is what you're controlling for → **Actually,** technically true and still suspicious. Check the individual variables and re-estimate if they look bad.

## RECALL

Q: What's the insight behind propensity score matching?
A: Closing back doors doesn't require removing all variation in the confounders — only the part of
them that determines whether someone is treated.

Q: Define the propensity score.
A: The probability that somebody is going to be treated.

Q: How is it usually estimated?
A: With a probit or logit model: regress whether you were treated on the matching variables and
predict the probability.

Q: What other approach is increasingly used, and why does it fit?
A: Machine learning — because estimating a propensity score is a prediction problem, and prediction
is what machine learning is good at.

Q: What does using a regression to estimate it cost you?
A: The linearity assumption you avoided by using matching instead of regression comes back in.

Q: Why doesn't propensity score matching suffer the curse of dimensionality?
A: Because it collapses all the matching variables into a single dimension — the score — rather than
measuring distance separately along each.

Q: Should you use the propensity score to pick a set of matches?
A: No. That has been shown to have statistical issues and doesn't work well. Use weighting instead.

Q: Give the inverse probability weights for treated and control observations.
A: Treated: 1 divided by the probability of being treated. Control: 1 divided by the probability of
*not* being treated.

Q: Which treated observations end up counting most, and why?
A: Those least likely to have been treated. The control group didn't get treated, so treated
observations resembling the control group are the ones that were unlikely to be treated.

Q: Which control observations count most?
A: Those most likely to have been treated but weren't — since they most resemble the treated group.

Q: What goes wrong with extreme propensity scores, and how do you fix it?
A: Weights become 1 divided by something near zero and blow up. Trim the score — drop observations
with very high or very low probabilities of treatment.

Q: What is common support?
A: Whether there actually exist untreated people with a high probability of treatment, and treated
people with a low probability. Without them you have no comparable observations.

Q: How do you handle a lack of common support?
A: Drop observations whose probabilities have nobody on the other side — for instance, if no control
had an 80%+ chance of treatment, drop treated observations above 80%.

Q: What is a failure of balance?
A: When groups are well matched on the propensity score but the individual matching variables still
look very different between them.

Q: Give the balance example.
A: Matching on April bill and age, both of which raise the probability of not paying: someone with a
very high bill who is very young and someone with a very low bill who is very old can have identical
propensity scores while being nothing alike.

Q: Is poor balance technically a problem, and what do people do about it?
A: Technically the score is what you're controlling for, so it needn't be — but it looks suspicious,
so people check whether the matching variables are at least roughly similar across groups.

Q: Describe the check-and-return approach.
A: Estimate the propensity score, do the matching, check common support and balance; if they look
bad, go back and change the estimation — add polynomial terms, add or remove matching variables — and
repeat.

## GLOSSARY

- **Propensity score** — the estimated probability that an observation receives treatment.
- **Inverse probability weighting** — weighting observations by the inverse of their probability of the treatment status they actually had.
- **Trimming** — dropping observations with extreme propensity scores to avoid exploding weights.
- **Common support** — the existence of comparable observations on both sides across the range of propensity scores.
- **Balance** — whether the matching variables themselves end up similar between the treated and control groups.
