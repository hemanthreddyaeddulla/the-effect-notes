## ONE SENTENCE

We know **exactly** how big omitted variable bias is — it just depends on two things we can't
measure — so assume **ranges** for those two, compute the bias each would produce, and ask the
question that actually decides it: **how strong would a missing confounder have to be to kill my
result?**

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

- The assumption under attack: that you controlled for **everything**. You almost certainly didn't.
- **Omitted variable bias has a known formula**: how strongly the missing variable relates to the **outcome**, times how strongly it relates to the **treatment**, over the variance of X.
- You can't estimate either piece — there's no data on the omitted variable. But you can **bound** them.
- **Cinelli and Hazlett (2020)** does this using **partial R²** values, giving a range of debiased estimates.
- **Benchmark against the controls you already have.** How strong would a missing confounder need to be, compared to one you measured?
- **Worked case:** UK sentencing. Men are 13 points more likely to be imprisoned. A confounder as strong as drug class takes it to 0.08 — not to zero.
- A confounder as strong as **all 43 existing controls combined** would kill it. Is that plausible? Probably not.

## WHY THIS EXISTS

You're running a regression to get the effect of one thing on another, and the assumption you're
relying on is that **you controlled for everything you needed to** — that you identified all the back
door paths and added controls for them.

**The problem.** It's very hard to convincingly claim you controlled for **every** back door path.
Either you forgot one you weren't aware of, or you found one but couldn't measure it convincingly. So
**something is left over**, and you have some bias in your estimate.

**Partial identification's answer:** yes, there's bias left — but that doesn't mean giving up and
going home. Make the weaker assumption instead. Rather than claiming you shut everything down, say
you controlled for **a lot** of it, and then ask: **how much might be left, and if some is left, how
would that affect the estimate?**

The method is from **Cinelli and Hazlett (2020)**, a common and increasingly popular way of thinking
about partial identification in a regression context.

## IDEAS

### The nice thing about omitted variable bias

**Plain English.** You know exactly how big it is. You just can't measure the ingredients.

**Precisely.** Write out the formula for a regression analysis and **you know exactly how big the bias
is** — it's an explicit term. And it combines a few things. If there's an omitted variable **Z** that
should have been controlled for but wasn't, the bias depends on:

| Component | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **β₂** | The effect of Z on the **outcome** |
| Covariation of **Z and X** | How strongly Z relates to the **treatment** |
| Variance of **X** | In the denominator |

So: **how strongly the omitted variable is related to the outcome, times how strongly it's related to
the treatment.**

**So why not just subtract it out?** Because you can't estimate either of those things — **you don't
have data on Z.** That's rather the point. It's an omitted variable.

**What you can do instead.** Think of **reasonable ranges** for those two values. With ranges, you can
**bound how large the bias might be.** You can't say exactly how large — if you could, you'd point
identify the estimate by subtracting it out. But you can say the bias is probably within this range,
or no larger than this amount.

Which gives you a **range of plausible estimates** — more informative than either giving up, or
assuming more than you can defend and announcing you have the right answer.

**Anchor image.** You can't weigh the stowaway, but you know the ship's displacement and you know what
a person weighs. You can bound how many there could be.

### Partial R²

**Plain English.** How much your treatment explains about the outcome, *after* everything your
controls already explained.

**Precisely.** **R²** is the share of variation in an outcome that's statistically explained by the
predictors — predict 50% of the variation and you get an R² of roughly 0.5.

**Partial R²** takes a step further:

1. Take one set of variables and use them to predict your outcome.
2. **Subtract that prediction out.**
3. Take a second set of variables and predict **what's left over — the residual**.
4. The **R² from that second analysis** is your partial R².

So it's the R² of how your treatment variable explains the outcome **after removing everything
explained by your other predictors**. That's what's *partial* about it — it takes only part of the
explained variation, the part remaining after the covariates are removed.

### The procedure

**Plain English.** Assume ranges for two partial R² values, compute the bias each implies, subtract.

**Precisely.** By working with a range of plausible partial R² values you get a range of estimates.
You might say the partial R² between the potential omitted variable and the **outcome** is between 0
and 0.3, and the partial R² between the omitted variable and the **treatment** is between 0 and 0.04
— to make up some numbers.

From those ranges you combine the values using the bias equation to calculate **exactly what the bias
would be** if those were in fact the true partial R² values. Subtract that bias out and you have the
**debiased estimate** that would follow from those assumptions.

In other words: **if there really were an omitted variable of a certain strength as a predictor, how
much would it mess up our estimate?**

### Worked example: gender and imprisonment in the UK

**Precisely.** A 2020 study by **Pina-Sanchez and Harris** looking at imprisonment in response to
crimes in the United Kingdom, asking whether there's a **gender disparity in lockup rates**: if you're
accused of a certain crime, what's your chance of actually going to jail — and does gender have a
causal effect on that?

Alongside regressing imprisonment on gender, they include **43 control variables** — socioeconomic
background, previous convictions, whether you have a child at home you're responsible for caring for,
and much more.

**The finding.** Men were more likely to be sentenced to prison for the same crime as a woman, after
controlling for all of that.

**The default estimate: 0.13.** For the particular set of crimes examined, men are **13 percentage
points** more likely to go to prison than women for the same crime, with the same controls.

**The question.** There are, of course, plenty of other things that might belong on that control list
and aren't there. So: if there really is some other control variable that should have been included
but wasn't — because you didn't measure it or didn't know about it — **would the result go away?**

### Benchmarking against the controls you already have

**Plain English.** "Reasonable" is hard to guess in the abstract — so compare to a variable you did
measure.

**Precisely.** You need a range of reasonable partial R² values, but what's reasonable? Use the
**existing control variables as benchmarks**.

You can ask: *the kind of drug you were caught with might affect whether you get sentenced, and might
also be related to gender somehow. So — if there were another variable out there that was as strong a
confounder as drug class, would that ruin things?* And you can answer, because you **have** data on
drug class, so you know how much bias adding that control removed. So ask what happens if there's
another variable explaining as much as that one.

**Reading the graph.** It shows what would happen if there were other, unobserved control variables as
strong as the ones displayed — would the 13-point effect go to zero?

**1× drug class.** Drug class doesn't actually explain much of the **treatment**: there wasn't a lot of
variation in the class of drug people were caught with **across gender**. Which means it won't produce
much bias — recall from the equation that bias combines the omitted variable's relation to the
treatment *and* to the outcome, so a weak relation to treatment means little bias. Drug class **is**
fairly strongly related to the probability of going to jail.

So if there were another omitted variable **one time as strong as drug class**:

| | Estimate |
|---|---|
| Original | **0.13** |
| With a confounder as strong as drug class | **0.08** |

The result is **reduced but not eliminated**. It doesn't reach zero, so it wouldn't overturn the gender
disparity finding.

**All 43 confounders' worth.** What if there were a whole set of omitted variables explaining as much
as the **entire set of 43 confounders** already controlled for? Then the effect **does** go away —
crossing the dashed zero line, meaning such a set would eliminate the effect entirely and potentially
**flip its sign**.

### The reframing

**Plain English.** The question isn't which scenario is true. It's how much you're willing to assume.

**Precisely.** Are you willing to assume you haven't missed a set of things **as strong as everything
you already included** — socioeconomic status, drug type, whether you have children at home? Those are
all fairly important. It seems **unlikely** you've omitted something as important as all of them
combined. Whether that's genuinely plausible is something you can consider and argue for in your own
research.

Is there something out there as strong as the **severity of the drug**? Maybe — that's believable. But
even if so, **it isn't enough to get rid of the result.**

So the graph tells you your result is **robust to certain kinds of omission but not to everything** —
which makes sense. And the remaining question is simply how many assumptions you're willing to make.

### A second view: extreme value analysis

**Plain English.** Suppose something explained the outcome *perfectly*. How gendered would it have to
be to erase your result?

**Precisely.** Instead of asking how strong potential omitted confounders are **relative to the ones
you already have**, ask a different question: what if there were something out there that **fully
explained the outcome**?

Something does determine whether you go to jail among people who are on trial. Suppose you had it —
suppose you could perfectly determine whether somebody goes to prison. **How strongly related to the
treatment would it need to be to eliminate the result?**

That's an **extreme value analysis**, and the graph **dips below the zero line pretty quickly**. Which
says that if you had something explaining **all** of why people go to prison, it would only need a
**slight gender difference** to fully explain away the result.

**Where it lands.** Again, judgement. Of course something out there explains why people go to prison
after being accused and going to trial. But is there really a gender difference in those things, after
already controlling for everything that's been controlled for? Perhaps it's reasonable to say it would
be below 0.01. Nick isn't sure.

**What both views share.** They're two different ways of asking the same thing: **what's a reasonable
amount of partial R² to assume**, because you simply need to bound it somehow. Once you have a range,
you can state exactly what range of plausible estimates follows — and say you believe it, because you
believe the assumptions that produced it.

Nick's verdict: a very valuable and powerful approach that lets you say things far more accurately
**without having to walk away from an analysis just because you can't run a quasi-experiment.**

## TRAPS

- Omitted variable bias is unknowable → **Actually,** its formula is known exactly. What's unknowable is the value of the two terms in it.
- If you knew the bias you could just subtract it → **Actually,** that's precisely why you can't: knowing it would give you point identification.
- A confounder related to the outcome will bias your estimate → **Actually,** it must be related to **both** outcome and treatment. Drug class barely varies by gender, so it produces little bias.
- Partial R² is just R² for one variable → **Actually,** it's the R² of predicting the residual left after your other predictors have already explained what they can.
- Cinelli and Hazlett tells you how big the bias is → **Actually,** it tells you what the estimate would become **if** the bias were of a given size. You supply the range.
- The sentencing result survived, so it's confirmed → **Actually,** it's robust to a confounder as strong as drug class and **not** robust to one as strong as all 43 controls combined.
- Extreme value analysis is a stricter version of benchmarking → **Actually,** it asks a different question — how gendered a perfect predictor of the outcome would have to be.

## RECALL

Q: Which assumption does this method weaken?
A: That you controlled for every back door path — that all confounders were identified and included.

Q: Why is that assumption usually false?
A: You either forgot a back door you didn't know about, or found one but couldn't measure it
convincingly.

Q: What's the nice property of omitted variable bias?
A: Its size is given by an exact formula in the regression analysis.

Q: What does that formula combine?
A: The effect of the omitted variable on the outcome, its covariation with the treatment, and the
variance of the treatment in the denominator.

Q: Why can't you simply subtract the bias out?
A: Because you have no data on the omitted variable, so you can't estimate either of the terms.

Q: What do you do instead?
A: Assume reasonable ranges for those terms, which lets you bound how large the bias might be and
produce a range of plausible estimates.

Q: Define partial R².
A: Predict the outcome with one set of variables, subtract that prediction out, then predict the
residual with a second set. The R² from that second analysis is the partial R².

Q: What does the Cinelli and Hazlett procedure do with partial R² values?
A: Takes a range of plausible values, computes exactly what the bias would be if those were true, and
reports the debiased estimates that follow.

Q: What did Pina-Sanchez and Harris study, and with how many controls?
A: Gender disparity in UK imprisonment rates — whether men are more likely to be sentenced to prison for
the same crime — using 43 control variables.

Q: What was the headline estimate?
A: 0.13 — men are 13 percentage points more likely to go to prison than women for the same crime.

Q: How do you decide what counts as a "reasonable" partial R²?
A: Benchmark against control variables you already have, asking what would happen if a missing
confounder were as strong as one you measured.

Q: Why does drug class produce relatively little bias?
A: Because it barely varies across gender — it's weakly related to the treatment — even though it's
fairly strongly related to going to jail.

Q: What happens with a confounder as strong as drug class?
A: The estimate falls from 0.13 to 0.08 — reduced, but not eliminated.

Q: What happens with a confounder as strong as all 43 controls combined?
A: The effect goes to zero and crosses the line, potentially flipping sign.

Q: Which of those is more plausible, and why does it matter?
A: A confounder as strong as drug class is believable, and it doesn't overturn the result. One as strong
as everything already included seems unlikely. So the result is robust to plausible omissions.

Q: What does extreme value analysis ask?
A: If something existed that fully explained the outcome, how strongly related to the treatment would it
need to be to eliminate the result?

Q: What did that analysis show here?
A: The line dips below zero quickly — a perfect predictor of imprisonment would need only a slight
gender difference to explain the result away.

Q: What do both approaches have in common?
A: Both are ways of deciding what range of partial R² to assume, since you need to bound it somehow
before you can state a range of plausible estimates.

## GLOSSARY

- **Cinelli and Hazlett (2020)** — a method for bounding omitted variable bias in regression using partial R² values.
- **Partial R²** — the R² obtained when predicting the residual left after other predictors have explained what they can.
- **Benchmarking** — judging how strong a hypothetical omitted confounder would be by comparison to observed control variables.
- **Extreme value analysis** — asking how strongly a perfect predictor of the outcome would need to relate to the treatment in order to nullify a result.
- **Debiased estimate** — the estimate that would result after removing a hypothesised amount of omitted variable bias.
