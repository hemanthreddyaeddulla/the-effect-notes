## ONE SENTENCE

Four designs the book didn't cover — **synthetic control**, which builds a weighted blend of control
groups that matches your treated unit almost exactly before treatment; **matrix completion**, which
fills in the counterfactuals you never observed; **causal discovery**, which builds part of the
diagram *from data*; and **double machine learning**, which hands the prediction step to an
algorithm.

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

- **Synthetic control:** mix many control groups with estimated weights into one synthetic control that tracks the treated unit before treatment. Prior trends hold **by construction**.
- Needs **multiple control groups** and **lots of pre-treatment periods**. The Basque region study is the classic.
- **Matrix completion:** fill in the question marks — the untreated outcomes you never got to see — using regularized regression, iterating by time and by individual.
- **Causal discovery:** use data to rule out arrows and, via **colliders**, even orient some. Partially builds the diagram without theory.
- **Double machine learning:** the predict-and-subtract of controlling for variables, done with lasso or random forests instead of regression.
- Don't limit yourself to the stock designs just because they're the ones you've heard of.

## WHY THIS EXISTS

There's nothing so unique about the designs covered so far that they can't be replaced. Plenty of
other methods exist — some more popular, some less, some newer, some older. Some overlap with what
you've seen; some are completely different. This is a rundown rather than a deep dive; the chapter
points you toward the full technical treatment.

## IDEAS

### Synthetic control

**Plain English.** Instead of picking a control group, build one out of a weighted blend of all the
candidates — chosen so it matches your treated unit's history almost perfectly.

**Precisely.** The closest thing this book came to giving another chapter. It applies where
difference-in-differences would: a treated group, a before and after period, and a set of control
groups — **plural**, since you need several.

**How it differs from difference-in-differences.** Difference-in-differences literally compares the
before-and-after change in the treated group against the control group. Synthetic control asks two
further questions: **which of all the possible control groups make most sense**, and rather than a
plain comparison, it **chooses a mix** — mashing several controls together using **estimated weights**
to create a single **synthetic control group**.

It picks those weights deterministically, by a process designed to make the comparison as clean as
possible, and it even uses the **pre-treatment outcome variable as a matching variable**. Which means
you end up **forcing prior trends to hold almost by design.**

**Anchor image.** You can't find one person the same height as your subject, so you take a weighted
average of several people until the average height matches exactly.

**Reading the graph.** In the pre-treatment period the treated line and the synthetic control line
follow each other **almost exactly**. That isn't luck in the data — it was **forced**. And having
pushed those two before-lines together, you might expect them to have continued sticking together
afterwards absent treatment. So any divergence afterwards is your effect.

**Worked example — the Basque region.** One of the very first implementations, back in **2003**.

There was a period of substantial additional violence in Spain due to sectarian differences, and the
question was: **what was the effect of that violence on economic growth?**

- **Treated group:** the Basque region, from the point the violence began in large amounts.
- **Control groups:** all the other regions of Spain.

Some Spanish regions are more comparable to the Basque region than others. So synthetic control takes
all the other regions and, based on their characteristics **and** their pre-treatment outcomes,
constructs weights so that averaging them together produces a line looking very much like the Basque
region's. In the pre-treatment period the two lines track closely — **something
difference-in-differences could only dream of.**

**A built-in test.** The violence began in **1970** but didn't really kick into gear for a few extra
years. Using 1970 as the treatment date, the lines **continue to stick together for those extra
years** before the violence ramps up. Which is exactly what you'd hope: you claimed they'd stay
together absent treatment, and here are a few years where treatment is effectively still absent, and
they do.

**Then they diverge** — so the violence really did reduce economic growth in the Basque region.

**Where it fits.** There's a lot to like: ways to check assumption plausibility that aren't available
in difference-in-differences, and **randomization inference** for standard errors. It's worth looking
into whenever difference-in-differences makes sense — **provided** you have multiple control groups
to synthesise from, and **a lot of pre-treatment data**, since you need many pre-treatment periods to
make the match clean.

### Matrix completion

**Plain English.** Fill in the outcomes you never observed, then compare against them.

**Precisely.** From machine learning. It applies to binary treatments that turn on or off for
different people — a panel setting like difference-in-differences — except the treatment can turn on
or off at **different points in time for different groups**. Very flexible.

**The problem.** You want to say: I see you're being treated right now, so your treatment effect is
what's happening to you now compared to what it would have looked like untreated. But you **can't
observe that counterfactual** — what some people call the **fundamental problem of causal inference**.

**What it does.** You have a lot of data, so try to **calculate** what the counterfactual would have
been. Take two matrices of outcome data: one for people **not currently treated**, and one for people
**currently treated**. Both are full of **question marks** where you didn't observe someone.

**Worked example.** Individual 2 in time period 1 has a question mark in the **untreated** matrix and
a value in the **treated** matrix. That tells you individual 2 was treated in period 1, so you don't
know what their outcome would have been untreated.

Matrix completion **fills in the question marks** in the untreated matrix, giving you something to
compare your treated data against. It uses **regularized regression** — a machine learning tool —
iteratively. First it works by time: *I'm predicting a value in period 1, so I'll use the other data I
have from period 1.* Then it works the other way: *I'm looking at individual 2, and I can see
individual 2 in other periods.* It goes back and forth, making the best prediction it can.

### Causal discovery

**Plain English.** Use the data itself to rule out arrows — and, using colliders, even point some of
them.

**Precisely.** This one is surprising, because it cuts against the running theme. Your causal
assumptions should come from theory, and there isn't much data can do to make or even validate them —
it's rare that a genuine assumption about something unobservable can be validated in data.

**That isn't always true.** Causal discovery says: write down a list of variables you think are
relevant, use data to test all the relationships between them, and thereby rule out certain causal
relationships — **partially building the causal diagram from the results.**

**Worked example.** Four variables — A, B, C, and D — and you start knowing nothing, so put a blank,
headless line between all four.

**Step 1 — delete an arrow.** Look in the data and find that **A and B are completely unrelated** — or
that there is a relationship, but **controlling for D makes it disappear entirely**. In either case
there's no *direct* arrow between A and B. Cross it out.

**Step 2 — orient arrows using colliders.** Now look at the relationship between A and B while
**controlling for C**. If that non-relationship suddenly becomes a **non-zero relationship**, that
tells you **C must be a collider** on the pathway between A and B — so **A → C** and **B → C**.
Because that is the only way controlling for C could create a relationship that wasn't there before.

From just those steps you've recovered part of the diagram, using nothing but the data beyond your
choice of variables. You may not get further — you might never work out D's relationships — but you've
done real work.

**Where it breaks.** It isn't a free lunch. Much of the time you won't get all the way to an answer.
And **you need a definition of what "unrelated" means**, which is an important and unclear part of the
procedure. Is it statistical insignificance? Nick doesn't think that's a great way to go about it.

### Double machine learning

**Plain English.** Controlling for variables is a prediction problem. Give the prediction to a
machine learning algorithm.

**Precisely.** Also called double de-biasing. Recall how controlling for a variable was first
introduced — as a conditional conditional mean: take your control variables, **predict the treatment**
as best you can, and subtract the prediction out; then see what you can predict of the **outcome**
with those controls, and subtract that out too.

Do that prediction using regression and you get **exactly the same results** as just running a
regression with the controls in it.

**But you don't have to use regression to do the prediction.** This is a **predictive problem**, and
prediction is what machine learning is genuinely excellent at. Machine learning has real difficulty
with causal inference — there are ways to use it, but it's hard. So if you can **separate out the part
of the causal inference process that is a prediction problem**, you can hand that piece to machine
learning, which will do it very well.

That's all double machine learning is: the same predict-and-subtract on both treatment and outcome,
performed with a machine learning algorithm — **lasso**, a **random forest**, perhaps a neural network
("I don't think I've ever seen that one, but you could"). The only difference from regression is the
predictive procedure.

**What it buys you.** Controlling for a great many control variables at once, or fitting a non-linear
functional form really well.

### The closing note

Don't limit yourself to the stock designs just because they're the ones you've heard of. If your
research question doesn't lend itself to one of them, there may be a design out there that suits it
better.

## TRAPS

- Synthetic control is difference-in-differences with extra steps → **Actually,** it constructs a weighted blend of control groups and forces pre-treatment fit, which difference-in-differences can't do.
- The matching pre-treatment lines in a synthetic control graph show the method worked → **Actually,** that fit was imposed by construction. The test is whether they *stay* together where treatment is still absent.
- Synthetic control works wherever difference-in-differences does → **Actually,** it needs several control groups and many pre-treatment periods.
- Matrix completion estimates the treatment effect directly → **Actually,** it fills in the missing untreated outcomes so you have something to compare against.
- Causal discovery means you don't need theory → **Actually,** you still choose the variable list, you often can't finish the diagram, and "unrelated" needs defining.
- Controlling for C can only remove relationships → **Actually,** if C is a collider, controlling for it *creates* one — which is exactly what causal discovery exploits.
- Double machine learning is a causal method → **Actually,** it's the same controlling procedure with the prediction step outsourced. The causal logic is unchanged.

## RECALL

Q: What does synthetic control need that difference-in-differences doesn't?
A: Multiple control groups to blend, and a lot of pre-treatment periods to make the match clean.

Q: How does synthetic control construct its comparison?
A: It estimates weights over many control groups and mashes them together into a single synthetic
control group.

Q: What does it use as a matching variable, and what's the consequence?
A: The pre-treatment outcome variable — so prior trends are forced to hold almost by design.

Q: Why isn't the tight pre-treatment fit evidence that the method worked?
A: Because it was imposed by construction rather than found in the data.

Q: What was the Basque region study asking, and when was it done?
A: The effect of sectarian violence on economic growth, in one of the first synthetic control
implementations, in 2003.

Q: What made the timing of the violence a useful test?
A: It began in 1970 but didn't ramp up for a few years — and the lines stayed together over those
years, exactly as the method assumes they would absent real treatment.

Q: What can synthetic control offer for standard errors?
A: Randomization inference.

Q: What problem does matrix completion address?
A: The fundamental problem of causal inference — you can't observe what a treated unit's outcome would
have been untreated.

Q: What do the question marks in the matrices represent?
A: Observations you didn't get to see — for instance, someone treated in period 1 has no untreated
outcome recorded for that period.

Q: How does matrix completion fill them in?
A: With regularized regression applied iteratively — predicting from other observations in the same
time period, then from the same individual in other periods, going back and forth.

Q: What does causal discovery claim to do?
A: Use data on the relationships between a chosen list of variables to rule out causal relationships and
partially construct the causal diagram.

Q: How do you delete an arrow between A and B?
A: Find that they're unrelated, or that controlling for D makes their relationship disappear entirely.

Q: How can a collider help you orient arrows?
A: If controlling for C creates a relationship between A and B where there was none, C must be a
collider — so A → C and B → C.

Q: Give two limitations of causal discovery.
A: You often can't recover the full diagram, and you need a definition of "unrelated" — which Nick
doubts should be statistical insignificance.

Q: What is double machine learning doing?
A: The same predict-and-subtract used when controlling for variables, but with a machine learning
algorithm doing the prediction.

Q: Why is that a sensible division of labour?
A: Machine learning struggles with causal inference but is excellent at prediction, and the controlling
step is purely a prediction problem.

Q: Name algorithms used for it, and what it buys you.
A: Lasso, random forests, potentially neural networks — allowing you to control for many variables at
once or fit non-linear functional forms well.

## GLOSSARY

- **Synthetic control** — constructing a weighted blend of control groups that matches the treated unit's pre-treatment outcomes.
- **Randomization inference** — a method of obtaining standard errors used with synthetic control.
- **Matrix completion** — filling in unobserved counterfactual outcomes using regularized regression.
- **Fundamental problem of causal inference** — that you can never observe the same unit both treated and untreated.
- **Causal discovery** — inferring parts of a causal diagram from patterns of conditional relationships in data.
- **Double machine learning** — controlling for variables by predicting treatment and outcome with machine learning and subtracting the predictions out.
