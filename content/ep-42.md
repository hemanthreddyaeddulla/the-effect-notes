## ONE SENTENCE

Generate your own data with a random number generator, run your method on it, and check whether
you get back the answer you built in — because you **chose the truth**, which almost never happens
with real data.

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

- **Simulation** = generate data yourself, apply your method, look at the result.
- Reason 1: find out **whether a method works**, without writing a proof.
- Reason 2: find out **when it breaks**, and how badly.
- Reason 3 — the big one: **you pick the truth.** You know what the right answer is, so you know whether the method found it.
- Method: start with data satisfying every assumption, confirm it works, then **break things one at a time**.
- Some broken assumptions barely matter. Some ruin everything. Simulation is how you tell which.
- The book has code in three languages; the videos stay generic.

## WHY THIS EXISTS

Simulation is an important tool to have in the applied statistician's work belt — and it answers a
question that's been hanging over this whole series. You've been told a lot about what certain
methods do and don't do, when they perform well and when they don't.

**How would you check any of that for yourself?**

You could write a statistical proof, working out the estimator's properties in mathematical terms.
That sounds very difficult, and it isn't especially intuitive — you can reach the answer and still
not understand it on a gut level unless you genuinely think in maths. Simulation is something you
can do yourself.

## IDEAS

### What simulation is

**Plain English.** Make up data on purpose, run your method on it, see what comes out.

**Precisely.** Generate your own data, typically using some kind of random number generator, then
apply your methods to that data and look at the result.

### Reason 1 and 2: does it work, and when does it break?

**Plain English.** Not just "is this method good" but "how wrong does it go when I violate its
assumptions?"

**Worked example.** You've been told regression gives you the causal estimate **if** you've
controlled for everything needed to remove omitted variable bias. But what if you haven't?

- How bad is the bias going to be?
- Is it worse or better if the omitted variable is **strongly** related? **Weakly**? **Negatively**? **Positively**?
- What if it's more related to the **treatment** than the outcome? More related to the **outcome** than the treatment?

Every one of those is a question simulation can answer.

### Reason 3: you pick the truth

**Plain English.** You built the data, so you know the right answer. Real data never tells you that.

**Precisely.** Because you're generating the data yourself, **you know what the right answer is** —
which is rarely the case with actual data. So when you apply your method, you know what it *should*
return if it's working properly. If you get something else, you know either the method is broken or
the data you gave it isn't a good setting to apply that method in.

**Anchor image.** Calibrating a thermometer in ice water. You already know it's 0°C — that's the
entire point.

### The method: start clean, then break things

**Plain English.** Confirm it works when everything is perfect, then sabotage one thing at a time.

**Precisely.** Start with a dataset that satisfies **all** the assumptions the estimator has —
the model is linear, you've controlled for everything — and check that it works there. If it does,
**start breaking things one at a time.** Make one of the true model features non-linear, and see
whether a linear model is really wrong, or whether it seems to work okay anyway.

**Where it breaks — in both directions.** There are a lot of cases in statistics where you can break
an assumption and it makes things wrong, but **only by a little bit**. Simulation is a good way to
ferret those out. There are also cases where breaking something messes things up **a really great
deal**, and simulation finds those too.

**Nick's own practice.** He runs a simulation whenever he faces a new method he isn't sure about, or
just wants to try something out. Sometimes he has an idea for an estimator and doesn't want to do
all the work of writing a proof — so a simulation is a first attempt to see whether it seems to work
at all. He does this quite regularly.

### A note on code

Simulation is inherently something that involves a lot of coding, and these videos have deliberately
avoided code. The book contains all the coding examples — in **three different languages**, which is
why the videos don't reproduce them. For the actual implementation, go to Chapter 15 and find your
preferred language.

## TRAPS

- Simulation is for people who can't do the maths → **Actually,** it's a legitimate alternative to a proof, and it's what Nick reaches for before committing to writing one.
- The point of simulation is to test a method on realistic data → **Actually,** the point is that you *chose* the truth, so you can tell whether the method recovered it.
- If a method fails on simulated data, the method is bad → **Actually,** it may instead mean that data isn't a good setting for that method. Both are worth knowing.
- Breaking an assumption invalidates a method → **Actually,** plenty of violations make results wrong only slightly. Finding out which is exactly what simulation is for.
- Start by simulating the messy case you actually care about → **Actually,** start where every assumption holds and confirm the method works, then break things one at a time.

## RECALL

Q: What is simulation, in this context?
A: Generating your own data — typically with a random number generator — applying your methods to it,
and seeing what result you get.

Q: What's the alternative to simulation for checking an estimator's properties, and why is it worse?
A: Writing a statistical proof. It's very difficult, and unless you think in maths it can be hard to
understand the answer on a gut level.

Q: Give the three reasons to run a simulation.
A: To find out whether a method works, to find out when and how badly it breaks, and because you get
to pick the truth so you know what the right answer is.

Q: Why is knowing the truth so valuable?
A: Because with real data you almost never know it. Knowing it means you can tell whether your method
returned the right answer.

Q: Your method returns the wrong answer on simulated data. What are the two possible conclusions?
A: The method is broken, or the data you gave it isn't a good setting in which to apply that method.

Q: Give examples of questions you could ask about omitted variable bias by simulation.
A: How bad is the bias when you fail to control for something — and is it worse or better if the
omitted variable is strongly or weakly related, negatively or positively related, or more related to
the treatment than the outcome?

Q: What's the recommended procedure for building a simulation study?
A: Start with data satisfying all the estimator's assumptions and confirm it works there, then break
things one at a time and see what happens.

Q: What are the two kinds of finding you're hunting for?
A: Assumptions you can break with only a little damage, and assumptions whose violation ruins the
results.

Q: When does Nick personally run a simulation?
A: Whenever he faces a new method he isn't sure about, or has an idea for an estimator and doesn't
want to do the work of writing a proof — as a first check on whether it seems to work.

Q: Why do these videos avoid simulation code?
A: The book contains the coding examples in three different languages, and he wasn't going to record

## GLOSSARY

- **Simulation** — generating data yourself with known properties and applying a method to it to test how the method behaves.
- **Random number generator** — the tool used to create simulated data with chosen statistical properties.
- **The truth** — the parameter value you deliberately built into your simulated data, against which the estimate is judged.
