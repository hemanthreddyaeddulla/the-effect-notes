## ONE SENTENCE

Two variables are related exactly when learning the value of one **changes the
distribution** of the other — so describing a relationship is nothing more than
describing how one variable's distribution shifts as you move across values of another.

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

- **Conditioning** means holding one variable at a particular value and asking what the other variable's distribution looks like there.
- If the conditional distribution changes as you move across values, the variables are **related**. If it doesn't change at all, they're **independent**.
- This works for every kind of variable: categorical distributions shift, density curves shift.
- Since you already know how to summarize a distribution, you get **conditional means** — and conditional medians, standard deviations, skews, whatever you like — for free.
- Conditioning on a *continuous* variable is awkward, because almost nobody shares an exact value. Binning is the crude fix; line-fitting is the good one, next episode.
- None of this is causal yet. It's description only.

## WHY THIS EXISTS

Causal inference is about the effect of one variable on another — which means, before
anything else, it's about the *relationship* between two variables. You can't ask which
part of a relationship is causal until you can say what the relationship is.

And the good news is you already have the machinery. Everything from the last three
episodes — distributions, densities, means, medians, spread — carries over untouched.
You just apply it one slice at a time.

## IDEAS

### Conditioning

**Plain English.** Hold one variable fixed at some value, and look at what the other
variable does *there*.

**Precisely.** To condition on a variable is to restrict attention to observations at a
particular value of it, and examine the distribution of a second variable within that
restriction. That's a **conditional distribution**.

**Anchor image.** A deck of cards face down: any card is equally likely. Someone tells
you it's a red card. You haven't seen the card — but the distribution over what it
could be just collapsed to half of what it was. That collapse *is* conditioning.

**Worked example.** The distribution of babies' sex is roughly 50/50. You're told
you'll meet a stranger; your best guess about their sex is 50/50.

Now someone mentions the stranger is named Sarah. Your distribution shifts hard —
maybe 95% female, maybe higher. Conditional on the name being Sarah, the distribution
of sex is nothing like 50/50.

That shift is the entire definition. Learning about one variable told you something
about another. **That is what it means for two variables to be related.**

### Independence is the case where nothing shifts

**Plain English.** If learning one variable's value leaves the other's distribution
exactly as it was, they're unrelated.

**Precisely.** Two variables are **independent** when conditioning on one produces no
change in the distribution of the other.

**Worked example.** A six-sided die: each face has a 1/6 chance. You roll and get a 1.
What does that tell you about the next roll? Nothing. It's still 1/6 for every face.
The conditional distribution equals the unconditional one, so the rolls are
independent.

Contrast: names and sex. "Sarah" gives you one distribution, "Chris" gives you a
different one. Different conditional distributions across values means the variables
are related.

### It works the same for every variable type

**Plain English.** Categorical or continuous, you're doing the identical thing — look
at the distribution, then look at it again inside each slice.

**Worked example.** Vitamin E supplements. Vitamin E wasn't recommended, then was
recommended by health professionals for a while, then wasn't again. That history makes
it interesting: while it was being recommended, the people who took it up were plausibly
the people who follow health advice in general — so vitamin E would *look* related to
all sorts of healthy outcomes, when really it's healthy people choosing to take it.
This is the setup behind a study by Emily Oster.

Start with the unconditional distribution: about **15% of people take vitamin E**, 85%
don't.

Now condition on smoking. Among smokers, the share taking vitamin E is **lower** than
among non-smokers. Two different conditional distributions — one for smokers, one for
non-smokers — so vitamin E use is related to smoking.

Now the continuous version: instead of *whether* you took vitamin E, look at *how much*,
which is continuous, and condition on whether you exercised vigorously in the last month.
The density curve shifts a little left or right depending on which group you're in. Same
conclusion, reached by comparing densities rather than proportions.

**Where it breaks.** All of this is description. You've established that vitamin E use and
smoking are statistically related. You have said nothing about which causes which, or
whether either does.

### Conditional means (and everything else)

**Plain English.** Once you have a distribution per slice, summarize each slice the way
you'd summarize any distribution.

**Precisely.** Apply any summary statistic within each conditional distribution and compare
across slices. Conditional means are by far the most common — most of causal inference is
comparing conditional means — but conditional medians, standard deviations, skews, and
interquartile ranges are all equally available.

**Anchor image.** You already own the measuring tape. Conditioning just means measuring each
room instead of the whole house.

**Worked example.** "Is the average amount of vitamin E people take different depending on
whether they exercise vigorously?" is a comparison of two conditional means. Useful detail:
for a binary variable, the **proportion is the mean** — so "15% take vitamin E" is already a
conditional mean when you compute it within a group.

### Conditioning on a continuous variable

**Plain English.** You can't hold a continuous variable at an exact value, because
essentially nobody shares one.

**Precisely.** Conditioning on, say, exactly 0.1376 grams of vitamin E gives you a slice
containing maybe one person, maybe none. The crude fix is to **bin** the continuous variable —
the same trick that turned a continuous variable into a histogram — and compute a conditional
mean within each bin.

**Worked example.** Bin people by BMI, and within each bin compute the proportion taking
vitamin E. Tabulate it, then graph it: conditional mean on the y-axis, BMI bin on the x-axis.
You can now see how vitamin E use moves with BMI.

```dag
node B 0 0 "BMI (binned)"
node V 1.6 0 "Share taking vitamin E" out
edge B V "conditional mean within each bin"
```

**Where it breaks.** Binning is arbitrary. Where do the cuts go? How many bins? Different
choices give different pictures, and nothing in the data tells you which is right. That
arbitrariness is exactly what line-fitting exists to replace.

## TRAPS

- The vitamin E and smoking distributions differ, so smoking must affect vitamin E use → **Actually,** all you've shown is a statistical relationship. Direction and causality are untouched at this stage.
- Conditional distributions are a special technique for relationships → **Actually,** they're the same distributions from Eps 3–5, computed inside a slice. There's no new machinery.
- Two variables are related if their *means* differ across groups → **Actually,** relatedness is about the whole distribution shifting. Means can coincide while the distributions differ in spread or shape.
- Binning a continuous variable is a neutral way to condition → **Actually,** the cut points and bin count are arbitrary choices that change the picture. It's a stopgap.
- Independence means the variables have nothing to do with each other in the world → **Actually,** it's a precise statistical statement: conditioning on one produces literally no change in the other's distribution.

## SAY IT OUT LOUD

> Here's a definition that made relationships between variables click for me.
>
> Think about the sex of a baby — roughly fifty-fifty. Now suppose I tell you you're about
> to meet someone, and their name is **Sarah**. Your fifty-fifty just became something like
> ninety-five percent female. You haven't met them. You learned one thing, and the
> distribution of a *completely different* variable moved.
>
> That's it. That's what it means for two variables to be related: learning about one
> changes the distribution of the other. It's called **conditioning** — you condition on
> the name, and you get a conditional distribution of sex.
>
> And the opposite tells you when things are unrelated. Roll a die, get a one. What does
> that tell you about the next roll? Nothing at all — still one in six for every face. The
> conditional distribution is identical to the unconditional one, so the rolls are
> independent.
>
> The nice part is you already know how to summarise a distribution — mean, median,
> spread. So you just do that inside each slice and compare. About fifteen percent of
> people take vitamin E overall; condition on smoking and the rate among smokers is lower
> than among non-smokers. Different conditional distributions, therefore related.
>
> One catch. Conditioning on something *continuous* is awkward, because nobody shares an
> exact value — you can't find the group of people who take exactly 0.1376 grams. You can

> line-fitting solves.
>
> And the thing to hold onto: none of this is causal. We've shown vitamin E use and smoking
> are related. We have not said a word about what causes what.

## RECALL

Q: What does it mean to condition on a variable?
A: To hold it at a particular value and look at the distribution of another variable within
that restriction.

Q: State the definition of two variables being related.
A: Learning the value of one changes the distribution of the other.

Q: What's the definition of independence?
A: Conditioning on one variable produces no change at all in the other's distribution — the
conditional and unconditional distributions are the same.

Q: Sex is ~50/50. You learn a stranger's name is Sarah. What has happened, in these terms?
A: You conditioned on name, and the conditional distribution of sex shifted to roughly 95%
female. The shift is what makes name and sex related.

Q: You roll a die and get a 1. Why does this demonstrate independence?
A: Because the distribution of the next roll is still 1/6 for every face — conditioning
changed nothing.

Q: About 15% take vitamin E. Among smokers the share is lower. What have you established, and what haven't you?
A: Established: vitamin E use and smoking are statistically related. Not established:
anything about causation or direction.

Q: Why is vitamin E a suggestive example in the first place?
A: It was recommended by health professionals only for a stretch, so uptake plausibly
tracked people who follow health advice generally — making it look related to healthy
outcomes because healthy people chose to take it.

Q: How do you condition on a continuous variable, and what's wrong with that?
A: Bin it and compute a conditional mean within each bin — as with BMI bins versus share
taking vitamin E. The problem is the bins are arbitrary in both placement and number.

Q: Why is "proportion" already a conditional mean for a binary variable?
A: Because the mean of a binary variable *is* its proportion, so computing a share within a
group is computing a conditional mean.

Q: Name three conditional summaries besides the conditional mean.
A: Conditional median, conditional standard deviation, conditional skew, conditional
interquartile range — any summary statistic works within a slice.

## GLOSSARY

- **Conditioning** — restricting attention to observations at a particular value of a variable.
- **Conditional distribution** — the distribution of one variable among observations sharing a value of another.
- **Independence** — the case where conditioning on one variable leaves the other's distribution unchanged.
- **Conditional mean** — the mean of one variable computed within a value or bin of another.
- **Binning** — grouping a continuous variable into ranges so it can be conditioned on.
