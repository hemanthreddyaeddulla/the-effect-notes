## ONE SENTENCE

Everything your model leaves out doesn't vanish — it goes into the **error term**, and
"close all the back doors" translated into regression means exactly one thing: **make your
treatment unrelated to the error term**, because if it isn't, OLS hands your treatment the
credit for whatever is hiding in there.

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

- **Residual** = actual minus predicted, for a data point. OLS picks the line making the squared residuals smallest.
- **Error term (ε)** = everything in the data generating process your *model* left out. Different concept.
- The error term is **not noise**. It's real determinants of your outcome — parents' wealth, lottery wins — that simply aren't in your equation.
- Adding a control variable **moves it out of the error term into the model**. That's what controlling *is*, in regression.
- The translation: **closing all back doors ≈ making X unrelated to the error term.**
- If X *is* related to it: **omitted variable bias**, X is **endogenous**, the effect isn't identified — all the same statement.
- Why it biases: OLS can't see Z, but it sees X hanging around while Z does good things. So it credits X.

## WHY THIS EXISTS

Part 2 of the book starts here — more technical, more about applying tools rather than
thinking about identification in the abstract. And it starts with regression, because
regression is enormously important and enormously common in causal inference.

You already met it in Chapter 4. Regression estimates a conditional relationship by picking a
shape — often a straight line — and fitting the best version of that shape. You can go further
than a line into a plane in 3D space by adding control variables, and that easy flexibility to
add controls is a big part of why it's so popular: it lets you isolate just the part of the X–Y
relationship not explained by the control.

So what's left to say? This: you've been focusing on the line you fit. Now focus on **what you
left out**.

## IDEAS

### Residual vs. error — two different things

**Plain English.** A residual is how wrong you were about one point. The error term is what your
whole model ignores about the world.

**Precisely.**

- **Residual** — the difference between your prediction and the actual value, written as actual minus prediction. Some points sit above the line (reality was higher than you predicted), some below. Any prediction method has residuals, and they arise for many reasons: things left out, sampling variation, whatever.
- **Error term** — the difference between what your model would predict *if it had all the data in the world and you had the best possible version of that estimated model*, and what would still be left over. It captures only one thing: what's in your model versus what's still missing from it.

**Anchor image.** A weather forecast. The residual is that it said 20° and it was 22°. The error
term is that the model never looks at the ocean.

**Worked example.** Predicting someone's wealth from their income. Higher-paying job, more wealth
accumulated — probably a strong relationship.

- **Residual:** you predict wealth from income, and sometimes you're a bit high, sometimes a bit low, for any individual.
- **Error term:** if income is the *only* thing you use, then everything else that predicts wealth — your parents' wealth, winning the lottery — is not in your model. It's still real, it still determines the outcome, and it has to go somewhere. That somewhere is the error term.

**Where it breaks — this is the important bit.** The error term is **not just random noise**.
Writing the data generating process as

```
y = β₀ + β₁x + ε
```

makes two claims: that x is in the data generating process for y, and that there's **other
stuff** too. If you're predicting wealth from income, it isn't that everything besides income is
noise — you can name several of those things, and they're real determinants of wealth.

### The linguistic jump: controlling = moving things out of the error term

**Plain English.** A control variable doesn't create information. It relocates it.

**Precisely.** Start with `y = β₀ + β₁x + ε`. If z is part of the data generating process for y —
if z was used in the creation of y — then **z is already in that first model**. You can't see it,
but everything determining y is in there somewhere, and if it isn't x, it's in ε.

Now add z as a predictor: `y = β₀ + β₁x + β₂z + ε`. You haven't discovered z. You've **moved z out
of the error term and into the model.**

**Anchor image.** A room where the mess is either on the shelves or under the bed. Putting
something on a shelf doesn't create it — it just stops being part of the unexamined pile.

**Why it matters:** this changes the relationship between x and the error term. If z and x are
related, then in the first model **x is related to the error term**. In the second, you've removed
the part of the error term x was related to — and maybe now x is unrelated to it.

**The jump.** We've spent chapters saying *close all the back doors to identify the effect of X on
Y*. In a regression context, that is very closely related to saying:

> **Make X unrelated to the error term.**

### Omitted variable bias, and its many names

**Plain English.** Leave out something correlated with your treatment, and your treatment absorbs
its effect.

**Precisely.** If x is related to the error term you have **omitted variable bias**. You may also
hear that x is **endogenous**. These, plus "the effect isn't causally identified" and "you haven't
closed all the back doors", are all largely the same statement.

The "omitted variable" part: you've omitted from your model a variable related to x. Because it's
omitted it lives in the error term, so x and the error term are related, and your estimate won't
reflect the actual causal effect of x on y.

**Anchor image.** A colleague who is always standing next to whoever fixes things. You never see
the fixer. You promote the colleague.

**Worked example — why the bias happens.** You estimate the relationship between y and x, and
there's a z out there. Suppose z is **positively related to x** and **positively related to y**,
but you fit the first model, so z sits in the error term.

Z is doing all sorts of positive things for y. OLS doesn't know z exists — you never told it. But
it *does* see that x happens to be around whenever all those positive things are happening.

**So it gives x the credit.**

That's omitted variable bias: OLS assigns z's effects to x, because it doesn't know any better.

```dag
node Z 1 0 "Z (in the error term)" unobs
node X 0 1 "X" treat
node Y 2 1 "Y" out
edge Z X
edge Z Y
edge X Y
note 1 1.8 "OLS can't see Z, so X collects the credit for Z's work"
```

**Where it breaks.** The coefficient on x will mix the effect of x on y together with all the
alternative explanations still sitting in the error term. It gives x the credit — or the blame —
for whatever those things are doing, as long as they're related to x.

### What this sets up

**Plain English.** Nearly everything in this chapter is about the error term in one way or
another.

**Precisely.** Several assumptions about the error term are coming. Understanding what's in it and
how it moves around matters for estimating **standard errors** and the **sampling distribution of
OLS coefficients**, as well as for relating a regression model to the causal identification you're
trying to achieve.

## TRAPS

- The error term is random noise → **Actually,** it contains real determinants of the outcome you can often name — parents' wealth, lottery winnings. Treating it as noise is the mistake.
- Residual and error term are two words for the same thing → **Actually,** the residual is prediction minus reality for a data point; the error term is what the model leaves out of the data generating process.
- If Z isn't in my model, Z isn't affecting my results → **Actually,** if Z determines Y then Z is in the model — hidden in the error term. Adding it as a control just moves it out.
- Adding controls gives the regression new information → **Actually,** it relocates information from the error term into the model, which is what changes X's relationship to the error term.
- Omitted variable bias, endogeneity, and unclosed back doors are three separate problems → **Actually,** they're largely the same statement in different vocabularies.
- Any omitted variable causes bias → **Actually,** it has to be **related to X**. Something omitted but unrelated to your treatment doesn't hand X its credit.

## RECALL

Q: Define a residual.
A: The difference between the actual value and your prediction for a data point — actual minus
prediction.

Q: Define the error term.
A: The difference between what your model would predict if it had all the data in the world and
the best possible estimated version of itself, and what would still be left out — everything in
the data generating process your model doesn't include.

Q: What does OLS minimise when choosing a line?
A: The squared residuals.

Q: You predict wealth from income. Give examples of what's in the error term.
A: Everything else that determines wealth but isn't in the model — your parents' wealth, winning
the lottery.

Q: Why is it wrong to think of the error term as noise?
A: Because it contains real predictors and determinants of the outcome that you can often name.
They're not random; they're just outside your equation.

Q: In `y = β₀ + β₁x + ε`, where is a variable Z that helps determine Y but isn't a predictor?
A: In the error term. Everything determining Y is in the model somewhere; if it isn't an explicit
predictor, it's in ε.

Q: What does adding Z as a control variable actually do?
A: It moves Z out of the error term and into the model itself.

Q: Why does that matter for identification?
A: Because it changes X's relationship to the error term. If Z and X are related, X was related to
the error term before; removing Z from it may leave X unrelated to what remains.

Q: State "close all the back doors" in regression language.
A: Make X unrelated to the error term.

Q: Name three ways of saying the same problem when X is related to the error term.
A: Omitted variable bias; X is endogenous; the effect isn't causally identified — equivalently,
you haven't closed all the back doors.

Q: Z is positively related to both X and Y and is left out. Explain the bias mechanism.
A: Z does positive things for Y. OLS can't see Z, but it sees that X is present whenever those
positive things happen, so it credits X with Z's effects.

Q: Does every omitted variable cause omitted variable bias?
A: No — it has to be related to X. Something omitted but unrelated to the treatment doesn't
transfer its effect to X's coefficient.

## GLOSSARY

- **Residual** — actual value minus predicted value for an observation.
- **Error term (ε)** — everything in the data generating process for the outcome that the model does not include.
- **Omitted variable bias** — bias arising when a variable related to the treatment is left in the error term, so the treatment absorbs its effect.
- **Endogenous** — a treatment variable that is related to the error term.
- **Ordinary least squares** — the line-fitting procedure that minimises squared residuals.
