## ONE SENTENCE

Two refinements soften the bandwidth and functional-form problems — **kernel weights**, which let
distant observations count a little rather than not at all, and **local regression**, which fits a
fresh line inside each small window and so builds the bandwidth in automatically while letting the
curve go anywhere.

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

- Both problems matter because you need the estimate **at the cutoff** to be as correct as possible.
- **Weights:** points closest to the cutoff count most, declining to zero further out — via a **kernel function**.
- **Triangular** and **Epanechnikov** kernels are the standard choices.
- Weights **split the difference**: more observations for precision, at reduced influence so they add less bias.
- **Local regression:** slide a window along the running variable, fitting a separate regression in each one.
- That builds the bandwidth in **automatically** and allows any shape of curve.
- Cost: **less precision** — and regression discontinuity is already precision-poor.
- Bonus: within a small window you barely need polynomials, because **zoom in far enough and everything looks linear**.

## WHY THIS EXISTS

The basic estimator left two problems open.

**Bandwidth.** How far outside the cutoff are you willing to look? Include a lot of data far from the
cutoff and you defeat the purpose of the design, which was that people **just** around the cutoff are
comparable apart from treatment. But limit the sample to just around the cutoff and you lose a great
many observations, with real problems for statistical precision.

**Functional form.** If the relationship between running variable and outcome is curvy, your line
should be curvy too.

Both are important, because **you need to get your estimate at the cutoff as correct as possible.**

## IDEAS

### Weights

**Plain English.** Don't let every observation count equally. The nearer the cutoff, the more it
counts.

**Precisely.** You don't have to let each point in your data count the same in estimation. Use
**weights** so some observations have more influence on the fitted line and others less.

The natural structure: **points closest to the cutoff count most.** If you're right next to the
cutoff, the comparison is essentially unbiased — you can put someone right on the other side against
you with no problem. As you move further away, you're less certain, then less certain still, and
eventually you don't think those people are comparable at all.

**The kernel function** puts exactly that idea into the analysis. A kernel is a function **centred
around zero**, typically **tallest at zero**, descending as you move away until it finally reaches an
actual weight of **zero**. Sit right at the cutoff and you get the biggest weight; move away and your
weight declines until you're far enough out that you don't count at all.

| Kernel | Shape |
|---|---|
| **Triangular** | Looks exactly like a triangle — straight-line decline |
| **Epanechnikov** | A bit more curved |

The basic idea is the same for all of them: closer to the cutoff means a bigger weight, declining to
zero as you move away.

**Anchor image.** A dimmer switch instead of an on/off switch. The far-away observations aren't
excluded — they're just turned down.

**What it buys you.** It lets you include a few more observations while reducing how much they
influence your bias. Limit the data to a narrow strip right around the cutoff and there aren't many
observations. Let everybody in and you may introduce bias you're unsure about. Weighting says: *these
people are okay, but only at a very reduced weight*, and puts most of the weight on the ones you're
really certain about.

That's the point — **splitting the difference** between the additional precision of allowing more
observations and the additional bias of allowing more observations. Best of both worlds, at least a
little.

### Local regression

**Plain English.** Don't fit one line per side. Slide a window along and fit a fresh line inside each
one.

**Precisely.** Instead of a single line on the left and a single line on the right, **local
regression** moves along the running variable taking a **little window of data at a time**. In each
window it fits a different regression — sometimes linear, sometimes polynomial — and produces a set of
predictions just for the data in that narrow range.

So the model can be curvy: here the slope looks flat, so put a flat line; move over a bit, it looks
steeper, so put a steeper line; steeper again after that. The slope changes **continuously**.

**It builds the bandwidth in automatically.** Think about what happens right next to the cutoff. You're
using **only observations in that little window** to estimate the regression at that point — so you're
automatically including a bandwidth, or a weight, depending on exactly how you construct the local
regression.

**The trade-off.** This allows **full curviness** — the fitted line can take any shape, which fits
nicely with what regression discontinuity is trying to do. But it is **less precise** than simply
fitting a regression. Allowing more flexibility in statistics almost always costs precision. And that's
a real problem, because with regression discontinuity **you're already in a low-precision zone**.

**The bonus — and the best image in the chapter.** By focusing on a small window at a time, you can use
a **more linear** approach. You don't need a pile of polynomials to fit a very curvy line — and you
probably **don't want** them, especially when using bandwidths.

Because **if you zoom in closely enough, pretty much everything looks linear.** You could have the
wildest curve imaginable, but look at just a tiny window and it's straight.

**Anchor image.** That's why the horizon looks like an almost straight line. It's a very zoomed-in
picture of a round Earth — you've simply zoomed far enough into a tiny portion of it.

## TRAPS

- Weighting is a way of excluding distant observations → **Actually,** it's the opposite: it lets you include them at reduced influence rather than dropping them entirely.
- The kernel choice determines whether the design works → **Actually,** triangular and Epanechnikov differ in shape, but all kernels do the same thing: most weight at the cutoff, declining to zero.
- Local regression is just a smoother-looking line → **Actually,** it also builds the bandwidth in automatically, since each local fit uses only nearby observations.
- Local regression is strictly better than fitting two lines → **Actually,** the added flexibility costs precision — and regression discontinuity is already precision-poor.
- More flexibility means you should add polynomials too → **Actually,** within a narrow window you barely need them, because everything looks linear when you zoom in far enough.
- These adjustments solve the bandwidth problem → **Actually,** they soften it. The bias–precision tension doesn't disappear.

## SAY IT OUT LOUD

> Two problems were left hanging: how wide to make your bandwidth, and whether your line should be
> straight. Both matter enormously, because everything depends on getting the estimate **right at the
> cutoff** correct.
>
> First fix: **weights**. Instead of a hard in-or-out bandwidth, let observations count *differently*.
> Someone right at the cutoff is an excellent comparison, so give them full weight. Someone further out
> is a bit more doubtful, so give them less. Far enough out, zero.
>
> That's a **kernel function** — tallest at the cutoff, tapering to nothing. A **triangular** kernel
> does it in a straight line, an **Epanechnikov** with a curve. And this is a genuinely nice compromise:
> instead of choosing between "too few observations" and "some bad comparisons," you say *fine, you can
> come in, but you barely count.*
>
> Second fix: **local regression**. Rather than one line on each side, you slide a little window along
> your data and fit a fresh regression inside each one. Flat here, so a flat line; steeper there, so a
> steeper line. The curve can go anywhere it likes.
>
> And notice this does the bandwidth work for free — when you're estimating right at the cutoff, you're
> only using observations from that tiny window anyway.
>
> The cost is precision. More flexibility always costs precision, and regression discontinuity is
> already short of it.
>
> But here's the lovely bit. Inside a small window you don't really need polynomials at all — because
> **zoom in far enough and everything looks straight.** That's exactly why the horizon looks like a flat
> line. It's a very zoomed-in photograph of a round planet.

## RECALL

Q: What two problems do these adjustments address?
A: How wide to make the bandwidth, and what functional form to use — both of which matter because the
estimate at the cutoff must be as accurate as possible.

Q: What's the basic idea behind weighting in regression discontinuity?
A: Let observations closest to the cutoff count most in estimating the line, with influence declining as
you move away.

Q: What is a kernel function?
A: A function centred around zero, typically tallest at zero, that descends as you move away until
reaching a weight of zero.

Q: Name two kernels and how they differ.
A: The triangular kernel declines in a straight line; the Epanechnikov kernel is more curved.

Q: What does weighting buy you over a hard bandwidth?
A: It lets you include more observations for precision while reducing their influence, splitting the
difference between precision and bias.

Q: Describe local regression.
A: Move along the running variable in small windows, fitting a separate regression inside each one and
producing predictions just for the data in that range.

Q: How does local regression handle curvature?
A: It lets the slope change continuously — flat where the data is flat, steeper where it's steeper — so
the fitted line can take any shape.

Q: Why does local regression incorporate the bandwidth automatically?
A: Because estimating the regression at any point uses only observations within that small window, which
is itself a bandwidth or weighting.

Q: What's the cost of local regression?
A: Less precision — more flexibility almost always costs precision, and regression discontinuity is
already precision-poor.

Q: Why do you need fewer polynomials with local regression?
A: Because within a small window, almost any curve looks linear — so a linear fit inside each window is
usually enough.

Q: Give the image for that.
A: The horizon looks like an almost straight line, because it's a very zoomed-in view of a round Earth.

## GLOSSARY

- **Kernel function** — a weighting function peaking at the cutoff and declining to zero with distance.
- **Triangular kernel** — a kernel whose weights decline in a straight line.
- **Epanechnikov kernel** — a curved kernel weighting nearby observations most heavily.
- **Local regression** — fitting separate regressions within sliding windows along the running variable.
