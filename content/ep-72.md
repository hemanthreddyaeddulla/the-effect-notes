## ONE SENTENCE

If your matching worked, you shouldn't be able to tell which member of a matched pair got treated —
so instead of assuming it's a perfect coin flip, assume it's **no worse than 60/40**, and ask how
badly mismatched your pairs would have to be before your result disappears.

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

- Matching's assumption: your matched pair is so alike that **you couldn't guess which one was treated**.
- **Rosenbaum bounds** weaken that: it needn't be exactly 50/50, just **no worse than** some bound — for **every** pair in your data.
- **Gamma (Γ)** is that bound, the worst mismatch you'll tolerate. It brackets the treatment probability between **Γ/(1+Γ)** and **1/(1+Γ)**.
- Procedure: raise Γ until you can no longer reject zero. Then ask whether you believe the mismatch could really be that bad.
- **UK sentencing:** the gender effect survives until **Γ ≈ 1.5** — meaning you'd have to distinguish a matched man and woman **more than 60% of the time** using everything you never observed.
- **Caveat:** Rosenbaum bounds only fit propensity score matching with **matched pairs** — which Chapter 14 told you not to do. Take the intuition; find another method to actually use.

## WHY THIS EXISTS

Matching rests on a strong assumption. You pick matching variables, find weights or matched pairs, and
then assume **you've closed all the back doors** — that you matched on the full set of variables needed,
so your matched pair is **perfectly matched**. If someone asked you to guess which of the two was
treated, you wouldn't be able to tell.

**Worked example — the propensity score version.** You have a treated observation with a propensity
score of **0.25**: a 25% chance of being treated, and they happened to be treated. You match them with
someone else whose propensity score is also **0.25**. Each had a 25% chance of treatment. So if you look
at the pair without knowing, **you genuinely can't tell which one it was.**

That's exactly what you want — it's essentially random which of the two is treated, and they're
comparable otherwise.

**But that's a strong assumption.** Probably you've left something out. And you don't want to respond by
saying *well, there's some bias left, I give up, I'm going home.* So go somewhere in the middle: accept
that you haven't perfectly removed all the back doors, then work out a reasonable range of assumptions
about how much bias remains — and get a **range** of estimates instead of a point estimate.

## IDEAS

### A caveat before starting

**Plain English.** This method only fits a setup the matching chapter told you to avoid.

**Precisely.** By far the most common way to do partial identification in matching is **Rosenbaum
bounds** — and there's a problem with them: they're very much designed for **one specific context**.
You're doing **propensity score matching** and you're **picking sets of matches** rather than doing
inverse probability weighting.

Which is exactly what Chapter 14 said not to do. If you're going to use propensity scores, use inverse
probability weighting; if you're going to pick matched pairs, don't use propensity scores to do it.

**But** Rosenbaum bounds are definitely the most common approach, they're **really intuitively good** for
conveying how this works, and a lot of the concepts carry through to other matching approaches. So:
**take the intuition from here**, and if you're actually going to do it, go to the chapter and look at
other sources for approaches that don't rely on picking sets of matches with propensity scores.

### The idea: how well could you guess?

**Plain English.** If matching worked, guessing which one was treated is a coin flip. If you can do
better than a coin flip, you have bias.

**Precisely.** You're working with matched pairs, and the idea is they're perfectly matched: no other
relevant information would tell you which one is treated. Someone could tell you everything else about
these two people and it wouldn't give you a clue.

**But maybe it would.** You matched on a bunch of things — but if someone told you **one new thing** about
them, perhaps that *would* tell you which one was treated. You might guess right **more than half the
time**. It's not a coin flip anymore.

If two people truly have the same propensity — 25% each — **you shouldn't be able to tell.** It should be
a complete coin flip.

**And if you can tell, you have bias.** Because if one of them really is more likely to be treated than
the other, you haven't actually matched well on propensity score. There's something left over —
especially damaging if the omitted thing is also related to the outcome, meaning it sits on a back door.

**Anchor image.** A blind taste test that works only if you genuinely can't tell the glasses apart. The
moment one has a chip in the rim, it isn't blind anymore.

### What Rosenbaum bounds do about it

**Plain English.** Don't assume it's exactly 50/50. Assume it's no worse than 60/40.

**Precisely.** Rosenbaum bounds say: I know it may not be exactly 50/50 — **but I'll put a bound on how
bad the mismatch might get.**

Where typical matching assumes **no bias at all** — zero deviation in the true propensity between the two
members of a pair — you instead say: *I don't think it's exactly 50/50, but I'm going to say it's **no
worse than 60/40**.* Or 55/45, or 70/30 — whatever bound you consider reasonable.

If it's no worse than that, you can still narrow down your treatment effect estimate. Not to a single
number — to a **range of reasonable values**, spanning the potential bias from zero up to the maximum
amount you're willing to consider.

**And note how demanding this is.** You're making that assumption for **every single pair in your data**.
If you say no worse than 60/40, you're claiming that for **literally every matched pair in your sample**
there is no additional information that would let anyone beat 60/40 at identifying which one was treated.

### Gamma

**Plain English.** One number for the worst mismatch you'll allow anywhere.

**Precisely.** The range of estimates is bounded by **gamma (Γ)** — the worst level of mismatch you're
going to allow among any pair in your data.

Set it up and you get a bound. The middle term involves **π_t**, the probability of treatment for the
member of the pair who **actually is treated**, and **π_u**, the probability of treatment for the member
who **is actually untreated**.

- If those two probabilities are **exactly the same**, that middle term works out to exactly **50%**.
- If the treated one was in fact somewhat more likely to be treated, it comes out above 50%.
- If the treated one was actually *less* likely to be treated, it comes out below 50%.

And you bound it between:

```
    Γ / (1 + Γ)      on the high end
    1 / (1 + Γ)      on the low end
```

Assume no mismatch worse than that, and you can bound your estimate. You're effectively saying: **there's
this much bias in there, I don't know which direction it runs, but it's no worse than this** — which
means my estimate has to lie in this range.

### How you actually use it

**Plain English.** Turn Γ up until your result dies, then ask whether things could really be that bad.

**Precisely.** Try a number of different gamma values and see **at what level the mismatch becomes so bad
that you can no longer reject zero** for your treatment effect.

You estimated an effect. You want to know **how bad the mismatch would have to be to get rid of it.** And
then ask yourself: **am I willing to believe the mismatch is no worse than that?**

### Worked example: gender and sentencing again

**Precisely.** The same setting as the previous episode — data on people accused of crimes, typically
drug crimes, in the United Kingdom. The question: is there a **gender bias** in the rate at which people
accused of crimes actually go to prison?

The matching variables include socioeconomic status, the kind of drug you're accused of working with,
and whether you have children at home to take care of.

**Why bias is plausible.** Some of these things might be related to gender. Within a matched pair, some
unobserved variable might tell you which one is the man and which the woman — which is a mismatch, since
you're trying to match men to similar women. And it might also relate to whether you go to prison. That
produces a biased result.

**The finding.** There **is** a gender bias: men are more likely to go to prison for the same crime than a
similar woman with similar levels of all those matching variables.

**But** something might be left out — and maybe the whole effect is just bias. **How much bias would there
need to be for it all to go away?**

**The answer: the effect goes away at roughly Γ = 1.5.** (The exact value depends on the matching
procedure you use; this is with the defaults set here.)

**What that actually means.** Run it through the equation and Γ = 1.5 works out to **not being able to
tell them apart more than 60% of the time.**

So: take a man matched with a woman who is similar on every characteristic you *do* observe. Then
magically learn about **all the stuff you never saw**.

| If that new information lets you tell them apart… | Then |
|---|---|
| **More than 60% of the time** | That's enough bias to explain **the entire effect** |
| **Only 55% of the time** | That would **reduce** your estimated effect, but **not take it to zero** |

That's the question being answered: **at what point does my effect go away? How robust is my result?**

### The closing reframing

**Plain English.** You don't have to claim perfection, and you don't have to give up. You just have to
say what you'd defend.

**Precisely.** With partial identification you ask **what assumptions you actually believe**. You don't
have to assume the pairs are exactly perfectly matched. You also don't have to say *they're not perfectly
matched, there's bias here, so I'm giving up and going home.*

Instead: **how well matched am I willing to claim they are?**

- Believe you couldn't tell them apart **more than 55% of the time**, even knowing everything unobserved → your original estimate is probably good to go, and there probably **is** gender bias in this sentencing procedure.
- Believe you could tell them apart **more often than that** → you can't demonstrate gender bias. There's simply too much bias in the estimate to distinguish it. **You can't see it.**

**Is that a reasonable assumption?** It depends — and you still have to argue for it. **But you at least
know what you're arguing about.** You no longer have to give up because some bias exists, and you no
longer have to make the probably-false assumption that you matched everybody perfectly with nothing left
over.

**It's just: what's the strongest argument you can make? What's the reasonable claim you can reach, based
on the things you think are actually true about the world? And that is the bonus of partial
identification.**

## GLOSSARY

- **Rosenbaum bounds** — a sensitivity analysis for matched-pair designs bounding how badly matched pairs could be before a result disappears.
- **Gamma (Γ)** — the parameter bounding the worst permitted mismatch between members of a matched pair.
- **Matched pair** — two observations, one treated and one not, selected as comparable.
- **Sensitivity analysis** — determining how strong an unobserved problem would need to be to overturn a finding.
