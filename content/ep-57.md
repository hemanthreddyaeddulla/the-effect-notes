## ONE SENTENCE

An instrumental variable is a randomized experiment you didn't run — you find something in the
world that randomly pushes people into treatment, and then do **the exact opposite of controlling
for a variable**: instead of subtracting out what it predicts, you **keep only** what it predicts.

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

- Everything so far closed back doors. This chapter **isolates the front door** instead.
- **The statue analogy:** carve away marble until the shape emerges (closing back doors), or pour metal into a **mould** so only the right shape can come out (finding a front door).
- IV is a randomized controlled trial where **you don't get to do the randomizing** — you find randomization already in the world.
- **Exogenous** means as good as random in the system you're studying — no back doors to the outcome.
- Mechanically: a control variable is **predict and subtract**. An instrument is **predict and keep**.
- Two assumptions: **relevance** (the instrument really predicts treatment) and **validity** (the instrument really is exogenous).
- IV doesn't remove the back-door problem. It **moves** it from a hard variable to an easier one.

## WHY THIS EXISTS

Every method so far — controlling for variables in regression, matching, fixed effects, event
studies, difference-in-differences — is about finding things on the back door, measuring them, and
cutting that variation out.

That isn't the only approach. Back in the finding-front-doors chapter, the alternative was to skip
closing back doors and simply **isolate the front door path you want**. That's what a randomized
controlled experiment does: take some data, randomize within it, and in *that* data the only path
that exists is the front door. You don't need to control anything away — you selected a part of the
data in which there is only a front door.

**Anchor image — two ways to make a statue.** You can take a big block of marble and **carve away**
at it until you find the statue inside — whittling away back doors until only the variation with a
causal interpretation is left. Or you can have a **mould** and pour molten iron into it, and the
exact shape you want comes straight out — because you only allowed variation of that shape in the
first place. That's finding a front door.

## IDEAS

### What an instrumental variable is

**Plain English.** A randomized experiment you didn't run.

**Precisely.** Instrumental variables is basically the same idea as running a randomized controlled
experiment, except **you don't get to randomize or control it**. You look for a form of randomization
that exists in the broader world and then isolate it statistically — and that gives you your causal
effect.

### Worked example: a trial where people don't comply

**Plain English.** You randomized who *should* take the drug. People did what they liked. Use the
assignment, not the taking.

**Worked example.** You run a randomized controlled experiment assigning people to get a drug or not.
But people don't always do what you tell them. Some assigned to take it don't — they forgot, or
didn't feel like it. And some assigned *not* to take it find their own way to get it: *you're running
a trial about a drug that might cure my disease, I'll go get it myself.*

So now there are two different variables:

| Variable | Random? |
|---|---|
| **Random assignment** to get the drug or not | **Yes** — you did it |
| **Whether you actually took the drug** | **No** — people chose whether to follow instructions |

The random assignment is a source of **exogenous variation**. Exogenous, in causal inference, means
it's **as good as random in the system you're looking at** — no back doors from it to your outcome,
so it might as well be random. Here it genuinely is random, but it doesn't have to be; there's just
some outside randomizing pressure that leads you toward treatment or away from it.

And the random assignment **affects** whether you took the drug. So random assignment is an
**instrumental variable for treatment**.

Treatment isn't completely randomly assigned — but a form of random assignment takes part in
determining it. **Isolate just that part**, and you've found the random portion of assignment. Focus
on that alone and you get the causal effect without worrying about back doors, because within the
part of the data you've isolated, there's random assignment to look at.

### The mechanism: the opposite of controlling

**Plain English.** Control variables subtract what they predict. Instruments keep it.

**Precisely.** With a **control variable**, you have a back door — an alternative explanation for why
treatment and outcome go together — so you see what you can predict with the control and **subtract
that out**.

With an **instrumental variable**, you have a source of random assignment to treatment, and that's
the *only* variation you want. So you do the exact same operation **backwards**: see what you can
predict with the instrument, and **keep only that**.

**Anchor image.** A sieve used two ways. One time you keep what falls through; the other time you keep
what stays behind.

### Worked example: the graphs

**Precisely.** A continuous treatment **x**, a continuous outcome **y**, and an instrument **z** that
for simplicity takes only two values, 0 and 1. Assume z is effectively randomized — a source of
exogenous variation, completely unrelated to y except that it helps determine how much treatment x
you get.

**The raw data.** There's a relationship between x and y, but it's largely a random cloud. The z = 0
and z = 1 clusters don't really overlap: the hollow circles sit toward the bottom right, the solid
ones toward the top left. So z is clearly related to x, and it appears related to y — but the
assumption is that it's only related to y **because it changes how much x you get**.

**Step 1 — predict x using z.** Get the average x for z = 1 and the average x for z = 0. Two
predictions, one for each part of the data.

**Step 2 — keep only the prediction.** Here's the departure from controlling. If you were controlling
for z you'd subtract out everything you just predicted. Instead, **subtract out everything you did
*not* predict.** All the variation in the data collapses down to just what z predicts — and since z
has only two values, you get exactly two points: mean x when z = 1, and mean x when z = 0.

**Step 3 — do the same to y.** Average y for z = 1 and average y for z = 0. Collapse everything to
those two points.

**Step 4 — draw the line.** You now have variation in x as predicted by z, and variation in y as
predicted by z. Since z is a source of random variation, **whether you end up in the z = 1 group or
the z = 0 group is randomly assigned** — so the change in x is random, and therefore the change in y
is randomly determined by your treatment x.

Draw a line between those two points and you have the relationship between the **random part of x**
and the **random part of y**. That line has a causal interpretation: increasing x from the first
value to the second decreased y from the first value to the second, so **x decreases y causally**.

### The two assumptions

**Relevance.** In the step where you use z to predict x, there had better be some actual prediction
going on — **z must genuinely be a good predictor of x**. It doesn't matter if z is truly randomized;
if it doesn't determine x in any way, then when you isolate the part of x explained by z there'll be
**no variation left to study**.

**Validity.** z really is a source of exogenous variation — randomly assigned in some way, or at
least randomly assigned once you've added control variables accounting for any back doors between z
and the outcome.

**Where it breaks — the honest framing.** The validity assumption makes clear that **you still have to
worry about back doors**. Instrumental variables is **not a get-out-of-jail-free card**. It **moves**
the assumption that all your back doors are closed from a tricky variable — your treatment — to one
that's perhaps easier: **the instrument**.

You still have to claim either that there are **no back doors between z and the outcome** — z is
genuinely randomly assigned, as in the drug trial — or that you understand the determinants of z well
enough to measure and control for everything needed to close them, so z is effectively as good as
random.

## TRAPS

- Instrumental variables removes the need to worry about back doors → **Actually,** it relocates the requirement from your treatment to your instrument. You still have to defend it.
- An instrument works because it's correlated with treatment → **Actually,** correlation is only the *relevance* half. Without validity it's just another confounded variable.
- An instrument should be a strong predictor of the outcome → **Actually,** it must be unrelated to the outcome except through the treatment. Predicting the outcome directly is disqualifying.
- IV and control variables are opposite in purpose but similar in mechanics → **Actually,** they're the *same* mechanics run in opposite directions: subtract what you predict, versus keep only what you predict.
- The drug trial's treatment is random, so you don't need IV → **Actually,** the *assignment* is random; whether people took the drug is not. That gap is exactly why you need an instrument.
- A genuinely random instrument is enough → **Actually,** if it doesn't predict treatment, relevance fails and there's nothing left to analyse.

## SAY IT OUT LOUD

> Everything up to now has been about **closing back doors** — find the confounder, measure it, cut it
> out. Instrumental variables does the opposite: it goes and grabs the **front door** directly.
>
> Think about two ways to make a statue. You can take a block of marble and **carve away** everything
> that isn't the statue — that's closing back doors. Or you can build a **mould** and pour metal in, so
> the only shape that can possibly come out is the one you want. That's a front door.
>
> And an instrumental variable is just a randomized experiment **that you didn't run**. You go looking
> for randomness that's already out there in the world.
>
> Cleanest example: you run a drug trial and randomly assign who gets the drug. But people don't
> comply. Some assigned to take it don't bother; some assigned *not* to take it go and get it anyway.
> So "did you take the drug" is **not random** — people chose. But "were you assigned to take it"
> **is**. And assignment pushes treatment around. That's your instrument.
>
> Here's the mechanic I love. When you **control** for a variable, you see what it predicts and you
> **subtract that out**. With an instrument, you do exactly the same thing **backwards** — you see what
> it predicts, and you **throw away everything else.** You keep only the part of the treatment that the
> randomness explains. Because that part is as good as randomly assigned.
>
> Two things have to hold. **Relevance** — your instrument actually has to predict treatment. Perfectly
> random and totally irrelevant leaves you with nothing to study. And **validity** — it has to be
> genuinely exogenous.
>
> And be honest about what that second one means: IV doesn't make the back-door problem go away. It
> **moves** it. Instead of defending your treatment, you now have to defend your instrument. The hope is
> just that it's an easier argument.

## RECALL

Q: How does this class of methods differ from everything before it?
A: Earlier methods find back doors and control them away. Instrumental variables isolates the front
door path instead.

Q: Give the statue analogy.
A: Carving away marble until the statue emerges is closing back doors; pouring metal into a mould so
only the desired shape can result is finding a front door.

Q: What is an instrumental variable, in one sentence?
A: A source of randomization existing in the world that you isolate statistically — a randomized
experiment you didn't get to run.

Q: In the drug trial, what's random and what isn't?
A: Random assignment to be told to take the drug is random; whether people actually took it is not,
because they chose whether to comply.

Q: Define exogenous.
A: As good as random in the system you're studying — with no back doors from it to your outcome.

Q: What makes random assignment an instrument here?
A: It affects whether you actually took the drug, so it supplies a random component to a treatment
that isn't otherwise randomly assigned.

Q: Contrast the mechanics of a control variable and an instrument.
A: With a control variable you predict and subtract that prediction out. With an instrument you predict
and keep only the prediction, discarding everything else.

Q: In the two-value graphs, what do you compute in step one?
A: The average x when z = 1 and the average x when z = 0.

Q: What happens to all the other variation?
A: It's discarded — everything collapses to the two predicted points, since z takes only two values.

Q: Why does the line between the two collapsed points have a causal interpretation?
A: Because whether you fall in the z = 1 or z = 0 group is randomly assigned, so the change in x is
random and the resulting change in y is randomly determined by treatment.

Q: State the relevance assumption and why it matters.
A: The instrument must actually predict the treatment. If it doesn't, isolating the part of treatment
it explains leaves no variation to study — no matter how genuinely random the instrument is.

Q: State the validity assumption.
A: The instrument really is a source of exogenous variation — randomly assigned, or randomly assigned
conditional on controls that close any back doors between it and the outcome.

Q: Does instrumental variables eliminate the back door problem?
A: No. It moves the requirement from a difficult variable, the treatment, to one that's hopefully
easier, the instrument.

## GLOSSARY

- **Instrumental variable** — a variable that randomly influences treatment and affects the outcome only through it.
- **Exogenous variation** — variation that is as good as random within the system, with no back doors to the outcome.
- **Relevance** — the requirement that the instrument genuinely predicts the treatment.
- **Validity** — the requirement that the instrument is exogenous with respect to the outcome.
