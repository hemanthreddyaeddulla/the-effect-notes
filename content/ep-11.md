## ONE SENTENCE

"One drink a day is good for you" comes from comparing one-drink-a-day people to
non-drinkers — but non-drinkers include people too sick to drink and recovering
alcoholics, and once you remove them the benefit disappears, which is identification
happening in public on a real question.

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

- When many studies on one topic reach wildly different conclusions, it can mean they're not answering the same question — the analyses differ even though the intent doesn't.
- The finding: people who have one drink a day rate healthier than people who have zero.
- The identification problem: **who has zero drinks a day?** People too sick to drink, and ex-alcoholics whose past heavy drinking still affects them.
- Wood et al. drop those people and re-ask. The apparent benefit of one drink a day **goes away**.
- They also adjust for background factors — sex, race, age — since those drive both drinking and mortality.
- A **placebo test** shows they still haven't got everything: the same method implies drinking more causes you to become a man.
- There is always another alternative explanation. The question is whether you've cleared enough of them.

## WHY THIS EXISTS

You've seen the headlines. A glass of wine a day is good for you. No, it's bad for you.
No, alcohol is fine. No, alcohol is the worst thing imaginable and one sip will kill
you.

Sometimes — not always — a pile of contradictory studies on the same topic is a signal
that the analyses aren't answering the intended question. They come out different
because they're really asking different questions, even when everyone meant to ask the
same one.

This episode takes one such question and walks the identification through: here's the
naive comparison, here's what's wrong with it, here's what fixing it does, and here's
how you can tell you still haven't fixed everything.

## IDEAS

### The claim and the comparison behind it

**Plain English.** People who have one drink a day look healthier than people who have
none. Therefore one drink a day is good for you.

**Precisely.** In the data, health ratings of people having one drink a day are higher
than those of people having zero — measured by cardiovascular health, fitness, how long
they live. The tempting conclusion is a positive causal effect of one drink a day on
health.

**The identification question:** does a comparison of zero-drinkers to one-drink-a-day
people actually answer *does drinking one drink a day rather than zero make you
healthier?*

Probably not.

### Who are the zero-drinkers?

**Plain English.** People with zero drinks a day are not just "the same people, minus
the drink."

**Precisely.** The zero-drinks group systematically contains people who are different for
reasons that themselves affect health:

- **Too sick to drink.** Their health rating is low, and their poor health is what causes them not to drink. The arrow runs backwards from the one you assumed.
- **Ex-alcoholics.** Someone who drank very heavily for a long period will still be carrying the health consequences, even having stopped completely.

**Anchor image.** Comparing people who take no medication to people who take a little,
and concluding a little medication makes you well. The people on zero include both the
perfectly healthy and the too-far-gone.

```dag
node H 1 0 "Poor health" bad
node D 0 1 "Zero drinks" treat
node M 2 1 "Mortality" out
edge H D "too sick to drink"
edge H M
note 1 1.75 "the zero-drinks group is selected on ill health"
```

**Where it breaks.** The comparison isn't meaningless — it might be an interesting result
in its own right. It simply doesn't answer the question that was asked.

### Remove the alternative, re-ask the question

**Plain English.** Take those people out of the zero group and run it again.

**Precisely.** Wood et al. attempt to identify the effect by removing that alternative
explanation: from the zero-drinks-a-day group, drop the people who are too sick to drink,
the ex-alcoholics, and similar cases. Then ask again whether more drinking is associated
with better health at low levels.

**Worked example.** Their result, plotted as drinks per week against a **hazard ratio** —
higher meaning less healthy, more likely to die sooner:

- For the **first few drinks per week**: not much effect. The apparent benefit of light drinking is gone.
- Past roughly **100 grams of alcohol per week**: the hazard ratio starts climbing.

That's one alternative explanation accounted for, and it took the original finding with
it. The relationship people were reading as "light drinking is good for you" did not
identify the question of interest.

### One down, many to go

**Plain English.** Drinking correlates with a lot of things, and most of them also
correlate with dying.

**Precisely.** The decision to drink is related to a whole range of background factors
that are themselves related to mortality. Men tend to drink more *and* tend to die sooner,
largely for reasons other than drinking. The same goes for class, race, location, and
region. So the study adjusts for a number of these — racial background among them — so
that if one of those is driving the remaining relationship, it's been conditioned away.

**Worked example.** After all that, the shape holds: little relationship at low levels of
alcohol, then a fairly rapid climb in the negative health effects past a threshold.

**Where it breaks.** They can't account for everything. There will always be some factor
they didn't measure or didn't control for — short of an extremely careful job of removing
*every* alternative explanation, which is very hard to do. Given what they controlled for,
you could argue things remain.

### The placebo test

**Plain English.** Run your method on a relationship you *know* should be zero. If it
isn't zero, your method is picking up something it shouldn't.

**Precisely.** A **placebo test** examines a relationship that must be zero if all your
assumptions hold. A non-zero result means something has been left out of the analysis.

**Anchor image.** Weighing an empty scale. If it reads 200 grams, you don't have a heavy
nothing — you have a broken scale, and every measurement you took with it is suspect.

**Worked example.** Same graph, same methods, same drinks-per-week on the x-axis. Only the
y-axis changes: **probability of being a man**.

The result says drinking more causes you to become a man.

That is not how it works. The line should be flat at zero. It isn't — so some alternative
explanation is still in there, unaccounted for, and it's producing relationships out of
nothing.

**Where it breaks.** Read the logic carefully. On the alcohol–mortality graph the effect is
non-zero, so either the causal effect is genuinely non-zero, or something else is going on.
The placebo test doesn't tell you which. It tells you that at least some of the second is
present — that there's residual something the method can't clear.

## TRAPS

- Contradictory studies on a topic mean the science is unsettled → **Actually,** sometimes it means the analyses are answering different questions despite intending the same one.
- Comparing zero-drinkers to light drinkers is the natural comparison → **Actually,** the zero group is selected on ill health. Some are there *because* they're sick, which is the arrow running backwards.
- The benefit vanished after adjustment, so the adjustment destroyed a real finding → **Actually,** the original comparison never identified the question. Removing the alternative revealed that.
- They controlled for race, sex, and age, so the estimate is clean → **Actually,** the placebo test shows something is still unaccounted for. Controlling handles what you thought of.
- The placebo test tells you the alcohol result is wrong → **Actually,** it tells you something is left out. The alcohol effect may be partly or wholly real; you can't tell which from this.
- A placebo test needs a special dataset → **Actually,** it's the same data and the same method, pointed at an outcome that must be zero.

## RECALL

Q: What can a pile of contradictory studies on one topic indicate?
A: That the analyses aren't actually answering the same question — they differ because
they're asking different questions, even when everyone intended the same one.

Q: What comparison generates the "one drink a day is good for you" finding?
A: People having one drink a day are compared to people having zero, and the one-drink group
rates healthier.

Q: What's wrong with the zero-drinks comparison group?
A: It contains people who are too sick to drink — whose poor health causes the non-drinking —
and ex-alcoholics still carrying the effects of past heavy drinking.

Q: What happens when Wood et al. remove those people and re-run?
A: The apparent benefit of light drinking disappears. Little effect over the first few
drinks per week, then the hazard ratio climbs past roughly 100 grams of alcohol per week.

Q: What is a hazard ratio doing on the y-axis of their graph?
A: Higher means less healthy — more likely to die sooner.

Q: Beyond the composition of the zero group, what other alternatives did they handle?
A: Background factors that drive both drinking and mortality — sex (men drink more and die
sooner for other reasons), plus class, race, location, and region. They adjust for things
like racial background.

Q: Define a placebo test.
A: Running your method on a relationship that must be zero if your assumptions hold. A
non-zero result indicates something has been left out.

Q: What was the placebo test here, and what did it show?
A: Same data and method, but with "probability of being a man" as the outcome. The method
implied that drinking more causes you to become a man — so an alternative explanation is
still unaccounted for.

Q: The alcohol–mortality effect is non-zero. What are the two possibilities, and can the placebo test choose between them?
A: Either the causal effect is genuinely non-zero, or something else is still going on. The
placebo test can't choose — it only establishes that some of the second is present.

Q: What's the general lesson about when you're finished eliminating alternatives?
A: There's always another one. Until you've narrowed down to exactly the part you want,
something is left over — and a placebo test is one way to detect that you're not there yet.

## GLOSSARY

- **Hazard ratio** — a measure where higher values mean less healthy, more likely to die sooner.
- **Placebo test** — applying your method to a relationship that should be zero under your assumptions; a non-zero result signals something left out.
- **Adjusting for** — conditioning on background factors so they can't drive the remaining relationship.
