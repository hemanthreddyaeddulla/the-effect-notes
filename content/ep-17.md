## ONE SENTENCE

A path is **open** when every variable on it varies and **closed** when even one doesn't —
so you close bad paths by removing variation, either by controlling for a variable or by
picking a sample that doesn't vary in it; except that **colliders** invert the rule, sitting
on paths that are already closed until you control for them and blow them open.

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

- **Open path:** every variable along it is allowed to vary in your sample. It can induce a relationship.
- **Closed path:** at least one variable on it has no variation. It can't do anything.
- Two ways to close: **condition/control** for a variable, or **select a sample** with no variation in it.
- Kill one variable and **every** path it sits on closes at once.
- Don't close good paths. Controlling for something your treatment caused is **post-treatment bias**.
- A **collider** is a variable on a path with **both arrows pointing at it**. That path is **pre-closed** — free of charge.
- **Control for a collider and the path opens.** You can do this by accident, just by choosing a sample.
- Collider is a property of a variable **on a particular path**, not a global label.

## WHY THIS EXISTS

You've got your paths sorted into good and bad. Identification means closing the bad ones —
but nothing so far says what "closing" physically is or how to do it.

And there's a trap waiting. The instinct that follows from "close bad paths" is *control for
everything you can*. Two things on this page make that instinct actively dangerous.

## IDEAS

### Open and closed

**Plain English.** A path works only if everything along it is free to vary. Freeze any one
variable and the whole path stops working.

**Precisely.** A path is **open** when all variables on it are allowed to vary in your sample.
It's **closed** when at least one variable on it has no variation. A closed path can no longer
induce a relationship between the variables at its ends.

**Anchor image.** A row of dominoes. Every one has to be free to fall. Glue any single domino
down and nothing past it moves — the whole line is dead, no matter how many are still standing.

**Worked example.** Wine ← Health → Lifespan. In ordinary data there are people who drink more
and less, people healthier and less healthy, people who live longer and shorter. Variation
everywhere, so the path is **open** — it's a live reason wine and lifespan correlate.

Now suppose you either statistically adjust for health, or pick a sample where everyone is
naturally healthy. Either way there's no variation in health left. You can no longer say
"naturally healthy people drink more and live longer" — in your data there *are* no more and
less healthy people. The path is **closed**.

**Where it breaks — and this is the useful part:** killing health's variation closes *every*
path health sits on, all at once. `Wine ← Income ← U1 → Health → Lifespan` shuts down too.

### The two ways to close a path

**Plain English.** Adjust it away statistically, or choose data that doesn't have it.

**Precisely.**

1. **Conditioning / controlling.** Measure the variable and adjust for it, making the data behave as though it doesn't vary. Take out the relationship between income and wine, and between income and lifespan, and income no longer influences either.
2. **Sample selection.** Choose a dataset with no variation in it — only healthy people, say.

**Where it breaks.** Both work. Only the first is deliberate every time; the second can happen
to you without your noticing, which is the collider trap below.

### Worked all the way through: wine and lifespan

**Plain English.** Six paths. Two you want, four you don't. Two control variables close all
four.

**Worked example.** Walk out every path from wine to lifespan and you get six.

**Front doors (good) — leave open:**

1. Wine → Lifespan
2. Wine → Drugs → Lifespan

**Back doors (bad) — must close:**

3. Wine ← Income → Lifespan
4. Wine ← Income ← U1 → Health → Lifespan
5. Wine ← Health → Lifespan
6. Wine ← Health ← U1 → Income → Lifespan

**Control for income.** Income sits on paths 3, 4, and 6 — all three close at once. What's
left open: the two front doors, plus path 5.

**Control for health as well.** Path 5 has only health on it, so health is the *only* thing
that could close it — there's nothing else to control for. Now paths 3–6 are all shut.

What remains: exactly the two front doors. **The effect is identified.**

### Don't control for everything

**Plain English.** Drugs is obviously related to all this. Control for it and you destroy part
of your answer.

**Precisely.** Controlling for drugs would close `Wine → Drugs → Lifespan` — a front door you
wanted open. You'd be removing part of the real effect. Controlling for something that is
*affected by your treatment* is **post-treatment bias**.

**Anchor image.** Measuring how much a tap fills a bucket while clamping the pipe halfway
along. The tap works fine; you disabled the route you were trying to measure.

**Where it breaks.** The instinct "it's related, so I should control for it" is exactly wrong
here. There are things you must not control for, and the diagram is what tells you which.

### Colliders

**Plain English.** A variable that two things point *into*. Any path through it is already
closed — until you control for it, at which point it opens.

**Precisely.** A **collider** is a variable on a path where **both arrows on that path point
toward it**. A path containing a collider is **pre-closed** — it can't induce a relationship,
and you don't have to do anything about it. But **controlling for the collider opens it back
up**, and it becomes a problem.

Critically: being a collider is a property of a variable **on a particular path**, not a fixed
label. The same variable can be a collider on one path and not on another.

**Anchor image.** A door that's locked by default. Leave it alone and nobody comes through. The
only way to create a problem is to walk over and unlock it.

**Worked example.** Does tutoring in high school raise intelligence? Say you even randomized
the tutoring, so you'd think you're fine.

```dag
node T 0 0 "HS tutoring" treat
node I 2.4 0 "Intelligence" out
node C 1.2 1.1 "Goes to college" bad
edge T C
edge I C
note 1.2 1.9 "both arrows point at college -> collider -> path pre-closed"
```

Tutoring helps you get into college regardless of intelligence. Intelligence also helps you get
into college. So `Tutoring → College ← Intelligence` is a path — and it's pre-closed, because
college is a collider on it. No problem.

Now: you decide to survey **college students**, because they're easy to survey.

You have just controlled for college. The path opens. And look at who is in that sample:

- Smart people who got in without needing tutoring
- People who got in *because* they were tutored, but weren't especially smart
- People who were both

Within college students, being tutored and being smart are substitutes for getting in. So
tutoring and intelligence will look **negatively related** in your data — even if they weren't
related at all to begin with.

**Where it breaks.** Nobody chose to control for college. It happened through a sampling
decision that felt like convenience. This is the everyday version of the collider trap.

## TRAPS

- Control for everything you can measure → **Actually,** two separate reasons not to. Controlling for a mediator on a front door removes part of your effect. Controlling for a collider opens a path that was closed.
- Drugs is related to wine and lifespan, so control for it → **Actually,** it's on a front door path. Closing it is post-treatment bias — you'd delete part of the real effect.
- A collider is a bad variable to have on a diagram → **Actually,** it's a gift. Paths through it are pre-closed at no cost. It only becomes a problem if you touch it.
- Colliders are a type of variable → **Actually,** it's a property of a variable *on a specific path*. The same variable may be a collider on one path and not on another.
- I didn't control for anything, I just chose a convenient sample → **Actually,** sample selection is controlling. Surveying only college students conditions on college.
- Randomizing the treatment protects me from all path problems → **Actually,** the tutoring example randomizes and still gets a spurious negative relationship, purely from who ended up in the sample.

## RECALL

Q: When is a path open, and when is it closed?
A: Open when every variable on it is allowed to vary in your sample. Closed when at least one
variable on it has no variation.

Q: What are the two ways to close a path?
A: Condition or control for a variable on it, or select a sample in which that variable has no
variation.

Q: You control for health. Which paths close?
A: Every path that health sits on, all at once — not just the one you had in mind.

Q: In the wine diagram, controlling for income closes which paths, and what's left?
A: Paths 3, 4, and 6 — every back door income sits on. Left open: the two front doors plus
path 5, Wine ← Health → Lifespan.

Q: Why must you control for health specifically to close path 5?
A: Health is the only variable on that path, so there's nothing else you could control for to
shut it.

Q: Why should you *not* control for drugs?
A: Drugs is on the front door path Wine → Drugs → Lifespan, which you want counted. Closing it
removes part of the real effect.

Q: What is post-treatment bias?
A: Controlling for a variable that is affected by your treatment, which closes a front door
path and deletes part of the effect you're trying to measure.

Q: Define a collider.
A: A variable on a path where both arrows on that path point toward it.

Q: What's the default status of a path containing a collider, and what changes it?
A: It's pre-closed — no action needed. Controlling for the collider opens it.

Q: Is "collider" a property of a variable or of a variable on a path?
A: Of a variable on a particular path. The same variable can be a collider on one path and not
on another.

Q: Tutoring → College ← Intelligence. You survey only college students. What happens and why?
A: You've controlled for college, opening the path. Within college students, tutoring and being
smart are substitute routes in, so they appear negatively related — even if unrelated in the
population.

Q: You randomized your treatment. Are you safe from collider bias?
A: No. Tutoring was randomized and the college-student sample still produces a spurious negative
relationship, entirely from sample selection.

## GLOSSARY

- **Open path** — a path on which every variable is free to vary, so it can induce a relationship.
- **Closed path** — a path on which at least one variable has no variation, so it cannot.
- **Conditioning** — adjusting for a variable so the data behaves as though it doesn't vary.
- **Sample selection** — choosing data with no variation in a variable, which closes paths just as controlling does.
- **Post-treatment bias** — controlling for a variable affected by your treatment, closing a front door path you wanted open.
- **Collider** — a variable on a path with both arrows pointing at it; the path is pre-closed unless you control for the collider.
