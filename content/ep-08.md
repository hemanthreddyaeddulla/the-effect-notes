## ONE SENTENCE

Controlling for a variable means explaining away everything Z can account for in both X
and Y, throwing that part out, and asking whether X and Y are *still* related in the
leftover — the **residual** — which is how you answer "isn't that just because of Z?"

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

- Any conditional mean splits an observation into an **explained part** and an **unexplained part**, the **residual**.
- The residual is the interesting bit: it's the variation that has *nothing to do* with the variable you conditioned on.
- Look for a relationship inside the residuals and you get a **conditional conditional distribution** — the relationship between X and Y that survives after Z is accounted for.
- That is exactly what "controlling for", "adjusting for", and "matching on" all mean.
- Mechanically: use any relationship tool to explain X and Y with Z, subtract, then look at what's left. Regression does it automatically — just add Z as a predictor, and the coefficient on X becomes the conditional conditional relationship.
- It's the standard reply to "isn't that just because of Z?"

## WHY THIS EXISTS

You find that people who wear shorts are much more likely to eat ice cream. You announce
a causal effect: make people wear shorts and they'll eat more ice cream. Someone
immediately says: no, it's just that both happen on hot days.

They might be right. You need a way to check — a way to strip out everything temperature
can explain about shorts-wearing and ice-cream-eating, and see whether anything survives.
That's what this episode builds, and it's the last piece of description before causal
inference proper.

## IDEAS

### Explained and unexplained: the residual

**Plain English.** A conditional mean tells you what to *expect*. The residual is how far
off the actual value is.

**Precisely.** Given a conditional mean of Y at some value of X, each observation splits
into the explained part (the conditional mean) and the **residual** (actual minus
expected). The residual is the variation in Y that the conditioning variable cannot
account for.

**Anchor image.** Weighing a loaded truck and subtracting the truck's known empty weight.
What's left is the cargo — and the cargo is the only part you were ever curious about.

**Worked example.** Nick is a college professor, and he drinks coffee. Suppose professors
average **2.3 cups a day**. He actually drinks **3**.

- **Explained:** 2.3 cups. That's what knowing "professor" buys you.
- **Residual:** 0.7 cups. The gap between expected and actual.

That 0.7 sounds like failure — you can't predict individuals from a group mean. But
notice what it *is*: the part of his coffee drinking that has nothing to do with being a
professor. Some other reason drives it. That leftover is the useful object.

### Conditional conditional: look for the relationship inside the residual

**Plain English.** Take out what Z explains, then ask whether X and Y are still related in
what's left.

**Precisely.** A **conditional conditional distribution** is the conditional distribution of
Y given X, computed *within* the variation remaining after conditioning on Z. If a
relationship persists there, Z alone doesn't account for it.

**Anchor image.** Someone claims the room is only bright because the lamp is on. Turn the
lamp off. Still bright? Then something else is contributing.

**Worked example.** Claim: brown-haired people drink more coffee. Objection: brown-haired
people are more likely to be professors, and professors drink a lot of coffee.

So go to the residual. Discard the 2.3 cups that "professor" explains and work only inside
the 0.7. Within that leftover, is hair colour still related to coffee drinking?

- **Yes** → there's something about brown hair beyond the professor story.
- **No** → the objector was right; being a professor was the whole thing.

### That's all "controlling for" ever meant

**Plain English.** Controlling for, adjusting for, matching on — same idea, different words.

**Precisely.** Each names the operation of conditioning on a variable and then examining the
relationship that remains within that already-conditioned variation.

**Worked example.** Shorts and ice cream. You observe they go together. The objection is that
both happen on hot days.

```dag
node T 1 0 "Hot day"
node S 0 1 "Shorts" treat
node I 2 1 "Ice cream" out
edge T S
edge T I
edge S I "still there after controlling for T?"
note 1 1.85 "control for temperature, then look at what's left"
```

Split by hot days versus not — or use all the values of temperature — see what temperature
explains about each of shorts and ice cream, and take the residuals. If shorts and ice cream
are *still* related in the leftover, temperature isn't the whole story and there may be
something causal. If the relationship vanishes, the objector was right.

**Where it breaks.** "May be something causal" is the honest ceiling here. Surviving a control
for temperature doesn't establish causation — it eliminates one alternative explanation. There
are always others, which is what the identification chapters are for.

### How to actually do it

**Plain English.** Explain what you can with Z, subtract it out of both variables, then look at
the relationship in what's left. Or just add Z to your regression.

**Precisely.** The manual version uses any relationship tool you already have — line-fitting,
means within bins. Explain X with Z and take the residual; explain Y with Z and take the
residual; then look at the relationship between those two residuals.

The easy version: instead of regressing Y on X, regress Y on **X and Z**. The coefficient on X
is now the conditional conditional relationship — regression performs the subtraction for you.

**Anchor image.** Two hills with a valley between them. Flatten both hills by the same amount
and whatever separation remains was never about the hills.

**Worked example — the geometry.** Picture Y, X, and Z. Z is positively related to X *and*
positively related to Y, so part of the X–Y relationship is really Z operating through both.
Flatten the Y–Z relationship. Flatten the X–Z relationship. Now Z is flat against both leftovers
— it can't be driving anything — and whatever X–Y relationship survives is the part not due to Z.

**Worked example — the numbers.** From last episode, vitamin E on BMI alone:

```
proportion taking vitamin E  =  0.110  +  0.002 × BMI
```

Plenty of reasons that relationship might not be about BMI. Older people may have higher BMI
*and* be more likely to take vitamin E. Gender could matter too. So add **age and gender** to
the regression:

| Model | Coefficient on BMI |
|---|---|
| BMI only | 0.002 |
| BMI, controlling for age and gender | **0.001** |

Read it carefully. The coefficient **halved**, so part of the original BMI–vitamin E relationship
really was age and gender. But it didn't vanish — conditional on age and gender, a one-unit
increase in BMI is still associated with a 0.001 increase in the conditional mean of vitamin E
taking. Some relationship survives the conditioning.

## TRAPS

- The residual is error — the part the model got wrong → **Actually,** it's the variation the conditioning variable can't explain, and it's the object you actively want. All the conditional conditional work happens inside it.
- Controlling for, adjusting for, and matching on are three different techniques → **Actually,** they're three names for conditioning on a variable and examining what remains.
- The relationship survived my control, so it's causal → **Actually,** you ruled out one alternative explanation. Others remain, and enumerating them is what identification is for.
- The coefficient dropped from 0.002 to 0.001, so controlling destroyed the finding → **Actually,** it halved and persisted. Part of the original was age and gender; part wasn't.
- You need special software to compute a conditional conditional relationship → **Actually,** any relationship tool works — explain with Z, subtract, look at the leftover. Adding a predictor to a regression is just the convenient version.

## RECALL

Q: What is a residual?
A: The difference between an observation's actual value and the conditional mean you'd have
expected for it — the part the conditioning variable doesn't explain.

Q: Professors average 2.3 cups of coffee; Nick drinks 3. Identify the explained and unexplained parts.
A: Explained: 2.3 cups, from knowing he's a professor. Residual: 0.7 cups, driven by something
other than being a professor.

Q: Why is the residual the useful part rather than a nuisance?
A: Because it's variation guaranteed to have nothing to do with the variable you conditioned
on — so a relationship found inside it can't be explained by that variable.

Q: What is a conditional conditional distribution?
A: The conditional distribution of Y given X, computed within the variation that remains after
conditioning on some other variable Z.

Q: Which everyday phrases mean the same as "conditioning on Z and looking at what remains"?
A: Controlling for Z, adjusting for Z, matching on Z.

Q: Shorts-wearers eat more ice cream; someone says it's just hot days. Describe the check.
A: Take out what temperature explains about both shorts-wearing and ice-cream-eating, then look
for a relationship between the residuals. Surviving means temperature isn't the whole story;
vanishing means the objection was right.

Q: How do you compute a conditional conditional relationship manually?
A: Use any relationship tool to explain X with Z and Y with Z, take both residuals, then examine
the relationship between the residuals.

Q: What's the shortcut in a regression?
A: Regress Y on X *and* Z instead of X alone. The coefficient on X becomes the conditional
conditional relationship.

Q: BMI's coefficient goes from 0.002 to 0.001 when age and gender are added. Interpret both facts.
A: The drop means part of the original BMI–vitamin E relationship was really age and gender.
The surviving 0.001 means that conditional on age and gender, a one-unit rise in BMI is still
associated with a 0.001 rise in the conditional mean of vitamin E taking.

Q: Your relationship survives controlling for Z. Have you shown causation?
A: No. You eliminated one alternative explanation. Others remain — handling that systematically
is what identification is for.

## GLOSSARY

- **Residual** — the part of an observation not explained by the conditional mean; actual minus expected.
- **Explained part** — the conditional mean itself; what the conditioning variable accounts for.
- **Conditional conditional distribution** — the distribution of Y given X, within variation already conditioned on Z.
- **Control variable** — a variable you condition on in order to remove its explanation from a relationship.
- **Controlling for / adjusting for / matching on** — three names for conditioning on a variable and examining the relationship that remains.
