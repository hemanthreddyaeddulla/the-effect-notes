## ONE SENTENCE

You care about *why* the world works the way it does, but data can only show you
*what* happened — so a research question is the bridge you build from a why to an
observable what and back again, and most research fails because that bridge doesn't
actually hold.

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

- Research starts with a **theory** — a claim about *why* something is the way it is.
- Data can't see why. It only reports what. So you need a **what** you can observe that genuinely informs your **why**.
- The test of a good question: imagine every result you could get, and ask whether each would change your mind. If you'd explain away the inconvenient one, your question isn't testing your theory.
- Then check the practical filters: is it **feasible**, is it affordable at **scale**, does a **setting** exist where the design works?
- And **keep it small**. One study answers one narrow question, not "why are some countries rich?"

## WHY THIS EXISTS

You don't do research because you want a number. You do it because you have an idea
about how the world fits together and you want to know if it's right. But the thing
you can measure is never the idea itself — it's some observable trace of it.

The gap between those two is where research goes wrong, and it goes wrong *before any
data is collected*. Get too deep in before checking, and you finish the analysis only
to realise the number you produced never had the power to change your mind about the
theory in the first place.

Everything here is quantitative empirical research. The advice doesn't transfer to
questions that aren't answered with data.

## IDEAS

### Theory is "why", data is "what"

**Plain English.** Your theory says *because*. Your data says *this is what we saw*.
Data is very good at what and very bad at why.

**Precisely.** A theory is any claim containing an explanation — a why, a because, a
mechanism. A research question names something observable that, if you saw it, would
update your belief in that theory. The job is: go from the why to a what you can
measure, then reason from that what back to the why.

**Anchor image.** You want to know whether the neighbour is home. You can't see
inside. You can see whether the lights are on. "Lights" isn't "home" — but if you
picked the right observable and you know how lights and people relate, the lights
genuinely tell you something. Pick the wrong observable and you're staring at a
timer switch, learning nothing.

**Worked example.** Theory: *being exposed to lots of different cultures makes you
smarter* — one reason some people are more intelligent than others.

The obvious what: look in data, check whether intelligence and cultural exposure move
together. They do. Does that inform the why?

```dag
node C 0 0 "Cultural exposure" treat
node I 2 0 "Intelligence" out
edge C I "the theory"
edge I C curve "just as consistent with the data"
```

No — because the correlation is equally consistent with smarter people *choosing* more
cultural exposure. The what you picked can't distinguish the two, so it can't move
your belief.

Now change the what: randomly assign some people to cultural exposure and follow up on
their intelligence later. Same two variables, same kind of relationship measured — but
now the arrow from intelligence back to exposure is broken by the randomisation, so the
result actually bears on the theory.

**Where it breaks.** Note what changed. Not the theory, not the variables, not the
statistic. Only the *question*, and with it the design.

### Imagine the results before you have them

**Plain English.** Before running anything, picture each result you might get and ask
honestly: would that change what I believe?

**Precisely.** Take every plausible outcome, including the ones that contradict you,
and write down your reaction to each. If a contradicting result would make you start
generating excuses, then a confirming result shouldn't have moved you either. That
asymmetry is the tell: the question isn't connected to the theory.

**Anchor image.** A bet you can't lose is a bet that isn't measuring anything. If the
coin comes up tails and you say "well, that one didn't count", you weren't betting.

**Worked example.** Correlational version: you find exposure and intelligence are
*negatively* related. Your reaction: "maybe smart people are too busy to travel", or
"maybe cultural exposure was measured badly". You've explained it away. So the positive
result you were hoping for wouldn't have been evidence either.

Experimental version: you randomly assigned people to more cultural exposure, and they
came out *less* intelligent. Much harder to wriggle out of. That's the point — you want
a question where the data can genuinely shoot your theory down.

**Where it breaks.** This is a check on the *question*, and it only works if you do it
before you see results. Afterwards you'll know which outcome you got, and you can't
un-know it.

### The three practical filters

**Plain English.** Even a question that would settle your theory is useless if you
can't actually run it.

**Precisely.**

| Filter | The question to ask | Failure |
|---|---|---|
| **Feasibility** | Does the data exist, and would anyone let me do this? | Nobody will let you randomly assign the minimum wage across states. |
| **Scale** | Do I have the money, the time, the people? | The answer needs every home transaction: hundreds of thousands of dollars and a year of several people's time. |
| **Design** | Does a setting exist where my approach applies? | Your plan needs students randomly assigned to a global studies course. Did that ever happen anywhere? |

**Anchor image.** A recipe you can't source the ingredients for isn't a dinner plan,
however good it would have tasted.

**Where it breaks.** Failing a filter doesn't kill your theory — it kills that
*question*. You go looking for a different observable what that reaches the same why.

### Keep it small

**Plain English.** One study answers one small, well-defined question. It does not
explain the world.

**Precisely.** Big questions — why are some countries rich, what's the best form of
government — can't be resolved by a single study. The common failure is the
"determinants of X" paper: thirty predictors of school dropout, none of them
identified, and no way to act on any of it.

**Anchor image.** Thirty half-answers stacked together aren't one whole answer. They're
a pile.

**Worked example.** Instead of "what are the determinants of school dropout?", carve
off a chunk: *does a free pre-school lunch help you stay in school?* Narrow, well
defined, and you'll know when you've answered it — and it still feeds the big theory
you actually care about.

**Where it breaks.** Small isn't the same as trivial. The chunk still has to bear on
the theory, or you've just made a well-identified answer to something nobody asked.

## TRAPS

- I found the correlation my theory predicted, so my theory has support → **Actually,** ask what a contrary correlation would have made you do. If you'd have explained it away, the confirming result was never evidence.
- The question is good because the theory is interesting → **Actually,** those are separate checks. A great theory paired with a what that can't discriminate between explanations produces nothing.
- More determinants in one study means a more complete answer → **Actually,** it usually means thirty relationships nobody identified. One narrow well-answered question beats a pile of mush.
- I can't run the experiment, so the theory is untestable → **Actually,** the *question* failed feasibility, not the theory. Find another observable route to the same why — which is most of what the rest of the book is about.

## RECALL

Q: What's the difference between a theory and a research question?
A: A theory is a claim about *why* the world works some way. A research question names
an observable *what* that, if you measured it, would inform the theory.

Q: Why can't data answer a "why" question directly?
A: Data reports what was observed. Multiple different whys can produce the same what,
so the observation alone doesn't pick between them.

Q: Theory: cultural exposure makes people smarter. Why does finding a positive correlation fail to support it?
A: The correlation is equally consistent with the reverse — more intelligent people
choosing more cultural exposure. The observation can't distinguish the two.

Q: What's the pre-analysis test for whether a research question is any good?
A: Imagine each result you could get, including contradictory ones. If a contradictory
result would make you make excuses rather than change your mind, the question isn't
testing your theory — and the confirming result wouldn't be evidence either.

Q: You want to know the effect of the minimum wage and plan to randomly assign it across states. Which filter kills this?
A: Feasibility. Nobody will let you do it. The theory survives; you need a different
question that reaches the same why.

Q: Name the three practical filters on a research question.
A: Feasibility (does the data exist, would anyone permit it), scale (money, time,
people), and design (does a real-world setting exist where your approach applies).

Q: What's wrong with a paper on "the determinants of school dropout" listing 30 determinants?
A: None of them are answered well, so the results are a mush you can't act on. One
narrow question answered properly is worth more.

Q: How should you handle a question that's too big?
A: Carve off a small, well-defined chunk that still bears on the theory — "does a free
pre-school lunch help you stay in school?" rather than "what causes dropout?"

## GLOSSARY

- **Theory** — a claim about why the world works a certain way; contains a because, not just a description.
- **Research question** — an answerable question about something observable, chosen so that answering it informs a theory.
- **Feasibility** — whether the data can be obtained and the study permitted at all.
- **Scale** — whether you have the money, time, and people the question would require.
- **Setting** — a real-world situation in which your intended design can actually be applied.
