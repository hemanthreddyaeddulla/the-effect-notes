## ONE SENTENCE

Logarithms fix two problems at once — they scrunch skewed variables so billionaires don't drag
your line around, and they convert additive changes into **proportional** ones — at the cost of
an interpretation step, which you can do exactly or with the approximation that **log(1 + small)
≈ small**.

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

- **Reason 1 — skew.** In social science, a few observations are many times bigger than the mass. OLS minimises squared residuals, so it chases the billionaire.
- **Reason 2 — proportional relationships.** Some relationships *should* multiply rather than add. Interest rates don't add dollars to your wealth, they multiply it.
- Logs keep the big values in the data but **scrunch them**, so they still count without over-influencing.
- Logs also give proportional interpretations, because `log(x) + log(y) = log(x·y)` turns addition into multiplication.
- The mantra still works — it's just about a one-unit change in the *log*, so you need a conversion step.
- **Approximation:** `log(1 + a) ≈ a` for small a. Breaks down fast — good to about 10%, useless at 50%.
- Logs can't take **zero** or negative values, and zeros are a real problem.

## WHY THIS EXISTS

Polynomials let a straight line curve. Logarithms solve two different problems, and they get
enormous use in the social sciences because we deal with a lot of highly skewed variables.

Remember the framing: it's fine to fit a straight line between some non-straight *things*. That's
what lets you model non-straight relationships with a procedure that only draws straight lines.

## IDEAS

### Reason 1: skew and over-influential outliers

**Plain English.** A few observations are astronomically bigger than everyone else, and OLS
contorts itself to accommodate them.

**Precisely.** A skewed variable has a mass of relatively similar observations and a few that are
way different. Crucially, it isn't just that there are outliers — it's that the outliers are *so
many times bigger* than the mass.

**Worked example.** Wealth. A typical person has a few thousand dollars, maybe tens of thousands,
maybe a hundred thousand depending on the group. Then there's someone with billions and billions.

**Why OLS struggles:** it minimises the sum of squared residuals. Suppose it's describing the mass
of people well — and then there's someone whose wealth it's off by *billions* on. That residual is
enormous, so the model changes to fit that one person well.

**Anchor image.** A see-saw with a hundred children on one end and a cargo ship on the other. It
doesn't matter how many children there are.

**Where it breaks — the honest nuance.** Outliers aren't necessarily wrong data, and they
**should** affect your analysis — they're part of your data. The problem is only that you don't
want them to have **undue, over-large influence** on your model. That's really what the concern
about outliers is.

Also worth noting: in the social sciences the skew is almost always in this direction. There
aren't many variables where a lot of people are very large and a few are very, very tiny.

### Reason 2: relationships that should be proportional

**Plain English.** Some things multiply rather than add, and a straight line can't express that.

**Worked example.** Your wealth over time depends on what's in your savings and the interest or
investment return on it. Regress wealth on investment return — that shouldn't be a straight-line
relationship. If the return rate goes up by a certain percentage, your wealth doesn't rise by a
certain number of *dollars*; it rises by a certain *percent*. If the interest rate goes up by 0.01,
that should **multiply** your wealth by 1.01, not add a fixed amount.

### What logs do about both

**Plain English.** They squash the giants without deleting them, and they turn plus into times.

**Precisely.**

- **Skew:** logs take those very large values and keep them in the data but scrunch them down, so there's less distance between them and the mass. They're still counted; they just don't pull as hard.
- **Proportionality:** logs have a convenient proportional interpretation, because `log(x) + log(y) = log(x·y)`.

**The mechanism.** A regression gives you additive changes — "a one-unit increase in X is
associated with a coefficient-sized increase in Y." But adding 1 is the same as adding `log(e¹)`.
And `log(x) + log(e)` is `log(x·e)`. So an *additive* one-unit increase in the log becomes a
**multiplication of the original x by e**. That's how a linear increase becomes a proportional one.

### Interpreting: the exact way

**Plain English.** Push the coefficient back through the log algebra until you get a percentage.

**Precisely — two directions.**

**Log on the left: regress log(y) on x.**

```
a one-unit change in x  →  β₁-unit change in log(y)
β₁  =  log(e^β₁)                    [natural logs, always, in social science]
log(y) + log(e^β₁)  =  log(y · e^β₁)
```

So y has increased **proportionally by e^β₁**.

**Log on the right: regress y on log(x).** What's the effect of a 1% increase in x?

```
a 1% increase means  x · 1.01
log(x · 1.01)  =  log(x) + log(1.01)
so log(x) rose by log(1.01) units
therefore y rises by  β₁ · log(1.01)
```

**Where it breaks.** That's the accurate route: convert the linear change your regression gives you
into a proportional change you can actually interpret — in either direction.

### Interpreting: the approximation

**Plain English.** For small numbers, `log(1.05)` is basically 0.05. So percentages and log-units
are nearly the same thing.

**Precisely.** `log(1 + a) ≈ a` when a is small:

| Expression | Approximately |
|---|---|
| log(1.01) | 0.01 |
| log(1.05) | 0.05 |
| log(1.1) | 0.1 |

**Where it breaks — quickly.** This is an approximation and it degrades fast as the change grows.
You would **not** say a 50% increase in x (a 1.5 proportional increase) is about a 0.5 increase in
log(x). That no longer works. Keep the change around **0.1 (10%) or lower**; above that, do it
properly.

**Worked example — three walkthroughs.** Start from the mantra and convert one stage at a time.

**Regress y on log(x):**

1. A one-unit change in log(x) is associated with a β₁-sized change in y.
2. Scale everything down by 100 so the approximation applies: a 0.01-unit change in log(x) is associated with a 0.01·β₁ change in y.
3. A 0.01-unit change in log(x) is approximately a **1% change in x**.
4. → **A 1% increase in x is associated with a 0.01·β₁ unit increase in y.**

**Regress log(y) on x:**

1. A one-unit change in x is associated with a β₁-unit change in log(y).
2. Scale down if needed — if β₁ is already around 0.01 you're fine; if β₁ is 3, you've got work to do. Divide by 100: a 0.01-unit change in x is associated with a 0.01·β₁ change in log(y).
3. Convert the log change to a percentage.
4. → **A 0.01-unit change in x is associated with roughly a β₁ percent change in y.**

**Regress log(y) on log(x):** conveniently the approximations cancel — whatever conversion you do
gets converted back — so you can work more simply:

→ **A 1% change in x is associated with a β₁ percent change in y.**

### The zero problem

**Plain English.** log(0) doesn't exist, and lots of real variables contain zeros.

**Precisely.** Logarithms can't accept negative values or zero.

- **Negatives:** rarely a problem. It's uncommon to have a variable taking negative values that you'd want to log anyway.
- **Zeros:** a genuine problem. Income is highly skewed *and* plenty of people earn zero, and you'd want them in your model. log(0) is undefined.

**The fixes:**

- **If the log is on the left** (log of y): you can probably drop the logarithm entirely and use something like a **Poisson regression**.
- **If the log is on the right** (log of x): you can't switch to Poisson. So people fudge — add one and take **log(1 + x)**, or use the **inverse hyperbolic sine** transformation, which does something similar.

**Where it breaks.** Both fudges let you estimate the model, include the zeros, and solve the skew
just like a regular log. But they **mess up the percentage interpretation**, particularly at
smaller values of x — which makes sense, because what is a percentage increase from a base of zero?
The calculations get weirder, and you want your variables to take large values, because things are
strangest near the bottom.

Which produces a genuinely odd result: your percentage interpretation works **better if you report
income in cents than in thousands of dollars**.

## TRAPS

- Outliers are bad data and should be removed → **Actually,** they're not necessarily wrong, and they *should* affect your analysis. The problem is undue influence, not their existence.
- Logs delete the influence of extreme values → **Actually,** they keep them in the data and scrunch them, so they still count without pulling as hard.
- A 50% increase in x is about a 0.5 increase in log(x) → **Actually,** the approximation breaks down fast. Keep it to about 10% or use the exact method.
- The coefficient on log(x) tells me about x → **Actually,** it tells you about a one-unit change in log(x). You need the conversion step.
- I'll just add 1 to my zeros and interpret percentages as usual → **Actually,** log(1+x) and inverse hyperbolic sine both distort the percentage interpretation, especially at small values.
- The unit of measurement doesn't matter for logged variables → **Actually,** with the zero fudges it does. Percentages behave better in cents than in thousands of dollars.

## SAY IT OUT LOUD

> Logs show up everywhere in social science, and there are exactly two reasons.
>
> **First: skew.** Think about wealth. Most people have a few thousand, maybe tens of thousands.
> Then there's someone with billions. And remember what OLS does — it minimises *squared* residuals.
> So it's fitting everybody nicely, and then it hits the billionaire and it's off by billions, and
> it warps the whole line to accommodate that one person.
>
> Worth being careful here: outliers aren't wrong data, and they *should* count. You just don't want
> them to have wildly outsized influence. And logs do exactly that — they keep the giants in your
> data but **scrunch them down** so they stop yanking the line around.
>
> **Second: some relationships should multiply, not add.** Regress my wealth on my investment
> return. If the rate goes up, my wealth doesn't go up by a fixed number of *dollars* — it goes up by
> a *percent*. A straight line can't say that. A log can, because log(x) plus log(y) equals log(x
> times y) — addition on the log scale is multiplication on the real scale.
>
> Interpreting them is the only annoying part. The mantra still works, it's just about a one-unit
> change in the *log*, which you don't care about. So you convert. And there's a beautiful shortcut:
> **log of 1-point-something is basically that something.** log(1.01) ≈ 0.01. log(1.05) ≈ 0.05.
>
> Which means for log(y) on log(x), it all collapses to: **a 1% change in x is associated with a β₁
> percent change in y.** Lovely.
>
> Just don't push it — that approximation dies fast. A 50% change is nowhere near 0.5 in logs. Keep

> And the practical headache: **log of zero doesn't exist**, and lots of people earn zero income. If

> interpretation near the bottom.

## RECALL

Q: Give the two reasons to take a logarithm.
A: To handle a highly skewed variable, and to model a relationship that's proportional rather than
additive.

Q: What makes a variable skewed in the sense that matters here?
A: A mass of relatively similar observations plus a few that are many times bigger — not merely the
presence of outliers.

Q: Why does skew cause trouble for OLS specifically?
A: OLS minimises squared residuals, so an observation it's off by billions on generates an enormous
residual, and the model shifts to fit that one point.

Q: Are outliers bad data that should be removed?
A: No. They aren't necessarily wrong and they should affect your analysis. The problem is only that
they can have undue influence.

Q: Give the proportional-relationship example.
A: Regressing wealth on investment return. A higher return rate doesn't add a fixed number of
dollars — it multiplies your wealth, so the relationship isn't a straight line.

Q: What log identity makes proportional interpretation work?
A: log(x) + log(y) = log(x·y) — addition on the log scale is multiplication on the original scale.

Q: You regress log(y) on x and get β₁. By what factor does y change per one-unit change in x?
A: y increases proportionally by e^β₁.

Q: You regress y on log(x). What's the exact effect of a 1% increase in x?
A: log(x·1.01) = log(x) + log(1.01), so log(x) rises by log(1.01) units, and y rises by β₁·log(1.01).

Q: State the approximation and its limits.
A: log(1 + a) ≈ a for small a — good to roughly 10%. A 50% increase is not approximately 0.5 in
logs.

Q: Give the interpretation for a log-log model.
A: A 1% change in x is associated with a β₁ percent change in y — the approximations cancel.

Q: Walk through y on log(x) using the approximation.
A: A one-unit change in log(x) gives a β₁ change in y; scale down by 100 to get a 0.01-unit change
in log(x) giving 0.01·β₁; a 0.01 change in log(x) is about a 1% change in x; so a 1% increase in x
is associated with a 0.01·β₁ increase in y.

Q: What values can logarithms not accept, and which is the real problem?
A: Negative values and zero. Zero is the real problem — negatives rarely come up in variables you'd
want to log.

Q: Your outcome is logged and contains zeros. What can you do?
A: Drop the logarithm and use something like a Poisson regression.

Q: Your predictor is logged and contains zeros. What are the options and the cost?
A: Use log(1 + x) or the inverse hyperbolic sine transformation. Both allow estimation, include the
zeros, and solve skew — but they distort the percentage interpretation, especially at small values.

Q: Why does the unit of measurement start to matter with those fudges?
A: Because the interpretation is weirdest near the bottom of the scale, so percentages behave better
if income is measured in cents rather than thousands of dollars.

## GLOSSARY

- **Skewed variable** — one where a few observations are many times larger than the bulk of the data.
- **Proportional relationship** — one where a change multiplies the outcome rather than adding to it.
- **Natural logarithm** — the log to base e; always what's meant in social science.
- **Log-log model** — both outcome and predictor logged, giving a percent-for-percent interpretation.
- **Inverse hyperbolic sine** — a log-like transformation that accepts zeros.
- **Poisson regression** — an alternative to logging an outcome that contains zeros.
